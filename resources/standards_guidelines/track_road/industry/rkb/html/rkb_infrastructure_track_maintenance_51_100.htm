Title Estimation of service life of rail welding joint concerning thermal stresses
 
Author Takao, K
Abe, N
Nagafuji, T
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Nowadays, rolling contact fatigue failure and rail welds bending fatigue failure are two main factors with regard to which great amow1t of rail renewal is carried out. In particular, the standard for continuous welded rails (CWR) renewal is practically decided by the rail welds bending fatigue. The authors have been tackling the estimating method of service life of CWR, that is to say. the rail welds bending fatigue damage. In this study, the earlier estimating method has been improved to be able to consider thermal stresses. Using the improved method, the influence of the difference ·in temperatures at installing CWR and that of rail surface grinding on the rail welds bending fatigue damage were analyzed. As a result, it was made clear that the influence of the thermal stresses on the bending fatigue damage of rail welds was about a few %, but that of the growth rate of rail surface irregularity was significant. Also the effect of periodical grinding of rail surface irregularity on extending the service life of CWR was understood.

Title Quality evaluation methods for rail welds in Japan
 
Author Tatsumi, M
Fukada, Y
Ueyama, K
Shitara, H
Yamamoto, R
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Kokubunji, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The subjects concerning rail welding processes in Japan are as follows: the application of GPW is expanded in Japan and/or overseas, and the reliability of the welds by fusion welding is promoted. And then, quality evaluation mechanism of GPW and improvement of reliability for fusion welding have been investigated. For GPW, the bulge removal by hot shearing method is applied in order to guarantee the quality of welds. This method, which makes it possible and easy to inspect the weld right after welding, has high workability as well as high reliability. This method is a kind of appearance inspection, and cracks formed by hot shearing on the surface of weld can be easily detected. These cracks occur due to plastic deformation generated by hot shearing. The bulge removal by hot shearing method is applied to most of welds by GPW in Japan. Therefore, the reliability of welds by GPW gets better. In Japan, welds performed by fusion welding such as EAW and ATW have less reliability than those of FW and GPW, because of causing some weld defects. Moreover, the damage of weld by fusion welding mostly occurs from weld defects in rail foot area. Therefore, ultrasonic inspection has been applied for the welds by fusion welding. However, the conventional ultrasonic inspection method is not practical for reliability evaluation of rail foot. The way of ultrasonic inspection for rail welds has been reexarnined, and the relationship between inspection results and properties of welds has been investigated. As a result, it is made clear that the suitable ultrasonic inspection method in order to detect discontinuity in rail foot is a double probe technique at rail foot side. The relationship between weld quality and property, such as rupture load and fatigue strength, is clarified by applying this method to rail foot. Accordingly, the rail foot rupture in welded rail has been gradually reduced since adoption of this inspection method for the welds of fusion welding.

Title Maintenance optimisation for railway equipment
 
Author Vatn, J
Hokstad, P
Oien, K
Affiliation SINTEF Industrial Management, Trondheim. Norway
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract European research and railway companies are presently carrying out a project funded by the Commission of the European Communities: Modular system for Reliability and Maintainability in European Rail Transport (REMAIN). One of the main issues will be to apply reliability prediction methods to investigate the cost-effectiveness of using new railway condition monitoring equipment. This new equipment (in particular for monitoring of turnouts) has been tested out for some time, and a demonstrator line (Dortmund - Hamm) has started to take this in use. The paper describes a methodology for arriving at an optimal maintenance plan for railway equipment. Cornerstones of this methodology are the RCM (Reliability Centred Maintenance) and LCC (Life Cycle Cost) techniques. RCM takes advantages of the Failure Modes, Effects and Criticality Analysis (FEMCA) and a dedicated decision logic to determine the correct preventive maintenance action. Further, the use of LCC analysis is necessary both for evaluating the economics with installing a condition monitoring system, and the trade-offs with respect to balancing the maintenance effort. The reliability prediction techniques are illustrated on the specific demonstrator equipment (turnouts). From the results of the RCM and LCC analyses, it will also be assessed to what extent the new condition monitoring equipment is cost effective. The partners in the project are Deutsche Bahn AG (Germany), RENFE (Spain), ISAP (Greece), VAE Aktiengesellschaft (Austria), AMBIT (Greece), SINTEF (Norway) and Fraunhofer Institut IITB (Germany)

Title Economic aspects of track maintenance strategies
 
Author Veit, P
Affiliation Technical University Graz, Graz, Austria
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract International banking institutions ask for economic key figures as for instance the internal rate of return for project decisions in the field of track maintenance. Track maintenance is a running work mainly influenced by the inherent quality of the new track. This close relation between the main investment, the types of maintenance work, the useful economic life of track, and the influence of operation is best described by lifecycle- cost. In the presented economic evaluation model track-work is understood as a cyclic work including the laying of new track. The track maintenance policies to be economically evaluated range between two points of view referring to extreme different intervention levels: track laying without subsequent maintenance and therefore frequent replacement or keeping the track on an high level of quality by investing in maintaining. The evaluation method aims at describing the economy of track maintenance for financing decisions and supporting the development of an economic track-work strategy in demonstrating effects comparing existing track maintenance policies with new ones. Therefore a dynamic investment calculation based on life-cycle-cost was developed calculating the cash-flow, the net present value, the internal rate of return, and the annuity. Taking into concern the ownership of railways infrastructure the evaluation model allows financial and economic calculation of the key figures. The standard calculation is a financial one, the economic internal rate of return is calculated by adding economic effects like time savings of passengers and freight, reduction of accident follow-up cost- and others. For calculating economic effects in countries transforming towards a market economy the method of shadow prices allows to work with specific economic figures. It can be demonstrated, that the internal rate of return does not fit for all evaluations, specific problems aris~ in evaluating internal rationalisation investments. Therefore the presented valuation model is using the internal rate of return combined with the function of the net present value for evaluating projects.

Title Technological innovation in railway structure system with ladder track system
 
Author Wakui, H
Matsumoto, N
Inoue, H
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Rails canying trains along a fixed right of way are highly flexible under heavy stresses and must be tightly fastened to a strong base, the railway ties, at frequent intervals to maintain their true alignment. Today's concrete crossties were developed to replace wooden crossties, and naturally took the same shape. Since the crosstie system provides poor load dispersal, however, the crossties gradually sink under the repetitive loads applied onto the track. Lateral movement resistance in this system is also weak. From the 1940s to the 1960s, experiments were carried out in France, the Soviet Union and Japan with longitudinal sleepers laid in parallel pairs under the rails. The goal was to create a railway track requiring a minimum of maintenance. However, none of the experiments were successful. Based on this experience, the Structural Engineering Group of Japan's Railway Technical Research Institute has developed the "Ladder Sleeper," in which parallel longitudinal concrete beams are held together by transverse steel pipes. It is a unified precast plant-manufactured product in ladder format. The lateral steel pipes are embedded into the longitudinal concrete beams. Ladder Sleepers provide continuous support to the rails and act with them as composite rails. When laid in stone ballast, the resulting ground pressure is reduced and more even for the same overall weight as for concrete crossties. On viaducts and in tunnels, where it is desirable to lay a non-ballasted track, Ladder Sleepers still offer advantages. They can either be laid on a resilient mat on the concrete trackbed, or they can be installed as a floating track insulated from the concrete trackbed by discrete resilient mountings. The ballasted and non-ballasted Ladder Tracks result in ensuring train safety, assuring maintenance reduction, and environmental improvements, promising major progress in achieving railway efficiency and improving railway management.

Title Evaluation of life cycle costs of alternate tie (sleeper)/fastener systems and their use in defining maintenance policy and practice
 
Author Zarembski, AM
Gauntt, JC
Affiliation ZETA-TECH Associates, Cheny Hill, NJ, USA
Railway Tie Association, Fayetteville, GA, USA
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The selection of the optimum track system configuration is very much dependent on the performance requirements and economic characteristics of the rail operation that is being configured. One of the key decisions in selecting an appropriate track system is the selection of the proper cross-tie (sleeper)/fastener system. The range of available systems include a wide selection of cross-tie (sleeper) and fastener types and materials and their associated purchase price, installation cost, and maintenance activities. As a result, the selection of the economically "optimum" system will vary greatly based on purchase prices, material and labor costs, and requirements of the rail operation itself . In order to help in this decision making process, economic analyses models have been developed and implemented. These models are life cycle costing models that take into account not only initial costs but maintenance activities which occur over the life of the track system. By accurately accounting for these costs, and their associated timing, it is possible to evaluate alternate design configurations and cost structures in order to help select the system that is "best" for a given operation and geographic location. One such model, the Railway Tie Association' SelecTie Model, has been used by various railways in making decisions as to where to use different tie/fastener configurations and systems. The SelecTie Model has been widely used in North America, by railroads representing over 200,000 miles (300,000 kilometers) of track under a broad range of operating conditions to include heavy axle load freight and lighter axle load passenger operations. Its focus is to assist in the decision as to the most cost effective (on a life cycle basis) cross-tie material. The model, with its easy to use format and stucture, has been the basis for decisions as to what ties to use (wood vs. concrete) and the definition of the usage boundaries (in terms of curvature and tonnage categories).

Title Modern, accurate track measurement and analysis: a basis for economically viable track diagnosis
 
Author Zuck, H
Rechel, M
Hohberger, H-J
Affiliation Deutsche Bahn AG, Minden, Germany
Deutsche Bahn AG, Munchen, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The need for a modern form of track inspection on the network logically leads to four main requirements being made on track recording cars. 1.) Measurement precision Adequately precise measurement, reliability and calibration are basic requirements. Adequate measurement precision is needed to ensure that differences between results obtained in two successive measurement runs are reliably recognized. Reliability is needed because an automated on-line evaluation of track condition follows the measurement process and an automatic unit only hardly may differentiate between correct and incorrect input. The calibration requirement is essential to high measurement precision and reliability. The track-parameters to be detected are: Gauge, cant, curvature, alignment and level. Precise track synchronization is also needed to make it possible to identify the track location to which the parameters apply. 2.) Precise error recording and detection of persistent errors A track bed error is a deviation from the originally intended positioning over a set line length. Using conventional systems such errors can only be detected with distortion. A modern track measurement system must be able to detect such errors precisely and accurately. Increasing speeds mean that a new system must also be able to detect errors over 50 meters reliably. Unfortunately, the conventional systems lose sensitivity so rapidly with error length that these are no longer recognizable or only recognizable with difficulty. A track measurement system sensitive enough to detect long track bed errors is thus essential. Research from DB AG confirms that these requirements can only be met by state-of-the-art inertial systems, for example those using high-precision gyro platforms.; 3.) Measurement speed and the influence of signaling technology Since normal operations are invariably disturbed by measurement runs given today's traffic density, future track recording cars must be designed to reduce this disturbance to a minimum by performing their functions at the highest' possible speed. The maximum speed for modern track recording cars of 250 km/h is already essential. To avoid the sensor equipment mis-activating the signals on the line, the measurement technology used must not completely change the characteristics of measurement rolling stock. Speed variation for operational reasons, such as acceleration and braking, must not influence measurement precision. Since any form of mechanical scanning influences permissible speed and track recording car characteristics, scanning the rails must be contact-free. 4.) Easy-view of the results as basis for maintenance steps Although the measuring- and data evaluation-technique is complicated it must be easy to understand, analyze and interpret the results of the measurements. For this purpose the data must be visualized within diagrams which also contain the limiting values based on inspection regulations. For a short overview the main parameters of 15 km track are shown on one sheet of paper in format DIN A 4. A more detailed information is given by a record on a scale of 1 : 5,000. All results and if wanted all primary data are stored on mass store devices for off-line evaluation.

Abstract The major part of the effort and money put into a railway system goes into a minor part of the total track length. Major bridges require significant expenditure during construction, but hopefully these areas are almost maintenance-free once the line is operating. This paper deals with a section of track that involved a major construction effort when it was built in the 1930's and today, 55 years later, is still involving far more than average maintenance for a length of railway line. The line from Unanderra near Port Kembla to Moss Vale was started in 1927 and finished in 1932, with much of the work being done by large gangs during the depression times. The first section of line, from Unanderra to Summit Tank on the edge of the plateau, rises for 750 metres. The gradient is at 1 in 30 passing across the face of a steep unstable excarpment, covered with rain forest and in an area of high rainfall. Due to its past strategic importance of transporting steel and industrial products from Port Kembla to southern New South Wales and Victoria, the cost of maintaining this line was never queried. In 1983, the Government gave approval for a new railway line to be constructed from Maldon on the southern line to carry heavy coal trains from western and near southern NSW, heading for the Port Kembla coal loader. This line joins the existing track on the escarpment, at Dombarton, and from there to Unanderra the track will be duplicated in this difficult section of country.

Source Permanent Way Institution (PWI)

Abstract The State Rail Authority's Way and Works Branch operates a section which is responsible for the ballast cleaning of the permanent way. The aims of this section are to maximise production whilst minimising expenditure. The benefit obtained by carrying out ballast cleaning is that the track structure, from formation level to underside of sleeper is returned to the as new condition. To appreciate this the following paper deals with the functions of ballast and why the ballast fails in its performance. The results of ballast and formation failure are related to causes of poor track drainage. To overcome these problems ballast cleaning plays a major role but in itself is not a solution. Ballast cleaning should only be considered along with track drainage and formation strengthening schemes, depending on the initial cause of the track failure. As far as the permanent way is concerned track failures are normally highlighted by the track geometry exhibiting rough riding qualities. At this stage a remedial action plan is required which may include track drainage, track reconditioning and ballast cleaning. Also geofabric covers may be required to strengthen the existing formation. The functions and operations of a ballast cleaner are then examined with a view to its resultant effect on the ballast structure. The process is detailed starting from the fouled ballast to the as new ballast profile. Typical production rates and costs per metre are analysed along with ballast return rates. These are compared to length of track possessions. The sections annual production and annual expenditure are compared to expected long term benefits and resultant drop in maintenance costs for the permanent way.
