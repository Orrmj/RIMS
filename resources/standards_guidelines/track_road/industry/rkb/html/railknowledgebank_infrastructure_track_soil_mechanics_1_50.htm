Abstract The quality of compaction of large-sized ballast particles with uniform gradation plays a vital role in trackbed performance of heavy-haul railroads; however, there are currently no unanimously-accepted techniques or quality control criteria available for ensuring adequate compaction of railroad ballasts in either laboratory experiments or field practices. This paper presented preliminary results of a laboratory gyratory compaction study where a real-time particle motion sensor (i.e., SmartRock) was employed to investigate particle movement characteristics during ballast compaction process and provide preliminary meso-scale explanation about compaction mechanisms. The results of this study can provide technical insights and guidance to field compaction of ballast layers in heavy-haul railroads.

Author Lane, J

Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Most railway embankments in the UK were built in the Victorian era and are of end-tipped construction, often using clay excavated from adjacent cuttings, resulting in a clod-and-matrix structure. The mechanical behaviour of such railway embankments is not well understood, and where the embankments are constructed from plastic clay fills, they can be vulnerable to significant irreversible strain. This strain can contribute to permanent track damage (e.g. loss of vertical track alignment) as a result of deformation of the earthwork. Embankment damage due to increased railway traffic has already been observed around the GB railway network and in the next decade railway traffic, particularly freight, is forecast to grow considerably. Consequently there is a need to improve the fundamental understanding of how increases in rail traffic loading may influence the behaviour of embankments constructed of clay fill of medium to high plasticity, and in turn how this can influence the track performance. The Rail Safety and Standards Board (RSSB) in conjunction with Network Rail (NR), has undertaken a unique programme of applied research into this topic, which is aimed at providing the means of predicting the response of the embankment to the applied loading. Analytical models, calibrated against the results of laboratory testing, have been developed which provide a basis for assessment of the embankment response to train loading. These models have been incorporated into an assessment framework which will provide a basis for discussion of the need for embankment remediation with the UK Office of Rail and Road (ORR), particularly where the growth of rail traffic/tonnage is predicted to require frequent interventions for track maintenance to correct the vertical track alignment. The framework will enable strategic and tactical level assessments of the capacity of an embankment to resist the applied loading. The assessment framework will also provide the capability to enhance reliability and availability of the infrastructure through the identification of prioritised and targeted embankment remediation works. The research will also contribute to the development of an economic cost model for embankments which can be used to support proposals for increased rail traffic, and for embankment remediation works, which are properly prioritised and necessary for increased network capacity. The ability to undertake the assessment of earthwork capacity to carry increased tonnage and axle loads, will contribute to the provision of route-based infrastructure capability data across the GB network.

Source Informa

Publication Date 2013-11
Abstract One of the main issues facing the construction of high speed trains traversing soft soil deposits is the development of critical velocity issues, including Rayleigh ground wave transmission and track resonance problems. As train speeds approach the ground natural speed high track amplifications can occur leading to the development of a ground Mach Cone and associated Rayleigh wave propagation to the far field. Several sites where this occured have been reported in the literature, e.g. Ledsgard in Sweden for the X-2000 trains (speeds of only 230 km/h). New high-speed developments are looking towards operational speeds in excess of 350 km/h where there is an increasing likelihood that strong Rayleigh ground wave transmission may develop at some locations, particularly if one of the train loading frequencies coincides with a particular ground natural frequency; potentially developing both conditions of Rayleigh wave velocity and track resonance. In this paper the ssue of track dynamics is investigated using results from 3-dimensional finite element simulations. In particular the relationship between ground wave transmission developed through Rayleigh wave velocity issues versus ground transmission through resonance effects, is explored. The work clearly shows the development of the ground Mach Cone and trailing Rayleigh waves as train speeds increase from sub to super critical velocities. This is then compared to ground transmission from track resonance effects and the associated transmission above the ground cut off frequency. The results are expressed in terms of peak displacements and velocities and the influence of the coupled train loading mechanism and ground non-linearity using the model highlighted. Recommendations are made in terms of track response at these critical speeds.
