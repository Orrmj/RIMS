Abstract The mainline railway track between Dublin and Belfast was constructed during the 1850s. Large sections of embankments were constructed over very soft peat bogs using fascines. Title Development and testing of geosand composite layers using a pumping index test
 
Author Burns, B

Geotechnics, Bristol, UK
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Conference Railway Foundations: RailFound 06: 1st International conference on railway foundations: applied research and application to underpin improved railway track design and performance, 11-13 September 2006, Birmingham, UK
Abstract Migration of fines from the subgrade soil into the overlaying ballast is a well known problem in the railway industry. It is normally remedied by incorporating a layer of sand between the ballast and the sub grade soil. Placing the sand blanket layer can be time consuming and expensive. Thus the use of an easily constructable more cost effective product is desirable. A new pumping index test has been developed and this paper shows how the test was used to help develop a geocomposite product as a replacement for the sand blanket layer. A prototype was developed by assessing its performance compared to the sand blanket layer. Four criteria were assessed; settlement, stiffness, sub grade strength, and amount of migrated clay. The pumping index test was used in the refinement of the design of the geocomposite and provided a quick, low cost development tool, prior to the product being put forward for larger scale or field trial testing.

Title The effect of climate change on the stability and settlement of railway embankments
 
Author Vorster, P
Grabe, PJ
Jacobsz, SW
Affiliation GIBB (Pty) Ltd, Johannesburg & University of Pretoria, Pretoria, South Africa
University of Pretoria, Pretoria, South Africa
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract The changing climate is a reality in South Africa as evidenced by variable weather patterns and unprecedented cycles of drought, record temperatures and very intense rainfall events. Embankment failures that have occurred on the heavy haul coal export railway line between Ermelo and Richards Bay were attributed to high rainfall shortly after a drought period. Embankment stability is greatly dependent on the material used for construction, as well as climatic effects impacting on the moisture content of the fill material. Embankments constructed from soils of high clay content are adversely affected by the seasonal shrinkage and swelling, which, in turn, affects the serviceability of the railway line. In addition to conventional foundation indicator tests, valuable insight into the behaviour of railway embankments can be gained by considering the soil water retention curve of the embankment material. Tests conducted in a geotechnical centrifuge on embankment models constructed from clayey sand showed the severity of shrinkage cracks due to moisture loss. Embankment stability is greatly affected by seasonal cycles; especially when soils that are prone to shrinking and swelling are used for embankment construction, resulting in tension cracks at the crest during wet periods and shrinkage cracks during dry periods.
