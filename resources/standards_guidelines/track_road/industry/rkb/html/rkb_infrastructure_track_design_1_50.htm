Title A track and subgrade structure of railway suitable for 40-tonne axle load
 
Author Han, ZL
Ma, ZG, Chen, F
Qie, LC
Pan, Z
Xu, LS
Affiliation China Academy of Railway Sciences Corporation Limited, Beijing, China
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract The designing axle load of the Simandou Railway in Guinea is 40tonnes. Based on its line and operating conditions, a calculation model integrating track and subgrade was used to compare and analyze the track bearing force and deflection under different ballast thickness conditions, and the requirement of ballast and subgrade under 40-tons axle was proposed. In view of the technical requirements of the track components under 40-tons axle load trains, two types of fastener and tie were developed, and were tested according to the AREMA. Aiming at the poor performance of Guinean stuffing, the improved soil subgrade structure was developed. And a new Foamed Light-Weight Soil (FLWS)transition section structure was developed, which can solve the problems of difficult subgrade compaction and large settlement deformation in transition section. The actual model test of track and subgrade structure was carried out in lab, with a maximum axle load of 45-ton and 15 million fatigue tests. The results show that the newly developed track and subgrade structure`s strength can meet the requirement under 40-tons axle load. The cumulative deformation of FLWS was little under the large load fatigue condition, which was less than 0.1mm, so FLWS had the ability to resist dynamic deformation steadily.

Title Assessment of the standards for laboratory testing of rail fastening systems used on heavy haul lines
 
Author Herron, D
Cox, SJ
Affiliation Pandrol, Addlestone, UK
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Standards for the laboratory testing of rail fastening systems are now in use in various forms around the world. These standards are used to assess the suitability of a fastening system for use in-track and therefore relate design for safety, reliability and durability. Two of the main standards for testing fastening systems for heavy haul applications are compared here, supported by an analysis of measurement data obtained on a very demanding section of a heavy haul line. The aim of this work is to identify the aspects of these standard tests which are successful, those where there is scope for further improvement and in the case of the latter, to make proposals as to how this can be achieved. It is found that there is a good case for combining key ideas in the two major standards to achieve a test for durability that simulates the load and environmental conditions found in-track appropriately.

Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Brazilian railroads operate mainly on two different track gages: wide gage (1.60 m) and narrow gage (1.00 m). In some areas, the different tracks overlap and produce what is called a dual gage track. A dual gage track consists of three rails, allowing the passage of trains of two different track gages, in this case, 1.60m and 1.00 m. There are two gage rails positioned close together (one for each gage) and a third common rail further away. At these points in the network, two different gage trains can operate on the same track thereby avoiding the need to transfer the cargo to another train or to use a different transportation mode. Today in Brazil there are 515 km of dual gage track divided between different operators. As the Friction Management programs continue to expand in its heavy haul network, VLI (Brazilian logistics operator) recognized the need to install gage-face and top-of-rail trackside friction management systems in dual gage tracks territories. A unique dual gage trackside applicator, capable of applying on 3 rails, identifying which gage to apply was developed. A total of 8 of these units were installed in 2018. This paper will present the details of the project, the challenges with a new application and the installation.

Title Innovative methods to enhance the stability of continuous welded rail (CWR) track and railroads productivity
 
Author Bao, Y
Barenberg, EJ
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The reduction of ballast lateral resistance resulting from ballast tamping and other track maintenance acti vities (like tie replacement, track raising and surfacing) accelerates track panel lateral shift and temporatily decreases track stability. Under certain conditions, this can cause CWR track to buckle in hot weather. Therefore, most railroads issue the slow order to the fo llowing trains on tamped CWR track until a certain tonnage has raised over the track. Slow orders decrease the producti vity of railroads on main lines and take a long time for the lower traffic lines to have them removed. This problem stirs railroads to look for a new method to recover track stability and to remove the slow order. Based on the theoretical analysis of track stability using the recently developed 3-D track model--ILLIBUCKLE, this paper presents two new methods to enhance track stability: Cross-Bracing-Bar and Stiffening-Pile Structure. Cross-Bracing-Bar Structure is recommended to improve track stability temporarily and can be removed after the recovery of ballast lateral resistance for the future application. Stiffe ning-Pile Structure is suggested to increase track stability permanently at a specific location such as a sharp curve or track with naturally weak ballast lateral resistance. With the application of these two structures, track stability after ballast tamping can be improved to a level which can be equal to or higher than that before ballast tamping. Theoretical analysis concludes that s low orders due to the reduction of ballast lateral resistance can be reduced or eliminated with the help of two suggested structures after ballast tamping under the certain conditions.

Author Bogacz, R
Affiliation Polish Academy of Sciences, Warsaw, Poland
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The development of the high-speed railway systems goes together with experimental and theoretical studies on dynamics of the train-track interaction. Particular attention is paid to the complex problem of generation of rails corrugation and wheels polygonization. The paper is devoted to this problem. The new results of studies on wheel/rail interaction are quoted which show that the dynamic overloading is greater as it was previously assumed and sufficiently large to cause plastic deformations. The inelastic effects are responsible for change of initial residual stress distribution, which during long time of operation is uniform. When the residual stress distribution reaches a critical level further plastification process is unstable. Loss of stability leads to initiation of corrugations or polygonization. Some results of numerical analysis and measurements are presented.

Title Computer simulation & laboratory testing: a way to minimise track testing time
 
Author Eickhoff, B
Benyon, J
Affiliation BR Research, Derby, UK
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Track testing has traditionally been widely used within the railways to assist in the development and optirnisation of new and modified roiling stock but this approach is increasingly under threat for a number of reasons and practical alternatives are needed. On-track testing has become increasingly expensive over recent years as track access charges frequently have to be paid, arranging train paths is complex and the availability of vehicles is limited. Additionally, by the time a new or modified vehicle is available for testing it is late in the development process and if problems are found it is expensive to make design modifications. Alternative methods to speed the development process, identify problems early and minimise the costs of testing are therefore of great importance. Computer prediction methods have developed significantly in recent years and can now address a wide range of vehicle design issues; structural questions, suspension behaviour, aerodynamics, braking performance etc. However all of these methods need careful validation and are dependent on reliable and realistic input data for accurate results. Work at BR Research has shown how suitable combination of theoretical computer modelling and laboratory testing of components, assemblies and vehicles can be used to give an accurate view of the behaviour of a vehicle whilst it is still relatively simple to make any necessary design changes. When the vehicle is then tested on the track there should be no surprise results. The behaviour should already be well understood and the tests serve as a confirmation that the various systems are working as designed. This approach can significantly reduce the amount of track testing required, and hence its cost, whilst also reducing the amount of time taken to bring a new vehicle into service. It will therefore have a significant effect on the cost effectiveness of the railway system as a whole.

Title Peaceful coexistence between A.C. and D.C. systems
 
Author Smulders, H.W.M
Koopal, R
Affiliation Movares. Utrecht. The Netherlands
Prorail. Utrecht . The Netherlands
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In The Netherlands a densely populated country, due to reasons of available space it is necessary to bundle infrastructure as much as possible. Therefore the new High Speed Line (50 Hz a.c. system) runs in parallel with the conventional line (1.5 kV d.c. system) over a length of approximately 20 km with a minimal distance (11 m). This causes an inductive and galvanic coupling between both systems. This lead to limitations in the use of the a.c. system. In order to increase capacity and make the system attractive to customers, the compatibility issue had to be addressed. Title The estimation method of wheel load and lateral force using the axlebox acceleration

Publication Date 2008
Abstract The track condition of Japanese high-speed railways, Shinkansen, is maintained by properly correcting and/or improving track irregularities. In general, interacting forces between wheel and rail, namely wheel load and lateral force can't be easily measured. On the other hand, it is very important to estimate wheel load and lateral force especially some values which are more than and/or less than prescribed thresholds, and locations at which those values take place, from the viewpoint of running safety of vehicles and the material deterioration of track components. There are some techniques for directly measuring wheel loads and lateral forces. However, they are so costly and fragile, that it is difficult to adopt them for frequent and regular measurement. This paper describes the method of estimating the extraordinary values of wheel loads and lateral force and their locations by using the axlebox acceleration which can be easily measured.
