Title Substantial cost reduction by intelligent management of railway ballast
 
Author Wenty, R
Affiliation Plasser & Theurer
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2003, Investing in Australian Rail - Strategies and Solutions, 17-19 November 2003, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2003
Abstract In the course of periodical maintenance, the target geometry of the track or the switch should be restored without substantial exchange of track material. This restoration of the target geometry is performed using levelling; lining tamping and stabilising machines while the ballast cross-section is formed by ballast profiling machines. The ballast bed is an important part of the system. It is required to distribute the forces caused by the rail traffic uniformly onto the substructure, to supply the track grid with sufficient resistance against lateral and longitudinal shift and to keep the track dry through good air and water permeability. Around 3000 to 5000 m3 of ballast are lying on every kilometre of a double-track route depending upon the track design and track spacing. The economical handling and administration of these gigantic quantities of material represents a great challenge for the railway and track infrastructure management. Today machine manufacturers are expected to contribute towards economical ballast management through intelligent concepts.

Title Track stiffness assessment

Civil Engineering Department, University of Caen, France
Centre d'Experimentation Routière, CETE - NC, France
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract A good track support and its homogeneity are very important parameters for long-term stability of track geometry and a good life cycle cost of the track. The overall vertical support is provided by various substructure layers including the ballast, subballast and subgrade layers. Weak modulus and non-uniform support from these layers can cause poor track geometry under traffic loading, which in turn will accelerate track component degradation and lead to the degradation of vehicle performance. Track reliability has nowadays become particularly important due to increased traffic densities, heavier axle loads, and shorter maintenance periods. To improve track maintenance efficiency as well as railroad operational performance, a continuous vertical track support measurement method was to be tested. We will present a feasibility research dealing with the suitable parameters to develop a new, non-destructive, continuous method to measure track stiffness have been tested. The aim of the research was to adapt the 'Portancemeter' to the track support measurement. This technique is implemented to measure the platform modulus currently in France. The improvements would be achieved through identification of high-priority track repair locations in order to aid in optimizing maintenance, and to observe track strength degradation. These improvements facilitate maintenance planning. This technique will be added to others tools used by SNCF for the assessment of the track support as the ground penetrating radar and platform drillings.

Affiliation Banverket, Borlänge, Sweden

Abstract Many currently available methods for the strengthening of subsoil require an interruption of railway traffic. Track and sleepers have to be removed, ballast, sub-ballast and often the embankment fill have to be excavated in order to perform the strengthening works. Such methods are complicated, time consuming and always very expensive. There is a desire to perform strengthening with a minimal impact on railway traffic without or with only marginal reduction of actual train speeds and axle loads. Practical application of such methods considerably eliminates the costs for remedial measures and expenses due to traffic interruption. The paper provides an overview of those methods that can be utilized for the strengthening of subsoil under existing railways with the minimal impact on train operation. The second part of the paper presents the description of a practical case where strengthening of subsoil using inclined limecement column walls is suggested.

Affiliation Hokkaido Railway Company, Sapporo, Japan
Toho Sheet & Frame Co., Ltd, Yachiyo, Japan
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract It is currently found that railway operators employ polycarbonate plate in passenger room windows in a railway vehicle to prevent the damage to the window, rather than traditionally used glass. The introduction of this method, however, is disadvantageous for application in limited express trains due to heat insulation, dew condensation and noise problems despite its cost-saving effect. Meanwhile, composite polycarbonate and double-grazing unit commercially used in Shinkansen bullet trains known as polycarbonate composite) includes such drawbacks as limited size of polycarbonate composite material to be processed and high cost, three times that of normal tempered glass. Also, glass can be damaged when ballast hits the window at high speed after bouncing from the ground and doesn't pass through the window due to its inability to completely absorb the resulting impact in a thin resin layer placed between the polycarbonate and glass. To solve these technical problems, a new method for coating existing glass windows with a polycarbonate plate with a certain space (guard by polycarbonate) was found. This method can protect glass windows from damage by absorbing the impact of ballast with a high-strength polycarbonate plate and removing the resulting deflection by the space provided between the polycarbonate and glass. This method is effective in remodeling existing train vehicles at a minimal level and improving shockproof property at low costs. Despite this significant advantage, however, there have been many reports in this 'guard by polycarbonate' method, in which dew concentration between the polycarbonate and glass was found in some vehicle cars to make the glass obscure. Moreover, additional problems, such as larger train weight and higher installation costs, must be solved. By examining the advantages in the guard by polycarbonate method, JR Hokkaido developed the Window Unit of Insulation Glass & Polycarbonate (IGP), composed of a polycarbonate plate (8mm thick), a glass (4mm thick) and an air layer (10mm thick) provided in between via a dedicated spacer with butyl rubber, installed in a stainless frame through packing, etc.

Title Study on under-floor flow to reduce ballast flying phenomena
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Ido, A
Saitou, S
Nakade, K
Iikura S
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Between the under-floors of cars and tracks, there are complex flows caused by interaction between them. Flow is a main cause of the ballast flying phenomena and snow-accretion on the under-floor of the cars. Accreted snow dropping from running trains can cause flying of ballast. To study under-floor flow the under-floor flow was measured in on-track tests and confirmed that smoothing the under-floor of the cars made the under-floor flow decrease. To investigate the possibility of reduction of the impact of the under-floor flow by modification of under-floor shape, wind tunnel tests were carried out. Comparing the under-floor flow in on-track tests and in wind tunnel tests, it was verified that the under-floor flow in the on-track tests was reproduced in the wind tunnel tests. The velocity profiles of the under-floor flow were measured. The more the under-floor shapes were smoothed, the more the under-floor flow decreases. A model running facility was developed that enables the estimation of the velocity of the flow on the ground when the train passes by. A study was performed on a new measure (passive flow control by deflectors with which car bodies are equipped) to reduce the snow-accretion. By wind tunnel tests, it was confirmed that, by means of deflectors, the velocity of the flow decrease and that the bigger the angle of the deflectors, the larger the velocity reduction effect is. To verify the effect of the deflectors to decrease the snow-accretion, wind tunnel tests were carried out using artificial snow particles. It was found that the snow particle flux decreased near the bogies by means of the deflectors and the effect of the snow particle flux reduction extended over large area near the bogies.

Title Improvement of aerodynamic properties of high-speed trains by shape optimization and flow control
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Krajnovic, S
Affiliation Chalmers University of Technology, Department of Applied Mechanics, SE-412 96 Gothenburg, Sweden
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Increase in speed of new high-speed trains has led to new requirements for improvement of their aerodynamic properties. Aerodynamic properties such as drag, crosswind stability, aero acoustics noise and upraise of ballast due to flow have to be treated simultaneously in a multiobjective optimization procedure. This paper demonstrates an efficient optimization procedure that uses metamodels in the form of polynomial response surfaces as a basis for search for optimal designs. Such simple models of objective functions make it possible to use genetic algorithms to explore design space. As a result of the suggested optimization procedure, a set of so called Pareto-optimal solutions was obtained that helps exploration of extreme designs and finding trade-offs between design objectives. Two examples are demonstrated for the purpose of the validation of the optimization procedure: optimization of a front of a train for the cross-wind stability and the optimization of passive flow devices (so called vortex generators) for drag reduction. Influence of turbulence models used in computer experiments on optimization procedure is explored. It was found that the choice of turbulence model influences the shape of the train with minimal lift force. Usage of Reynolds Stress (RSM) and RNG k e model was found to produce noisy data that prevented construction of response surface models.

Title Study on narrow gauge track mechanics for speeding-up to 160 km/h
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Conles, E
Novales, M
Affiliation University of A Coruna, Railways and Transport Group, A Coruna, Spain
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Nowadays, there are about 250,000 km of narrow gauge railway lines in the world (approximately 20% of the world's rail network), with a strong role in Africa, Eastern Asia, South America and Oceania. Traditionally, these kinds of lines have been considered as a low performance rail network, but they can be suitable, by means of certain upgrade works, for carrying out passenger services with a top speed up to 160 km/h. The current speed over such lines is not usually greater than 80-100 km/h (Japanese and Queenslander cases are exceptions), so the development of speeding-up projects would improve the capabilities of narrow gauge lines to save travel times and offer a more attractive service for potential customers. Therefore, this paper focuses, from the point of view of mechanics, on analyzing the physical effects that the increase of the maximum wheel load induces on the track components, taking into account the higher weight of the required new rolling stock, the quasi-static curving effects and the dynamic changes of wheel load caused by a higher speed. To evaluate these increases, four vehicle typologies have been considered. The current one is represented by a conventional (non-tilting) vehicle with an 8.5 t/wheelset static load and, as the most suitable for future operation, a tilting vehicle with a 12.0 t/wheelset static load and angle of body tilt of 5o has been adopted. Thereby, the effects of the maximum wheel load increase on the rails bending moment, on the rail's foot and head stresses, on the sleepers bending moment, on the pressure at the ballast-sleeper contact surface and on the stress at the top of the formation layer have been computed. Moreover, the behaviour of these mechanical quantities has been analysed depending on several parameters as the coefficient of ballast, the sleepers' dimensions, etc. As a result, the most suitable characteristics of track structure have been obtained in order to meet future requirements. Furthermore, the lateral deformation resistance of the track has been balanced with the increased transversal forces in order to avoid excessive permanent track displacements; in addition, track buckling conditions have been analysed for long welded rails in curves and tangent alignments. For the layout redesign, minimum radii have been fixed for several curve speeds according to the restrictions of unbalanced lateral acceleration experienced by passengers, and these radii were compared with the lateral deformation criterion results. Finally, it has been concluded the ability of narrow gauge track for the mentioned speeding-up, taking into account the need for the proposed upgrades in the alignment-layout and in the track structure.

Title Characterization and modeling of flying ballast phenomena in high-speed train lines
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Benigno, LJ
Gonzalez, E
Rodriguez, M
Rodriguez, M
Osma, S
Iglesias, J
Affiliation Universidad Politecnica de Madrid, Spain
SENER Tres Cantos, Spain
ADIF Madrid, Spain
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract A full scale experimental investigation of the flying ballast phenomena taking place in high-speed train lines is presented. The aerodynamic load associated to the flow induced by the train passage has been characterized by Pitot-static and hot-film anemometry. Using the available Pitot-static data, each hot-film sensor undergoes an individual calibration for every flow realization. Ensemble averaged information of the mean and fluctuating air velocities at different height from the ballast bed is obtained as a function of the relative train passage time. The transport of ballast particles is characterized using high-speed video recording of particles positioned in selected locations of the track. The aerodynamic measurements indicate that most of the time the turbulent flow developing close to the ballast bed approximately follows a turbulent Couette structure that can be characterized by a friction velocity and a temporally modulated equivalent sand roughness, with the air velocity exhibiting strong fluctuating activity down to the surface layer located over the ballast bed. The flow structure is completed with strong perturbation events triggered by the passage of the rolling stock lower wall topology details. The high-speed video recording data allows construction of preliminary statistics of the motion of ballast particles. The aerodynamic measurements are used as input to simple models intended to describe some aspects of the ballast transport. The obtained preliminary results indicate that some scaling laws and transport trends can be captured by the proposed, simplified description.

Title Improving ballast tamping process
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Paderno, C
Affiliation Oxand Sàrl, Lausanne, Switzerland
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Under train loading, ballast particles rearrange and gradually induce an unhomogeneous settlement of the track. A tamping process is applied in order to restore the correct geometrical position of the ballasted track and in order to guarantee the safety and comfort of traffic. During tamping, penetration of the vibrating tines into the ballast causes a progressive rupture of the edges of the grains that generates smaller particles. On the other side, the traffic wears the ballast particles surfaces, which become rounder and smoother. Long term, this leads to a progressive loss of the elasticity and drainage properties, as the spaces between the particles are filled with these fine particles. The degradation of ballast angularity also induces faster settlement in the future, because the interlocking is less effective as the particles become round. The main consequence is that the interval between two tamping operations decreases during service life, from one intervention every 5 to 8 years to even twice a year! Hence, ballast degradation occurs both under train running, and geometry restoration. This vicious circle can be broken only by ballast renewal, as the tamping process is neither effective nor economic anymore. It has been estimated that the maintenance of the ballast and geometry represents 40 to 50% of the total railway maintenance budget during a track’s service life. These motivations lead to a PhD research at the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology in Lausanne. The aim is a better understanding of ballast degradation during traffic and tamping, in order to decrease the degradation of ballast during its lifetime by improving the ballast process. The research is organized in two parts: real scale laboratory tests and finite elements 2D and 3D simulation. This paper treats only the experimentation.

Title On the damaging effects of the ballast tamping operation
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Perales, R
Saussine, G
Milesi, N
Descantes, Y
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
RFF, Paris, France
LCPC, Bouguenais, France
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract The performance of ballasted railway tracks is largely controlled by the condition of the ballast bed. A typical active railroad cross-section is composed of a clean ballast layer, then a transition layer of mostly clean ballast and finally a fouled ballast (or subballast) layer directly in contact with the subgrade. Subballast is sand or gravel used to provide drainage and, along with ballast, to distribute the dynamic loading applied by trains into the subgrade. The ballast and subballast systems are commonly referred as the granular layer supporting track with a design thickness of typically 45–75 cm. Over time, ballast is progressively contaminated by the fine-grained aggregate of the transition layer and metal dust that fill the void space between ballast stones. This contamination is commonly referred as fouling. The accumulation of fine particles in the voids reduces the porosity, drainage properties and elasticity of ballast layers, which in time may create the conditions for undrained failure at certain depth below the track surface, when combined to heavy rainfall. In this respect, it was reported that the primary cause of ballast layer clogging was the degradation of aggregates, which accounts for up to 40% of the proportion of fouling materials. Tamping is a maintenance procedure applied to the ballast bed when critical levels of deformation and performance degradation of the ballasted railway track are reached. The tamping process consists in compacting the ballast below the sleeper through vibrations and pressure squeezing forces in order to restore the settlement of the substructure. This operation is carried out at each sleeper:
the sleeper is lifted to a desired level,
the tamping tines are inserted into the ballast through vibrations in order to facilitate their
penetration,

Publication Date 2011
Abstract This paper presents an analytical methodology for determining optimum intervention time for track surfacing based on economic minimization of maintenance and operating costs, as well as the application of the methodology to nearly 800 miles of Class 1 track. As part of the analysis, the track is divided into homogeneous analysis zones where optimum surfacing intervention times are determined for defined segments (typically a quarter-mile) based on actual history of tamping and track quality degradation unique to the individual segment. Track surface degradation in the form of surface quality index is developed for each segment as a function of cumulative tonnage and cumulative spot tamping following out-of-face surfacing.
