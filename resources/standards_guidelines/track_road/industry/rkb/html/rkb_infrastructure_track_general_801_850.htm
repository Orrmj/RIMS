Publication Date 1989
Abstract This paper describes work done jointly between Westrail, BHP Long Products Division and BHP Melboume Research Laboratories into the performance of interspersed steel sleepers. Two sites were established on Westrail's narrow gauge track, with the primary results indicating that through the adoption of careful installation procedures, similar rail seat loads and deflections can be achieved by adjacent timber and steel sleepers. Publication Date 1989
Abstract A number of commercial wheel/rail lubricants together with some specially formulated lubricants have been tested in-track at FAST and in laboratory tests. Some qualitative consensus has been developed, to show that the effect of graphite content on the performance of a calcium soap base grease is open to question. Title A survey of the causes of service failure of railway sleepers in Queensland
 
Author Nutt, WS
Affiliation Queensland government railways, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference First International Rail Sleeper Conference, 13-17 August 1973, Sydney, Australia
Publication Date 1973
Abstract As a prelude to a proposed in-depth study of the use of timber railway sleepers in Queensland, a survey was made during the 1971 calendar year to determine the causes of failure of sleepers condemned in the track. The survey was based on condemnation of sleepers in the course of normal annual sleeper testing by permanent way staff. Failures were classified in six categories which were recorded on standard field reports and subsequently processed. Decay accounted for 61. 7% of total failures, with spike kill following at 18. 5%. Results of the survey indicate that further investigation into methods to extend the service life of sleepers is warranted.

Abstract In constructing and maintaining railway track the Australian systems have traditionally employed sleepers cut from naturally durable timber. Except in Western Australia, the rate of consumption of such timber for railway and other purposes has exceeded the rate of replacement by natural regeneration, and it has been recognised for at least 20 years that some alternative to the naturally durable timber would have to be used, progressively, when the supply no longer met the demand. At this time, also, the price of Western Australian.sleepers escalated sharply and attention was given in South Australia and Victoria to alternatives to the traditional article.

The present situation of ties, treated and untreated. - Pressure treated ties have been used for twenty years. Except on a few branch lines, all ties have been replaced by treated ones,, From the tie service record, it has been found that the average life of treated ties is estimated to be only 12! years. This short service life is largely due to inadequate air seasoning, insufficient penetration of preservative and lack of tieplates. Efforts are being made to overcome these defects. In addition, double elastic fastenings are going to replace dog spikes.

Title Rail sleeper supply in Western Australia : 1976-1990
 
Author Forests Department of WA
Affiliation Forests Department of WA
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract Sleeper supply in Western Australia over the past 10 years has been characterised by the marked rates of change - embracing; variations in sleeper requirements - in terms of quantity, dimension, species, treatment and engineering standards; change in attitudes to timber and competing materials - commercial introduction of concrete sleepers - wide use of pressure treated sleepers - import of treated sleepers; change in the economics of sleeper production, installation, treatment and maintenance; decline in the timber resource for sleepers; decline in sleeper milling capacity, particularly from the traditional small sleeper mill - see Table I and Graph I. Change has brought continuing re-appraisal of alternatives and a need and desire among major users to keep their options as wide as possible. Change continues - and imposes on buyers and suppliers an imperative need for close communication, close co-ordination of buyer demand and supply - and clear indications of likely future demand and supply. The capacity of local sawmillers to service sleeper requirements remains substantial - of the order of 60,000 m at present, or over 800,000 sleepers per annum. It is capable of meeting a high proportion of expressed demand over the next 10 years, the extent depending on price relativities, dimensions, etc., together with any success which may attend industry efforts to develop Marri as a sleeper timber and to utilise small logs either as heart in sleepers or in fabricated sleepers. There will clearly be problems in meeting demands for heavy sleepers (over 130mm in thickness). After 1985 the level of demand as forecast will considerably exceed the sleeper cutting capability of the local timber industry.

Title Sleeper supply in Queensland
 
Author Department of Forestry, Queensland
Affiliation Department of Forestry, Queensland
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract Initially most sleepers in Queensland were either hewn with broad-axe at felling point or at ramp in the bush or produced from sleeper blocks delivered to strategically-located Government Railway Sleeper Mills, or by private sawmills. The main limitation in the extremely labour intensive operation of hewing was that the trees had to be reasonably free splitting, as well as being of acceptable species. Better job opportunities, increasing affluence, and availability of machines after the War saw the transit ion from hewn sleepers to sleepers produced more cheaply by buzz saws and from a standard of log unusable by the Broadaxe men. The Railway sleeper mills were generally located on rail, drawing blocks from the fringe of the main hardwood sawmilling industries and operating mainly on the forests of heavy, durable narrow leaved ironbark which were not highly sought after by the sawmilling industry. These forests were generally regarded as "Sleeper Bushes". As the overmature and mature components of the hardwood forests diminished with logging, the competition for logs from these forests became more acute and the sleeper mills accepted logs of a lower standard, poor form, smaller size, and less acceptable and more difficult species. As with the hewn sleepers. this changing pattern of utilisation within the Railway sleeper mill supply areas forced the Queensland Railways Department to reconsider its method of obtaining supplies and resulted in the closure of nine Railways sleeper mills in the last 15 years, and currently there are none in operation. Sleepers now come mainly from a number of privately-owned sawmills specifically designed to produce sleepers as well ns from the forestmil type of plant, developed, with several variations, from the buzz saw principle of the saw moving through a stationary log.

Title Timber for railway sleepers in New Zealand
 
Author Whiteside, ID
Affiliation NZ Forest Service
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract All timber rail sleepers in New Zealand are of preservative-treated radiata pine. Saw logs for sleeper production are obtained from the country's extensive radiata pine plantations, and future supplies are virtually guaranteed. Sleeper production is not an objective of forest management, but sufficient suitable logs for sleepers will be produced incidentally regardless of the management regime followed. Radiata sleepers conforming to a fairly exacting quality specification and treated with oil-borne preservatives are giving very satisfactory service life. Co-operative research between NZ Railways and the Forest Research Institute of the NZ Forest Service continues in attempts to find and test alternative treatments that will reduce costs, improve performance, or reduce objections on environmental grounds.

Author Campbell, KD
Affiliation Cement and Concrete Association of Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract Internationally the concrete sleeper is recognised for iJs superior technical advantages, durability and cost over alternative sleepers. The only major system which does not use concrete sleepers in large amounts is the U.S. A. where the track beds are considered to be generally poor quality compared with European Tracks. The ability of the heavy concrete sleeper to stabilise tracks with long welded rails is a well known fact, Since the last International Sleeper Conference these advantages for concrete sleepers have been proven in Australia. There now exists an efficient concrete sleeper manufacturing industry and efficient installing methods for both new track and cyclic replacement, The adoption of the concrete sleeper in the track structure heralds the end of a random decaying system requiring constant inspection and maintenance. The aim of the Railways managements must be directed towards reducing the proportion of the revenue dollar spent on maintenance. It cannot be reduced by continuing with labor intensive tasks.

Title Producing a specification for preservative treated rail sleepers
 
Author Tamblyn, N
Affiliation CSIRO
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract At one time in the past, most Australian rail systems could be kept supplied with timbers which performed very well as rail sleepers without preservative treatment. These were typically heavy eucalypts which combined adequate strength and other desirable properties with sufficient natural durability to give service for 20-30 years or more under widely varying climatic, biological and mechanical hazards. Over the last several decades, this favourable supply position has been slowly changing until today most Australian rail ways are accepting a considerable proportion of timbers which would benefit from preservative treatment. In addition there are other timbers, particularly exotic conifers, which cannot be used safely as rail sleepers without preservative treatment. As yet, preservative treatment of rail sleepers is only a small industry in Australia, practised mainly for mining company railways and only to a small extent for Government rail systems. As no Australian standard exists for treated rail sleepers, specifications have generally been applicable only to the local requirements of a particular contract and at least in some cases have not been well conceived. The Rail Sleeper Association has therefore decided that a specification should be prepared which would attempt to cover most Australian sleeper timbers and most conditions of service. A draft of this specification has now been prepared, which in the treatment sections, is largely an evolution from the paper presented by the author at the first International Rail Sleeper Conference. In the sections dealing with dimensional tolerances and wdod quality it has borrowed largely from the Australian standard for untreated sleepers, crossing timbers and bridge transoms. This specification can certainly be tmproved and any comment or criticism will be welcomed by the drafting committee. The purpose of this paper is lo discuss briefly some of the requirements which have been set, to explain some of the difficulties which have had to be met and to thereby assist your informed comment - now or in the near future.

Title Specification for preservative treated rail sleepers for use in Australia second draft (May 1976)
 
Author Tamblyn, NE
Affiliation Standards Committee Rail Sleeper Association, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract In the past, Australia has been fortunate in having a generous supply of heavy, strong, naturally durable timbers, very suitable for use as rail sleepers and crossing timbers. Under most conditions these timbers have given long service without any preservative treatment. Over the years the supply of sleepers from elite timbers has gradually dwindled and it has been necessary for railways to accept an increasing proportion of sleepers cut from other species with less desirable properties. Today, many species are accepted which would benefit from preservative treatment and there are also a number of other timbers which cannot be safely used for rail sleepers unless treatment is given. These many timbers, totalling about 100, include a wide range of eucalypts, some rainforest hardwoods and both indigenous and exotic conifers. Their properties vary so greatly that in this specification it has been considered necessary to classify them into 4 groups so that preservative composition, retention, and penetration can be adjusted to the natural durability of a species and to other properties affecting its use as a rail sleeper. With some species, classification is still tentative because all properties have not yet been determined, In a large country such as Australia, with its diverse climates, intelligent preservative treatment of rail sleepers must take into account the type and severity of the exposure hazard as well as the inherent properties of the timbers available. Thus a hot dry inland climate typically imposes a severe weathering hazard with a negligible decay problem, while in a warm wet tropical climate the position is largely reversed. To cope with such extremes in the exposure hazard this specification has recognized 3 climatic zones and has-attempted to meet their different requirements by varying the fungicidal content of the preservative oils. No treatment of rail sleepers should be considered reliable if the amount of preservative used can be expressed only as a mean retention for each charge. In addition to this mean figure it is essential to be reasonably assured that the spread of retentions for individual sleepers is being controlled within acceptable limits. Where sleepers are seasoned before treatment commences, this can be established by regularly weighing some sleepers before and after impregnation so that individual retentions can be calculated. Unfortunately, this simple control method cannot be applied to sleepers dried in the cylinder by the Boulton process. As this process is part of a whole treating operation in which drying and impregnation are overlapping and inseparable, the weight change in an individual sleeper is an unreliable index of preservative retention. For this reason, in a Boulton treatment, establishment of preservative retention in individual sleepers must rely on assay of borer cores or plugs. Although this assay ls simply achieved by solvent extraction of each sample, the small size of the sample and the possibility of local variation in the treated wood do indicate that cautfon is necessary in applying the result to the whole sleeper. In this specificatiun the section (6.4) requiring the establishment of individual sleeper retention has therefore been left somewhat open until judgement can be based on wider commercial experience than is available at present.

Title The performance of creosote-treated eucalypt sleepers in a dry inland area of New South Wales
 
Author Johnstone, RS
Affiliation Forestry Commission of New South Wales, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract In the proceedings of the International Rail Sleeper Conference held at Sydney in August, 1973, preliminary details were given by Johnstone (1) of an exploratory test of creosote-treated eucalypt rail sleepers of a range of species. The object of the test was to assess such factors as procurement, preservative treatment, installation and performance. This test was initiated by the Public Transport Commission of N. S. W., the treatments w.ere carried out by Koppers Australia Pt:y. Ltd. , and the overall supervision undertaken by the Forestry Commission of N. S. W. Some important points derived from the processing of the sleepers were: (1) Boultonising with creosote produces good results with most eucalypts. (2) lncised timber gives significantly better treatment than timber not incised. (3) A great difference exists between the treatabilities of different Eucalyptus species. The treated sleepers were subsequently installed in the rail way track between Dubbo and Coonamble in July, 1971, and an assessment for defects carried out in April 1976.
