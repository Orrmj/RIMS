Title Fault diagnosis for railway point machines based on sound analysis
 
Author Sun, Y
Cao, Y
Xie, G
Wen, T
Xie, M
Affiliation School of Electronic and Information Engineering, Beijing Jiaotong University, Beijing, China
National Engineering Research Center of Rail Transportation Operation and Control System, Beijing Jiaotong University, Beijing, China
Shaanxi Key Laboratory of Complex System Control and Intelligent Information Processing, Xi’an University of Technology, Xi’an, China
Industrial Research Institute, CRSC(Xi'an) Rail Transit Industry Group Co.,Ltd., Xi’an, China
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Railway point machines (RPMs) play an important part in railway systems. Condition monitoring for RPMs is a vital way to keep train operation safe and reliable. Considering easy-collection and convenience property of sound signals, a fast fault diagnosis method is proposed based on sound analysis. First, time-domain features are obtained and normalized using z-score standardization method. In order to select the most optimal discrimination features, binary particle swarm optimization (BPSO) is then utilized. Finally, the collected data set is divided into training set and test set. The optimal features of training set obtained by BPSO are used to train the support vector machine (SVM), while the test set is used to test the effectiveness of the proposed method. Experiment results indicate that BPSO can select the most significant discrimination features and eliminate the ineffective features which may reduce the identification accuracy. The identification accuracy on the test set reaches 100%, which demonstrates the proposed sound based fault diagnosis method for RPMs is feasible. Besides, the proposed method is easy to be implemented in practice. It provides the possibility of automatic fault diagnosis and condition monitoring for RPMs, which can provides technical supports to the maintenance staff, to reduce manpower and material resources.

Title FOSphAT: fiber optic sensing for permanent and holistic assessment of track
 
Author Vidovic, I
Marsching, S
Affiliation Institute of Railway Engineering and Transport Economy, Graz, Austria
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract For the assessment of railway tracks, infrastructure managers use various measurement and evaluation methods. Thus, in-depth knowledge about track condition, rolling stock and executed maintenance actions is necessary for the assessment and can only be achieved by data analyses, data interpretation and the derivation of appropriate actions. The assessment of a track's condition is carried out mainly by analyses of data obtained with track-recording vehicles and manual inspections. Furthermore, wayside train monitoring systems (WTMS) are deployed at strategic locations and measure the load of trains, the condition of its wheelsets or the running performance. As a track needs to be maintained within its life cycle, several maintenance actions are executed. None of the actual used monitoring and measurement methods provide a continuous and comprehensive monitoring of railway infrastructure and rolling stock. This contribution elaborates the potential of distributed fiber optic sensing for railway infrastructure condition monitoring. Distributed sensors deliver continuous and permanent information for track evaluation by recording sense pressure changes in an optical fiber. These cables can be easily installed alongside specific track sections – in a cable trough or on the rail foot.

Title Detecting singular track defects by time-frequency signal separation of axle-box acceleration data
 
Author Baasch, B
Roth, M
Havrila, P
Groos, JC
Affiliation Institute of Transportation Systems, German Aerospace Center (DLR e.V.), Berlin, Germany
Institute of Transportation Systems, German Aerospace Center (DLR e.V.), Braunschweig, Germany
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Singular railway track irregularities, such as squats and corrugation have a major impact on the ride stability, noise emission, comfort and safety of freight and passenger trains. Therefore, the detection and monitoring of such defects play an important role in railway track maintenance. Embedded low-cost sensors on in-service vehicles provide the opportunity of quasi-continuous condition monitoring of railway tracks and can thus enhance existing track maintenance strategies. In this paper we demonstrate a processing sequence to detect singular track defects from noisy axle-box acceleration (ABA) data. The data are acquired with a multi-sensor prototype measurement system on a shunter locomotive operating on the industrial railway network of the inland harbor of Braunschweig (Germany). A blind signal separation (BSS) algorithm based on non-negative matrix factorization is applied to the ABA data in the time-frequency domain. It is completely data-driven and hence does not rely on a priori knowledge or physical models. The algorithm makes use of different time-frequency characteristics of the signal components and is thus able to separate quasi-continuous band-limited signal components from transient broad-band components. The magnitude of the transient components reflects the strength of track singularities along the track and can hence be used to detect and quantify short track defects. Through georeferencing the identified defects can be localized, mapped on the track and be used to guide specific maintenance actions. Additionally, the BSS algorithm shows the potential to reduce the dimensionality of the data without significant loss of information.

Title Ultrasonic phased array measurement of near-surface cracks in the railhead
 
Author Anandika, R
Lundberg, J
Stenstrom, C
Affiliation Luleå University of Technology, Luleå, Sweden
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Ultrasonic measurement is one of the non-destructive techniques used to inspect defects in the rail body. Ultrasonic measurement is known inappropriate to inspect near-surface defects because of a challenge, called dead-zone. It is signal noise or ringing phenomenon located at near-field in front of the transducer when measuring a material. Due to this, there is difficulty in analysing any measurement signal at this zone, including signals from near-surface defects. In this study, the dead zone was eliminated by attaching wedge to shift the location of noise signals relative to the near-surface defect locations. First, a known-depth defect was measured by using phased array ultrasonic testing (PAUT) to calibrate the ultrasonic equipment and found the best signal gain to eliminate diffuse scattering from defects. Second, a cracked spot on railhead was inspected at the right and left direction of the spot. After the measurement, the inspected spot was sliced into 0.65 mm-thick pieces. From those pieces, the actual crack depth could be observed directly. Based on those sliced pieces, the ultrasonic measurement results were verified. From this study, PAUT delivered accurate measurement result of 3.51-mm crack tip depth with an absolute error of 0.8% - 18%. This accurate result indicates that PAUT can be an alternative to inspect the near-surface crack in railhead.

Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract The trial of a novel rule-based point machine health assessment system was implemented on hundreds of assets across three Network Rail routes in the United Kingdom to investigate the potential for automatic diagnostics of point machine faults. Data was collected from the five most common types of point machine on the UK rail network. The system used a robust expert system approach to define rules using existing railway know-how that was powered by an autonomous feature extraction engine that converted point machine waveforms into phases and features that are understandable to maintenance engineers and that has been shown to be functional in the challenging environment of the operational railway. The results showed the ability to detect 40% to 70% of faults that result in point machine failures over one week in advance with accurate diagnosis of the fault modes. Some of the biggest challenges faced along the way involved introducing automatic diagnostic algorithms to the maintenance culture of the engineers in Britain and developing the required trust with the users. This system is intended to be used as a bridge between threshold alarming that is currently in use and modern machine learning methodologies that require complex training regimens.

Publication Date 2019
Abstract Today, track engineers face increasing cost pressure and strict budget restrictions. This leads to growing difficulty in legitimizing crucial maintenance and renewal measures. Hence, infrastructure managers must spend the available budgets as sustainable and efficient as possible. These boundary conditions require an objective tool enabling both, a component-specific condition evaluation and a preventive maintenance and renewal planning. The present research introduces such a tool for railway track based on innovative track data analyses. Based on the condition evaluation, regression models are applied to predict the quality behaviour in the future. These technical investigations are combined with economical and operational considerations to plan reasonable maintenance lengths for different components in the next years. In a next step, economic evaluations by means of annuity monitoring are executed to find the ideal point in time for the reinvestment of a track section. This information enables to decide whether a necessary measure should be carried out as a maintenance task or if the renewal of the section is the wiser decision. This methodology also allows for calculating an economic damage by neglecting the ideal point in time for reinvestment. Based on this economic damage, it is possible to rank projects in case of insufficient budgets and to spend the available money in the most reasonable way.

Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract This paper reports the results of field trials, developed within the Italian railway network, to demonstrate the potential of optical fiber sensor systems for monitoring large railway infrastructures. Condition monitoring is becoming a key part of the management process in order to control infrastructures (in an alternative way to traditional track monitoring techniques) in order to constantly guarantee rail operation and identify potential risk situations for railway traffic. Structural health monitoring of sensitive sites like bridges, viaducts and slopes potentially subject to landslides, can be directly performed in real time by measuring the geometrical rail track condition using optical fiber sensors directly installed or embedded in the track. The proposed hybrid fiber optics sensor system provides a key technology to support the management of large rail infrastructures, increasing their safety of operation and reliability, minimizing disruptions and allowing reactive maintenance procedures at low cost. The use of optical fiber sensors, with their multiplexing capabilities, extensive range of operation and immunity to electromagnetic interference, plays a key role to move toward an intelligent infrastructure with a special focus on sensitive areas. The proposed system, based on Fiber Bragg Gratings (FBG) sensors and Raman distributed temperature sensing (RDTS), measures in real-time the static strain and temperature spatial distribution along the tracks; from these data the geometrical rail track condition is calculated using a linear elastic model, providing early warning if pre-defined thresholds are exceeded in terms of longitudinal, horizontal and cross-level defects. The proposed technology has been first validated in railway laboratories and then on trial sites in railway operation.

Title A data model for heat-related rail buckling: implications for operations, maintenance and long-term adaptation
 
Author Jaroszweski, D
Fu, Q
Easton, J
Affiliation University of Birmingham, Birmingham, United Kingdom
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Heat-related rail buckling is a significant operational and safety issue for the railways of Great Britain (GB). Although continuously-welded rail is pre-stressed to a stress-free temperature of 27°C, degradation and local topographic and microclimatic factors can lead to failures occurring at lower temperatures. These buckle events can cause widespread knock-on delays. As a common risk mitigation approach, speed restrictions are imposed for forecasted heat events, with these measures also being associated with non-trivial delays and disruption. The recently published UKCP18 climate projections indicate that the frequency, duration and magnitude of heat wave events will increase in the coming decades. This paper presents a data model to explain the occurrence of heat-related disruption incidents on GB’s rail network. This model is built on historical delay data from Network Rail – the owner of GB’s railway infrastructure, given explanatory variables for important meteorological and infrastructure features from published research in the literature. The model is implemented at two scales. Firstly at the national scale, including all Network Rail operational routes, and secondly for the South East of England (climatologically the warmest part of the country and hence at perceived greater risk) including the operational routes of Anglia, Wessex and South East.

Title Rail short-wavelength irregularity identification based on wheel-rail impact response measurements and simulations
 
Author Sun, YQ
McClanachan, M
Wilson, A
Kaewunruen, S
Kerr, M
Cole, CR
Affiliation CRC For Rail Innovation
Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Qld
Conference 9th International Heavy Haul Conference (IHHC 2009), Shanghai, China, 22nd-25th June, 2009
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Long and short-wavelength dip defects unavoidably exist on railway track top surface. Several manual measurement methods and AK track measurement car were used to measure and identify these types of defects in a selected track section. The paper considers rail dip irregularities in detail when measured, the dip can be considered as two components. The first is the large, long dip profile, which can be measured by survey leveling. The other is a small short dip profile which is superimposed on the large profile and can be measured using the dip gauge. The corresponding measurement and processed data from AK car were analysed and compared with manual measurements. It has been found that the severities as indicated by the AK car for the short-wavelength dips in the band width of 0.1 - 1.0m were in agreement with those measured by the dip gauge. Simulation analyses were also carried out in order to fully understand wheel-rail impact behaviours due to dip defects using the VAMPIRE package and CRE-3D VTSD model. There was good agreement between the simulation results and the measurements from the AK car, and the simulations show that the impact accelerations are principally caused by the small dip profile.

The specific findings of these tests are;
The tests conducted on rubber pads show that the response at the axlebox in the 10- 100Hz range, due to equal magnitude force inputs at various wheels, is mostly composed of nearly equal contributions from both wheels on the measured axle (approx. 85%), with a small contribution from the other wheelset on the bogie. Abstract The presented results of the research are devoted to the issues of work planning and organization on rail grinding with the use of rail grinding trains. In the course of the organizational and technological analysis of the production process of rail grinding, its drawbacks in the used approaches are revealed and the ways of their elimination are offered. On the basis of the conclusions made in the article, a fundamentally new approach in the planning of technological influences is proposed. The developed classification of rail defects according to their importance is taken as a basis for the new approach. Based on the performed ranking of defects, the targeting of rail grinding works is determined on the basis of solving a stochastic problem, in which the process of making a decision on the necessity of grinding is represented by three states. Dynamic programming methods were applied to solve this problem. In order to form an optimal strategy, due to which the income of the company will be maximum and the expenses - minimum, the process structure is represented in the form of a matrix, the elements of which are the income values occurring at transition of rails from one importance group to another when they are affected by the grinding process. The matrices of transition probabilities and arising incomes depend on the possible solution alternatives available to the enterprise performing these works. The presented methodology of work planning for rail grinding contributes to accelerating the process of removing defects, which have prerequisites for catastrophic development and carry a direct threat of emergency situations. Defects which do not have any tendency for active development due to operating conditions of rail track are treated secondarily, if necessary, which results in economic effect without reducing traffic safety.
