Title Influence of the tie-ballast interface on transition zone performance
 
Author Wilk, ST
Stark, TD
Rose, JG
Sussmann, TR
Thompson, HB
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, United States
University of Kentucky, Lexington, United States
Volpe Center, Washington D.C., United States
Federal Railroad Administration, Washington D.C., United States
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Many locations of railroad track consist of abrupt changes in track stiffness and underlying substructure called transition zones. These regions historically experience greater track settlement than the surrounding track requiring more frequent track maintenance from railroad companies. This paper emphasizes how discontinuities at the tie-ballast interface can be indicative of future track geometry problems and further deteriorate transition zone locations. This is supported by non-invasive field instrumentation using video cameras to measure tie displacement, accelerometers to measure tie acceleration, and three-dimensional dynamic numerical modeling. Transition zones with reoccurring track geometry defects often display unsupported ties and field instrumentation and numerical modeling suggest this condition can further increase tie loading and ballast deterioration. Potential solutions to avoid discontinuities at the tie-ballast interface are balancing transient and permanent displacements between the bridge and transition zone, providing a cushioning layer at the tie-ballast interface, and ensuring good substructure support and ballast condition underneath the transition zone ties. Examples of these transition zones are included in the paper.

Title Distributed track condition monitoring using train-based telemetry systems
 
Author White, JR
Hardie, G
Affiliation Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract The monitoring of track in large railway networks is a challenging task. There are many attributes that cannot be monitored by track inspection vehicles or instrumented rollingstock, requiring the installation of potentially high-cost wayside monitoring units. Additionally, many rail networks do not have complete cellular coverage; as a result, wayside units outside the coverage area often must either be manually downloaded or transmit data by more expensive satellite links. A proposed solution to this challenge is the use of low-cost wayside monitoring units that can collect data over a period of time and transmit it to a passing train. These units require no cellular or satellite equipment but instead utilise a medium-range radio transceiver, with a corresponding unit on the train. The train-based unit can store the data until the train enters cellular coverage, eliminating the need for costly satellite communications. Additionally, power consumption on the wayside unit can be reduced by designing it to only transmit when it receives a signal from an approaching train, leveraging the available power on-board the train and reducing the amount of energy generation and storage required at the wayside unit. An initial prototype was developed using a pair of small single-board computers connected to radio modules. One was set up as a wayside unit, the other as a train-based unit. Software was developed to collect data on the wayside unit and then transmit it to the train-based unit upon reception of a signal from the train-based unit. Both ends were required to verify data integrity to ensure the data had been transmitted successfully. Testing in the lab yielded transfer rates up to 50KB/s (kilobytes per second). Initial field testing showed a range of approximately 500 metres either side of the wayside unit, with transfer rates falling with distance. Further field testing was then carried out to confirm the implementation’s viability in normal rail operations. The development of a train-based telemetry system that can collect data from wayside units as the train passes is a promising application of technology to solve the problem of monitoring track condition in large rail networks.

Publication Date 2017
Abstract Current rail infrastructure maintenance often requires personnel to be within the rail corridor in order to perform visual inspections. As such, inspections are inherently dangerous. A range of safety protocols have been introduced to minimise risks. However, there are still many instances in which costly track possession or occupation is required to inspect regions in which there are no clearly accessible safety zones or places. This project investigates the implementation of a small-scale multi-rotor unmanned aerial system (UAS) as a railway culvert inspection tool. In the current study the UAS is deployed at the entrance to a culvert and it then traverses through the culvert in a semi-autonomous fashion. This system allows engineers to safely conduct regular inspections and collect dimensional measurements without needing to enter either the rail corridor or the confined space itself. Further research is required to deal with the stability of a UAS in a confined space.

Moreira, LP
Godefroid, LB
Faria, GL
Candido, LC
Affiliation VLI, Belo Horizonte, Brazil
UFOP, Ouro Preto, Brazil
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Three different types of eutectoid steels are used in the FCA (Central Atlantic Railroad), a Brazilian railroad operated and maintained by VLI Company. Premium, Intermediate and Standard types of rails are classified depending on the presence of certain alloying elements and the heat treatment done on the rail head. This work characterizes the rail steel microstructure and determines the fracture toughness and fatigue behavior of five of those steels, coming from different countries. The microstructure was evaluated by light optical and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). Pearlite colony size and pearlite interlamellar spacing were measured. In addition, tensile, fracture toughness, and fatigue crack propagation tests were performed. Fracture surfaces of the specimens were analyzed by SEM to confirm the mechanism of material fracture. Metallographic examination showed the presence of fine pearlite in all steels, with a normal distribution of inclusions, but with significant differences between the microstructural characteristics. Mechanical tests showed a significant difference among the steels. The microstructural aspects were correlated with the results of fracture mechanics and fatigue crack propagation in order to show the effect of the microstructure on the mechanical behavior of the steels. This provides relevant information for an adequate selection of steel for railroad applications. It was confirmed that a more refined microstructure steels has greater tensile resistance, lower value of fracture toughness KIC and worse behavior in fatigue, with smallest value of fatigue propagation strength in crack growth region I. The fractography confirmed the brittle behavior of those materials. Through analysis results, we can recommend a rail metallurgical performance ranking.

Abstract Track support is a critical necessity for reliable railroad track infrastructure. Poor track support conditions can accelerate track condition deterioration. Specific examples include rail and ties that experience increased loading in these zones. These components often shed load in poorly supported zones as components fail or material properties degrade. Since the load must be reacted somewhere, the load shed from poorly supported ties tends to concentrate on the adjacent, well supported tie(s). Several measurement techniques are being adapted for continuous assessment of track condition including deflection measurements and ground penetrating radar. Title Applicability of the Niyama criterion to predict the volume fraction shrinkage porosity in aluminothermic welds
 
Author Sitimela, TM
Msibi, LL
Affiliation Transnet Freight Rail, Johannesburg, South Africa
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Aluminothermic or thermite welding is a very common process used to join two rails in the railway industry. Challenges associated with the process are the formation of macro and micro-porosity defects such as shrinkage porosity, gas and wormhole porosity. Current mathematical models used to predict casting defects are limited to casting processes and not thermite welding. This study aims to employ a developed mathematical criterion function that will predict not only the presence but also quantify the volume of micro-porosity defects in a thermite weld. The Niyama criterion was used by taking into account the fundamentals of the mushy zone in steel castings and integrating them with the thermite welding process. The same assumptions used in a casting simulation were used for this investigation. These include cooling rate, temperature gradient and the fact that there is sufficient amount of super-cooling where dendritic formation occurs which acts as a site for pore formation. A filling and solidification simulation of a thermite weld was conducted using the standard SKV-M process taking into consideration extreme temperature gradients. From the solidification characteristics it was found that there is a direct correlation between the dimensionless Niyama criterion and the volume of micro or shrinkage porosity in the welds. A 3D casting simulation software (MAGMAsoft) was utilised where mapping contours were developed to indicate the regions on the weld or casting with micro or shrinkage porosity and quantity of the defects.

Title Preventative rail maintenance by grinding – exploring the limits
 
Author Schoech, W
Affiliation Speno International SA, Meyrin, Switzerland
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Rail grinding has become an essential part of track maintenance. Today, many infrastructure managers have changed from corrective work towards preventive strategies in order to keep rails in good shape from the beginning. The intention is to avoid the development of any severe surface defects, supported by continuously maintaining optimal wheel-to-rail contact conditions. In practice, moving towards a preventive regime requires a long transition phase. Corrective work needs to be planned first. And then, maintenance plans need to be adapted to local line and traffic conditions, as railway networks are complex structures. With mixedtraffic networks, theoretical programs cannot always be strictly implemented under daily conditions, as operational requirements, traffic changes and urgent track repair measures may provoke unforeseen changes. Planned maintenance may be delayed and not compensated for later. New defect phenomena appear, influenced by new rail material characteristics, modified target profiles or by pushing metal removal productivity to the limits. This paper discusses reasons for varying defect development and highlights the need to react correspondingly. In particular, the topic of partly removed defects is addressed. Balancing preventative and corrective work based on recordings is proposed in order to apply the grinding technology in the most appropriate way avoiding unexpected negative side effects.

Cole, C
Affiliation Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Gladstone, Australia

Abstract With the need of increased tonnage transportation through heavy haul railways, ensuring safety and reliability is always of the highest priority. Early detection of rail flaws ensures transportation safety and avoids unplanned downtime owing to derailments. Several conventional non-destructive techniques have been proposed for rail flaw detection including eddy current, infrared imaging, and vibration detection, but these are all slow speed applications. Laser ultrasonic guided wave based detection stands out as a potential non-contact high speed detection method. In this technique, the rail surface is subjected to laser pulses which results in propagation of guided waves in the rail. These waves get scattered when they meet cracks or deformities. The rail flaws are then detected by sensing the scattered ultrasound waves using air coupled sensors. These waves also trigger surface rubbing at rail cracks generating heat in the vicinity, which enhances thermal imaging of flaws. This paper presents results of finite element simulations for crack detection using ultrasonic guided waves at different excitation frequencies and sensor positions. These results are used to identify the best suited frequency and sensor position for defect identification. The outcome of this work will lead the way for a high speed rail flaw detection methodology based on laser induced guided wave principles.

Abstract Canadian Pacific Railway operates, among many other services, a heavy axle- load coal train operation between Golden and Roberts Bank in British Columbia. Rail maintenance problems have been very severe, as the pressure of heavy-haul operation is compounded by unfavourable track conditions in the very difficult terrain of the Rocky Mountain Range. These conditions include: (a) Track with a high proportion of sharp curves is subject to critically-high lateral wheel forces under the passage of the three-piece trucks used in the fleet. As a result, several million dollars were spent each year on out-of-face rail grinding to keep this problem to a manageable level.

Title A computer model for the prediction of sleeper replacement requirements in railway track
 
Author Lamson, ST
Affiliation Queen's University at Kingston, Ontario, Canada
Source RTAA
Conference Sixth International Rail Track Conference, 17-19 March 1986, Melbourne, Australia
Publication Date 1986
Abstract In North America there are currently more than a billion wood ties in track, with an annual replacement rate of more than thirty million (derived from Bowlby, 1983). Ties are one of the most significant cost items in track maintenance. In an effort to better manage this cost, a number of railways and railway research organizations have tried to develop mathematical models to reliably predict the rate of tie deterioration in service. There are usually two key objectives: to accurately attribute a share of tie damage to each of the traffic categories in a mixed traffic operation; and to accurately predict future tie replacement requirements if track is subjected to a certain level of traffic. The first objective is often sought in freight rate analysis. The second is an important requirement for efficient resource allocation and planning. The construction of a tie model is very difficult, however. On the one hand, the behaviour of wood ties in a railway track environment is influenced by many factors whose roles are virtually unknown quantitatively. On the other hand, records of tie deterioration and related traffic do not contain sufficient details necessary for a proper analysis. Within this context, this paper attempts to synthesize current knowledge of tie/track behaviour into a tie life model which is useful in attaining current railway objectives. The model can function with the existing (incomplete) data base, but the quality of the results will improve as the data base is improved. Knowledge gaps have been filled with "best estimates" and are identified as areas for further research to improve the model. Thus, the general philosophy was to produce an immediately useful tool, using available knowledge, while identifying what additional data are needed.
