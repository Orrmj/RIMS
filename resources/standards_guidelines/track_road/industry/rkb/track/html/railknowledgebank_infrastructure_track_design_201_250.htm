Title Measurement of lateral track strength using AAR's track loading vehicle
 
Author Li, D
Shust, W
Kalay, S
Affiliation Association of American Railroads
Transportation Technology Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
Conference 6th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1997), 6-10 April, 1997, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 1997
Abstract This paper presents the three test methodologies for measuring lateral track strength using the Association of American Railroads' Track Loading Vehicle. The three test methodologies are stationary panel shift testing, in-motion panel shift testing, and inmotion stiffness profile testing. Stationary testing provides a means of studying fundamental behaviors of lateral track strength influenced by load and track variables. In-motion panel shift testing measures track misalignment growth and determines critical lateral loads causing loss of track stability. In-motion stiffness profile testing is intended to measure variable track strength along a track for detecting weak track zones. Results presented in this paper represent tests conducted on the test tracks at the Transportation Technology Center in Pueblo, Colorado.

Title Lateral buckling of continuous welded rail track due to thermal and vertical loading
 
Author Van, MA
Affiliation Delft University of Technology, Delft, The Netherlands
Conference 6th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1997), 6-10 April, 1997, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In this paper the effects of several relevant track parameters on the pre- and postbuckling behaviour of CWR-track are investigated. The parameters considered are the lateral and longitudinal ballast resistance, the torsional resistance of the fasteners, the track curvature and the track misalignment amplitude/half-wave-length. The track is loaded thermally in longitudinal direction and vertically by discrete axle loads. The discrete vertical axle loads effect the lateral ballast strength according to the MohrCoulomb model. A sensitivity analysis is performed to determine the most significant parameters affecting the stability. It is concluded that the curvature, the horizontal ballast strength and the misalignment are the important buckling parameters.

Title The development of a steel sleeper system for heavy haul railways
 
Author Brown, JH
Skinner, DH
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract This paper deals with the design and testing of a steel sleeper for use under 35 tonnes axle loads, where up to 90 MGT is transported annually. The design is based principally on a fatigue analysis in which the sleeper is considered as a beam with welded abutments and subsequently as a beam on elastic foundation. Consideration is taken of dynamic effects, the distribution of axle load into a group of sleepers and the different ranges of load on a sleeper. Other factors which are discussed include provision of cant and of longitudinal restraint, centre binding and vertical track buckling. Laboratory fatigue testing of the sleeper was undertaken to provide a basis for evaluating and improving the design. Prototypes were tested in a ballast bed and their performance was monitored by measuring strain and subsidence into the ballast and by inspection of the sleepers and rail fasteners with crack detection equipment. Various rail fasteners and insulation systems for use under high axle load conditions were developed and evaluated. Insulation materials were tested for mechanical and electrical properties and stability to U-V radiation. Fasteners were tested to give appropriate rail restraint and fatigue life.

Abstract The South African Railways has entered the field of low-speed high-axle-load transportation. The disadvantage is an increase in the track structure maintenance cost or first cost or both. A design procedure based on classical theories, permissible stresses and some qualitative and quantitative performance correlations is explained. This procedure is considered useful for the selection of track standards for a given traffic mix and maximum axle load on the basis of an equivalent maintenance requirement in comparison to a known index condition. Abstract Recent and current investigations into the specific costs of heavy-haul unit-train operation are revealing that cost experience on such railroads is appreciably different than that traditionally found on "conventional" rail roads. To provide an economical design for a new or modified heavy-haul rail road, it is recommended that these new cost relationships be carefully considered in the design process. A systems approach is presented which enables the designer to select various combinations of design features so as to minimize the total costs of constructing and operating the railroad. Design features include such items as alignment, car size and train consist. The costs that are affected by the choice of alternative design features include initial construction and equipment costs, annual fuel cost, and annual operating and maintenance costs. The conceptual relationships between these costs and design variables are described, and recent costs from actual heavy-haul railroads are presented. An illustrative example then demonstrates the application of such cost relationships to design of a hypothetical railroad. Comparisons are made between the conclusions drawn regarding design of conventional vs. heavy-haul railroads, and the importance of such a systems design approach is demonstrated.
