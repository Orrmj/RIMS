Title Analysis of rail head stresses in the heavy haul regime
 
Author Chipperfield, CG
Groenhout, R
Hellier, AK
Affiliation BHP Research Laboratories
University of NSW
Source Alan Hellier
Conference Railway Engineering Symposium 1983, Melbourne, 30 August - 1 September 1983
Publication Date 1983
Abstract Three-dimensional finite element analyses have been made of elastic contact stresses in a rail head. Two different contact situations were considered: firstly, that of wheel/rail contact on top of the rail, and secondly, gauge corner contact. The former was also treated using an analytical approach based on the theory of Hertz. Stress components resulting from these analyses have been referred to the local axes of a typical incipient flaw, and used to map out the susceptibility to fatigue cracking of various regions of the rail head.

Title The need for track friendly vehicles
 
Author Hiensch, M
Zoeteman, A
Linders, M
Affiliation Technical University Delft, Railway Technology, Delft, the Netherlands
DEKRA Rail, Utrecht, the Netherlands
ProRail, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Infrastructure managers and operators have a shared need to reach an optimal railway system behaviour in terms of safety, availability, reliability and total cost of ownership and operation. This requires the right incentives for investments, e.g. those in track-friendliness of railway vehicles aiming to reduce track degradation. From previous research the impact on wear and RCF is largely understood. Within the present study, further vehicle-track simulations are performed regarding vehicle design optimization in relation to noise and vibration, presenting how these environmental aspects can be valued. A full appreciation of measures to enhance track-friendliness, ensuring the right incentive for the train operator company regarding the design of their vehicles, is needed and therefore the goal of the here performed study. In order to quantify the benefits of any new design, a benchmark freight wagon vehicle is selected based on Y25L bogies. The potential of proposed design measures regarding the improvement of vibration and squeal noise behavior is studied from the related wheel-rail contact forces and slip. For this purpose, a sensitivity analysis is carried out by means of track-train simulations. From these results alternative approaches regarding the set-up of Track Access Charging (TAC) regimes are evaluated. A TAC regime offering a discount per traveled axle kilometer seems a suitable approach for vibration friendly vehicles. Squeal noise friendly vehicles however should be valued according to the TAC track degradation cost model, calculating the overall squeal noise related cost for the travelled route and should include a well-balanced representation of narrow radius curves and switches.

Title Continuous railway track monitoring using passenger trains
 
Author Roberts, C
Entezami, M
Stewart, E
Yeo, G
Gonzalo, AP
Hayward, M
Ainsworth, P
Affiliation Birmingham Centre for Railway Research and Education, University of Birmingham, Birmingham, UK
MoniRail Ltd., Birmingham, UK
Network Rail High Speed UK, Kent, UK
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Efficacy in railway track maintenance is not only critical for safe operation, but by reducing delays and increasing capacity, can result in economic benefits for rail operators. Currently, rail infrastructure managers have dedicated vehicles to carry out track measurement. These vehicles are, however, expensive to procure and run as they need specially trained crews to operate them. Furthermore, they require uninterrupted access to the track, and so are difficult to schedule around other services. Consequently, the UK only has 6 such measurement vehicles and the mainline network is only inspected on a 4-6 weekly cycle (less regularly for other services). This work demonstrates the application of an inexpensive inertial measurement unit (IMU), installed on an in-service passenger train, and using novel algorithms, to provide continuous monitoring of the track and its degradation. Two IMUs were fitted, one on the bogie and one in the cab. Inertial data from the bogie mounted IMU was collected and processed to provide vertical and lateral displacement profiles of sections of track from multiple passes of a rail vehicle. A modified Kalman filter was used to produce the associated dataset profiles to within 0.2 mm location accuracy. Profiles calculated from multiple passes of the same area of track were compared to monitor track degradation. Inertial data from the IMU mounted in a driver’s cab was also collected and used to calculate the ride comfort using BS EN 12299. The ride comfort indices were compared to recommended limits on acceleration and change of acceleration (jerk). Analysis of the results were used to determine whether trains on the measured routes meet current standards for ride comfort. Reported issues on the measured routes from the Network Rail Fault Management System (FMS) and data from New Measurement Train (NMT) were used to evaluate the performance of the IMU based system against current UK industry track monitoring practices and results were comparable. The findings from this project show that an IMU mounted on the bogie of an in-service railway vehicle allows track geometry data to be measured at more frequent intervals than with a dedicated track measurement vehicle and at a fraction of the cost.

Title Identification of local track defects using axle box acceleration of in-service vehicles
 
Author Karlsson, H
Qazizadeh, A
Stichel, S
Berg, M
Affiliation KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Division of Rail Vehicles, Stockholm, Sweden
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Today, everyone is aware of the high cost that the railway stakeholders need to pay for the maintenance of infrastructure and rolling stock. Despite this, local failures which undermine the reliability and safety of the services are experienced every now and then. Failures occur mainly because the system is not under continuous observation and instead time-based or distance-based maintenance is performed. The next step in the maintenance industry is the transition to condition-based maintenance where components are repaired or changed based on their current state. One crucial step in condition-based maintenance is thus to detect and identify the upcoming faults by continuously collecting information from a condition monitoring system. Hence this paper proposes methods for identification of one type of local track defects using in-service vehicles. Two methods are proposed for identification of local track settlements using axle box acceleration (ABA) signals. The first method is based on comparing ABAs and Mexican hat functions and the second method takes advantage of short-time Fourier transforms. Both methods show good results as far as the track settlement is local and not stretched more than four meters. It is further shown that both methods can be improved by using ABAs from several wheels simultaneously.

Title Maglev ground transportation with High-Temperature Superconductors (HTS)
 
Author Werfel, FN
Floegel-Delor, U
Schirrmeister, P
Riedel, T
Koenig, R
Kantarbar, V
Liebmann, M
Affiliation Adelwitz Technologiezentrum GmbH (ATZ), Torgau, Germany
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Today’s magnetic levitation (Maglev) trains are based on magnetic fields from of 0.5 to about 5 Tesla, provided either by conventional high-current electromagnets above a metal guideway or beyond 2 Tesla by superconductors, moving and generating eddy currents. The Transrapid electromagnetic system (EMS) and the Chuo Shinkansen electrodynamic system (EDS) are the most prominent train candidates experience high-speed and routine operation on an industrial level. Based on the two techniques we investigate the replacement of EMS and EDS by high –temperature superconductor (HTS) bulk materials on PM guideways. Bulk HTS is fundamentally self-stabilizing without active control, act as trapped-field magnets (TFM) which allow higher levitation performance than conventional PMs. HTS bulk levitation is possible at all speeds and can be cooled by liquid Nitrogen (LN2) which lowers the operational costs significantly. We report about the design and construction of mobile vacuum cryostats as universal magnetic devices up to forces of 0.5 ton per module, investigate their properties and cryogenics in test vehicles, and by assembling several devices on vehicles rise the transport capacity up to 10 ton.
