Abstract Ballastless track structures become attractive when ballast is scarce and a low maintenance solution is sought. In desert conditions, ballast fouling by wind-swept sands is so severe that the ballast is unable to perform its major functions and the performance of the track structure is seriously compromised. This paper will introduce the use of a ballastless track structure that is ideally suited for rail roading in desert conditions. The results of finite element modelling, laboratory tests and field experiments in which the performance of this track structure was evaluated, will be discussed. The investigations ranged from the structural design of the concrete beams to the performance of the track foundation. The field tests include the evaluation of turnouts on a heavy haul line, a 120 m test section on a general freight line and various other tests on operational lines. Comparative analysis between classic ballast track and the ballastless track system is used to demonstrate the superiority of this non-conventional track structure for specific and suitable applications. The advantages of the system in heavy haul applications are highlighted and substantiated by experimental results. The paper concludes with three specific case studies where a ballastless track system was used for heavy haul and/or extreme desert conditions in South Africa, Namibia and Saudi Arabia.

Title Extending bridge life for heavy axle load traffic
 
Author Otter, D
Joy, R
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc., Pueblo, Colorado, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract The paper provides an overview of several bridge life extension methodologies that have been investigated and tested for use in North American heavy haul operations. Strategies include reduction of dead load, residual stresses, and impacts. Nondestructive evaluation techniques have been investigated to enhance inspection. In addition, testing of steel bridge repair techniques is underway. Conversion of a ballast deck bridge to an open deck can provide additional live load capacity. Testing recently began on a 1912-vintage steel span converted from a ballast deck to an open deck. Removal of the ballast deck provided considerable additional capacity compared to the previous installation. Ultrasonic impact treatment (UIT) of weld details in steel bridges has proven effective at reducing residual stresses that can lead to fatigue cracking. By reducing residual stresses, more capacity is available for live load stresses. Impact loads have been reduced in concrete bridges by using ties with impact attenuating materials (engineered tie-bottom pads, timber, composite) as well as by using an engineered ballast mat. Removal of rail joints has also reduced impacts on both concrete and steel bridges. In steel bridges, presence of rail joints has been linked to increased crack activity. Inspection is critical to operation of heavy loads, particularly on fatigue-prone welded steel bridges. Nondestructive testing using electrochemical fatigue sensors (EFS) or acoustic emissions (AE) can provide an indication of crack activity and growth rate. Appropriate decisions can then be made regarding repairs, retrofit, or replacement. Effective repairs can restore a bridge to full functionality, or at least delay the need for more extensive retrofit. Repairs under test at the Facility for Accelerated Service Testing (FAST) at the Federal Railroad Administrationâs (FRA) Transportation Technology Center include bolted splice repairs, in-kind member replacement, stop-hole drilling, and repair welds. A revenue service test is underway to determine effectiveness of UIT in conjunction with welded repairs.

Nurmikolu, A
Affiliation Tampere University of Technology, Tampere, Finland
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract In the areas of cold climate frost action in ballast, subballast or subgrade can cause severe problems in maintaining the smoothness of track in required level. The major concern in frost protection is to avoid the freezing of frost susceptible subsoil by using sufficient thickness of subballast and relying on the material itself being non-frost-susceptible. In this paper the role of subballast materials of the existing rail network of Finland is studied in respect to their possible cause behind repeated frost heave problems in 30 sites observed during winters 2003-2007. In the sites the calculated frost depth did not reach the level of subsoil. Frost susceptibility of 60 subballast samples was evaluated by grading analyses and constant temperature frost heave tests. About 60% of the samples exceeded the limits for practically non-frost-susceptible materials suggested in earlier studies. This indicates that frost susceptibility of subballast may be a cause of frost damages on certain spots of Finnish railway network. Still over 25% of subballast samples were classified to be non-frost-susceptible although the sampling was targeted to frost problem locations, which shows that also other causes exist.

Title Analysis of damaging factors in thermite welds through multi-axial fatigue criterion
 
Author Salehi, I
Mutton, PJ
Kapoor, A
Affiliation Faculty of Engineering and Industrial Sciences, Swinburne University of Technology, Hawthorn, Vic, Australia
Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Clayton, Vic, Australia
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Thermite welding is widely used in heavy haul railway systems for in-track welding due to the low capital cost of equipment and the flexibility it provides. However thermite welds typically exhibit variable and often inferior performance compared to flashbutt welds. Fatigue failure as a result of surface defects or harsh geometrical features in the presence of high axle loads and tractive forces gives rise to the so-called straight break and horizontal split web fractures. In this study, the reduction of fatigue performance due to the geometrical inhomogeneity and the presence of defects is investigated using a multi-axial high cycle fatigue criterion based on the concept of critical plane. A thermo-structural finite element model of a track containing a thermite weld is linked with a customized computer code to perform fatigue crack initiation analysis; the effect of inclusions and defects is implicitly invoked through their effect on material fatigue parameters. Two geometrically different thermite welds which are widely used in Australian heavy haul railways are investigated in terms of fatigue probability, to facilitate a better insight into the effect of weld collar shape and toe geometrical parameters on fatigue behaviour. The effect of track support conditions on fatigue damage is also included in the analysis, which enables the effect of ballast deterioration, a common problem at the vicinity of thermite welds, to be quantified. The results show that the amount of fatigue damage critically depends on the geometrical features of the collar edge, especially at the underhead fillet which undergoes severe fatigue at some operational conditions. The condition of track support also shows to be highly influential in the probability of fatigue crack nucleation at regions of the weld foot.

Title Investigation of coal dust composition affecting railroad ballast behavior
 
Author Tutumluer, E
Moaveni, M
Read, D
Li, D
Lees, H
Clark, D
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, IL, USA
Transportation Technology Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
BNSF, Topeka, KS, USA
Union Pacific Railroad, Omaha, NE, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Railroad ballast is an important structural element in the track system that provides rail lateral stability and distributes wheel stresses on top of subballast/subgrade. The ballast layer is an assembly of uniformly graded coarse aggregate particles that are commonly crushed and clean of finer particles to provide adequate shear strength and friction, sufficient aggregate interlock, and proper drainage. Fouling refers to the condition of railroad ballast when voids in this unbound aggregate layer are filled with finer materials. Fouling has a detrimental effect on the track structure; it reduces the aggregate interlock in ballast. As a fouling agent, coal dust coming from heavy haul coal trains and accumulating in ballast has become a major concern for railroads especially during the wet conditions. Although pure coal is what is commonly transported in coal trains, samples of coal dust collected from different track locations and track substructure layers i.e., ballast, subballast, may be found as mixed with other fine particles such as plastic soil dust and mineral filler. In 2005, two derailments occurred in the BNSF/UP joint coal line in Powder River Basin (PRB), which threatened to interrupt the supply of coal to power plants. In 2008, Tutumluer et al. used a large direct shear box equipment to run shear strength tests on ballast samples that were fouled by different percentages of coal dust collected from the South PRB Orin Sub in Wyoming. They found that with an increase in coal dust percentage by weight of clean aggregate, the shear strength properties of the ballast samples decreased significantly. This paper aims to identify different coal dust compositions that may exist around heavy haul railroad track structures focusing on the characteristics of coal dust obtained from the PRB Basin in Wyoming. Findings are from an ongoing track substructure research initiative in the US aimed to investigate properties and detrimental effects of various coal dust compositions on drainage and load carrying functions of railroad ballast layers serving heavy haul coal lines.

Title The development of a geocomposite to prevent mud pumping
 
Author Sharpe, P
Roskams, T
Valero, SN
Affiliation Phil Sharpe Ltd
GEOfabrics Ltd
GeoConcepts
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract In 2010 GEOfabrics launched a new anti pumping geocomposite in the UK, which could be placed directly on the sub-grade or on a failed sub-ballast and prevent the development of mud pumping. Subgrade erosion (mud pumping) is a serious problem in the UK. The upwards migration of fine soil from the subgrade into the ballast can significantly reduce the life of the ballast, typically from an expected 1 OOOMGT sometime to less than 100MGT. Although the percentage of track renewal sites affected by mud pumping annually is small, typically less than 10%, the renewal costs are proportionally much higher, as they require deep excavation and installation of additional granular layers. The implications of not treating the source of the problems are a potential risk to safety of the line; trackbed failure may occur sometimes a little as 1 year after reballasting. While the use of an anti-pumping geocomposite does not offer significant savings on material costs, it has been found that the simplified logistics and reduction in construction time typically allow trackbed installation time to be reduced by up to 50%. Alternatively, the length of track that could be treated in a given track possession could be doubled. This paper summarises the main causes of subgrade erosion. It gives a historical perspective on the identification and treatment of mud pumping problems in the UK. It then outlines the rigorous product development/test program undertaken by GEOfabrics over several years using a full scale track bed load test facility simulating the harshest conditions encountered under a main line railroad. The paper concludes with a case history summarizing performance on one of the first commercial installations.

Title Advantages of biaxial welded geogrids in railway rehabilitation and upgrade
 
Author Shahkolahi, A
Crase, J
Barati, P
Affiliation Global Synthetics Pty Ltd, Qld, Australia
Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract Improvement of the subgrade is often necessary during rehabilitation and upgrade of railway lines. In many cases, the required bearing capacity of the subgrade cannot be assured economically or achieved with traditional granular layers alone. A layer of biaxial welded geogrid with structured uniform rigid bars in combination with a nonwoven geotextile offers not only an economical solution, but also an environmentally friendly method to provide the required strength and support to the track formation. When designing railway foundations with geogrids, several design properties such as stiffness, torsional rigidity and installation damage should be considered. In this paper, traditional solutions of improving the subgrade bearing capacity are compared with this solution of track reinforcement using biaxial geogrids. Design parameters of various geogrids in regard to physical and mechanical properties are also compared and the advantages of biaxial welded geogrids are explained in detail. A technical assessment is made and various test results are presented. Results show that the biaxial welded geogrid can reduce the thickness of the required ballast layer by up to thirty percent. A biaxial geogrid can provide an economical solution for improving the bearing capacity of the weak subgrade over traditional methods of placing deep granular layers alone. Results also show that the biaxial welded geogrid with structured uniform rigid bars has enhanced design properties when compared to other geogrid types. This can lead to safer designs and greater durability without an increase in cost. A case study on the major network assets upgrade in Adelaide including two primary sections; Gawler and Noarlunga lines, from where biaxial welded geogrids were used, is also presented.

Title Monitoring of ballasted rail track with geosynthetic reinforcement
 
Author Anantanasakul , P
Indraratna, B
Nimbalkar, S
Neville, T
Affiliation University of Wollongong (UOW)
Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2012, Global perspectives, conference on railway engineering, Brisbane, Queensland, 10-12 September 2012
Publication Date 2012
Abstract Large repetitive stresses induced from heavy train traffic can cause significant degradation of ballasted rail tracks, leading to poor track geometry and stability. This, in turn, results in decreased track performance and increased maintenance costs. Inclusions of geosynthetics in the track substructure can decrease the impact of these adverse effects. Thus, understanding the effects of geosynthetic reinforcement becomes necessary for improved design procedures and construction practices for ballasted rail tracks. However, the complex nature of the stress-strain response of composite track system is often difficult to assess using only laboratory tests on reduced-scale models. It is imperative to also understand this behaviour through in-situ monitoring of rail tracks. A series of full-scale field experiments was undertaken on track sections near Singleton, New South Wales to investigate the effects of geosynthetic reinforcement on the performance of the track. These experimental track sections were built on subgrade soils with varying stiffness, and various types of geosynthetics were installed at the ballast-subballast interface. Transient and accumulated deformations of the track substructure as well as variation of traffic-induced stresses in the track were routinely monitored. It was found that geogrids can decrease vertical strains of the ballast with obvious benefits of reduced track maintenance costs. It was also found that a few selected types of geogrids can be effectively used for soft subgrade soils.

Abstract Geogrids and geocomposites have been used successfully to overcome such issues as track deformation, with an economical yet technical solution.
