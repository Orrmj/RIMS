Title Spot replacement of timber sleepers with concrete sleepers
 
Author Campbell, KD
Affiliation C.A.C.A. Victoria, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Sixth International Rail Track Conference, 17-19 March 1986, Melbourne, Australia
Publication Date 1986
Abstract Since the first International Rail Sleeper Conference, August 1973, there has been a dramatic reduction in durable timber of suitable log size for cutting into sleepers. A principle reason has been an increase in alternative demands for timber which promise a more attractive financial return than sleepers. Initially, in the Rail Track Association it was considered that the future of track structure was in preservation of lower quality timber sleepers. The timber industry has been faced with a progressive decline in average trunk size, and in availability of durable timber. Earlier predictions of an expanding future for effective treatment of less durable timbers have not .proved correct, because of lack of acceptance of the process by the Trade Union Movement. In the 1980's, there has been considerable pressure from ecological conservationists to significantly restrict forests available for cropping. The growth of the wood chip industry and associated publicity has accelerated the loss of available forests. It is significant that many European countries who have been traditional timber producers, such as Sweden, Russia, and Germany, have adopted a policy for the general use of concrete sleepers. More recently Brazil, a major South American timber producer, has adopted concrete sleepers. A parallel industrial use of timber also highlights the development since the early 1970's. The pole market, for lighting and power was almost exclusively timber. Supply difficulties have resulted in a major new industry, in the production of concrete poles. The major pressure timber treatment operator, Koppers Australia Pty Ltd, sponsors of our first conference, have joined the large concrete sleeper manufacturers Monier and Humes, in the concrete pole manufacturing industry. In 1973 the only concrete sleepered track in Australia was the standard gauge line from Pt Augusta to Whyalla. Since then we have seen major installations of concrete sleepered track by West Rail, A.N. Q Rail and in suburban applications in V-Line. There have also been major applications of steel and concrete sleepers in Mt Newman and Hamersley Iron Railways. It is accepted that there is considerable pressure on Governments for Capital Expenditure, in particular in public transport. Present indicators suggest that most reconstruction will be carried out in stages due to the scarcity of available funds. The traditional me thod of handling sleepers will change rapidly, even for spot replacement, to mechanised methods. A principle motivation for this change wil l be the insistence by Unions to restrict weights lifted to a maximum of 16 kgs. An example of this weight restriction is the elimination of concrete blocks production in Victoria of unit weight greater than 16 kg; this decision reduced the number of available sizes of blocks significantly. The major change in the availability of durable timber since the early 1970's and the lack of acceptability of preserved lower quality timber requires a reassessment of the sleeper market for the 1980's and the 1990's. There have been significant developments in the use of higher quality sleepers in spot replacement, particularly in the U.S.A . The most impressive perfonnance had been in the Kansas City Southern Rai lway System in the Mid West and Southern States . The K.C.S. system have adopted sound engineering techniques to achieve success in spot replacement of timber sleepers with concrete. The techniques used in both the U.S.A., the U.K. and South Africa in the types of concrete sleepers could assist in the developing of suitable units for spot replacement in this country.

Title Boron rod trials in Queensland
 
Author Moffat, A
Norton, J
Davies, C
Source RTAA
Conference Tenth International Rail Track Conference, 12-14 September 1994, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1994
Abstract In November 1989, fused boron rods were installed in existing track at Mundubbera, Queensland. The purpose of the trial was to evaluate the extent of boron diffusion in eucalypt timbers and to determine if there was enough boron present to stop degrade due to fungal attack. In February 1993, nine sleepers were removed from track and shipped to Timber Research in the Queensland Forest Service for evaluation of boron penetration. Sleepers were cut into 25 mm slices and exposed surfaces were examined for the presence of boron. With the boron penetration in each secticn defined, computerised visual imaging was carried out to obtain a three dimensional picture of boron diffusion in each sleeper. Seventy three samples were chemically analysed to determine the levels of boron achieved. Results showed that boron did diffuse from the rod into the sleeper and in some instances, levels of toxicant may be enough to prevent attack by decay organisms. The trial is to be sampled after five years exposure (November 1994) to determine the effectiveness to the penetrated boron in preventing degrade.

Abstract Rail Track Infrastructure the world over is being recognized as an important asset in the smooth functioning of transport networks vital to economic prosperity. In this era of economic rationalization and privatization of public sector industries, the continued well being of this important asset needs to be ensured. In the context of rail track networks, the sleepers form an important component. Even though it is not the most expensive component of the track structure, its continued satisfactory performance and durability are important for the maintenance of the rail infrastructure. Traditionally rail tracks have been built on timber sleepers, which are becoming increasingly scarce as a resource given the degradation of forest reserves of appropriate quality and the pressures from environmental activism. Simultaneously funds available for rebuilding of existing networks are becoming scarcer than ever. This has led to the consideration of two issues - firstly, the reconstruction of existing rail networks will have to take place over long periods of time in a phased manner, and secondly the existing degraded timber sleepers will have to be replaced by sustainable sleepers made out of alternative materials such as concrete. Partial Replacement Sleepers (PRS) have been designed as replacement of existing timber sleepers in interspersed fashion, where typically every third or fourth sleeper is replaced with a new sleeper. Spot Replacement which replaces degraded sleepers as needed and not in a standard pattern can also be assumed to be a special case. A number of issues have to be considered for PRS. One important aspect is the profile of the sleeper such that it is as close to the existing sleeper profile as possible resulting in easy installation and minimal ballast rearrangement. More importantly if the height of the replacement sleeper can be designed to match that of the timber sleeper, then a number of components associated with the rail track structure need not be modified in order to use concrete sleeper as a PRS. Other than the profile, the difference in stiffness of the sleepers, the fastening system, the weight and handling of the sleepers needs to be considered. Concrete sleepers for PRS made from prestressed concrete provide the opportunity to design into the sleeper the above performance requirements by suitably combining geometry, concrete strength, pre-stressing force, strand layout etc. Such a sleeper has been developed from years of research and development at Rocla and has been tested in the laboratory and the track, both in Australia and the US. The initial test results are indicating satisfactory performance of these sleepers contrary to some of the mixed experience reported in the past. The paper describes the design basis and approach as well as the early data obtained from the trials being carried out.
