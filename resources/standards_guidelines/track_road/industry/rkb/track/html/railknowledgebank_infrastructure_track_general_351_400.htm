Title IVES – a combined bottom-up / top-down track slab approach as an alternative to ballasted track
 
Author Kowalski, M
Vocks, H
Affiliation Rhomberg Rail Consult GmbH
Rhomberg Rail Australia Pty Ltd
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2015, Doing it Smarter. People, Power, Performance, 24-26 November 2015, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract IVES is an outcome of the latest development in non-ballasted track technology. It stands for Intelligent, Versatile, Efficient and Solid. All these characteristics are embodied in its integrated system, which is based on analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of various slab track systems. The aim of IVES is to combine the benefits of existing slab track systems while eliminating their weak points. Another target is to achieve cost optimisation and make it more feasible for a wide range of applications, both: for known applications like high-speed tracks and also to use this technology as an alternative to ballasted track in common lines with lower speeds. This could be achieved by applying a holistic approach during the selection and implementation of track components, materials, as well as work methods.

Title The rectangular embedded rail system: could it work in the unique Australian rail environment?
 
Author Mumford, N
Penny, C
Affiliation Arup
Embedded Rail Technology Ltd
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2015, Doing it Smarter. People, Power, Performance, 24-26 November 2015, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract This paper examines the key features of the “Rectangular Embedded Rail System” (known by the initials ERT) and then considers the main challenges to be addressed to the introduction of the system into Australia. The five main challenges appear to be:
1. The system represents such a fundamental change to current trackform solutions that it requires a culture change in the rail mindset. It takes people out of their historical and risk averse comfort zones.
2. The European view that significant whole of life cost savings can be achieved by the system has not yet been “tested” in the Australian market.
3. There is a fundamental issue of “disbelief” that non-ballasted systems can be constructed at equal or lower cost to ballasted track.
4. A transition from the current product licence ownership to a more accessible arrangement for the Australian market needs to be completed.
5. The Rail Industry in Australia is relatively fragmented in comparison with Europe and this discourages innovation to some extent. Stronger linkages between Rail Industry Bodies and Research Institutions will assist this process.
Balfour Beatty Rail (UK) has over the past 10 years developed a track system conceived by Charles Penny, their Engineering and Development Director. The ERT system makes a step change in the conventional approach to design, construction and maintenance of the railway system. Short test sections of this embedded rail system have been constructed and are now in use in the UK and Spain; and the system is fully accredited by Network Rail (UK) and the German Railway Authorities. However, since 2012, Balfour Beatty has been financially constrained and unable to further invest in construction of the system. During a visit earlier this year Charles explored opportunities for its implementation in Australia. Arup supported that visit and also carried out a detailed and critical technical review of the system to check the engineering rationale.

Title Improvement of rail creep data to measure the stress state of a tangent continuously welded rail (CWR) track
 
Author Ahmad, SSN
Mandal, NK
Chattopadhyay, G
Powell, J
Micenko, P
Affiliation CRC for Rail Innovation, Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Centre for Railway Engineering
CQUniversity, Australia
Queensland Rail Australia
Australian Rail Track Corporation Ltd, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Monitoring the longitudinal rail movement or rail creep is regarded as one of the effective ways to understand the rail-stress states in critical locations such as fixed structures (level crossings, turnouts and fixed top bridges), in sags at the bottom of gradients or any track segment with a history of buckles. However, rail stress can be changed due to thermal and mechanical loads in both longitudinal and vertical directions. Many railway companies use a fixed schedule to assess rail creep, particularly in critical locations. This may not be adequate to reduce the risk of summer buckling and winter pull-apart problems. Current practice of creep measurement does not consider the effect of rail temperature, the vertical movement of track, inherent inaccuracy of the measurement system and any non-uniformity in track structures. In a field test, Total Station Survey equipment has been used to monitor the changes in longitudinal and vertical displacements of the rail at points on a tangent track section in the heavy haul Blackwater system of the QR National network at Edungalba. This 26 tonne axle load track section with 1% gradient and 60 kg/m rail was instrumented with strain gauges, thermocouples and rail stress modules. The creep in this section has been quantified in terms of rail strain and rail stress with respect to the rail temperature. In this paper, a method is proposed considering the longitudinal creep data, vertical displacement of the rail and rail temperature to accurately predict the stress state of a tangent rail track.

Title Improved rail joints and inspections systems
 
Author Akhtar, MN
Davis, DD
Garcia, G
Maal, L
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc., Pueblo, Colorado, USA
Federal Railroad Administration, Washington, DC, USA
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Joint bar caused accidents have been steady during recent years and are safety and reliability issues for the railroads. The current bolted rail joint bars (fish plate) provide vertical and horizontal rail alignment while allowing for longitudinal movement to offset rail movement due to temperature changes. In continuous welded rail (CWR) territory, joint bars should resist longitudinal loads due to temperature changes. Also, strength of insulated joints depends on adhesive, which degrades over time, causing electrical shorts in the signal system. This paper discusses joint bar research funded by the Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) and the Association of American Railroads (AAR). Failure modes are discussed and strategies to increase service life are recommended. Design features of prototypes that address the current failure modes are discussed, and their performance in revenue service is presented. The paper also discusses the effects of track parameters on joint bar stresses, such as foundation configuration, joint bar configuration, track geometry, track surface, and fastener conditions. Although railroads are performing regular required inspections and joint bar related derailments are decreasing, continuing joint bar service failures suggest that the methods and effectiveness of joint bar inspections can be improved. Visual inspection is currently the primary method used for joint bar inspection. Two recently developed automated inspection systems are discussed in the paper.

Title Temperature flow in railway tunnels and its consequences for operation and maintenance
 
Author Andrén, A
Dahlström, L-O
Affiliation Swedish Transport Administration, Borlänge, Sweden
Luleå University of Technology, Luleå, Sweden
NCC Engineering, Gothenburg, Sweden
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Every winter several railway tunnels in Sweden are affected by problems related to water leakage and freezing temperatures, which causes ice to form along the tunnel contour in the form of icicles and pillars that can damage the tunnel structure, handrails, cable racks, installations and drainage system. The tracks can become covered with ice because freezing of water leakage from the roof and the walls, which can cause derailment. The ice formations can obstruct train passages and cause time delays. When the ice occurs in the fracture network close to the tunnel contour or in the interface between the rock and shotcrete, it can cause fall-outs in both materials. To maintain safety and prevent traffic disturbance, many tunnels requires frequent maintenance efforts, and the removal of ice, loose rock and shotcrete is costly and also connected with risks for the maintenance workers. To facilitate for the reduction of maintenance cost it is necessary to improve the knowledge of frost penetration along tunnels as well as the effect of ice pressure and frost shattering on the load-bearing construction.

Title Use of ultrasonic phased array testing for detection of in service defects in heavy haul railway draft gear
 
Author Arcus, KC
Cookson, JM
Affiliation Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Victoria, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Long trains and high axle loads combine to create high in-train draft and buff forces in a modern heavy haul railway. These high forces can result in damage and fatigue cracking of draft components, and result in costly train breaks. Regular inspection of the draft components can significantly reduce the incidence of train breaks; however, the inspection procedures must be capable of examining the locations where cracking typically originates. These may not be readily accessible for traditional inspection methods which typically involve removing the draft components from the rail vehicles. This removal for inspection and subsequent replacement is time consuming and expensive. Ultrasonic Phased Array Testing (UPAT) has been implemented at two heavy haul railways in Australia to allow in-vehicle inspection of critical areas of the main draft components. This eliminates the need for removal to perform a visual or magnetic particle inspection, is less costly and much less time consuming. The ultrasonic phased array inspections described in this paper have been very effective in the detection and identification of fatigue cracks, which were confirmed on removal of the component from the vehicle. The UPAT inspections can be performed without increasing the time taken to service the rail vehicles, and has resulted in the removal from service of many fatigue-cracked or defective components, thus greatly reducing the risk of train breaks. These techniques can also be readily applied at the manufacturing stage, to provide inspection of internal solidity of the critical areas of these components.

Title Determination of optimum intervention time for track surfacing based on economic minimization of maintenance costs
 
Author Bonaventura, CS
Zarembski, AM
Palese, JW
Affiliation ZETA-TECH – A Harsco Rail Business Unit, Cherry Hill, NJ, USA
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract This paper presents an analytical methodology for determining optimum intervention time for track surfacing based on economic minimization of maintenance and operating costs, as well as the application of the methodology to nearly 800 miles of Class 1 track. As part of the analysis, the track is divided into homogeneous analysis zones where optimum surfacing intervention times are determined for defined segments (typically a quarter-mile) based on actual history of tamping and track quality degradation unique to the individual segment. Track surface degradation in the form of surface quality index is developed for each segment as a function of cumulative tonnage and cumulative spot tamping following out-of-face surfacing. Cost components for the economic minimization are segment specific and include costs for surfacing (out-of-face and spot), train delay and slow order costs, damage costs for rail, ties, and ballast, and wasted fuel costs. Track damage and wasted fuel costs are computed as functions of track quality and, as such, grow as cycle time increases and track degrades. The optimum intervention point for track surfacing is based on the lowest overall operating and maintenance costs for each segment. The application of this method to nearly 800 miles of North American Class 1 track shows that the model can be successfully interfaced with existing railroad data and that optimum surfacing cycles calculated by the model are realistic and consistent with expectations and experience. The track analyzed included 11 different homogeneous zones, both wood- and concrete-tie locations, and annual tonnages ranging from 6.0 to over 200 MGT. The resultant model is flexible enough to be applied system wide using available railroad data for a broad range of track and traffic conditions and offers the potential to interface with other maintenance management tools and systems to generate an optimum track maintenance program.

Title Improved defect detection in the non-stop testing process in heavy haul operations
 
Author Boyle, JL
Crocker, R
Nottelmann, MA
Havira, RM
Affiliation Sperry Rail Service, Danbury, USA
Sperry Rail International (UK) Ltd., Derby, United Kingdom
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract The use of new ultrasonic transducer configurations in the non-stop testing process has been shown to greatly benefit the ultimate goal of the Heavy Haul Railway rail testing strategy – specifically the improved defect detection and reduction of service failures. The focus of this paper is to address advanced methods of improving detection of the most numerous and potentially dangerous defect type, detail fractures (a.k.a. detail transverse defects, abbr. TDD). The TDD is of particular concern to heavy haul railways. The fact that the TDD grows from sub-detection size to terminal size in remarkably short timespans has been well documented by heavy haul railways and research institutions from all parts of the world. This is a critical problem in need of a technical solution. This paper will first briefly describe an advance in ultrasonic technology aimed at closing the gap between time of detection and a TDD reaching terminal size. This technical advance involves the use of angled transducers to skew the ultrasonic beam under rail surface conditions. One such skewed transducer configuration is known as X-Fire. X-Fire, in combination with traditional test sensor options such as induction and standard-angle ultrasonic transducer arrays, makes the detection of TDDs better than ever before. Next, this paper will describe the need and solution for reducing the time interval in bringing new technology to bear on the TDD problem. The need to reduce the test frequency and make more efficient use of available track time is particularly important to heavy haul railways. This paper will describe the method to close that gap and enable test vehicles to better co-exist with the diminishing available track time given for rail testing. It is our belief that the combination of traditional ultrasonic transducer angles and induction, complemented with new angled X-Fire transducers, implemented in the non-stop testing process, is the best rail testing strategy for the heavy haul railroad industry to reduce service interruptions and increase track availability.

Publication Date 2011
Abstract The Transportation Technology Center, Inc. (TTCI) has been actively involved in the rail industry’s research in improving rail life. The Facility for Accelerated Service Testing has played an important role in enabling improvements to rail life through supporting tests of improved rail and other track components, as well as through testing innovative maintenance practices. An industry-wide survey of rail life shows that from 1994 to 2008, rail life increased approximately 12 percent on tangent track and from 24 percent to 69 percent on curved track, depending on degree of curvature. Curved track rail life has improved the most, because of technological improvements such as premium rail, fastening systems, and gage face lubrication practices. Changes in track standards for design and maintenance have also contributed to the increase in rail life.

Abstract Upgrading track quality offers potential safety and service quality benefits because it can reduce both derailment rate and transportation time.
