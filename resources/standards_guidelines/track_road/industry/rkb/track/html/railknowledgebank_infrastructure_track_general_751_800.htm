Author Gay Neto, A
Refachinho de Campos, PR

Affiliation Polytechnic School at the University of São Paulo, São Paulo, Brazil

Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract In computational mechanics, contact formulations aim at evaluating the total forces and/or contact pressure and tangential stress fields experienced on the interface between solid bodies. In railway vehicle dynamics, contact forces are essential for understanding the full vehicle dynamic behavior. Moreover, contact forces are critical parameters in the study of wear and rolling contact fatigue phenomena. Several recent developments have been made in the science of contact mechanics under railway applications, especially in the field of computational simulation for the assessment of contact interactions. This work employs recent developments in the field of computational contact mechanics to present a novel, but introductory approach to model the wheel-rail contact interaction. The aim of this first work is limited to obtain normal and tangential contact forces for simple scenarios of accelerating and braking of a vehicle in tangent track. Results show that the technique is promising and may be enhanced for more complex scenarios and geometries of rail/wheels.

Title Track structure design: breaking the narrow gauge 30 ton barrier
 
Author Hewson, DJ
Gräbe, PJ
Shaw, FJ
Affiliation R&H Rail (Pty) Ltd, Randburg, South Africa
University of Pretoria, Pretoria, South Africa
Tubular Track Technology (Pty) Ltd, Pretoria, South Africa

Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Axle load is one of the defining characteristics of freight railways, with a continuous requirement to pursue heavier axle loads. This is often done within the context of an existing rail system necessitating the re-evaluation of the current infrastructure and what can be done to maximise its performance. In the narrow gauge arena, axle loads of 26 t have been reliably and safely operated for many years. This paper seeks to evaluate the track superstructure for 30, 32 and 36 t axle loads by assessing the sensitivity of the component parameters (rail section, sleeper spacing and ballast depth) in the determination of rail seat load and applied load at the ballast/formation interface. The resulting configurations are considered against the existing track standard and current maintenance inputs that are currently in place to identify optimal structures for the three load cases. The track structure is analysed using conventional theoretical methods with a variance in the component parameters that populates a model indicating sensitivity of the identified parameter. The combinations are considered against practicality requirements of implementation before confirming the “best case” result for each load case with a corresponding Finite Element Analysis (FEA) of each of the load cases and track structure. The analyses illustrate how the axle load can be increased from 30 t/axle to 36 t/axle with a resulting 6.2% increase in the pressure on the formation through the reduction of the sleeper spacing to 600 mm and the introduction of a heavier rail profile.

Title Fundamentals of track buckling prevention and rail stress management
 
Author Kish, A
Affiliation Kandrew Inc. Consulting Services, Peabody, MA USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Track buckling and its prevention is a major safety and maintenance concern to the heavy-haul industry. It is a complex vehicle-track interaction problem resulting in a sudden loss of lateral stability as manifested by large lateral displacements under the train which can result in derailments. Buckled track induced derailments in the USA rank in the top three out of all the track failure caused accidents in terms of number of derailments annually, and rank number one in the causative dollar damage. Buckling prevention requires not only a sound understanding of the fundamental aspects of track buckling mechanics including the causes, key parameters and safety concepts, but also responsive continuous welded rail (CWR) maintenance procedures to more effectively mitigate. This summary paper covers principal results of over 25 years research on the track buckling problem and on its prevention. In particular, the paper presents the fundamentals of track buckling in terms of the principal causes and influencing parameters, track buckling safety criteria and buckling temperature concepts, and key relevant aspects of rail stress and neutral temperature (RNT) behavior. These elements are formulated into a simple and versatile safety and maintenance criterion to serve not only as a track buckling prevention guideline, but also as a “critical neutral temperature” based approach to more effectively maintain CWR stress and neutral temperature to safe levels. The relevance and application of the new safety criterion to improving hot-weather speed restrictions for buckling risk reduction is also presented. It is expected that the proposed concepts, criteria and their applications will lead not only to an improved understanding of track buckling and its prevention, but also to the development of more effective CWR maintenance strategies leading to increased network safety and capacity.

Affiliation Central South University, Changsha, Hunan, China
Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Increasing the operating axle load of in-serve and new heavy-haul railway lines has been the development goal of railroad transportation in China and other places around the world. As the axle load increases, ballasted track at the transition zone of railway bridge approaches is even more susceptible to dynamic responses and differential settlements caused by the abrupt stiffness change in track support. This could further accelerate the deterioration of track geometry and consequently result in both increasing maintenance demand and compromised ride quality. As part of a comprehensive study, this paper was aimed at exploring from the micromechanics perspective the underlying mechanisms and related factors that are responsible for permanent differential track settlements at the transition zone of heavy-haul railway bridge approaches. A customized and validated discrete element method (DEM) approach was employed to realistically model the micromechanical interactions of three-dimensional (3D) irregular ballast particles. A realistic DEM model of the track transition zone at typical heavy-haul railway bridge approaches was developed with two different ballast shape levels and varying subgrade stiffness to identify and evaluate appropriate methods for mitigating differential settlements. It was found that both ballast quality and subgrade support (stiffness) conditions are vital for differential settlement control.

Abstract This paper introduced wheel-rail contact based traction control into conventional Longitudinal Train Dynamics (LTD) simulations via co-simulation and parallel computing techniques. A LTD simulation package was connected with a multibody vehicle system dynamics simulation package called GENSYS using the TCP/IP protocol. The LTD simulations replicated the dynamic environment (in-train forces) of train operations while the vehicle system dynamics simulations integrated wheel-rail contact models and mechatronic traction control models for locomotives. In-train forces and a traction reference were sent to GENSYS to determine traction effort values which were then sent back to LTD. Publication Date 2017
Abstract Crossings are a key part of the railway track structure. The restricted geometry of rigid crossings leads to an impact with increased vertical contact forces during the transition of the wheel from one rail to the other. This process is accompanied by high contact pressures and may induce damage. Typical damage modes are wear, head checks but also subsurface damage. For steels with high hardness, subsurface damage initiation can occur in crossing noses. This damage mode can be predicted using the Dang-Van criteria, which is based on arising hydrostatic and shear stresses. Before installation in track, the surface geometry of three crossing noses with different materials was measured. A simplified FE model is developed using extracted geometry parameters and an elastic-plastic material model to describe the loading of these crossings. 55 load cycles are calculated to evaluate the run-in geometry for different worn wheels. The Dang-Van concept is used to predict subsurface damage initiation including the material history and developing residual stresses. The calculated parameters predict subsurface damage initiation for two crossings, which does not correspond to observations in track. This indicates that the use of the DV concept for crossings has to be studied in more detail and combined with other methods.

Title Permanent and resilient deformation behaviour of heavy haul formation materials subjected to environmental changes
 
Author Vandoorne, R
Mpye, GD
Grabe, PJ
Affiliation University of Pretoria, Pretoria, South Africa
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract The formation of a railway track is subjected to varying environmental conditions during its service life. These environmental conditions affect the physical state of the soil as expressed by moisture content, matric suction or resilient modulus. Current design methods are largely empirical and require a single representative value of resilient modulus to be chosen in order to estimate the design life of the formation. A study of the literature available shows that the resilient modulus is significantly influenced by environmental conditions. Therefore, the resilient and permanent deformation behaviour of a formation will change over time as the material is subjected to environmental changes. Deformation data from a test site named Bloubank (instrumented with multi-depth deflectometers) is presented and analysed. It is shown that the permanent and resilient deformation behaviour is not describable over the long term using simple closed form functions such as the logarithmic function or variants thereof. The deformation behaviour appears to be linked to environmental changes within the formation over time. Due to the strong link between matric suction variation and environmental conditions, it is suggested that new formation design methods are approached from an unsaturated soil mechanics perspective and that future formation condition assessments take into account analysis of the matric suction within the soil.

Since 1965 the SAR has experimented with laminated sleepers manufactured from local pine.
