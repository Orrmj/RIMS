Title Rail materials: alternatives and limits
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Mädler, K
Zoll, A
Heyder, R
Brehmer, M
Affiliation Deutsche Bahn AG, DB Systemtechnik, Brandenburg-Kirchmöser, Germany
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract DB Systemtechnik has for years been investigating operationally induced flaws to rails and wheels in order to arrive at means of slowing down or actually preventing damage processes. One option here is to use rail materials of greater strength that accordingly have a higher resistance to rolling contact fatigue (RCF). Over recent years, DB Systemtechnik has been investigating the suitability of pearlitic and bainitic rail steels for use on lines with rolling contact fatigue problems with the aim of cutting track maintenance input without increasing the level of vehicle maintenance due to increased wheel wear. The present findings indicate that, as well as reducing wear, higher-strength pearlitic grades also result in shallower head checks, enabling them to at least delay the attendant damage done to the rail. Furthermore, with suitable alloying and control of the form that sulphides take, non heat-treated, naturally hard steels could well be developed as an alternative to the head-hardened rail, especially since the wear at weld joints to which head-hardened rails are subject would cease to exist. Bainitic rail steels may produce a balance between the processes of wear and rolling contact fatigue at a low level. However, minimum strengths need to be observed when using them, though, due to the altered wear mechanisms of pearlitic steels. The present findings indicate that high-chromium steels with a medium carbon content and hence of considerably greater strength deliver better wear behaviour than high-manganese steels with alow carbon content.

Title Recent findings in the understanding of vehicle/track interaction on track damage and rolling contact fatigue (RCF)
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Burstow, MC
Dembosky, MA
Gurule, S
Urban, C
Affiliation Network Rail, London, UK
TTCI UK Limited, London, UK
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract An understanding of how different vehicles respond to variations in track geometry and alignment is essential in the study of rail damage, such as rolling contact fatigue (RCF). This paper describes how detailed vehicle/track interaction studies have been used to understand the causes of different forms of rolling contact fatigue (RCF) damage, and how the formation of clusters of RCF is related to particular wavelengths of lateral alignment features which become embedded in the track geometry. The occurrence of RCF on the low rail of curves, which is becoming increasingly common in the UK, is investigated using detailed vehicle dynamics studies of a particular curve. It is shown that this form of damage is the result of the forces generated by the trailing wheelset of a bogie, and that the forces generated by the leading wheelset can result in spalling of material from the cracked surface, which is a characteristic feature of much low rail RCF. Studies are also described which have investigated the interaction between track alignment and wheelset kinematic behaviour. A methodology is also described which has been developed to estimate the dynamic response of a vehicle to a given track input from quasi-static vehicle behaviour to predict the degree of surface damage which is likely to result.

Title The integration of cross wind forces into train dynamic calculations
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Baker, CJ
Bouferrouk, A
Perez, J
Iwnicki, SD
Affiliation School of Engineering, University of Birmingham, Edgbaston, Birmingham B15 2TT, UK
Rail Technology Unit of Manchester Metropolitan University, Manchester, UK
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract In order to address the issue of train overturning due to cross winds, the study proposes a robust method for the integration of turbulent cross wind forces into train dynamic calculations. The unsteady forces are determined through the concept of aerodynamic weighting function from experimental data of aerodynamic admittance. For constant and sudden winds, the risk of overturning is investigated for a matrix of three mean cross wind and vehicle speeds, and two values of track twist. Limitations on large time scales cause the weighting function to filter out the turbulent fluctuations, making the derailment ratios to be dominated by the transient behaviour of the steady cross wind forces. It is found that a track with cant deficiency presents a higher risk of overturning than one with cant excess. Higher mean wind and vehicle speeds generally increase the risk of overturning. For the derailment mechanism, roll over is more likely to occur than flange climbing, and is also more sensitive to the mean wind speed. The effects of sudden cross winds on derailment are more significant compared with constant cross winds.

The object of the research described in this paper was to understand the relationship between short-wavelength lateral track geometry quality and the formation of RCF, and whether track maintenance standards could be improved to reduce the risk of RCF initiation. The research was undertaken through application of vehicle dynamics simulations of a range of vehicle types interacting with different track alignment faults on a range of curves. This was an important finding which influences the way track geometry is measured and analysed since existing track maintenance standards are based only on the amplitude of the lateral alignment and not on its wavelength.
