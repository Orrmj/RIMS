Title Steel sleepers: an engineering approach to improved productivity
 
Author Mitchell, R
Baggott, MG
Birks, J
Affiliation Mt Newman Mining Co.
Westrail
Source National Committee on Railway Engineering of the College of Mechanical Engineers of the Institution of Engineers, Australia
Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1987: Conference on Railway Engineering, 14-16 September 1987, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1987
Abstract This paper provides the background to the commercial and technical environments through which timber, steel and concrete sleeper types have evolved. Timber is a resilient material capable of absorbing energy input at the wheel/rail interface but suffers from variable quality and scarcity aspects. Steel and concrete sleepers though durable and more uniform in quality are less resilient and may produce a harsher ride which is reflected in rail and sleeper problems. Recent developments and initiatives adopted to control the problems brought about by the alternative materials are presented. It is argued that steel and concrete sleepers will continue to have their place in railways where market forces are strong determinants in the decision process and that continued efforts are needed to improve their performance.

Title A mechanistic approach for estimation of track modulus

Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1998 Engineering innovation for a competitive edge Conference on Railway Engineering 7-9 September 1998, Yeppoon, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1998
Abstract Track modulus is a key parameter in predicting track behaviour under passing traffic. As the prime measure of track stiffness, it affects calculations of track deflections, rail bending stresses, bearing stresses in track components, and the response of track to dynamic loadings from trains. Traditionally, rail authorities use a range of empirical moduli drawn from measurements of various types of track, but conducting in-field measurements is time consuming and costly. A mechanistic model is needed so that any type of track structure can be considered for the prediction of degradation. This paper describes a mechanistic method initially developed at Queen's university in Canada for estimation of static track modulus using elastic foundation models. The paper then goes on to discuss calibration of the method for local conditions and to present sample applications for typical Australian railway tracks. It is found that the mechanistic method gives reasonable estimations of track modulus. The effect of rail gauge on track modulus is not as dramatic as perceived by Australian railway authorities, nor is the effect of different sizes of rails lying on the same track foundation. The method provides a practical means for eliminating large-scale time-consuming field tests.
