Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract We conducted statistical analyses and used modeling techniques to develop derailment probabilities for freight trains and freight cars operating on North American railroads. Knowing the expected frequency of derailment and the conditional probabilities of derailment for individual cars enables estimation of the derailment risk as it is affected by train length, operating speed, and positioning of cars in the consist. These results can be used to quantify the benefit in terms of reduced accident probability and severity of various changes in railroad operating and safety practices.

Author English, GW

Affiliation TranSys Research Ltd. Kingston, ON, Canada
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract This paper presents the results of the latest phase of a multiyear test and analysis research program. The latest test phase involved two freight cars equipped with instrumented wheel sets and operated in consist with Canadian Pacific railway's (CPR) track evaluation car. Twenty-four occurrences of LIV> 1.0 were observed, with the highest observed value exceeding 1.5. An engineering-based LIV predictor model has been developed, which can predict LIV ratios in real time for all axles of, multiple vehicles at multiple speeds for both directions of travel. The predictor performed well in isolating track conditions that will produce high L/Vs, while the existing track standards identified very few sites. The majority of the model's missed predictions and false positive predictions could be explained by variation of parameters that influence LIV, but which are not measured by the geometry car.

Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract Jinzhou Railway Scientific & Technical Research Institute developed a mobile gas pressure rail welding process and its equipment. The process has been used throughout the railway system in China for rail defect repair welding and rail renewal welding because of the superior service performance and the low cost of gas pressure rail welds. It is the preferred rail welding process for heavy haul coal lines in China. The process needs about one hour of track time to complete a rail repair welding - joining a plug rail into track using two gas pressure welds. The in-track process involves a rail bending procedure to provide the necessary rail upset amount without using additional rail pulling device. The GPW equipment is composed of the main body (rail presser), burner, hydraulic pump, cooling system, gas flow control box, and gas cylinders. The compact design of the equipment makes the welding process very portable and suitable for field applications. In addition to its application for in-track welding, the process is also frequently used in trackside rail joining. The failure rate of gas pressure rail welds has been very low even in the Datong-Qinhuangdao line, a heavy haul coal line.

Title The effects of short-term post-weld heat treatments on residual stresses in flash butt welds
 
Author Tawfik, D
Mutton, PJ
Chiu, WK
Affiliation Mechanical Engineering Department, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Institute Of Railway Technology, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract Flash butt welding is commonly used in the manufacture of continuously welded rails (CWR). The finished welds typically exhibit high levels of tensile residual stresses in the rail web. In addition, the surface condition of the web may contain shear drag or other defects resulting from the shearing process. When combined with torsional loading of the web under service loading (particularly at high axle loads), these conditions may contribute to fatigue failure of the weld in a horizontal split web mode. The risk of weld failure may be alleviated by reducing the magnitude of the residual stresses. A thermo-mechanical finite element (FE) model, incorporating the transformation characteristics of the rail material and temperature-dependent mechanical property data, has been used to examine the effects of various localized, short-term postweld heat treatment procedures on residual stresses in the web region of flash butt welds in AS60 and AS68 rails. The results have indicated that the (tensile) residual stress levels in both vertical and longitudinal directions of the web can be reduced by either reheating the base of the foot directly after welding, or air quenching the web. Both treatments have no effect on the microstructure or mechanical properties of the finished weld. An experimental program is currently being undertaken to validate the predicted results. Thereafter, both numerical and experimental approaches will be used to develop modifications to the flash butt welding procedure that should result in improved weld performance under high axle load conditions.

Title Management of rail longitudinal forces under heavy axle loads
 
Author Read, DM
Kish, A
LoPresti, J
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
Kandrew Inc, Peabody, MA, USA
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract Transportation Technology Center, Inc. (TTCI), Pueblo, Colorado, has recently performed tests designed to measure the effectiveness of stress free temperature (SFT) adjustment procedures. The objective of the tests was to measure and evaluate continuous welded rail (CWR) longitudinal response behavior during winter rail break and summer rail de-stress simulations. These tests addressed fundamental maintenance issues regarding how much rail to add or remove and the rail adjustment length necessary to restore the original rail to the original stress free condition. The TTCI tests measured the effect on the SFT distribution along a length of CWR when the rail was cut under high-tension forces simulating a winter rail break. The tests then measured the effectiveness of rail adjustment procedures at restoring the SFT to the pre-cut condition. The TTCI tests have provided valuable data that will enhance CWR maintenance procedures and management of thermal rail stresses. In particular, this paper will address the fundamentals of SFT behavior during a rail break/repair scenario, provide a summary of salient test results addressing de-stressing and SFT readjustment, and identify potential improvements over the current practice and presents conclusions of practical interest. The paper also presents results of both analytical and experimental studies conducted under the United States Department of Transportation's (US DOT) Volpe National Transportation Center (VNTSC) and Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) in the mid 1990's. An important aspect of reducing the stress state on the railroad deals with controlling the longitudinal forces in rails to safe levels, thereby reducing the number of buckled track incidents and rail defect failures. Hence the efficient management of longitudinal thermal forces in CWR becomes an important safety and productivity aspect of heavy haul operations. Track is prone to lateral buckling when the rail is under high compressive forces. When high tensile forces dominate, rail defect growth can accelerate causing rail fractures, weld failures, and rail joints to pull apart. The magnitude of thermal forces is governed by the rail neutral or SFT, or the rail temperature at which the net force is zero. Railways specify that CWR be installed and maintained at an optimum SFT range to minimize damaging force levels. However, research has shown that SFT can change over time, and degradation of the track structure and components caused by heavy axle load operations can have an effect on SFT behavior. One of the critical heavy haul track maintenance issues, based on the assumption that substantial rail repair activity takes place in a heavy haul environment, is how best to maintain a desired SFT when repairing broken rails and welds or removing rail defects. Short repair rails (plug rails) that are installed when the rail temperature is below the specified SFT create the potential for buckled track in warmer weather unless the rail length is adjusted back to the required SFT. Because it is difficult to measure SFT and base management of CWR on a known condition, railways rely on procedures and practices to maintain an acceptable SFT based on these limitations.

Baeza, L
Affiliation Chalmers University of Technology, Gothenburg, Sweden
Volvo Car Corporation, Gothenburg, Sweden
Universidad Politecnica de Valencia, Valencia, Spain
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract Wheel-fail impact loads due to wheel flats are calculated using a validated train-track interaction model. A parametric study is performed to illustrate the influence of train speed, axle load and wheel flat dimensions on impact load magnitudes. Results are discussed with reference to the Swedish wheel removal criterion and to statistics from four wheel impact load detectors on the Swedish heavy-haul line Malmbanan. The load history effects from wheels with and without wheel flats, seasonal variation in thermal stresses and residual stresses from manufacturing on fatigue crack growth in rails are studied. An FE model developed for simulation of the stress state in rails due to train-track interaction is used. The results from the stabilised stress response in the rail, after several wheel-fail contact load passages, are used to investigate the sensitivity in crack growth in the rail cross-section using fracture mechanics. The results show that there is a remarkable decrease in fatigue crack growth resistance of a rail that suffers from repeated wheel flat impact loads, as compared with a rail that does not sustain any such loads. Further, thermal stresses have a significant effect on the fatigue life of the rail.

Title Modeling the probability of train presence on adjacent tracks in railway vehicle intrusion scenarios
 
Author Lin, C-Y
Barkan, CPL
Affiliation Rail Transportation and Engineering Center, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, Illinois, U.S.A.
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Adjacent track accidents (ATA) refer to an accident scenario in which one train derails and intrudes onto an adjacent track and is struck by another train on that track. As with other collisions, ATA can cause severe consequences including infrastructure damage, railway vehicle damage, system disturbance, lading loss, environmental impact and casualties. A typical ATA consists of three sequential events: an initial derailment, an intrusion, and a collision with another train on an adjacent track. When an intrusion occurs, if there is another train next to, or approaching, the intrusion location, there is a risk that this train will collide with the intruding train. This paper describes a probabilistic model and methodology to quantify this probability, which is determined by the available distance between two trains when an intrusion occurs, and the braking capability of the train on the adjacent track. The model provides critical safety information on railway corridor planning. Understanding the locations along a multiple-track railway corridor that have higher ATA risk when an intrusion occurs helps to determine the most effective ways to allocate risk reduction resources to prevent railway vehicle derailments, and if one does occur, prevention of intrusions and subsequent collisions.

Title Load environment and force transfer in railway fastening systems: a case study of the broken spike mystery
 
Author Roadcap, T
Dersch, M
Edwards, JR
Affiliation RailTEC at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Illinois, USA
Visiting Fulbright Scholar at Technical University of Munich, Munich, Germany
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract North American railways have experienced at least 10 wide-gauge derailments since 2000 that were due to broken spikes or screws in timber sleepers, and similar failures have also been observed in other parts of the world. Derailment investigations found that spikes or screws were broken within the sleeper such that they appeared in good condition visually. Broken spikes and screws present a significant safety risk to the railway industry, as well as added costs and the challenge of additional inspection techniques. Further, the problem bears mechanistic resemblance to other fastening system failures experienced worldwide. This paper presents a summary of the research conducted at the University of Illinois to understand the nature of these broken spike failures, as well as some of the research currently underway to investigate its causes. A literature review, survey, and field visits show that spike breakage frequently occurs in locations with significant curvature, new sleepers, and premium elastic fastening systems. One railway visited has 70 curves with broken spike problems, including one curve where 150 broken spikes were found. Another railway is subject to special inspections by the government due to spike breakage problems. Currently, infrastructure owners must have personnel regularly conduct walking inspections of curves, tapping each spike to determine if it is broken. Hypotheses and finite element modeling suggest that longitudinal loads are a key part of these spike/screw failures, and that the lateral load environment also plays an important role.
