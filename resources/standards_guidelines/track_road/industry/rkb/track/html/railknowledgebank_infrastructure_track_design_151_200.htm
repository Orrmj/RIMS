Conference First International Rail Sleeper Conference, 13-17 August 1973, Sydney, Australia
Publication Date 1973
Abstract The Tasmanian Government Railway's system has about 600 miles of 31 6" gauge track laid on untreated native hardwood sleepers, mainly Eucalyptus obliqua (stringybark), E, sieberiana (ironbark), E. amygdalina (peppermint) and E, globulus (blue gum), The sleepers are cut to nominal dimensions 71 x 9" x 5" and the rails are spiked with plain dog spikes or elastic spikes, generally without the use of sleeper plates, In 1954 and 1968 the Forest Products Division of the CSIRO undertook a survey in Tasmania, aimed at determining the service life and causes of failure of sleepers, The findings in 1968 indicated that the prime cause of failure was rail seat deterioration, caused by a combination of timber decay, chemical breakdown and mechanical loading.

Title Steel rail sleepers
 
Author Gillett, DC
Affiliation The Broken Hill Proprietary Company ltd, Melbourne
Source RTAA
Conference First International Rail Sleeper Conference, 13-17 August 1973, Sydney, Australia
Publication Date 1973
Abstract Steel sleepers are used throughout the world but have not been used to any extent within Australia, They have a proven life of some 50 years and, being steel, have the unique property of being able to be economically repaired and reworked so that a total life of 8 0 years is not uncommon. With the advent of levelling/tamping/aligning machines steel sleepers can meet the stringent requirements of the modern railway track without requiring additional maintenance or incurring extra costs over any other form of sleeper. The geometrical quality and the behaviour of tracks laid on steel sleepers are equal to those of tracks laid on other types of sleepers, including continuously welded rails. When the installed cost of a steel sleeper is compared with other sleepers on an annual basis, the steel has a clear economic advantage. Further i steel sleepers are the only type that have a guaranteed high re-sale value when finally removed from service. Advantage can be taken of the proven long life and uniform qualities of steel sleepers to renew them as a complete section rather than the random manner required for other types. The recovered steel sleepers can be examined, repaired and replaced in service at a minimum cosL European experience (1) indicates that 85% of recovered steel sleepers are returned to service. Research work currently progressing in Australia will establish optimum shape and relationships between rail size and sleeper weight.

Title The insulated steel sleeper
 
Author Gillett, DC
Affiliation The Broken Hill Proprietary Co. Ltd, Melbourne, Vic, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract The steel sleeper, very suitable for non-circuited track, does require some form of insulation to electrically isolate one rail from the other for circuited track, It is a relatively simple matter to provide the required electrical insulation but an extremely complex matter to maintain this insulation against the mechanical damage sustained at installation and during the service life. Although there are many forms of insulators available, it was felt that none of the existing systems complemented the advantages of a steel sleeper, particularly with elastic fastenings. Steel sleepers have the unique advantage that they do not require a soft rail pad to prevent localised stressing such as used on concrete sleepers-and thus enable insulators to be used with low creep characte.ristics and enable the clip design toe loads to be maintained throughout the life of the system. It has been reported that clip toe loads have been reduced by 20% over a short period of operation using soft rail pads on concrete sleepers. Various materials were tested electrically and for mechanical strength in the laboratory, The selected materials were then given full scale tests before being installed in the track to assess their service life. Due regard was given to the Australian environment with the concentration of ultra-violet light and its effect upon the insulating materials, The selection of material was rated also upon its creep characteristics which, as expected, would favour the harder materials. It was recognised very early in the testing that the type of fastening system had a major effect upon the life of the insulator. Metal plates to prevent the deformation of the insulating material by the rail fastening clips were quite effective but tended to move out of position until they were clear of the clip and of no value. The insulator quickly deformed and ceased to function as an electrical insulating medium. A fastening system that did not require protection of the insulator against deformation and was not driven over the insulator proved very successful in overcoming damage during installation and promises to give a long life. Although at the time of preparing this report field testing was not complete, it is expected to confirm that a successful, long life, economical insulated steel sleeper has been developed.

Title Timber sleeper development at Deutsche Bundesbahn
 
Author Schulz, G
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract The development of the wood sleeper at the Deutsche Bundesbahn is reported on. Together with the new 2, 80 m long B 75 type concrete sleeper a new 2, 70 m long wood sleeper is undergoing in-track comparison trials to prove its suitability for high speed operation. The gauge uniformity has been improved still further and new protective measures against sleeper-end splitting are examined. The preservative treatment efficiency is increased due to creosote circulation during pressure treatment. New treating diagrams have been developed for Keruing and Kempas sleepers. An experiment with creosote impregnated beechwood sleepers carried out under the time-accelerating climatic conditions of the tropical rain forest of Liberia gives initial inferences on the aging characteristics, the optimum composition and required retention of coal-tar creosote as well as on the suitability of creosoted beechwood sleepers. A new rail fastening is in the process of being tested and in future pads are to be made only of plastic instead of poplar wood.

Title Automated track structure design
 
Author Doyle, NF
Affiliation The Broken Hill Proprietary Co. Ltd., Melbourne, Australia
Melbourne Research Laboratories, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract Despite a background of over a hundred years of successful railway operations, the design of new track essentially depends upon the engineering experience of the track designer. Due to problems of relating an applied track loading to a large number of observed track responses many of the currently used design expressions are at best semi-empirical in nature. When determining stress levels in individual track components the track designer can in certain areas select from a range of alternate design expressions and criteria. Therefore an understanding 6f the significance of the assumptions and limitations that are implicit with the use of these expressions is required. Many of the existing track design criteria have, historically, been derived for the specific operating conditions typical of the period. These conditions would include such parameters as axleload, speed, rail section size, sleeper dimensions and spacing, ballast depth and the level and standard of track maintenance. With regard to the expected future demands of heavier axleloads and higher operating speeds, there are consequently significant reservations of our ability to extend the existing current track design practice to meet these conditions. A study was undertaken on behalf of the Bureau of Transport Economics in order to develop an Interactive Track Design Model for use in the track design process. This study was divided into two distinct phases. During Phase I the available track design expressions together with the relevant engineering rationale were collated and a literature review was produced, Doyle (1979 a). This review was used in Phase II as a source document, thereby enabling an Interactive Track Design Model to be developed. Using this program parametric analyses were performed to identify cost sensitive areas in the current track design practice, and areas for future design development where existing track design data are insufficient to make rational cost decisions, Doyle (1979 b). This paper will describe the Interactive Track Model developed for current track design practice and presents some of the significant conclusions reached during the study.

Title Design and performance of steel sleepers
 
Author Brown, JH
Affiliation The Broken Hill Proprietary Co. Ltd., Melbourne, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract This paper deals with the development of a range of steel sleeper systems for general use; the development has included design, laboratory testing and field evaluation. The design is based on a fatigue analysis in which the sleeper is considered as a beam with welded abutments and subsequently as a beam on elastic foundation. Consideration is taken of dynamic effects, longitudinal restraint, centre binding and track buckling. Laboratory fatigue testing of the sleepers was undertaken to provide a basis for evaluating and improving the designs. Prototypes were tested in a rail way simulator rig and performance 1-1as monitored by measuring strain, subsidence and deterioration. Various rail fasteners and insulation systems were developed and evaluated. Insulation materials were tested for mechanical and electrical properties and stability under U-V radiation. Fasteners were tested to give appropriate rail restraint and fatigue life. Extensive field evaluation of steel sleepers has been undertaken, including the BHP Heavy Haul Sleeper system in the Mt. Newman Mining railway and the Secondary Track sleepers in South Australia. Results show the sleepers to have lives up to 40 years and to be performing generally better than timber types.

Title Development of two-block steel & concrete sleepers the "armason s. 75" rs-type sleeper for heavy duty lines (mining and high speed lines) as per the "A.R.E.A." recommendations
 
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract The reasons why R. Sonneville has developed two-block sleepers rather than prestressed monoblock sleepers: The increase of axle-loads, desired in order to lower transportation cost, is limited by the fatigue due to wheel-rail contact (Hertz fatigue). Rolling surface wear (corrugations, shelling) quickly leads to dynamic fatigue of all track components as well as rolling-stock. The simultaneous increase of speed and axle dynamic load (due at the same time to static load and to the unsprung mass of vehicles) has given rise to a new phenomenon: the "squats" that R.A.P. Sweeney has very well analysed in his publication "Some track problems associated with projected conventional high speed North American locomotives" (A.R.E.A. Bulletin No. 669 Sept. Oct. 1978). In order to reduce the increase of dynamic stresses due to wheel/rail contact, one must reduce the unsprung masses of the vehicles, the mass of the sleepers (which act as anvil) and increase the track elasticity. However, the present trend tends to do exactly the contrary and particularly to find a track modulus as high as possible. It is the "easy way" to lessen the fatigue of the substructure. It must be stressed that this solution can prove disastrous for rails and rolling-stock if geometry and micro-geometry of track, rails and wheels are not perfect.

Title The important role of the fastening
 
Author Dobson, GD
Affiliation Pandrol Australia Pty. Ltd., Blacktown, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract The prime purpose of this short paper is to focus attention on the importance of the rail track fastening, especially the role it plays in the upgrading of the track. In seeking to emphasise the particular significance of the fastening in railway track improvements an account is taken of historical background and that of associated track componentry. The advantages of studying the history of railways is particularly stressed, especially with respect to the wealth of knowledge it embraces and the lessons which can be learned from it. The one significant problem confronting the early railway track pioneers was that of providing an adequate "permanent way" on which to operate trains. They rapidly concluded that the permanency of the track depended upon the duty it was required to serve. As train loads and operations increased the track had to be improved accordingly and so "track upgrading" commenced with the renewal or change of the first component. The degree of track improvement at any particular time being based upon the future duty it had to perform, safety, cost and the economic benefits accrued by the operator. By way of introduction to the subject it is essential to define the term "track upgrading". This is seen as the product of evolution to keep pace with changing circumstances and is achieved by either the total re-building of the track structure or preferably by utilising as much of the existing componentry as can remain technically acceptable for the new conditions. Opportunities to totally replace the track structure seldom present themselves and generally track upgrading is achieved by replacing or strengthening one or more of the four major track components, i.e. rails, sleepers, ballast and subgrade. It will be noticed that the track fastenings have been omitted from the list and this has been purposely done to draw attention to the importance of this fifth major component. Unfortunately fastenings have not always been known by this classification and this oversight has resulted in costly problems later. The engineer who treats fastenings with the prominence they deserve will benefit by his wisdom in the long run. In the context of track "upgrading" then, it is absolutely essential that the engineer is acquainted with all the options he has for modifying and improving the track. Therefore special emphasis and encouragement should be given to researching the important area of track fastenings for solutions. Whilst it is true that better fastenings will not necessarily overcome major track problems, they will substantially reduce them and increase the life of the existing structure in general and that of the other components in particular. The fastening area should receive the same emphasis as that placed upon the adequacy of design for connections used in other spheres of engineering. For example it is inconceivable that an automotive engineer would seek to remedy poor engine mountings by modifying the engine or chassis in order to cater for interaction between these two major dynamic components of the motor vehicle yet in some raillvays vie can observe examples of modification to major track componentry when largely the problem exists in the rail to sleeper connection. Possibly one of the reasons for this, is that connection design often falls into the category of the "too hard basket" and therefore railways are by no means immune when it comes to this problem. Once a fastening is chosen it is installed in very large numbers and for all to criticise, no doubt this alone can be a very good reason for focusing attention elsewhere and why modification to the other track components has been a more acceptable approach. When track is allowed to operate with an inordinate amount of interaction between componentry then this causes rapid deterioration within the structure, resulting in a particularly dangerous condition. Modern fastenings do much to relieve and limit the interaction between rail and sleeper thus promotiny greater overall stability. One area that certainly cannot be ignored or sidestepped is cost and for those seeking better economic results, simple fastening systems which have been specifically designed to allow speedy application and removal can substantially reduce the initial construction and future maintenance costs. Furthermore the standardisation of appropriate rail fastenings which may be used commonly in conjunction with track sleepers or sub-structures built from various materials (i.e. steel, concrete and timber) reduce storage and field complications (for examples see Fig. 8). Thus promoting savings in storehouse inventories, handling etc. Last but not least, improvement in safety and reduction in potential derailment hazards can be realised where greater confidence and reliance can be placed in the sleeper to rail connection. All these points have been dealt with at much greater length in papers presented to this Association in 1973 and 1976 - particularly in the two papers by Mr. C.R. Colville and the author. Rather than repeat their contents here it is recommended that this paper be read in conjunction with the earlier ones so that continuity and depth is available to the recipient .. Finally then at this point in the paper, attention is drawn to an article published by the United Steel Companies Ltd. of Great Britain in the late 1950's. This was written at a time when resilient rail fastenings were in their early stage of development and use, however the criteria quoted is still valid and a valuable reference.
