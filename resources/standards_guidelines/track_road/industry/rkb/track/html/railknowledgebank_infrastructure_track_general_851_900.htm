Title Experience with high pressure treatment of low durability species
 
Author Hurse, AM
Affiliation Victorian Railways, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract The Victorian Railways has 7,700 kilometres of main line track and 1, 570 kilometres of sidings. Practically all the tracks are timber-sleepered using species available within the State, at a density between 1,200 and 1,650 per kilometre. It is estimated that there are 13-million to 14-million timber sleepers installed on the System. The sleeper size is 250 mm x 125 mm x 2. 75 m, and sleepers are purchased in accordance w'ith A. S. 097-1963 Grade 2 Rectangular Sleepers. The accompanying bar chart shows the total number of sleepers renewed each year since 1952 and the proportion of the high durability species (Red Ironbark, Grey Box, Red Gum, Yellow Gum (Euc. leucoxylon) comprising that number. The average annual replacement rate over that period during which main line has reduced 390 km and sidings 124 km, is 541,000, but trends and observations in recent years suggest that a number of factors are combining to increase the useful life of sleepers. These factors are: (i) removal of joints by relaying and welding; (ii) 100% sleeper plating of tracks; (iii) improved track geometry resulting from mechanised maintenance; (iv) more sensible sleeper marking with mechanised gang operation as distinct from manual gangs; (v) improved ballast and drainage conditions.

Title Railway track fastenings - development selection, invention & manufacture
 
Author Dobson, GD
Affiliation Elastic Rail Spike Company (Aust.) Pty. Ltd., Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Second International Rail Sleeper Conference, 9-13 August 1976, Perth, Australia
Publication Date 1976
Abstract The purpose of this paper is to present the current position in relation to track fastening development and endeavour to set out useful guide lines for those who have to assess and report on their adoption. In this presentation there is special emphasis placed upon the role of fastenings in conserving and extending sleeper life especially that of timber at this current time of shortages and high costs. The relationship fastenings bear to concrete and steel sleepers could also be construed as prolonging their life, but rather respective to their efficiency or otherwise in the track. In dealing with development and in respect to fostering new ideas, an attempt is made to outline salient factors which will influence any invention, especially the long term and historical features which must be taken into account. Finally, the role of the manufacturer in the field of track fastenings is discussed with the aim of relating some of the areas of concern and common interest. If we look at the total world advances to date and the trends for the future through the eyes of eminent scrutineers, we can see that most of them agree that we will not continue at the same rate of development nor is it desirable or possible without creating wastage and environmental problems on a grand scale. Our railway industry will be vitally affected by these changes and parallel with most others we are already being asked to consolidate and further develop the achievements we have already made. We must realise that there is no singular solution and that the answer lies in making the best use of all materials available. Bringing this closer to the railway industry I think we will find that there is going to be a continued use of all types of sleepers for the purposes they are most suitable and economic chiefly because fundamentally the rail/sleeper/ballast railway concept is very hard to replace. With large capital investment in the existing railways most attention must be focused on their continued improvement and the industry must grapple with ways to implement these with careful judgement. For the benefits of development to be fully realised they must be brought to the attention of the railway industry and those charged with their adoption need every assistance to assess their potential use. Promotion of new ideas must be fostered from inside the industry and external interested sources. Inventive acumen must not be allowed to lapse for want of suitable outlets and information on how to proceed to possible fruition. The support industries which produce railway components need to ensure that they achieve a very close li.aison with the end user. Railway administration should be aware of the functions carried out by private enterprise and the dependency they have upon the success or failure of this branch of the industry.

Title Track economics principles of life cost minimisation and design of minimum capital cost track
 
Author Gentle, NF
Heacock, RH
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract At the Second International Rail Sleeper Conference in Perth, the Bureau of Transport Economics presented a framework for evaluating track standards (1). That paper examined the evaluation process from the point of view of various railway decision makers concerned with track standards. All costs were considered, embracing the range from the direct readily apparent costs, such as capital, to the not-so-apparent costs such as environmental damage. Included in the paper were a number of conceptual diagrams illustrating certain principles of track optimisation. Of particular interest to this present paper are the relationships between capital costs and track standard and between maintenance costs and track standard. The intuitive idea is illustrated by Figure 1 in which the capital cost curve represents the minimum capital cost track for each standard. Similarly the maintenance cost curve represents the lowest cost method of maintaining the track at the appropriate standard during the life of the track. For the assumed shape of the curves, the total cost curve will exhibit a minimum. The question that arises is whether this minimum occurs at a track standard which is higher than that determined solely by technical or safety reasons. Put another way - under what conditions can we increase the initial quality of the track (thus increasing capital costs) and expect lower life costs for the track? The track design model discussed by Neil Doyle at this conference allows us to start to put some numbers on the cost curves. We are now in a position to get some initial ideas about worthwhile tradeoffs between capital and maintenance costs.

Title The total track structure and its response to its environment
 
Author Lucas, JC
Affiliation British Rail, Derby, England
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract The components, which when put together make up the track structure, tend to be designed in isolation and generally the main design criteria for each are that they should have adequate mechanical strength and be relatively inexpensive. However, most of these components interact with one another in at least one of their basic track functions and the fact that we can still have failures of components indicates that probably we do not take all these interactions into account. In a perfect railway system, the rail would be smooth without any vertical or lateral irregularities, the vehicle wheels would be perfectly circular and smooth and each sleeper would be supported uniformly and in a manner similar to its neighbours. Under these conditions, the force between the wheel and rail would increase only a few percent with respect to the static value for speeds up to the present maximum operating values. Under practical conditions rails are not smooth and can have discrete irregularities in the head. Likewise wheels are not perfectly circular and smooth and can also have discrete irregularities in the tyres, whilst sleeper support is non uniform and can vary from sleeper to sleeper. Generally the effect of these excursions from the oerfect conditions is to increase the forces generated between the vehicle and track and these forces nonnally increase with respect to train speed. In the design of track these dynamic effects are taken into account by increasing the design static loading by an empirical relationship to give an equivalent dynamic loading. For example, sleeper design is based on the assumption that the live loading and subsequent response can be represented by an equivalent static system. This loading includes not only the forces transferred from the rails to the sleeper, but also an assumed ballast reaction to the sleeper. These assumptions simplify design calculations and the interaction of components such as rail fastenings and rail pads are omitted. However, high rates of loading due to vehicles passing over track defects can be such that normal quasi static analysis is invalid and in such cases rail pads, fastenings and sleeper bending stiffness can have an influence on the system response. The development of methods of measuring strains, forces and displacements dynamically has increased our knowledge of the way track behaves, but probably qf equal significance has been the ability to model mathematically the dynamics of the track system and have the means to evaluate these models. These mathematical models have permitted extrapolation to conditions beyond those normally available and have made it possible to investigate the relative importance of the different pa~ameters of the track components on forces, stresses and displacements. A very important aspect of these two approaches has been the ability to verify the validity of the mathematical models and hence increase confidence in predictions derive~ from the models. The response of rails to vehicle loading has been dealt with by C.O. Frederick and this paper is concerned mainly with sleeper response, rail pad forces and possible effects on ballast. Track response is considered when vehicles traverse dipped joints, dipped welds and rail corrugation. Also the effect of wheel flats on track response is discussed.

Title Pressure treated and untreated hardwood sleepers some performance data from wet tropical Australia
 
Author Barnacle, JE
Weatherhead, TF
Affiliation CSIRO Division of Building Research, Melbourne, Australia
Department of Forestry, Queensland, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract Literature relating to the performance of rail sleepers under wet tropical exposure conditions is briefly reviewed. A continuing service test of untreated and preservative treated sleepers of four northern Australian hardwoods exposed to wet tropical conditions at Deeral, North Queensland is described. Meteorological data for this site are given and also a general track description. Timber species, preservatives used and sleeper installation details are provided together with a resume of the performance of species/preservative groups after 17 years' service between 1961 and 1978. The number of sleepers in each species/preservative group which survived before, and after a derailed truck ran through the entire test track in May, 1978 is recorded. The performance of oil-treated sleepers is clearly superior to that of sleepers of the same species treated with a waterborne copperchrome- arsenic preservative and to that of the untreated naturally durable control species used. Factors influencing the performance of sleepers of the various species are discussed and estimates of mean service lives are given. The future of the test is considered.

Title Upgrading the treated sleeper
 
Author Dale, FA
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract Just as railways are under pressure from governments to reduce running costs while carrying greater tonnages and under pressure from other forms of transport, so the wooden railway sleeper is under attack. It is expected to last longer with less maintenance while carrying heavier loads and subject to greater physical abuse from track maintenance machinery. The wooden sleeper cannot do this without continuous improvement, which will be possible only if the results of past and future research and industrial expertise are applied energetically and railways are prepared to pay more for a more reliable, longer lasting sleeper. That rail fastenings must be improved at the same time is axiomatic. Almost a century of research and development has gone into producing better sleepers with better preservative treatment in many countries, but it is a sad fact that much of this knowledge is ignored or forgotten and sleeper improvement is neglected until a major accident or accelerated track breakdown forces engineers and railway management to act. In such situations they may act unwisely, particularly under pressure from politicians or the media. It is also indisputable that the total effort put into wooden sleeper improvement, in relation to the total research and development effort of railways and allied industries, is far too little. This paper offers some suggestions, based on the writer's 30 years' experience in timber preservation, much of it spent on rail sleeper research, for improving the serviceability and extending the life of treated wooden sleepers. Among the possible ways of improving sleeper life, the following deserve most attention, not necessarily in order of importance. (1) Improved preservative penetration and retention. (2) Modification of the preservative. (3) Anti-splitting devices. (4) Improved weather protection. (5) Improved fastenings. The first three are the responsibility of. the sleeper supplier/treater, the others require the cooperation of the permanent way engineer and railway management.

Title Upgrading the timber sleeper
 
Author Vaile, RC
Affiliation Bunning Bros, WA, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract Timber sleepers, particularly jarrah, without treatment are continuing to provide the most economical sleeper for a number of railways. I believe that this situation is unlikely to change for some time in areas of Westrail, British Railway, Belgian Railways and many others. The need for the W.A. Timber Industry to look closely at its product is the result of events occurring during the 1960's within Western Australia. Up to this time the ~J.A. Timber Industry had supplied jarrah sleepers with complet~ confidence that they would give 25 years' service under virtually any conditions. Historically th'is was a matter of record, v1ith sleepers having given these lives in the West Australian Government Railways (now Westrail), Commonwealth Railways, British Railways, South African Railways and many others when exposed to the weather, and 50 plus years when used in the more protected environment of the London Underground. Jarrah (eucalyptus marginata) is almost totally resistant to decay and insect attack, thus, as the sleepers supplied to the North West Iron Ore Companies (Hamersley Iron and Mt. Newman) were among the largest sleepers produced by the West Australian Industry, they were confidently expected to give a 20 year life. The performance of W.A. hardwood sleepers in the Pilbara should not be underestimated as they continue to carry many millions of tonnes of ore and trains. Nevertheless, these North West Australian railways have proved to be an accelerated test bed for many items associated with railways. Jarrah sleepers were no exception and problems which usually became evident after 15-20 years were occurring in a third of this time. Within 5 years of track commissioning a percentage of jarrah sleepers were split, broken, suffered from spike kill and had apparently weathered to be collectively indicative of an ageing rate of up to 3-4 times the norm. To be fair to the jarrah sleeper, it had been designed for 25 tonne axle loads but was subjected, in some cases, to 40 tonnes, some sleepers had been broken when installed on the heavily cambered formation and in the tight curves, where the worst spike kill and splitting occurred together, each sleeper contained no less than 16 dog spikes. End protection and climatic conditions govern the rate at which timber dries and the faster this occurs the more the shrinkage of timber compounds the difficulties. Whilst base plates reduce rail cut by spreading the load, they also provide fixed spacing for spikes in timber which, if bored green, will shrink to reduce the spacing between the holes. If the sides of the sleeper are restrained by large metal plates and, in effect, nails, some splitting forces will result. Whilst considering base plates it is worth noting that the problem still exists if the sleepers are bored green to the exact pattern of the plates and then installed dry when the spacing laterally between the holes is slightly less than the plates. - The use of plates under these circumstances compounds the basic tendency of the dog spike to split the timber anyway. The longest lives of jarrah sleepers are generally recorded in the U.K. which means that the sleepers had some opportunity to dry before boring and installation (in any event, climatic conditions allow the timber to remain at a higher e.m.c. certainly than the North West of Australia) while chairs and screw spikes play their part in minimising the externally applied splitting force. This background information enabled us to look at the W.A. Hardwood sleeper, remove it from its historical context and consider the future of W.A. Hardwoods in modern railtrack. I am pleased to say that the W.A. Industry is now offering not only an improved sleeper but is also capable of providing technical advice on its use and installation.

Title British railways high speed track
 
Author Purbrick, MC
Affiliation British Railways, London, UK
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract British Railways system serves a densely populated island and is the legacy of the history of the development of a multitude of private railway companies in the 19th century. Very little of the system was designed with high speeds in mind and much of the route mileage has a high percentage of curve which limits maximum speed. Whilst the inter-city networks serve most centres of population in the country there are four principal trunk routes (Fig. l); the line from London to Edinburgh on the east side of the country, from London to Glasgow on the west side of the country, from London to South Wales and the West Country, and the cross-country route from north east to south west. During the decade from 1955 to 1965 most of the traction was changed from steam operation to diesel. A modest amount of electrification was carried out principally between London, Manchester and Liverpool. Subsequently the electrification was extended through to Glasgow. During the same decade as dieselisation was being carried out the construction of modern track and signalling was proceeding and the equipment used was suitable for higher speeds without much additional investment. Over the period, speed of operation was gradually raised to a maximum speed of 160 kph, together with widespread improvement in average journey time. The results of the new services on the electrification mentioned above were an increase of passenger journeys of 100% in the four years following completion. Market research showed that the highest single factor which led to members of the public travelling by rail rather than alternative forms of transport was that the journey was quicker and less tiring. On this basis B.R. started to look at further ways of increasing speeds and reducing journey times. A number of railway administrations throughout the world were investing in entirely new routes to achieve high uniform speeds. A study was carried out within B.R. to determine whether such an investment could be justified in connecting London to any of the major industrial centres in the country. It was concluded that within the strict financial criteria applied to the investment in B.R. no case could be made for new lines in a country which is compact, highly developed and densely populated. This solution was therefore discarded and efforts were directed towards better use of the existing network both from the point of view of the track and signalling and of the traction and rolling stock which runs upon it.

Abstract Service life distributions were derived from 25-year data on 40 groups of hardwood sleepers of low natural durability (mainly preservative treated) in a test track in Victoria, Australia. Title Technical aspects of fastening system selection
 
Author O'Rourke, MD
Affiliation Broken Hill Proprietary Co. Ltd, Melbourne, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Fifth International Rail Track Conference, 12-18 September 1983, Newcastle, Australia
Publication Date 1983
Abstract The design and selection of rail fastening systems is still largely based on individual experience, and qualitative rather than quantitative assessment. As a result no consensus exists within railway systems regarding the required properties of a fastening system. Even prior to the introduction of the new breed of elastic fastenings, and their growing use particularly on steel and concrete sleepers, uniformity did not exist with different railways using different sizes and numbers and types of spike, on timber sleepers. Obviously the decision facing railway engineers is more difficult today with the many 'brands' of fastening systems available e.g. Pandrol, Safelok, Fist, Traklok, Wilclip. Superficially these systems are technically similar in that they each can be simply designed to meet the technical specifications of railways with regard to toe load, longitudinal creep, insulation, etc. The questions which must be raised then are: (i) Do current technical specifications adequately examine fastening system performance? (ii) Is performance in specified tests a means of differentiating between fastening systems during the selection process? This paper will attempt to examine these questions by considering some commonly specified tests e .g. toe load, longitudinal creep resistance, as well as discussing other tests which would also provide relevant data . The discussion will concentrate on concrete and steel sleepers since their introduction has fueled the development of a number of elastic fastening systems. The discussion will also emphasise 'system' performance. Concrete sleepers require a pad and insulators, while steel sleepers require an insulation system to meet track circuiting requirements. Factors such as pad stiffness will influence the overall resilience of a system and effect the transfer of force between the r ail and t he sleeper. Specifications whe re possible should consider these aspects.

Author Gelbstein, EE
Lindop, RDM
Affiliation Transmark, London, UK
Source RTAA
Conference Fifth International Rail Track Conference, 12-18 September 1983, Newcastle, Australia
Publication Date 1983
Abstract Track and signalling are perhaps the rnost obvious and the most essential fixed assets of any railway. This is reflected in the high proportion of operating revenue that is devoted to their maintenance and renewal. In 1982, British Rail spent 25% of its income on track and signalling. Both tracf and signalling. occupy the same physical part of the railway, and both are vital to the safe, efficient operation of the system. Both require a high quality of maintenance, the more so as technological development has led to increased sophistication, in order to maintain the standards which are necessary in a modern high speed railway. Yet, despite these similarities, historically the signalling engineer and the civil engineer have always gone their separate ways. Today there is increasing need for co-operation, but at the same time there are a growing number of areas where the prime aims of the two disciplines are in conflict. The civil engineer is looking for smoothly aligned, easily and cheaply maintained permanent way, but he continues to find the signal engineer waiting to interfere with his rails (by fitting insulated joints, cable terminations or point machines) or his formation (by burying cables or erecting signals) . This can be seen by permanent way staff as trespass on their domain, and signalling equipment gets treated vdth less than the respect it deserves. In the authors' experience, sometimes as many as 50% of failures of signalling devices (track circuits, point motors, and so on) can be attributable to civil engineering staff at work, and all too often this is because there is a lack of understanding of the signal engineer's problems and needs. This short paper outlines (but not from a Civil engineer's point of view) some of the major areas of conflicting requirements between the civil engineer and the signal engineer, and looks at the methods that are being used to solve or obviate them.

Title Rail rectification - meeting the challenge
 
Author Lombardo, AB
Affiliation Speno International Group, Perth, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Sixth International Rail Track Conference, 17-19 March 1986, Melbourne, Australia
Publication Date 1986
Abstract Rail is one of the major assets of all railways and therefore its efficent use is of prime concern to railway management. Overall efficiency must consider both the revenue earning functions and the costs of owning the asset. In the case of rail, the revenue earned and the cost of rail purchase and installation are usually only part controllable by the railway management. Maintenance costs on the other hand can he more positively controlled and a large contribution to overall efficiency and profitability can be made by minimising the life cycle costs of the rail. Many variables contribute to the life cycle costs of rail and the relative importance of each is not the same for all railroads. The end of life for rail, with the exception of replacement because of track upgrading, is determined by its state of deterioration due to wear, plastic flow, fatigue defect formation and occasionally corrosion . The main costs of owning rail are the initial costs such as purchase, welding and installation plus the maintenance costs due to deterioration in service . The costs due to rail deterioration clearly depend upon the rate and mode of deterioration. Traditionally the rate and mode of deterioration has been considered to be virtually fixed for any given track.and traffic system. Maintenance is then carried out when the degree of deterioration reaches some pre-determined criteria. Minimisation of life cycle costs then depend upon optimising maintenance criteria and minimising the cost of each maintenance operation. The rate and mode of deterioration of rail need not be fixed and inevitable for a given track and traffic systern. New rail grinding techniques - such as Assymetric Rail Profiling - are being used significantly to reduce life cycle costs by decreasing rail rolling stock wheel wear rates, removing and preventing recurrence of rail corrugation, increasing the time between track tamping cycles and reducing fatigue defect formation and growth. Speno International have been involved with the Rectification of Rail by grinding for the past 25 years. In that time Rail Grinding techniques and Rail Grinding machine technology have taken many changes. The following paper has therefore set out to provide a resume of the so called conventional rail grinding methods, the more recently developed Assymetric Rail Profiling grinding methods as well as modern technological changes which have been made to the peno rail rectification machines to keep up with the everchanging requirements of todays railways.

Title The performance of rail sleeper materials in track. What, in comparative terms do we really know?
 
Author Barnacle, JE
Affiliation CSIRO Division of Forestry & Forest Products, Melbourne, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Seventh International Rail Track Conference, 18-21 October 1988, Auckland, New Zealand
Publication Date 1988
Abstract All too frequently in literarure exaggerated claims are made about service lives of different sleeper materials, or types, without the support of valid evidence. Also, the sleepers being compared are, almost invariably, not adequately defined, and the conditions under which the stated service lives were obtained i.e., the bases of comparison are not given. The existence of such comparisons in literature suggests that they are acceptable only because they say what many want to believe. Some examples are discussed in detail to demonstrate how such information confuses rather than clarifies distinctions becween the sleeper materials being compared. As the increasingly complex interactions of track and rolling stock parameters that influence sleeper (and rail) life are being recognised, the need is stressed for: (i) recognition of the general lack of real long-term data for any sleeper material in heavily loaded service tracks, (ii) clear distinctions to be made becween claimed or inferred performance, and factual in-service data for the different sleeper materials or types being compared, and (iii) simultaneous testing under defined in-service conditions, if real comparative performance data are to be obtained for different sleeper materials or types.
