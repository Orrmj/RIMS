Title Rail/wheel lubrication
 
Author Gorman, J
Millington, B
Affiliation Fessl Pty. Ltd., Parkes, Australia
Payne Industries
Source RTAA
Conference Seventh International Rail Track Conference, 18-21 October 1988, Auckland, New Zealand
Publication Date 1988
Abstract Rail wear is a problem which railway men have faced for a long time. Theoretically, under perfect conditions, side wear on rails should be minimal as the flanges of the wheels should never bear against the rails. On straight track the coning of the wheels should keep the wheel flanges from attacking the rails, and on curves the superelevation should counteract the centrifugal force tending to force the wheel flanges against the outer rail. New rail and wheel profiles are designed to minimise contact and hence wear. However, in practice, rail wear does occur, to which all track men will testify. Wheel flange wear is equally of great concern. When the flanges become particularly worn, they create greater rail wear. Without efficient lubrication, rails, in some locations, may last only six weeks before reaching wear limits, and the wear on the wheel flange leads to premature machining and maintenance. The need for lubrication is dictated by the wear pattern shown on both the rail and wheels. The benefits of tangent and curve rail lubrication can now be demonstrated. Curve rail lubricants have been used for many years with great success. Only recently have the benefits of tangent rail lubrication come to the forefront. Lubrication is also necessary to reduce rolling resistance in negotiating curves and travelling straight track. The friction between wheel and rail is such that an appreciable proportion of the tractive effort of the locomotive is used to overcome this alone. In tests carried out in the United States, where curved and tangent tracks were lubricated primarily to observe effects of various lubrication systems on rail and wheel flange wear, a noticable reduction in fuel consumption was recorded. This prompted further tests to determine the percentage of fuel savings gained under different circumstances. It was stated at the recent symposium on lubrication held in Memphis Tennessee, that effective rail/wheel lubrication can extend the life of rails by up to four times, the life of wheels by up to ten times and save fuel by up to thirty percent. The potential savings are enormous , and all railways are persuing this cost effectiveness by selecting the lubrication system best suited to their operations. There are a variety of lubrication options available including track mounted, road/rail vehicle mounted and locomotive mounted or a combination of these. Various railways, research establishment, and supply companies are exploring the options in each of the following elements: 1. The lubrication medium. A variety of oils and greases are under test. 2. The optimum quantity of lubricant to be applied in various track conditions and train configurations 3. The relative merits of the large variety of lubricant applicators. 4. The actual fuel savings of comparative systems. The object of this paper is to present a resume of recent publications on these findings largely published following the 1987 2nd International Symposium on wheel/rail lubrication referred to earlier.

Abstract Before speaking about track maintenance on the Netherlands Railways it would be a good idea to make a few general comments. For these reasons you will understand that improvement of the substructure on the NS is not just a current problem - it has always been a fundamental requirement for track construction.

Title Characterising track profiles from the response to measured data of a simple model of a freight vehicle
 
Author Ford, R
Affiliation School of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering UNSW, Kensington, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Tenth International Rail Track Conference, 12-14 September 1994, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1994
Abstract Instead of assessing track quality from the extent to which measured geometry exceeds set values engineers can assess it by calculating how a simple mathematical model of a vehicle responds dynamic to measured track profiles. In this way problem areas of track can be identified from predicted track forces and/or predicted vehicle acceleration - characteristics which are close to the operating concerns of railways. In this paper a function damped model is used to represent typical freight vehicles. and results for several speeds are presumed simultaneously to give an immediate overall impression of track quality from which the nature of any defects present can be inferred. Because the model is simple the analysis is fast and the results easy to interpret. Possible applications include avoiding damage to lading calculating the fatigue life of cars and scheduling track maintenance.

Title Deformation behaviour of rails
 
Author Marich, S
Burnham, K
Affiliation BHP Research Melbourne Laboratories, Melbourne, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Eleventh International Rail Track Conference, 27-30 October 1996, Adelaide, Australia
Publication Date 1996
Abstract This paper provides details of a study conducted at BHP Research-Melbourne Laboratories which sought to simulate the deformation behaviour of rails using a Henzian contact stress analysis. This aspect has become of critical importance because of the requirements for the prevention of sulb-surface defect formation and hence potential rail failure, particularly in situations where rail wear has been reduced by improved lubrication and/or modified wheel and rail profiles. Rail characteristics observed in two known systems, namely BHP Iron Ore's Newman-Hedland railroad and the Mass Transit Railway of Hong Kong, were used to validate the analysis by comparing predicted with acrual performance. The procedure was then applied to assess the technical feasibility of using rails with reduced depths of hardening under particular operating conditions. Resistance to subsurface defect formation was the major criterion used to detennine the minimum required depth to which the rail head should be hardened, subject to acceptable rail surface hardness. The results obtained indicate that 'shallow' hardening to depths greater than 12 mm from the gauge comer of the rail head may be expected to provide the performance required by mass transit systems.

Title Recent experiences with the performance of aluminothermic rail welds under high axle loads
 
Author Offereins, GA
Mutton, PJ
Affiliation BHP Iron Ore Pty Ltd, Melbourne, Australia
Monash University, Clayton, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Thirteenth International Rail Track Conference, 11-14 November 2001, Canberra, Australia
Publication Date 2001
Abstract Aluminotherrnic welding is used by BHP Iron Ore Pty Ltd for all field welding requirements. The preferred process utilises a short preheat and a portion hardness of 340-360 HB to provide weld characteristics compatible with high strength rail grades used in mainline tangent and curved track. In response to recent failures in aluminotherrnic welds, and in recognition that such welds represent one of the major limitations to further increases in axle loads, a program was undertaken to reduce the risk of premature failures, and to develop improved rail welding and track maintenance practices which would meet the perfonnance demands of higher axle loads. Alternative aluminotherrnic welding procedures were assessed using a combination of laboratory and in-track testing. A detailed analysis of weld failures and extensive in-track instrumentation program were undertaken to correlate failure modes with the in-track loading environment. Revised inspection procedures, including radiographic testing, were used to identify defective welds and audit welding procedures. The results of this program highlight the limitations of existing aluminotherrnic welding procedures as axle loads approach 40 tonnes, and provide the basis of a performance-based specification for aluminotherrnic welding of rails for high axle load conditions.
