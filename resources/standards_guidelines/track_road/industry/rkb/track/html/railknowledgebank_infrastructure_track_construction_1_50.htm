Title Improving line profitability: ten years of research and line tests for an innovative ballastless railtrack
 
Author Santini, M
Affiliation Studio Santini & C railroad consultants, Mombello Monferrato, AL, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Exactly 10 years ago SI.AR Consortium was established for financing the development of a profit making ballastless railtrack system. The authors have been charged of the whole plan. This report describes the research performed and the results obtained. Since the ancient dispute between the great engineers Stephenson and Brunel, in the heroic age of the past century, the track has been based on rails and ties, a peculiar structure giving the right continuous geometry and stability of the line. So the traditional basic philosophy "rails & ties" has been maintained, but the ballast bed, whose behaviour is inherently stocastic and often weak, has been replaced by high reliability, no maintenance, industrialized in processing and laying down, components. Such components have been designed, produced and estensively tested, both in laboratory and on site for years. Three sections have been built along different railway lines in Italy, and monitored for some time during common train traffic; they are still on duty and no component of the whole innovative track, resilient ones included, showed any sign of wear or tear. The railtrack system here described in brief, proved to be fast and easy to lay down, with a very quick control of the final track geometry; and moreover with the same philosophy a wide family of different railtracks can be tailor-made to meet customer requirements - Light rails systems - freight dedicated lines - high damped systems for environmental protection from train borne vibrations, so there is still room for customizing the system to local costs & required performances.

Author Govender, S
Affiliation Transnet Freight Rail, Vryheid, South Africa
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Ballast is a critical element of the superstructure of the track as it maintains the track stability. The maintenance of ballast is frequent and expensive due its constant degradation and the subsequent loss of track geometry. Neoballast is a new innovative ballast aggregate, developed by COMSA in Barcelona, that is coated with recycled rubber originating from recycled tyres. One of the main purposes of this new ballast is to reduce the stiffness of the track as well as to increase the abrasion resistance. Therefore, this study intends to investigate the viability of Neoballast on the Heavy Haul lines in South Africa in terms of its mechanical properties based on the minimum required specifications for Heavy Haul lines, as compared to the current ballast on the Coal line. Results from the Los Angeles Abrasion, Mill Abrasion, Soundness, and Water Absorption tests conducted, indicated that Neoballast provided more abrasion resistance, weathering resistance and less water absorption as compared to the current Coal line ballast. Thus indicating a reduction in ballast fouling which will result in a decrease in ballast layer settlement. Furthermore, the Neoballast indicated to be suitable for the Coal line. Neoballast will lead to a decrease in the deterioration of track geometry and economic costs of maintenance on Heavy Haul lines.

Title Development of the ideal sleeper
 
Author Sue, M
Kumakura, T
Konishi, T
Affiliation East Japan Railway Company (JR-EAST), Saitama, Japan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract JR East has, since the pre-privatization Japanese National Railways (JNR) era, developed and deployed many types of prestressed concrete sleepers (PC sleepers), including those for local lines. The design strength of sleepers applied up to now is based on theories established in the development of standard sleepers for main track in 1961, during the JNR era. However, the service life of PC sleepers is still unknown. In light of that, we decided to survey the deterioration level of the sleepers used for about 30 to 50 years after being laid and re-examine design strength and other standards. As a result, we can conclude that the strength of the sleeper body is maintained with the majority of the sleepers. For the design load with which the design strength of sleepers is calculated, we have to take account of the conditions of sections and sites where the sleepers are to be laid and precisely identify the behavior of the sleepers when a train runs on them, such as the dispersive power and the wheel load fluctuation rate. If we achieve those, we will be able to review the current design strength to design a thinner PC sleeper. USPs have been developed for purposes such as controlling subsidence of track, with rubber being the main material used in Japan, and the effects of those have been confirmed. However, in Europe, many types of USPs using urethane have been commercialized and introduced. With understanding of the characteristics of urethane USPs, we decided to start development of a urethane USP that will suit conditions in Japan. We conducted tests in areas such as subsidence characteristics of resilient sleepers with USPs (rubber or urethane) used in Japan and other countries. The tests were done using a full-scale track tester. The results indicate that resilient sleepers have an effect of controlling subsidence of track when compared with sleepers without USPs. When comparing the USPs of different materials, the amount of subsidence of track tends to be smaller with urethane USPs than with rubber USPs. Urethane is excellent in terms of flexibility and shock-absorbing properties, and the material itself has elasto-plastic deformation characteristics. Having those characteristics, urethane USPs increase the contact area between the bottom surface of a resilient sleeper and the gravel, preventing the gravel from being worn or rolling away. They also alleviate the load transmitted from the sleeper to the ballast, so further effects in controlling subsidence of track can be expected. Achieving the development of a thinner PC sleeper and “Ideal Sleeper” with the optimal USP will allow selection of materials suiting the conditions for individual sections, significantly reducing costs of material and maintenance work. We also believe that we can expect such sleepers to be adopted taking into account life cycle cost of materials and the like and innovation to maintenance management concepts.

Publication Date 2016
Abstract In the ballasted track, when sleepers are hanging from the rails without contacting the ballast under no train loads they are called hanging sleepers. When trains pass on the track under these conditions, the hanging sleepers contact the ballast below and generate impact loads that can break ballast and/or cause mud-pumping. This causes track conditions to deteriorate quickly. Hanging sleepers tend to occur mostly in the vicinity of rail joints and the boundary between ballasted and ballastless tracks, or at other places where the settlement of ballasted tracks are discontinuous. RTRI has been developing different versions of automatic irregularity-correcting sleepers (AICS) that will automatically compensate for the discontinuous settlement of ballasted tracks and minimize the occurrence of hanging sleepers. This paper introduces the final version AICS; a low-cost short-sleeper type (hereinafter referred to as “Level Keeper”), which will soon be commercialized. Authors performed cyclic loading tests on life-size track models, which simulated the transition zone. In addition, authors have performed a practical test in the transition zone on an operating line. The authors, thereby, made it clear that the Level Keeper have enough potential to prevent hanging sleepers on the existing line.

Abstract We started from the assumption that wheels on train’s axes are not the only regular and “predictable” elements in the railway environment which can be employed in order to link space with time and extract speed information. We wanted to go beyond the on-board dynamic systems. Paying attention to the ground, we noticed that the basic track’s elements were going to repeat indefinitely and no interruptions or discontinuities are admitted. The "basic cell" of the railroad is basically composed by two portions of rails maintained at a fixed distance by a transverse, concrete or steel-made, plinth, known as “sleeper”, which acts also as a “sink” for the weight and stress forces due to a high speed train passage. The iron bars, the sleeper and the docking element between the two can be seen as an elementary cell, which is “infinitely” repeated throughout the longitudinal direction of the railway. Its profile can be tracked during the train’s movement. The time requested to pass from a “cell” to another one gives the link necessary to measure the current speed. The best solution to “see” the ground-elements passing below the train axes is tivially an “electronic eye”: an on-board camera. But, are we really interested on acquiring and processing all this data? Obviously not. To reach our scope we were searching for a sensor which can draw the profile of the longitudinal path tracked by a fixed point on board. A profile which can be obtained recording the fast-changing distance between that fixed point of the train (where the sensor is placed) and the nearest element on the ground. In conclusion we need a distance-meter. This profile is, theoretically speaking, very regular. Anyway a maximum error in spacing exists and it is guaranteed by certain infrastructure requirements: it can't be greater than 5 mm, and it is periodically checked by maintenance works. This must be considered the most influent limit of accuracy for our goals. Our experiments considered the docking element as a reference, because it is the most improbable to be covered by ballast or other disturbing objects.The measurement of the amount of elapsed time between the repetition of two subsequent basic element of the track profile leads, with few calculations, to the measure of the current speed. The methodology seems to be clear and effective, however it is important to parallel identify the limits beyond potentialities.

Abstract Improper track geometry can lead to derailments with severe consequences, thus avoiding such geometric deviations is a priority in ensuring safe railway operation. In the North American railway network, derailments have been caused by gauge widening due to deteriorated concrete sleepers and fastening systems, despite the fact that concrete sleepers generally hold gauge better than timber sleepers. Similarly, a review of literature shows that sleeper cracking from center binding is among the most critical concrete sleeper and fastening system problems internationally. As ballast support conditions are critical to sleeper performance, there is a need to fully understand the behavior of these poorly supported sleepers. To quantify the influence of support conditions on sleeper deflection and propose a methodology to predict gauge widening, laboratory experiments were performed at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. Using a static structural loading frame, new and cracked concrete sleepers were subjected to different supporting conditions through the use of rubber pads. Simulated conditions included center bound sleepers, newly tamped track, and track under high impact loads. Poor support can force sleepers to bend and crack when subjected to high loads, which potentially makes the track more prone to geometry deviations, including gauge widening. This paper presents a correlation between ballast support conditions, structural health of concrete sleepers, and their effect on track gauge. Using statistical tools, it is shown that there is no significant difference between experimental results of new and cracked sleepers. In addition, the gauge widening effect due to pure concrete sleeper bending seemed to be minimal.
