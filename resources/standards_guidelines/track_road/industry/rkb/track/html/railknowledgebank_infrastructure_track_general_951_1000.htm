Title Defective rail fracture under dynamic, thermal, and residual stresses
 
Author Igwemezie, JO
Kennedy, SL
Feng, X
Rowan, W
Affiliation Applied Rail Research Technologies Inc Markham, Ontario, Canada
Canadian Institute of Guided Ground Transport, Kingston, Ontario, Canada
Transport Development Centre, Transport Canada, Montreal, Quebec, Canada
Conference 5th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1993), 6-13 June, 1993, Beijing, China
Abstract In countries such as Canada, that experience significant temperature changes throughout the year, thermal, residual and dynamic stresses can combine to cause rail 10 fracture. The resulting derailments and clean-up operations can be expensive. Several research efforts have been directed towards defining and improving the fracture toughness of rail steel. For these efforts to be fruitful, the results have to be integrated into the daily monitoring of dynamic wheel load from defective train wheels. This paper reports on the findings of a rail fracture resistance research program which attempts to define rail critical dynamic fracture load as a function of rail fracture toughness, detect size, location and orientation, rail ambient temperature, and residual stresses. This was achieved through fracture testing of 1 8 foot (5.5 m) long rails removed from service. The rails were instrumented and then placed in a 25 foot (76 m) half track bed located in a cold chamber. The dynamic loads were simultaneously measured using shear gauges location on the rail web and a 200 kips (8896 kN) impact force transducer. Defects such as vertical split beads, pipe web, head/web separations and horizontal split heads were not susceptible to failure from impact bad due to lack of crack opening forces. Rail head transverse type defects readily produced fracture. The location size and orientation of the defect as well as tensile thermal forces and residual stress were found to be critical parameters in determining the rail fracture load. Finally, an expression is presented for relating dynamics loads to rail fracture toughness, defect size, temperature change and residual stresses.

Title Effect of residual stress on rail web-cracking
 
Author Urashima, C
Sugino, K
Affiliation Yawata R&D Laboratory, Nippon Steel Corporation, Tokyo, Japan
Conference 5th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1993), 6-13 June, 1993, Beijing, China
Abstract Propagation characteristics of rail web-cracking has been discussed in connection with residual stress in roller-straightened rails. To clarify the effect of residual stress on rail web-cracking, a few studies were conducted. First, testing methods for rail web-cracking were examined and, a DWWT method with high ,accuracy and good reproducibility was newly proposed. Secondly, using this test method, rail web-cracking of several kind of rails was evaluated. DHR rails and NHR rail that were hardened by heat-treatment showed better web-cracking properties than standard carbon rail and low alloy rails. The predominant property of the heat-treated rails is due to the existence of compressive residual stress in rail head. By removing the residual stress in the rails, the length of web-cracking became almost identical among several kinds of rails. The preferred distribution of residual stress in rail for suppressing web-cracking was discussed.

Abstract The track irregularity record obtained by the Inertia method has the distortion of waveform. Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia

Abstract Chromium-molybdenum (Cr-Mo) steel rails have been in trial service since 1975, and favorable reports have been received from all test locations. These Cr-Mo rails, containing nominally 0.75% C, 0.85% Mn, 0.75% Cr, and 0.18% Mo, exhibit life expectancy equal to that of head-hardened and fully heattreated rails. Rails of this composition require a special flash-butt welding cycle to delay the cooling rate of the weld to provide a uniform heat-affected zone microstructure. In this study, the Cr-Mo rail steel composition was modified by reducing the manganese and chromium contents while maintaining the same as-rolled hardness. It was found that a 0.75% C, 0.60% Mn, 0.60% Cr and 0.21% Mo steel has heat-affected zones which are virtually martensite-free under the conventional flash-butt welding conditions. This lean Cr-Mo rail steel composition appears to be well-suited for heavy duty rail production.

Title Locomotive wheel-rail loading: A systems approach
 
Author Koci, HH
Swenson, CA
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract With the use of heavier and larger cars, more powerful locomotives, increased train lengths, and changes in track maintenance practices, new problems of interaction between rail vehicles and interaction between wheel and rail have been manifested. This has led to intensified research of these interactions as they are influenced by vehicle and track parameters as well as train operating practices. As a manufacturer of locomotives for world-wide markets, Electro-Motive Division of General Motors Corporation (EMO) has conducted experimental and analytical studies of these vehicle and wheel-rail interactions. Koci and Marta (1965) reported results for the basic curve negotiation loads developed between wheel and rail with 2, 3, and 4-axle locomotive trucks, and they described the curving phenomenon as it relates to wheel-rail creep and friction. In the late 1960's, a number of tests were conducted, in cooperation with railroads in the USA and Canada, to further study operating conditions and train handling procedures which were thought to influence derailment tendency. Influences of locomotive braking practice, location of empty and long overhang cars in trains, rail profile, and locomotive maintenance have been reviewed by Koci and Marta (1971). In the 1970's, much of this type of research has been focussed and combined under the Track-Train Dynamics (TTD) Program coordinated by the Association of American Railroads. While contributing to the TTD Program, EMD also continues to conduct its own analytical studies and use its test car and locomotives to conduct full-scale tests. This paper is an overview of EMD's work in locomotive wheel-rail loading, and it is intended to reflect recent developments. These developments are mainly in two areas: use of the modern, high speed digital computer as an analysis tool, and the comprehensive measurement of dynamic wheel-rail loads at sites specifically chosen for deteriorated or special track conditions which were found to maximize wheel-rail loads.

Title The characteristics and control of long pitch rail corrugation at heavy axle loads
 
Author Mair, RI
Jupp, RA
Groenhout, R
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract A study has been undertaken on the formation of long pitch rail corrugation at heavy axle loads. From spectral analyses of corrugation profiles and track vibrations a correlation has been noted between track/vehicle resonance and the dominant corrugation pitch. It is concluded that corrugation development is best controlled by the use of high strength rails and recommendations are given on the strength level required for different wheel load/wheel diameter combinations.

Abstract The present situation of high strength rails in Japan is described. Fine pearlitic microstructure is found desirable for both wear resistance and fatigue crack prevention. Title Special rails on heavy haul railways
 
Author Oliveira, JHS
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract This paper presents a brief description of the works carried out at the superstructure on two of the more important Brazilian Railways, aiming at capacitating them for the transportation of iron ore in large scale. Besides, it focalizes the behaviour and control of performance of the so-called "special rails" in present use and also, of those still under testing. A model method for testing rails is presented as an annex to this paper, which may be utilized by interested railways facing similar problems, producing significant and standardized results.

Title Track condition monitoring via strain gauging, during longwall mining subsidence effects on the line
 
Author Widders, R
Affiliation ETRS (NSW), Australia
Conference CORE 1993: Contracting Railways - Safety, Standards and the Surroundings, 17-20 October 1993, Newcastle, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 1993
Abstract The first instance of longwall coal mining beneath railway tracks in Australia occurred at Booragul, south of Newcastle, in June-July 1991. The rail section above the mine path was fitted with strain gauge sensors, and the strains / stresses monitored with a datalogger to directly indicate the effects of local subsidence on the rail tension. This paper outlines the methods used in analysing the changing stress levels in the rail, so that re-tensioning could be scheduled. In this manner, the rail tension was maintained within acceptable limits, hence avoiding the potential of local buckling or damage to the track due to the possible high ambient temperature conditions. The monitoring and analysis ensured that the safety of the track was not impaired in any way, and that passenger as well as freight trains could continue to use the track, with suitable speed restrictions, during the passage of the longwall mine beneath.
