Title Railways of Australia vehicle/track studies Westrail track studies
 
Author Holm, AB
Notte, A
McNaughton, LD
Marich, S
Affiliation Westrail
NSW Public Transport Commission
Melbourne Research Laboratories, BHP
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract When considering expenditure on any new project, particularly in the harsh economic environment existing today, one must ask oneself whether the expected returns justify the expenditure. Today, engineering research with the consequent technological advancements, is being heavily criticised and largely blamed for much of the evil which exists in our present day society. Yet, most of the industrialised nations of the world continue to expend funds on research projects in all the fields of engineering with, in more recent times, a major effort aimed at railway engineering. Therefore, one must conclude that returns from research projects continue to justify expenditure. In other words, research continues to have a role in engineering and more particular railway engineering. The objectives of research in engineering are twofold: (1) to minimise uncertainty in engineering decisions or design and hence limit the risk of failure on the one hand or the economic penalties of conservative design on the other; (2) to pioneer new techniques in design, construction or maintenance and hence provide new opportunities for the business. Engineering research is therefore concerned with either saving costs whether it be capital costs, maintenance costs or cost of failures, or increasing revenue. As transport organisations, railway systems are vitally concerned with saving costs and increasing revenue and hence provide a better service for its customers. However, their economic viability is largely dependent upon the cost of providing and maintaining the physical assets of the track and rolling stock. What is more, no matter how effective the activities of the marketing people, their ability to win or hold traffic is dependent to a large extent upon the ability of the track and rolling stock to accept that traffic and to transport it economically under acceptable conditions of time and reliability. Following the recession of the early sixties, we are witnessing· a welcomed revival of the conventional steel wheel on steel rail due largely to the demand for mass haulage, in particular heavy minerals, and the potential of railways for greatly increased speeds and reduced energy consumption. This is particularly so in Australia where the resource wealth has only recently been realised. This revival, however, has brought with it new and added responsibilities. The increased axle loads and speeds and the improved safety and reliability demanded by the expanding transport and resource industries of Australia have placed a heavy burden on our rail systems. To maintain the competitive position of railways with the other modes of transport it is essential that we thoroughly understand the technical and economic consequences involved in meeting these demands, having in view the heavy capital investments involved in new construction and asset restoration of track and rolling stock as well as the high costs incurred in operating our railway systems. The opportunities and problems afforded by this revival have underlined the need for an increased research effort in the technical, economic and social fields in railway engineering. As axle loads and speeds of rolling stock increase, we can expect failures to appear in our track structure, failures that cannot be predicted by our current knowledge and experience. Oddly enough, while we may think we know a great deal about the properties of the track, in fact, we still know very little about such properties under the dynamic conditions caused by contact with the moving wheel. While improvements have occurred to individual components of the track, such as the sleeper and rail, there has been a significant absence of research into the total track structure system in relation to the vehicle. It is in this area that the world's progressive railway systems have launched a major research effort which continues today.

Title The need for a co-ordinated approach to rail track development
 
Author Mair, RI
Marich, S
Affiliation The Broken Hill Proprietary Co. Ltd., Melbourne, Australia
Melbourne Research Laboratories, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract Railroads provide an efficient and highly reliable transportation mode, particularly for the movement of bulk materials including iron ore and coal. Of the total system costs, considered as capital and operating costs, the track structure represents the greater proportion in most cases. To maintain and increase the railroad's competitive position it has been necessary to continually upgrade the technology applied to component development and to the total track assembly. In its simplest sense a railroad track structure is a comparatively loose assembly of rails, fastenings, sleepers and ballast designed to transmit the dynamic forces from a moving vehicle to the underlying roadbed. Alignment of the track structure is dependent upon the ability of the ballast and roadbed to sustain the repeated applications of contact stress due to each wheel passage (1). Design criteria and constraints have evolved from experience as a guide to future track development (2, 3) and have been incorporated into computer aided design procedures to select the most cost effective track structure (4, 5). Notwithstanding this apparently sophisticated development in track design the underlying analysis procedures and behavioural theory are comparatively elementary and do not enable a complete specification of track to be ascribed. For instance, current design practice enables only a crude estimate to be made of maintenance requirements likely to arise from the adoption of a particular track structure (5), especially where the intended operating practices extend beyond the range of experience of the designer (6). In addition, the interaction between vehicle and track is usually limited to estimates of dynamic wheel load factors. Indeed, track structure design is a strength based procedure which, with the recent exception of joint impact factors developed by British Rail (7, 8) pays little attention to tolerances of components and assembly and their consequent effects upon track - train interaction, future track maintenance costs and operating safety. Component and assembly tolerances are frequently established independently without due regard to component interactions and the costs associated with over-specification of tolerances or the consequences of inadequate control. In defence of the designer, however, the methodology for taking these factors into account has not always been available and in fact is only now in its infancy. From a consideration of the sleeper, the following paper will illustrate some of the many interactions that exist between components and the total track behaviour and their relevance in the development of new components or track structures. An awareness of the many interactions that arise is essential as part of a co-ordinated approach to track design if cost reductions are to be achieved without reducing operating safety.

Title A saga of steel sleeper manufacture
 
Author Gillett, DC
Affiliation The Broken Hill Proprietary Co. Ltd., Melbourne, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract The Broken Hill Proprietary Company Limited has been involved on several occasions on the production of steel sleeper sections but it is only in the last decade that complete steel sleepers have been manufactured. Traditionally, a steel company does not seek to compete with its customers in the conversion of its steel products to manufactured items but due to lack of other suitable facilities, steel sleeper manufacture was undertaken at Whyalla. Following the first international sleeper conference (Ref. 1) in 1973, the Mt. Newman Mining Company placed an order for 3,000 beam type steel sleepers for track evaluation. The section rolled was a modern version of the Carnegie Sleeper Section being a universal beam as shown in Fig. 1. These sleepers were installed in the mainline track. As they were not able to be insulated, they were subsequently re-located in sidings when C.T.C. was installed. Following this first trial, a major installation of trough type steel sleepers was installed in a track in South Australia. Interest was shown by Mt. Newman Mining Company in trialling steel sleepers of that design since they could be readily insulated. Several sleepers installed in South Australia were strain gauged, which revealed stress data contrary to popular concepts and formed the basis of design for a new rolled sleeper section. A very extensive research programme was established to prove the proposed designs and optimise the final shape. This research has been fully covered in papers by Dr. J. Brown (Ref. 2). It should be pointed out that we were working in areas of the fatigue strength of complicated shapes, especially the sleepers to suit Pandrol type fastenings, and little information was available to confirm decisions. Although it had been our firm intention to produce a laboratory proven design and supply the sleeper bar to a selected manufacturer the closure of the B.H.P. Shipyards at Whyalla made facilities available sufficient to construct a steel sleeper pilot plant. At this stage Mt. Newman Mining Company placed a firm order for 5,000 complete steel sleepers; thus a concentrated programme of development began.

Title Review of performance at FAST track (wood and concrete)
 
Author Guins, SG
Affiliation Association of American Railroads Pueblo, USA
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract As North American Railroads increased the number of 100-ton freight cars in their fleet consists, the need for track maintenance increased. Yet a varied mixture of equipment operating on various portions of the track makes it impossible to develop relationships between axle loadings and track deterioration. The problem is serious enough to require a definitive answer. In 1975, a program was developed designated FAST (Facility for Accelerated Service Testing) by co-operation of the Federal Railroad Administration, Association of American Railroads, the Railroad Industry, and the Railroad Supply Industry. Construction started during the summer of 1976 at the 50 square mile facility of the Transportation Test Center outside Pueblo, Colorado, that already had a 15 mile high-speed track and a section of track designated for Track Train Dynamics study. To date, FAST has accumulated over 400 MGT. The advantage of FAST is, of course, the ability to control consist and operating conditions which is not practical on an operating railroad with a broad mixture of traffic. The FAST Track is located in Southeastern Colorado and has a semi-arid climate with an annual rainfall of 14 inches. This is not representative of the climate of Eastern, Midwestern, Southern or Western United States, and this eliminates the effect of moisture on wood tie life. The above must be taken into consideration in interpreting results we obtain on performance of track components elsewhere.

Title Railway application of stud welding
 
Author Diener, L
Affiliation Omark, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Third International Rail Sleeper Conference, 24-28 September 1979, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1979
Abstract Like many things in this World, stud welding is not new. It has in fact been around for over 70 years, but prior to the World War II period, no acceptable system for applying and controlling the process had been evolved. Early in the 20th Century, it had been found, that like other forms of electric welding, metal articles could be joined by applying electric current, and for a time, that was about as far as it went. For over 20 years, all kinds of experiments were conducted in the search for·the optimum parameters, e.g., the right voltage, current etc., but beyond that, early experiments were nothing more than two articles being brought into contact with each other, and another person throwing a switch. It can be well imagined that results were most erratic. It was during World War II that the potential of stud welding was recognised, particularly in the field of ship construction, when systems were developed making the process infinitely controllable, and easy to operate. The form of stud welding which is eminently suitable for use in railway fastening is known as Arc stud welding where the process has an electric Arc drawn between the parent material and a specially designed welding stud. A ceramic arc shield is mounted at the weld base end of the stud, this component performing several essential functions. Arc Stud Welding is a modern, high-speed way to permanently attach fasteners to a metal surface. The process has become widespread in various industries, including shipbuilding, railroad-car manufacturing, and general construction and metalworking operations, where it serves as a rapid, versatile and economical joining method. Technological improvements in arc stud welding equipment have resulted in increased applications for critical situations where the integrity of welded fasteners is of paramount importance. Stud Welding is a process recognised and accepted by various building codes, and has for many years been employed in city building and bridge construction. One such example is known as "composite beam construction" where headed studs called shear connectors stud welded to steel beams, the entire mass then being encapsulated in concrete. The Sears Tower in Chicago Ill. is one such building where some 800,000 shear connectors were used. Both semi-automatic and fully automatic arc welding systems are commercially available. a variety of stud shapes and sizes. Each can weld Arc Stud Welding systems consist of three basic components: the power source, a control unit and the stud welder. Motor-generator, or transformer-rectifier sources may be used from which direct current of relatively high amperage may be drawn to provide the welding energy. The control unit, which allows weld time to be pre-selected, comprises an electronic timing device and contactor, capable of initiating and interrupting the weld current as required. Upon completion of the weld cycle and while metal is in the molten state, weld current ceases and the stud plunges into the molten pool. The stud welder can be a manual, pneumatic, hydraulic or electrically-actuated tool that must provide accurately controlled lift and plunge of the fastener in conjunction with the control unit. This system can be integrated with other sequence and control systems for on-track railway welding equipment, meeting the needs of various industries.

Title The zig zag track slabs ten years later
 
Author Lord, B
Affiliation State Rail Authority of NSW, Sydney, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Seventh International Rail Track Conference, 18-21 October 1988, Auckland, New Zealand
Publication Date 1988
Abstract The Zig Zag Track Slabs have been in operation for 10 years. These continuous paved slabs extend for 5km of dual railway track and traverse 10 tunnels with cuttings and embankments between as they wind their way down the Western side of the Blue Mountains Range in New South Wales, Australia. The slabs were constructed in 1978 to remedy a situation where the previous conventional rail track structure of timber sleepers on rock ballast had deteriorated to the point where a safe, reliable, fast and efficient rail service could not be provided to rail passengers or freight customers. On completion of its construction the new track structure was seen as a great ach ievement capable of providing that safe, reliable and efficient service needed at speeds only constrained by the severity of the curves and grades. In 1978 it was being predicted that the track structure would be maintenance free for a period of 50 years. Now, 10 years later, the track slabs are continuing to perform well, however, they have not been maintenance free and have required some structural repairs. This paper will compare the track slab structure in 1988 as compared to 1978, detail the mai ntenance and repair requirements over the 10 year period and attempt to explain why the defects requiring structural repair occurred in a "maintenance free" structure.

The only real problem to dale has been very high noise levels generated by the rail/wheel interaction. which will be alleviated by profile grinding of the rail, new wheel profiles and lubricating the rail at intervals of two hours. Abstract In heavy haul as well as high speed applications continuously welded track has been the state of the art for many years. As a cost effective procedure it is flexible with regard to its execution. A track closure is usually not necessary. With the introduction of the SKVF weld and the related short preheat of the rail ends considerable improvements in the reduction of defects in rail welding could be achieved. Fatigue fractures originating in the rail foot due to the dynamic stresses for example are unknown with the SKVF weld. The development of the THERMIT technology today is directed both towards improving the reliability of the process in terms of reducing the influence of process variables on weld quality by an optimisation of the mould design and improvements to weld performance under heavy axle load conditions. In this context it is to be mentioned that along the European high speed tracks since their exposure to traffic, no THERMIT weld fracture has occurred. However the severe conditions of heavy haul including high dynamic loads. high hardness requirements at the weld running surface and often high rail residual stresses requires improved THERMIT welds. Current developments relate to the optimisation of ductility and toughness properties whilst maintaining the required wear resistance at the running surface. The new generation of the THERMIT technology. currently being introduced. includes the following advancements: Two alloying techniques two post heat treatment procedures and a mould design optimisation.

The project was delivered in stages so that works could be delivered within the nominated track shutdown periods. This made asphalt a more suitable construction material due to its early strength and accessibility almost immediately after placing and rolling. One year after the completed installation the lessons learnt and experiences of constructing and monitoring a new track slab system in Australia will be discussed in this paper.
