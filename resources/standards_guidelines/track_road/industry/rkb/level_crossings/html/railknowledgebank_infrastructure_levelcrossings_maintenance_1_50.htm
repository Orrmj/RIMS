Title Railway level crossing risk assessment and maintenance prioritisation based on the big data analysis approach
 
Author Wu, H
Zhang, R
Affiliation Western Sydney University, Sydney, Australia
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Level crossing is an important track component and plays very important role in rail and road traffic safety. In Australia, although the numbers are declining, however there are still multiple fatalities (around 30) every year and collisions resulting in serious injuries. The level crossing safety management and maintenance work is an infrastructure intensive task that relies on significant amounts of information and data to support for level crossing condition evaluation, risk assessment, safety impact study for track and road components failure, and maintenance/upgrade planning. In recent years, vast amount of information and data are available and accessible for the track engineers and maintenance planners. However, how to effectively collect, process, analysis and utilise these “big data” for the decision making of level crossing maintenance and overhaul works and make the accurate maintenance plan is a new challenge for the railway engineers. The risk assessment and maintenance prioritisation of the level crossings is a very complex and systematic work, it is very important to ensure that all stakeholders associated with the particular level crossing are involved with the determination of the final recommended treatment, i.e. all the technical characteristic of both of the railway track and road are all must be addressed. A special developed level crossing risk assessment and prioritisation model is applied to undertake the analysis of the level crossings. This analysis model is based on the utilisation of the “big data” of the condition and characteristic information of railway and road.

Title A safety level index for grade crossings
 
Author Campos, VBG
do Carmo, RC
Freita, AM
Affiliation IME – Brazilian Army Military Institute, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
MRS Logistics, Juiz de Fora, Brazil
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Grade crossings are crossings of road and railroad. The choice of the improvement to be implemented in each situation depends on the conditions of the place, based in parameters that influence the safety of each passage. In this work based on a widely used indicator, the Weighted Factor of Accidents (WFA), related to the number of accidents in the grade crossing, and on a Critical Index (CI) recently developed and related to the physical and operational characteristics of the PN, it is proposed a Safety Level (SL) for grade crossings. This indicator aims to measure the degree of risk of a crossing, prioritize the maintenance according to the criticality and that the risk will be properly treated.

Title Enhancing safety at Central Queensland level crossings through baseline condition assessment and upgrade efficiency
 
Author Livingston, J
Lawton, S
Affiliation Aurizon, Queensland, Australia
AECOM, Queensland, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract Aurizon is continuing to seek to improve the safety of public and private level crossings across the Central Queensland Coal Network, through a multi-pronged attack to prevent level crossing incidents. Aurizon partnered with AECOM to undertake a baseline condition assessment and to develop an upgrade scope of all private level crossings across the network. As part of the project significant improvements to the data collection and identification of infrastructure improvements were achieved by the combined project team through the development of an electronic data collection form and the development of a condition based assessment guideline. The purpose of the project along with other baseline condition assessments and the ongoing analysis of the information collected is to provide a robust assessment of the existing condition of all level crossings in the Central Queensland Coal Network, produce a scope of improvements to enhance safety at the crossings, and to produce a robust method for assessing the network wide safety of all level crossings over time. The existing condition and identified improvements allow Aurizon to clearly define their level crossing assets, prioritise a program of capital and maintenance works for level crossings, and inform the key restrictions and responsibilities to be included in interface agreements with the road authorities and users of private crossings.

Title Identification of capacity improving options for South East Queensland passenger rail
 
Author Caldwell, D
Chandra, L
Affiliation Parsons Brinckerhoff, Sydney, Australia
Parsons Brinckerhoff, Brisbane, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract In order to support an understanding of how best to prioritise rail network improvements on the South East Queensland rail network, detailed network-wide simulations were conducted. These simulations explored the interplay between network configuration, peak and off-peak service plans and train stabling in the context of future passenger demand projections. Simulation provided a means by which to experiment with both rail infrastructure and service plans to identify capacity constraints and test various infrastructure and operational solutions. Collaboration with Queensland Rail enabled a detailed simulation model platform to be rapidly developed, and assisted in refining and verifying the model to the benefit of all parties. This paper provides an introduction to the South East Queensland rail network and its key challenges, and describes a systematic approach to examining and resolving capacity constraints. Three capacity constraint case studies spanning operations and engineering are also presented. The first is in relation to a single track section in an otherwise double-track line, the second is in relation to the interaction between timetable planning and an undesirable stabling yard access configuration, and the third is in relation to the practice of route-setting for long trains at critical junctions. These diverse cases have two things in common; they were all modelled and resolved using simulation, and they all incorporate a capacity improvement opportunity realised entirely or substantially from operational and signalling measures. The nature and solution of these constraints are instructive from a general rail infrastructure and operations planning perspective. The observations are of an exploratory nature and do not represent Government policy.

Title Vital disabling release (VDR) for Aurizon
 
Author Wilkins, C
Truscott, C
Walker, K
Affiliation Program Delivery, Aurizon, Brisbane, QLD, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract One of the most dangerous areas in Australasian Railway Networks are level crossings. Maintenance activities in this area add to this risk and these activities to a large extent have been ignored. All Railway Authorities have processes in place to temporarily alter the operation of the installation to allow maintenance vehicles to work in and around the level crossing whilst minimising the amount of time that the crossing is closed to road users. These processes must maintain a very high level of safety at all times. In today’s railways, every operational interruption due to maintenance activities is costly and must therefore be challenged to seek more efficient ways of providing safe and timely access to track and to minimise delays to services and the public. This paper details Aurizon’s introduction of the Vital Disabling Release (VDR) to address some of the Safety, Financial, Commercial and Operational issues that occur as a result of Maintenance activities at Level Crossings.

Title Vital disabling release for Aurizon
 
Author Wilkins, C
Truscott, C
Walker, K
Source Institution of Railway Signal Engineers (IRSE)
Conference IRSE Australasia Technical Meeting: Launceston, 19 July 2013
Publication Date 2013
Abstract One of the most dangerous areas in the Australasian Network are level crossings. Maintenance activities in this area add to this risk and these activities to a large extent have been ignored. All Railway Authorities have processes in place to protect Motorists during level crossing maintenance activities by not closing the road to Motorists unnecessarily or for extended periods. As the Vital Disabling Release (VDR) is tested during the initial commissioning, it can subsequently be applied to the crossing in a safe and timely manner by non-Signalling Staff after the appropriate training e.g. Track Protection Officer (TPO) who is in charge of the worksite. These processes also protect maintenance machinery operating in the vicinity of the level crossing. This paper sets out Aurizon’s direction for introducing a VDR which we believe provides a structure to managing these risks from both a cost and more importantly a safety perspective. Maintenance Engineering Staff have recently requested that the installation of VDR’s become standard practice in all level crossing design. A cost/benefit has been investigated between the existing operation and the VDR. There is a reduction in labour expenses and improved safety after a VDR is installed.
