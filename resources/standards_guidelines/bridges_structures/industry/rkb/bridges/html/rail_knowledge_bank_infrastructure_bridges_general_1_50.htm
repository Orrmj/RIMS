Title Field investigation of the effects of under ballast mats on the track transient displacements in a ballasted bridge
 
Author Lima, De O

Orozco, RJQ
Edwards, JR
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, Illinois, USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Railway transition zones between open track and rigid structures such as bridges and culverts often experience differential movements due to abrupt changes in track stiffness and complicated dynamic loading effects. Such conditions commonly lead to track geometry issues, consequently increasing the magnitude of impact loads at these areas and resulting in excessive degradation of ballast and other track components. Under ballast mats (UBMs) have seen increased use in the last decades especially in the area of improved vibration mitigation and track stiffness reduction at bridges due to their flexibility and damping capabilities. This work presents results from a field instrumentation effort to investigate how UBMs can affect track transient displacements on a heavy haul ballasted-deck concrete bridge.

Title Preliminary screening of railway deck girder bridges for fatigue
 
Author Dick, SM
Affiliation Purdue University, West Lafayette, Indiana, USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Riveted steel deck girders comprise most of the deck girders on North American Railroads, and they have been in service for decades. Fatigue cycle accumulation creates concern that crack development will require repair or replacement of these spans. Given the number of spans, examination of individual locations is time-consuming and inefficient. Railroad bridge designs have been standardized which means a fatigue check can also be standardized. A method is presented that takes advantage of design parameters of girders for a range of span lengths designed to a specific loading to check for potential fatigue cycle accumulation. Advantages of the method are that multiple span lengths are checked with the checks performed against actual equipment instead of an assumed loading. Another major advantage of this method is the ability to determine whether fatigue cycle accumulation will occur at the rate of one cycle per railcar or one cycle for the entire train. The method allows a quick assessment of a typical group of spans designed to a standard pinpointing areas where more detailed analysis is warranted. An assessment of past and present railcar equipment is presented compared to standardized AREA/AREMA girder designs.

Title Continuous welded rail on railway bridges
 
Author Fryba, L
Affiliation Institute of Theoretical and Applied Mechanics, Prague, Czech Republic
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Railway bridges induce additional forces in continuous welded rails, thereby limiting its use. For this reason , the problem is given great attention, since there is a general tendency to use long welded rail wherever possible. Its use results in a significant reduction in dynamic effects and a smoother vehicle ride and consequently has ecological and economic advantages. In addition, it facilitates the mechanised maintenance of railway tracks. Railway bridges have a significant effect on the stresses and displacement of continuous welded rails. The greatest stress increments in the rails and the greatest displacements between the rail and the bridge occur above the movable bearings of the bridge. The theoretical solution of the problem investigates the most important cases of the arrangement of fixed and movable bearings of bridges with one, two and more spans. It is the arrangement of bearings and not the static system of the bridge that determines the expansion length of the bridge. The case of two movable bearings on one pier is the most unfavourable case for long welded rail. The permitted expansion length of bridges was determined by the observance of four conditions : strength, gap, displacement and stability. The strength and gap condi tions determine the expansion length of bridges with ballast, while the displacement condition limits the expansion length of bridges without ballast. The stability condition is fulfilled until the permitted rail stress is exceeded.

Abstract The opening of the first railway line in New South Wales on 26 September, 1855, between Sydney and Parramatta, initiated a rapid development of the railway system in this State. Its growth expanded outwards from the capital, Sydney, and Newcastle, 100 miles to the north. The great natural barrier of the Hawkesbury River between Sydney and Newcastle delayed completion of the rail link between the two cities and it was not until early in 1885 that tenders were called for the design and construction of a railway bridge across the Hawkesbury River. The design of the foundations and super-structure submitted by the Union Bridge Company of New York, except for minor alterations, was considered the most suitable for the project, and the contract was awarded to that Company in May, 1886. The first Hawkesbury River railway bridge was opened to traffic on 1 May, 1889. Its completion formed the last link in a continuous rail connection between Australia's principal cities of Adelaide, Melbourne, Sydney and Brisbane.

Title Monitoring method of railway bridge substructures by using new soundness diagnosis indices
 
Author Abe, K
Natori, T
Kominato, Y
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The soundness of structural stability of railway bridge substructures constructed in a river deteriorates over time because the overburden layer of the riverbed can be gradually eroded by river flow. Hence, it is necessary to diagnose the soundness of structural stability of the substructures over time. The soundness of a pier can be diagnosed by the magnitude of natural frequency of a primary vibration mode of it to which an impact force is applied. We proposed a new methodology to diagnose the soundness of the substructure of the bridge through a structural health monitoring approach. New soundness diagnosis indices based on microtremors and vibrations of piers during train passage were proposed. In order to confirm the applicability of the indices to the soundness diagnosis, a correlation between the proposed indices and the natural frequencies was investigated through model experiments and field measurements. In addition, a long term monitoring of actual railway bridge substructures was carried out using sensors that can automatically measure the indices.

In particular, a feasibility study on the implementation of early warning systems able to stop immediately running trains after earthquakes is being carried out. Abstract To ameliorate the characteristics of the wooden sleeper, the synthetic sleeper has been developed and fabricated in Japan since 1980. In these twenty five years 1.3 million of sleepers are in the track replacing the wooden sleepers in turnout, on steel bridges and in directly fastened track. About 100 thousands synthetic sleepers are supplied per year now. It is made by stratifying two or three FFU (Fibre reinforced Foamed Urethane) plates and gluing them. The plates are made in the factory by setting necessary numbers of long glass fibres, introducing them into the metal form continuously, impregnating foamed urethane in it, hardening it and cutting it with a rotating saw to a predetermined length. Because it has nearly the same workability as wood, the sleepers can be easily worked in the factory, for example to set the superelevation on curved steel bridge, to prepare the turnout sleepers with different length or to produce the short sleeper for the directly fastened track on concrete roadbed. Furthermore, damaged sleepers are reused by repairing them, and FFU tips collected in the factory and discarded sleepers are crushed and remolded as sleepers and used in lower class lines.

Publication Date 2009
Abstract The current standards for road and bridge barriers AS 3845 and AS 5100 assume the main risk of an errant vehicle is to the occupants of the vehicle. In the majority of cases, this is a valid assumption. However, when a rail corridor is parallel to or crosses the road corridor, the risk profile changes. If an errant vehicle crosses the path of a high speed commuter train, the risk profile resulting from the possible death or injury to hundreds of commuters means that a different approach is required to road and bridge barriers to reduce the likelihood of such an event. This paper presents the rationale for developing a risk matrix and a sensible interaction when road and rail corridors are in close proximity. It also outlines a design method for road and bridge barriers related to vehicle type, vehicle weight, road and rail status, effective height, performance levels and site environment. The method was based on a single risk management analysis for both bridge and road barriers.

Title AS 5100 Bridge Design standard: focus on safety for railway bridges
 
Author Rapattoni, F
Affiliation Parsons Brinckerhoff
Source ATRI
Conference Austroads Bridge Conference, 7th, 2009, Auckland, New Zealand
Publication Date 2009
Abstract The new Australian bridge design standard AS5100 contains provisions to maximise safety at bridges and structures over and under railway lines. The intent of AS5100 is to avoid catastrophic failures of bridges due to loading or collision impact by derailed trains or road vehicles and to provide a forgiving environment to minimise the probability of injury to the occupants of derailed trains. The provisions are a legacy of the Granville (Australia) and the Eschede (Germany) bridge disasters and are designed to protect both the bridge and the train occupants in the event of a train derailment. These are general principles and it is the responsibility of bridge designers to apply them in any situation where there is a risk of bridge collapse due to a de-railed train. The application of the provisions to some recent projects have highlighted that there is disagreement among designers and Rail Authorities in regards to the intent of the standard and interpretation of the provisions. The application of some of the prescriptive requirements is very difficult if not impossible in some situations and may be too costly in low risk situations. A risk analysis at the concept stage may be the only way to determine the appropriate course of action. This paper discusses the intent of the standard, the application of the provisions in a case study and proposes some guidelines to assist designers and Rail Authorities in making decisions about the application of the provisions. A methodology using a risk analysis approach is required to clearly, objectively and consistently assess optional bridging solutions taking safety aspects into account. A model to assess the risk profile of a project by quantifying the probability of derailment and by establishing the likely consequences has been developed by PB to the concept stage. This will be discussed with the Rail Authorities for further development.
