Title Increasing track uptime by using a portable equipment for measuring track alignment from regular trains
 
Author Gustafsson, C
Larsson, D
Affiliation Damill AB, Luleå, Sweden
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Tightly scheduled trains on track makes it of interest to reduce track possession time needed for maintenance. One activity to be reduced in time might be time needed for dedicated track measurement vehicles. In Sweden, a small portable equipment is now tested with good results. The name of the system is Track Logger and it can be installed on any in-service railway vehicle. It runs unattended and is a compact highperformance data logger connected to accelerometers and a gyro mounted on both bearing boxes of a wheel axle. The unit is also connected to a GPS-receiver. Data is logged with minimum 1280 Hz and all data are stored onto a USB HDD that handles up to four days of continuous sampling. The sensors are selected and mounted to withstand heavy weather conditions including snow and ice build-up. Data is studied in a specially designed software which presents raw data expressed as acceleration or twist angle as well as calculated alignment displacement data expressed in unit [mm]. By swapping the installation between a heavy haul iron ore train and a low or medium axle load passenger coach, the track’s response to change in axle load can be studied. This has probably not been done before as measurement system are normally designed for permanent installation.

Title Innovating system for fixing underpass reinforced concrete structure below railway lines, operating without temporary bridges and beams
 
Author Eboli, I
Cicora, F
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract This system allows to inset underpass reinforced concrete structure below operating lines without demolition of track formation and superstructure, and with speed restriction/or the fixing period only. The basic element of the system is a special shield with four cutting components (upper, lower and two laterals). The fore even part of cutting elements (knife) cuts the ground and divides it into two parts (internal and external); the internal part is destined to be removed. Behind the knife there's the ploughshare that breaks up the soil; the ploughshare is followed by the ribbon-holder. The ribbon, which consists of an antifriction material, runs on the covering of the reinforced concrete structure in order to reduce the friction bet,veen soil and concrete, and to guard the integrity of superstructure of the railway crossed lines. This system has already been tested by FS in some occasions.

Title Technological innovation in railway structure system with ladder track system
 
Author Wakui, H
Matsumoto, N
Inoue, H
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Rails canying trains along a fixed right of way are highly flexible under heavy stresses and must be tightly fastened to a strong base, the railway ties, at frequent intervals to maintain their true alignment. Today's concrete crossties were developed to replace wooden crossties, and naturally took the same shape. Since the crosstie system provides poor load dispersal, however, the crossties gradually sink under the repetitive loads applied onto the track. Lateral movement resistance in this system is also weak. From the 1940s to the 1960s, experiments were carried out in France, the Soviet Union and Japan with longitudinal sleepers laid in parallel pairs under the rails. The goal was to create a railway track requiring a minimum of maintenance. However, none of the experiments were successful. Based on this experience, the Structural Engineering Group of Japan's Railway Technical Research Institute has developed the "Ladder Sleeper," in which parallel longitudinal concrete beams are held together by transverse steel pipes. It is a unified precast plant-manufactured product in ladder format. The lateral steel pipes are embedded into the longitudinal concrete beams. Ladder Sleepers provide continuous support to the rails and act with them as composite rails. When laid in stone ballast, the resulting ground pressure is reduced and more even for the same overall weight as for concrete crossties. On viaducts and in tunnels, where it is desirable to lay a non-ballasted track, Ladder Sleepers still offer advantages. They can either be laid on a resilient mat on the concrete trackbed, or they can be installed as a floating track insulated from the concrete trackbed by discrete resilient mountings. The ballasted and non-ballasted Ladder Tracks result in ensuring train safety, assuring maintenance reduction, and environmental improvements, promising major progress in achieving railway efficiency and improving railway management.

Title Repair and modification of NZ railway tunnels
 
Author Ramsay, G
Affiliation Beca Infrastructure Limited
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2010, Rail - Rejuvenation and Renaissance, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wellington, New Zealand, 12-15 September, 2010
Publication Date 2010
Abstract The current NZ rail network is approximately 4000km long with 145 tunnels ranging in length from 50m to 9km and with a total length of 87km. The tunnels were opened between 1867 and 1980 and have been constructed in a diverse range of geological conditions. Tunnel cross-sections and lining arrangements have varied over the period and only two tunnels can be considered as being modern designs. The tunnels have performed remarkably well and the major reasons for modifications have been invert lowering to provide greater clearances for larger rolling stock or to permit electrification. A few tunnels have required works to repair lining failures due to swelling ground pressures and in a small number of tunnels lining repairs have been required to remedy lining deterioration due to a low standard initial construction. This paper describes the philosophy and approach taken in the repair and modification of a number of tunnels over the last 30 years.

Title Use of 3D scanning in heritage rail tunnel condition assessments
 
Author Howe, S
Gardiner, D
Robertson, A
Morris, B
Affiliation SMEC Urban, Victoria, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2017, Rail's Digital Revolution, 21-23 November 2017, Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Traditional asset risk monitoring and condition assessment techniques in rail tunnels typically use visual assessments and material sampling and testing techniques including non-destructive testing (NDT). The usefulness of these techniques can be augmented with the use of 3D-scanning surveys and associated 3D modelling and analysis. In recent years, SMEC has successfully trialled the use such techniques in brick-lined rail tunnels up to 140 years old. By use of advanced modelling techniques, the scan data can be used to create as-built design plans where none exist, monitor risks and develop detailed defect maps which can be used for comparative studies, estimates of remaining life and failure risk and for scoping remedial works. 3D scans are not as prone to site condition limitations as some other emerging technologies such as drones, which can be sensitive to wind conditions and are often not able to be used at night or in poorly illuminated spaces such as tunnels. Examples are presented of data cloud modelling outputs in heritage-listed rail tunnels in Victoria including the generation of tunnel cross sections, comparisons to design profiles and the generation of defect ‘heat maps’ which show the locations and severity of asset defects and tunnel deformations.

Publication Date 2019
Abstract Tokyo Metro Co. Ltd. operates 9 routes in capital area of Japan; total length of tunnels is 166km. In Japan, subway tunnels were generally constructed by the cut & cover method and have typically horizontally oriented double cell box tunnels with rectangular cross sections. Because some tunnels had to be built under narrow roads in Tokyo, there exist some vertically oriented double cell box tunnels due to land acquisition problems. And there exist some parts with special cross sections changing gradually in the vertical or horizontal direction, hereinafter called tunnel sections with non-uniform heights. The Earthquake Resistance Standard for Railway Structures in Japan [1] contains no explanation of the complicated structure such as the tunnel sections with non-uniform heights. To explain the earthquake resisting of the complicated structure, it is important to analyze behavior of the ground and structure such as the complicated shape, and grasp the sectional force of the structure associated with the ground movement at the time of the earthquake. Thus, to grasp them, we made the model ground and a model tunnel and carried out a vibration experiment using a vibration examination device and shear soil vessel to simulate the ground movement situation and the tunnel behavior of at the time of earthquake. And we carried out the reproduction analysis of the experiment by FEM and inspected the validity of the analytical technique. In result, we grasped deformation behavior and sectional force of the tunnel sections with non-uniform heights associated with the ground movement at the time of the earthquake, and got knowledge to establish the examination method of tunnel sections with non-uniform heights.

Title Evaluation of a laboratory model test using field measurements of frost penetration in railway tunnels
 
Author Andrén, A
Dahlström, L-O
Nordlund, E
Affiliation The Swedish Transport Administration, Borlänge, Sweden
Luleå University of Technology, Luleå, Sweden
Journal Cold Regions Science and Technology
Publication Date 2022-12
Abstract Despite extensive grouting efforts to prevent water from leaking into tunnels, water seepages remain. When exposed to freezing temperatures, ice formations occur. During the winter, the Swedish Transport Administration's railway tunnels are affected by major problems caused by ice, such as icicles from roof and walls, ice loads on installations, ice-covered tracks and roads, etc. To ensure safety and prevent traffic disruptions, many tunnels require extensive maintenance. Improved knowledge about frost penetration in tunnels is required to reduce maintenance of the tunnels. Frost insulated drain mats are often used at leakage spots to prevent ice formation along the tunnels. To find out which parts of a tunnel are exposed to freezing temperatures, the University of Gävle and the Royal Institute of Technology in Stockholm conducted a laboratory model test on behalf of the Swedish National Rail Administration (now the Swedish Transport Administration). The laboratory model test aimed to find a method to determine the expected temperature conditions along a tunnel to decide which parts of the tunnel require frost insulation to protect the drainage system from freezing and prevent ice formation. To evaluate the laboratory model test, the Swedish Transport Administration in collaboration with Luleå University of Technology have performed field surveys in two Swedish railway tunnels. The field measurements involved monitoring temperatures in air, rock surfaces and rock mass, as well as measuring wind direction, wind and air velocity and air pressure. The measurements in the tunnels show that the frost penetrates further into the tunnels than was expected from the laboratory model test, which was based on a completely uninsulated tunnel. Frost insulated drains do not only prevent the cold air from reaching the rock mass, but also prevent the rock from emitting geothermal heat that warms up the cold tunnel air. Consequently, the frost penetrates further into the tunnel than it would do if the heat from the rock mass was allowed to warm up the outside air on its way into the tunnel. The number of frost insulated drains and how much of the tunnel walls and roof are covered thereby affect the length of the frost penetration.
