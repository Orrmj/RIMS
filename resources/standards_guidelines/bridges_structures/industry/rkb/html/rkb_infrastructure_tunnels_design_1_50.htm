Title Ground-borne vibrations due to trains in underground lines
 
Author Castellani, A
Panzeri, P
Pezzoli, P
Affiliation Politecnico of Milan, Milano, Italy
ISMES, Seriate, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract To attenuate disturbances coming from vibrations and aerial noise along railway lines, special types of track bedding have been designed, based on fixation of the rail on a floating -or "resilient "- concrete slab. Elastomeric materials are used under the slab to introduce deformability and energy dissipation. With respect to the conventional ballast track, floating slab solutions offer the possibility to suitably fit the track to the soil conditions and the type of train. It is thus conceivable to utilize the proposed design including, among other requirements, the level of allowable vibrations. One of the difficulty in the numerical simulation of train induced vibrations, is due to the mechanical behavior of elastomeric materials. Laboratory tests on small elastomeric pads show that the energy dissipation, under cyclic loading of constant amplitude, can be suitably represented by a combination of a viscous dashpot, which provides dissipation proportional to the frequency, and an hysteretic damping, which provides dissipation independent from frequency. A meaningful strain-rate sensitivity is also evident. As a first approximation, the stiffness has been represented as an increasing function of frequency, characterized by an asymptotic value. The frequency range of 0-100 cps has been investigated. Eight types of elastomeric materials have been tested. Comparison shows that the stiffness increases with the increase of the material density. The subsequent analytical model is linear with respect to displacement, although with coefficients which vary with frequency. It has been checked under two different experiences. 1) A prototype of a floating slab track, 14.8 meters long, has been assembled in laboratory and tested under fixed amplitude, harmonic loads. While the experiments on small pads were restricted to uniform loading conditions, the track prototype was excited by a concentrated load, thus inducing different deformations along the specimen, decreasing with the increase of the distance from the point of loading. It has been observed that, at low frequency, the model underevaluates the displacement far from the point of loading, while at high frequency, even far from the point of loading, it operates satisfactory. The way the hysteretic dissipation and the strain-rate sensitivity are represented is responsible for the approximations. 2) An experimental track, 100 in long, made up of the same set of elements of the prototype, has been inserted in a railway line. A series of vibration records has been collected during train passages. The comparison between measured and computed vibrations were used to provide a specific notion of the relevance, in a practical problem, of the above approximations. Errors in the prevision of rms of displacements are limited to a few percent. For the sake of brevity, in the present paper only the first experimental tests are discussed, the second one offering similar comments, although in a wider context.

Title A numerical study of pressure wave propagation in double-track tunnel for high speed rail
 
Author Mok, JK
Choe, K
Tabbal, A
Kermal, PD
Masbernat, F
Dumas, JC
Affiliation KHRC, Seoul, Korea
Engineering Systems Intl., Rungis, France
GEC ALSTHOM, Saint-Quen, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Three-dimensional compression wave generation and its propagation behaviors in tunnel resulting from high speed train entering double-track tunnel entrance were studied. For this simulation, finite element method for three-dimensional Euler equation of fluid neglecting viscosity and conductivity was used. The simulations were performed with TGFLO tool, from GEC ALSTHOM and developed by ESI. CAD data with actual dimensions were used to make the model of train and double-track tunnel for transient simulation. The length of train model was 100 in and the train velocity was 300km1h. The tunnel entrance was inclined 42 with respect to ground in order to reduce the pressure gradient of the compression wave at tunnel entrance. The length of tunnel was 402m. For the single train running into double-track tunnel, the asymmetric features of compression wave were sustained until the compression wave propagated about 40m from tunnel entrance. This simulation shows the physical phenomena of pressure wave generation, propagation, reflection and superposition for train-tunnel interaction problem. The main results are as follows; I) maximum pressure gradient was 5,127PaIs at fully developed compression wave front, 2) maximum pressure was 102,210Pa and minimum pressure was 100, 364Pa, which was generated by the superposition of the two expansion waves from each direction of tunnel. These results can be used for the reduction of noise intensity at tunnel exit and air tightness design of cabin. This study will be compared with experimental data in near future.

Title Tunnel wall ground borne vibration prediction for Copenhagen metro
 
Author Olivieri, M
Milocco, A
Lazzaretti, M
Affiliation Ansaldo Ricerche Sri., Genova, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract For the Copenhagen Metro, specific allowable limits have been prescribed for the vibration velocity levels induced on the tunnel wall from train pass-by, depending on the category classification of adjacent built-up areas. Predictions of the induced tunnel wall vibrations have been therefore performed at an early stage of the design, by including variations of main parameters. A three steps prediction model has been developed and used consisting of: the evaluation of the tunnel wall impedance functions; the prediction of the global forces acting at the tunnel invert; the evaluation of the tunnel wall velocity induced with the predicted forces. Tunnel wall impedances have been evaluated by mean of a finite element plane strain model of the tunnel wall interacting with the surrounding soil. The train loading spectrum at the tunnel invert has been evaluated by implementing a condensed model including the effects of rail mass and damping at the rail support and floating slab track systems. Tunnel wall response velocities have been finally computed, for comparison with the allowable levels, by combining the results of previous first and second steps analysis. For all analysed soil conditions and track bed configurations, tunnel wall velocity spectra have been determined and finally expressed as octave band spectra velocity levels. The obtained results will allow an optimized selection of the specific track configuration for different built-up area categories.

Title Ventilation of modern underground metros
 
Author Pope, CW
West, A
Affiliation Mott MacDonald, Croydon, England
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract A ventilation system is necessary for an underground metro to provide a safe and comfortable environment. The paper reviews the requirements a ventilation system has to fulfil and outlines a general scheme for ventilating an underground metro. The ventilation system features shafts at the ends of the stations for providing draught relief, outlet paths for under platform cooling flows and forced ventilation for traffic congestion and smoke control in the event of fire. Where the distance between stations exceeds 1 km intermediate shafts are provided. These perform the dual role of providing routes for forced ventilation and passenger escape. It is shown that the achievement of acceptable train induced flow velocities in stations can be a difficulty. This, however, may be resolved by segregating the station from the running tunnels with platform screen doors. The use of platform screen doors increases the effectiveness of the ventilation system and allows economies to be effected in the design.

Title Transit space: the art of fitting the square peg in the round hole: applied to Perth new metrorail tunnels
 
Author Matthews, A
Chugh, J
Affiliation GHD, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2008, Rail; the core of integrated transport, Conference on Railway Engineering, Perth, Western Australia, 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract The Southern suburbs railway will connect Mandurah to the City of Perth and the northern suburbs. A key aspect of the implementation of the new railway was the construction of tunnels taking the railway from the Narrows Bridge, under the city, and connecting to the existing Northern Suburbs Railway. Transit Space diagrams were used to optimise the size of the bored tunnel and cut-and-cover tunnel early in the design process. Transit Space diagrams allocate spaces within the tunnel for track, overhead electrification, signalling and tunnel systems. These spaces are configured within the tunnel such that the overall tunnel size is minimised without conflicting with the space required for the dynamic train envelope and electrical clearances. In the case of the bored tunnel both the track bed design and overhead electrification design have a major bearing on the tunnel diameter. For the cut-and-cover tunnel and open dive structures the need to provide for signalling system assets and overhead electrification weight tensioning equipment increased the complexity of determining the final Transit Space allocations. Efforts were made early in the design to anticipate the space required for the various components. However, these issues had to be balanced with the desire to minimise the tunnel diameter to reduce costs and also reduce potential ground settlement above the tunnels. The tunnel diameter had to be locked in well before details of the track, overhead electrification, signalling and tunnel systems were fully developed and so tunnel sizing had to be based on preliminary, but workable designs in these areas. Later in the design process it became apparent that, with the chosen tunnel diameter and the preliminary systems designs used, there would be very little spatial flexibility and so designers would still be faced with a challenge to ‘make things fit’. To compound the complexity of the transit space design, some of the tunnel equipment was designed, supplied and installed under a separate contract. This paper details the design process, problems encountered and how these were resolved. It provides some lessons learned that might benefit future projects.

Title Rail tunnel fire safety system design in a SFAIRP context
 
Author Procter, T
Henderson, L
Affiliation R2A, Melbourne, Australia
Arup, Melbourne, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Australian rail and workplace safety legislation places specific duties on rail system designers, including the requirement to eliminate or (failing that) reduce safety risks so far as is reasonably practicable (SFAIRP). This obligation places significant importance on designers’ decisions, especially given those measures which are considered ‘reasonably practicable’ are only ever determined in a court of law after an incident. The people making this legal determination are generally from a non-technical background, and are rarely engineers. It thus behoves us as engineering designers to consider how this determination would be made in relation to our designs, should an incident or failure occur, and to structure our safety-related decisionmaking processes and language accordingly. This paper presents issues we have identified with the current approaches to safety decision-making in addressing SFAIRP requirements in rail tunnel fire safety system design. We discuss the context within which these approaches sit, including legislation and design standards, and how current approaches address (or fail to address) critical aspects of SFAIRP requirements. We then present an alternative approach that allows for straightforward decision-making to address designers’ SFAIRP requirements and how they would be assessed after an incident. We present an example of how our approach and associated techniques allow for more efficient rail tunnel fire safety system and general sound design practice, while promoting innovation within an appropriate decisionmaking framework. Finally, we note some challenges facing the move to more diligent rail safety design approaches, including information gaps, leadership and communication issues.

Abstract The passage of trains through tunnels may lead to strong pressure waves resulting in high loads on the installations of the tunnels and the occurrence of the so-called tunnel-boom or passenger discomfort due to the high pressure amplitude. During the train-tunnel passage, the safety doors inside the tunnel are subject to these pressure loads and can even be severely damaged. The purpose of this study is to investigate the loads on the safety doors caused by the pressure waves in more detail. The idea is to adapt the geometry or size of the cavity in which the door is located in order to reduce the pressure amplitude and therefore the loads. The influence of the cavities on the pressure waves was studied both experimentally and numerically. Tests using a moving-model rig were performed, where the pressure inside of a model tunnel with different cavity geometries was measured with pressure transducers. The train-tunnel passage was also studied numerically to validate the experimental results and to extend the range of parameters. Abstract With the rapid increase of urban rail transit networks, the environmental problems induced by vibrations from subway traffic become more and more prominent. Most subway tunnels are constructed by the shield machine, which are assembled by tunnel linings connected by joints. This study investigates the effect of the tunnel longitudinal joints on the train-induced vibrations from a subway shield-driven tunnel in a homogeneous half-space. The shield-driven tunnel is considered as an elastic lining ring connected by longitudinal joints, while the ground is simplified as a homogeneous elastic half-space. With advances in tunneling technology, the many long tunnels in use around the world today hold valuable lessons for CHSR, particularly with respect to minimization of ground disturbance and improved passenger and operator safety. The primary objective of this project is to determine the state of the art for construction and operation of long tunnels used for high-speed rail. With an abundance of long tunnels successfully completed and already in use around the globe, an examination of those projects can provide the State with the benefit of their experience at little cost. The research team analyzed the data to determine the factors that should be considered in planning long tunnels for HSR projects. Analysis results were documented in a systematic manner to compare with potential tunnels for the Palmdale-to-Burbank segment of the California HSR system. It is hoped that the trends identified from the aggregate data will help inform decisions for the tunnel projects being considered for the Palmdale-to-Burbank segment of California High-Speed Rail.
