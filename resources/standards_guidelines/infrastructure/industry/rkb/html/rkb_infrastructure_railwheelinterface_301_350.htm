Title Improved RCF and wear properties of railway wheel by new micro alloyed steel
 
Author Kato, T
Wakasu, Y
Yamamoto, Y
Affiliation Nippon Steel & Sumitomo Metal Corporation, Steel Research Laboratories, Hyogo, Japan
Nippon Steel & Sumitomo Metal Corporation, Osaka Steel Works, Osaka, Japan
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Higher durability is recently required for railway wheels for heavy haul cars. In order to improve the wheel life, Nippon Steel & Sumitomo Metal Corporation has developed new microalloyed high hardness wheel steel (NM steel). In this study, rolling contact fatigue (RCF) tests, finite element (FE)-analyses and wear tests were conducted to evaluate shelling and wear properties of NM steel. There are three main results. First, NM steel has about two times longer life for shelling than conventional carbon steel (CL-C steel). Second, from FE-analysis results, it is found that NM steel can reduce crack propagation rate with water penetration into cracks decreasing because of higher material strength. Third, wear volume of NM steel is 30 percent less than that of CL-C steel. Moreover, a field service test was carried out in cooperation with Rio Tinto to evaluate the actual wheel performance of NM steel. Test results showed both tread damages and wear of NM steel wheels were comparatively small. Therefore, it is believed that NM steel wheels can show superior performance regarding shelling and wear when used for heavy haul car wheels.

Title Heavy haul coal wagon/car wheel load environment: rolling contact fatigue investigation
 
Author Cakdi, S
Cummings, S
Punwani, J
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center Inc., Colorado, USA
Federal Railroad Administration, District of Columbia, USA
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The objective of this study is to increase the understanding of the conditions under which rolling contact fatigue (RCF) damage accumulates and compare the performances of nonsteering (3-piece) and passive steering (M976) bogies/trucks in terms of RCF generation via the wheel load measurement in heavy haul service of North America. A 32-metric ton axle load aluminium body rotary dump gondola coal wagon/car was furnished with an instrumented wheelset, a Global Positioning System receiver, and an unmanned data acquisition system (UDAC) to collect wheel and rail vertical, lateral, longitudinal loads along with contact locations with respect to the wheel tape line. Approximately, two 2000 km loaded trips were achieved between a coal mine and a power plant with 3-piece and M976 bogies/trucks, for a total of 8,000 km. Shakedown theory was followed to predict RCF initiation conditions and locations. Some site inspections were conducted to verify the predictions. Using shakedown theory, the results showed the M976 steering bogie/truck was less likely to develop RCF than the conventional 3-piece bogie/truck. The combination of curvature, track superelevation, and train speed are important factors in RCF for both bogie/trucks. Most occurrences of shakedown exceedances for the current study were entrance or exit curves of yards, turnaround loops of a power plant and a coal mine. Generally, extreme cant excess at 430m radius (4-degree) and tighter curves are RCF predicted locations.

Title Improving track-friendliness of rolling stock
 
Author Hiensch, M
Wiersma, P

van Ravenswaay, W
Affiliation Delft University of Technology
Dekra Rail, Utrecht, the Netherlands.

Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract In this paper the results are presented of an investigation into the optimisation of wheelset yaw rotation rigidity in trains, more specifically the wheelset guiding by the radial arm bush. The selected approach modifies the suspension design between the wheelset and bogie frame by introduction of frequencydependency. This paper addresses the potential of this design change to reduce wear and tear at the wheelrail interface, thus enhancing the so-called ‘track-friendliness’ of trains. The evaluated train is of the Dutch EMU double stock type. Assessment of vehicle running behaviour was carried out by means of vehicle-track simulations within the VAMPIRE multibody simulation software. At the wheel-rail interface, the impact of the suspension design modification is quantified in relation to wear and rolling contact fatigue (RCF) damage of the rail, vehicle stability and the passenger ride quality. The research has shown that for the concerned vehicle type, RCF damage and wear can be reduced significantly, thereby increasing expected maintenance intervals and wheel and rail life. Results further demonstrated vehicle stability not to be influenced negatively. A dynamic stiffness limit value has been established for the assessed train type; above this limit value, vehicle stability is ensured. Lifetime calculations of a selected suspension component were carried out based upon a load collective derived from vehicle-track simulations. This showed the expected component lifetime to comply with the operational interval for bogie overhaul.

Title Managing the transition from rail wear to rolling contact fatigue in a heavy haul environment
 
Author Wessels, L
Oswald, S
Welsby, D
Mutton, P
Affiliation Rio Tinto Iron Ore, Perth, Western Australia
Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Victoria, Australia
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The transition from wear to rolling contact fatigue (RCF) as the dominant rail deterioration mode presents a number of significant challenges in a heavy haul environment. Due to the inherently higher risks of rail failure and service interruption, managing these risks requires a strategy that involves correct identification and measurement of the extent of RCF damage, and the development of preventative actions, which may include use of alternative rail grades, improved rail grinding procedures, or in some cases implementation of a friction management approach for the rail-wheel interface. The ease of implementation and cost effectiveness of each of these actions may vary between individual systems. In the heavy haul operations at Rio Tinto Iron Ore, RCF damage in rails was associated with the introduction of modified (wear-adapted) wheel and rail profiles and increasing use of higher strength materials. However, the recent expansion of the network presented a number of new challenges associated with the optimum use of rail material grades in different track sections, the capacity to adequately maintain the rail by grinding, and the impact of rolling contact fatigue on allowable rail wear limits. The experience in managing these challenges within the Rio Tinto heavy haul network has demonstrated that it is possible to address the risks associated with RCF damage in rail using a combination of operational experience coupled with research which is aimed at quantifying some of the “unknowns” associated with the influence of loading conditions, rail head loss, and rail material grades and directing this knowledge to improved rail management procedures. The demands of ever-increasing haulage rates, in conjunction with further developments in high strength rail steels, are likely to present future opportunities to lessen the impact of RCF damage. For this reason, further research into the rolling contact fatigue behaviour of these newer steel grades, combined with the development of improved rail welding procedures, are considered necessary.

Title The effects of friction management materials on rail with pre existing RCF surface damage
 
Author Hardwick, C
Fletcher, DI
Lewis, R
Stock, R
Affiliation L.B. Foster Rail Technologies, U.K.
The University of Sheffield, UK
L.B. Foster Rail Technologies, BC, Canada
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Management of rolling contact fatigue (RCF) risk is a critical maintenance activity in heavy haul railways. Practical means of RCF mitigation involve: 1) preventative and corrective grinding to remove RCF cracks; 2) management of wheel and rail profiles to minimize peak contact pressures; and 3) selection of appropriate rail metallurgy. In addition, reduction of traction forces by application of dry film Top of Rail friction modifiers (FM) has recently been shown to reduce crack growth and extend grinding intervals. Hydro-pressurization and crack face lubrication are processes by which liquid materials (e.g. water), enter pre-existing RCF cracks and under wheel / rail pressure and cause accelerated crack growth, leading to spalling and shelling on rail and wheels. Thus, any liquid material added deliberately to the wheel/rail interface should be considered carefully in terms of the potential for aggravating RCF damage. This study compares the impact on hydro-pressurization and crack face lubrication of different types of materials designed for application to the top of rail using twin disc testing. One type of FM material is water-based and dries to a thin dry film. Two other types are oil or oil-plus-water-based that do not naturally dry and have been introduced more recently to the market. In addition, a commonly used gauge face lubricant (grease) was evaluated. Twin disc testing used R8T wheel material and 350HT grade rail. Surface RCF cracks were first generated under dry conditions, then friction control materials were continuously applied. The test continued for a fixed number of cycles and samples were evaluated at the end. Results show that all the non-drying oil-containing materials cause significant rail damage (spalling) consistent with hydro-pressurization and crack face lubrication mechanisms. By contrast, the drying friction modifier material showed no such damage and prevented further growth of the RCF cracks. These results suggest considerable care should be employed in employing non-drying oil-based materials where pre-existing RCF cracks can occur; e.g. in curves. Careful field testing is recommended to further evaluate this issue clearly seen under laboratory conditions.

Title Studs and squats: the evolving story
 
Author Grassie, SL
Affiliation Stuart Grassie Engineering Ltd, Cambridge, UK
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Several railways suffer from a defect that has been christened a “stud”, which appears superficially similar to a squat but is very different in character. Although both initiation and propagation of studs are poorly understood, at least a couple of railways have already benefitted from exploiting the less malevolent nature of studs, particularly the fact that these do not themselves initiate transverse defects. This paper is based on field work undertaken primarily in NSW to reveal some of the characteristics of stud defects, in particular in contrast to rolling contact fatigue (RCF) of which squats are a classical example. Studs are associated with sites where there is high traction, such as the exit from stations. In NSW they commonly initiate at about 10°-20° to the vertical towards gauge on the high rail in curves, then grow into the rail at an angle of about 20° to the surface. Studs can at first develop very quickly e.g. to a depth of 2.2mm in 6MGT. The stud fans out across the rail from the initial surface crack, developing across the rail at a substantially constant depth of 3-6mm. If the stud is left, it may rise to the surface at the opposite side of the railhead, giving rise to an ugly spall with a fracture surface typical of a conventional fatigue crack. There is no evidence whatsoever that studs become transverse defects, nor should this occur with a crack that develops across rather than along the rail as there is an absence of flexure to drive the crack. Studs have grown in rails in which there is no significant plastic work, and accordingly also no significant depth of compressive residual stress. These characteristics are very different from classical squats. Although some transverse defects (TDs) have been associated with studs, both gauge corner cracking (GCC) and studs have coincided in all of the cases examined. In these cases the TD has clearly developed in the conventional and well understood manner from the GCC. The stud has given rise to a dynamic load that accelerates growth of the TD. But if the GCC had not existed, the TD would not have developed. The paper provides some guidance on treatment and maintenance of studs as well as updating a hypothesis that still rests substantially on circumstantial evidence.

Abstract Managing the Top Of Rail (TOR) wheel/rail coefficient of friction, using friction modifiers (FM), has consistently demonstrated significant benefits to the heavy haul industry including fuel/energy savings, wheel & rail wear reduction, and reduced state of stress of the track infrastructure. Traditionally, the preferred method of FM application has been trackside systems. However, in some situations, it is desirable that friction modifiers are applied from on-board a train set due to several different factors. Simple cost benefit analysis modelling is shown to favour on-board FM application over trackside FM application.

Title On the convective heat transfer from railway wheels
 
Author Cuperus, JL
Venter, G
Affiliation University of Stellenbosch, South Africa Transnet Engineering, Pretoria, South Africa
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract The rate of heat transfer to the atmosphere is a key factor in the performance of wheels that are subjected to tread braking. Excessive heating can cause yielding, possibly resulting in a permanent change in the gauge of the wheelset or a reduction of the compressive residual stress in the rim. In order to predict where these limits are for a wheel design, accurate heat transfer data is required due to the heavily temperature dependent behaviour of the wheel and the material (experienced during severe tread braking applications). This paper deals with the numerical simulation of heat transfer from an 863 mm (34 inch) trailing stock wheel and the comparison of convection data to published heat transfer models of fundamental cases. The numerical simulation results agree fairly well with the heat transfer model for the fundamental case of a rotating flat disc in parallel flow. This would suggest that it is far more important to take the correct fluid flow conditions into account, rather than the specifics of the geometry. Therefore, it is likely that the heat transfer model derived in this project (or that of the rotating disc in parallel flow) could be used for other axisymmetric railway wheels.

Title Heavy haul wheel optimisation combining material and structural design towards better performance and higher axle loads
 
Author Demilly, F
Hencoco, S
Fortin, F
Affiliation MG Valdunes, Trith Saint Léger, France
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Heavy Haul railroads usually operate with an axle load between 26t and 30t with some of them achieving 37,5t to 42t/axle. The demand is now for higher tonnage par axle and wagon to optimize transportation performance and costs. Wheels and axles are made from steel that can be optimized to improve the global service of the components. The performance of the wheel/rail interface depends on the dynamic mechanical properties of the wheel rim and of the rail. High pressure and friction can modify the shear stress values and their location during service. At the same time, the rim wear depends on the hardness of the steel including tread and flange area that can be optimized considering higher hardness of the material vs. a good level of toughness to limit crack propagation. The rim properties are obtained after water quenching and tempering able to provide an adapted stress release of the structure. The plate only undergoes a normalizing cycle. In this case the properties are only given by the chemical analysis of the steel that cannot be reinforced by a higher cooling ratio during heat-treatment. However the strength of the global structure will depend on the mechanical properties of the plate that need to cumulate high stiffness to support the load and elasticity to allow thermal loading with low deflection. All these constraints on a composite product as a railway wheel can be managed by a mix of calculation (wheel/rail contact, plate optimization to support load up to 50t) and material design using metallurgical optimization (chemical analysis, heat-treatment), mechanical and fatigue tests.

Abstract Proactive maintenance with intelligent data analytics for railways is a promising advancement for improving wheel rail interaction, reducing wheel and rail damages, downtime, maintenance cost and life cycle cost. This paper suggests a reliable data analytics to support the decision for eliminating unhealthy wheels on the Swedish heavy haul line. Abstract The study of railway track and rolling stock has traditionally been divided into two separate and distinct disciplines, namely civil and mechanical. The total track-train system has not received the attention it deserves, to the detriment of the overall system performance. A similar situation has occurred in the canefield railways of Australia. Problems which became apparent in the operation of the "unit trains" of the mineral industry also appeared in the canefield railways with the introduction of faster and heavier locomotives and longer trains. For these reasons it was decided to undertake an investigation into the dynamics of the track train system to determine the basic forces acting on the rolling stock and track. The forces considered were the vertical and lateral components of the wheel-rail force and the components of the coupler force acting on the waggon. From a knowledge of these forces it was hoped to be able to develop standards for the design and maintenance of rolling stock and track. Typical sugar industry trains generally consist of 100 to 150 unbraked four wheel waggons having a gross weight of approximately five tonnes. The longest trains being hauled on a regular basis are made up of 200 waggons of five tonne gross weight. Longer trains of up to 240 waggons are hauled on occasions. The newer type of cane field locomotive is a diesel hydraulic, B-B configuration unit of between 24 and 32 tonnes capable of speeds of up to 50 km h. The use of brake vans on long trains is becoming widespread throughout the industry. The vans are three axle, 16 to 24 tonne units controlled by the locomotive driver via a radio link capable of selecting one of seven brake pressure settings.
