Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul Association STS Conference, (IHHA 1999), 14-17 June, 1999, Moscow, Russia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract This paper summarizes current North American heavy-haul freight specifications and maintenance practices for wheels and rails. It lists the most common causes of wheel and rail removal, and describes work completed and in progress to extend the life of these major assets. Finally, it considers how the interactions between wheels and rails affect component life and service performance. Special attention is given to the use of rail grinding to extend rail life (with reference to the Illinois Central Railroad grinding strategy), and the development of new criteria to reduce wheel-climb derailment risk.

Title Tribological aspects of rail/wheel interface
 
Author Zakharov, SM
Zharov, I
Komarovsky, I
Affiliation All-Russian Railway Research Institute, Moscow, Russia
Conference International Heavy Haul Association STS Conference, (IHHA 1999), 14-17 June, 1999, Moscow, Russia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract Factors determining wheel/rail tribological parameters are discussed. The requirements for the local physical models to achieve sufficient adequacy of tribodynamic models are formulated. Tribological factors are studied both with the help of mathematical and physical models. Several quasi-statical models are used to study the influence of the instantaneous axis of a wheel set rotation position and moments, resulting from forward displacement of wheel flange contact patch on the parameters responsible for wheel flange and rail head wear. The influence of the three-piece freight car bogie on the parameters of tribological behavior of wheel and rails moving in sharp curves is studied. Modeling of the wear process between wheel flange and side face of the rail head is studied and discussed. Four wear modes, referred as mild, severe, heavy and catastrophic have been identified in the laboratory tests. The wear modes which occur in worn wheel flanges and in side-worn rails have been found to correlate with those found on the rollers during laboratory tests. The boundaries of wear modes are found. Basing on the results of laboratory study, a wear law was derived and suggested to be used in dynamic models of wheel/rail interaction.

Title Wheel-rail contact and forces in curves
 
Author Fairweather, DMS
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract The forces acting on a six-wheel bogie in a curve are compared with those on a four-wheel bogie using the Heumann graphical method of determining forces. It is shown that unless the centre or end axles have adequate lateral float, replacing four-wheel bogies by six-wheel, atthough reducing nominal wheel-load by about 30%, may not reduce the wheel-rail contact forces on the leading wheelsets. The relationship between wheel and rail profiles is discussed, emphasising the need for close control of track gauge and care in matching wheel and rail profiles, particularly on railways where intensive traffic is to be introduced on rail of fairly light section.

Chua, KH
Lo, KW
Lee, SL
Affiliation National University of Singapore
Conference CORE 1993: Contracting Railways - Safety, Standards and the Surroundings, 17-20 October 1993, Newcastle, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 1993
Abstract This paper presents an investigation study of impact noise generated by train wheels running over rail discontinuity at an insulated rail joint. An analytical model of rigid wheel on a flexible rail is proposed. The equivalent stiffness and lumped mass of rail are computed by finite element analyses of a cantilever beam subjected to static load and impact load, respectively. A dynamic analysis is carried out to determine the separation time of wheel and rail and the resulting impact velocity. The acoustic power is assumed to be proportional to the change in kinetic energy before and after impact. The proportionality constant is obtained by calibration against published results. Field measurement was conducted to measure rail response and sound pressure level of impact noise at an insulated rail joint on a viaduct. The analytically predicted SPL is found to agree well with measured SPL.

Abstract Curved track simulation modelling for primary sprung bogie vehicles has been used to define contact conditions at the wheel-rail interface. Contact stresses were evaluated in relation to shakedown limits and were used to assess rolling contact fatigue performance. Vehicles with a lower yaw stiffness are preferred, with a shift to stiffer suspensions being accompanied by significant increases in wheel-rail energy dissipation, wear and rolling contact fatigue. Title Wheel and rail roughness estimation based on rail vibration measurement
 
Author Li, W
Dwight, R
Affiliation University of Wollongong, Wollongong, NSW
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract The surface roughness of interacting wheels and rails, which is the major contributor to the generation of railway rolling noise, has been investigated by considering the separate roughness components of wheels and rails and their total effective roughness. The total effective roughness is the combined roughness of wheels and rails by further allowing for the attenuation effect resulting from the finite sizes of wheel and rail contact areas. Two methods are used to obtain the total effective roughness: direct measurement by scanning wheel and rail surfaces with a pin-tipped sensor-based device and indirect measurement by measuring rail vibration. A transfer function with default track parameters is used to convert measured rail vibration to roughness at the wheel and rail interface. It is based on the same train that the rail vibration induced induced and the wheel roughness were measured. Track decay rates, as an indispensable element of the indirect method, were also measured from impact hammer tests and compared with the results inferred from measured rail vibration. It is found that the total effective roughness meaured using direct and indirect methods matches each other in the wavelength range from 12.5 to 3.15 cm; the two track decay rate measurement methods have good agreement at most of the frequencies below 5 kHz for the tangent ballast-supported track investigated. The indirect track decay rate measurement method is suitable for wide applications on tracks with similar conditions.

Title Best practises in wheel detection and axle counting: the evolution of level crossing control systems
 
Author Popplewell, N
Rendell, S
Affiliation Rail Control Systems Australia, Melbourne, Vic
Rail Planning Services, Sydney, NSW
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract When considering what is the standard active level crossing arrangement used in New South Wales (NSW), Australia, not a great deal has changed on the technology of the control system in a significant time. Early active systems used simple Direct Current (DC) track circuits for train detection with shelf-mounted relays performing the control of the flashing lights, barrier machines and bell. These systems have evolved to utilise miniature relays in lieu of the shelf mounted type, with installations still relying upon DC track circuits for train detection. The only recent evolution of these systems, is that many newer installations are solar powered to reduce installation costs. The next significant innovation was the introduction of track circuit based grade crossing predictor systems, where the train detection and control system were managed from within a single safety system. These systems utilise audio-frequency track circuits for train detection and speed prediction. It was not until the acceptance of the axle counter train detection systems that level crossings in regional locations could be unshackled from the risks associated with poor wheel to rail electrical contact. More recently there has been a systematic push to implement new technology that will retain a SIL4 safety reliability rating and enable a reduction in the installation and ongoing maintenance costs. A collaborative effort has taken place between the authors to integrate axle counter technology, the Frauscher FAdC, and a modern solid-state control system, the HIMA HIMatrix F35 safety controller. The result is an integrated level crossing control system. During October 2016, a trial system was commissioned just outside Bathurst in New South Wales, Australia. This site's equipment was completely isolated from the live railway, except for the axle counter system that counted every train that passed the location. This system takes advantage of the Frauscher Safe Ethernet (FSE) protocol that allows the HIMA control system to communicate directly with the Frauscher FAdC axle counting system's FSE communication module. The Ethernet communication and system architecture allows greater system functionality and more detailed logging to be achieved. The system is more tolerant to certain types of equipment failure, and intended or unintended interference from the likes of vandals and maintenance workers, respectively. This allows the normal operation of the level crossing warning equipment to continue uninterrupted in many circumstances. The system is also able to tolerate a local method of working where road/rail vehicles, also known as hi-rails, may mount the track at the level crossing and depart the site without be left disturbed, thus requiring a manual system reset. The integrated level crossing system developed can be programmed to recognise the movement and suppress the operation of the level crossing warning equipment whilst monitoring and logging the wheel counts within the system.

Abstract High strength heat treated pearlitic rail steels are used extensively in heavy haul rail systems to mitigate rail degradation due to wear and rolling contact fatigue damage, and improve rail life. However, these benefits may be offset by the potential occurrance of localised degradation at flashbutt welds due to the discontinuous nature of both microstructure and mechanical properties relative to those of the parent rail. Of particular concern is the higher sensitivity to cumulative plastic deformation (ratcheting) and rolling contact fatigue damage in the heat-softened zones. As part of ongoing research into improving the performance of welds in these rail grades, the influence of different welding parameters on the ratcheting behaviour of the heat-softened zone in flashbutt welds has been systematically investigated. The results show that both maximum ratcheting strain and maximum ratcheting strain rate can be significantly reduced and so the reliability and the performance of flashbutt welds can be improved by narrowing the width of the softened zones.

Title Simulation of track-locomotive interactions
 
Author Wu, Q
Sun, Y
Spiryagin, M
Cole, C
Affiliation Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Qld
Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation, Canberra, ACT
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract This paper introduces a new method to study track dynamics and locomotive dynamics as well as track-locomotive interactions. The method uses a two-way co-simulation technique to link a detailed track model that is developed using an in-house software package and a detailed locomotive model that is developed using a commercial software package. The track model has considered the rails, fasteners, sleepers, ballast, subballast, and subgrade. The locomotive model has considered the carbody, bogie frames, wheelsets, traction motors and gear boxes. A detailed wheel-rail contact model with up to three simultaneous contact points for every wheel on each rail is used. In the two-way co-simulation technique, wheel-rail contact forces that are simulated in the commerical software package are sent to the track model and attached to their corresponding positions on the rails. With these attached wheel-rail contact forces, the track model can determine the positions and postures of the rail which are then sent to the commercial software package. The rail profiles in the commercial software are updated according to the rail positions and rail postures. Compared with conventional methods, this method has the advantages of being able to use both detailed track models and detailed locomotive models. The feasibility of the method has been demonstrated by using two simulations to model a locomotive negotiating a section of track with and without an unsupported sleeper.

Abstract In the railway system, geometrical discontinuities in the rail such as switches and crossings induce high dynamical wheel/rail contact forces, which can be several times greater than the static force. They can accelerate damage and fracture of different railway components such as wheel, rail, fastening system and sleepers. Therefore it is a major issue for a railway operator to characterize accurately these forces in order to design robust components and to define relevant maintenance politics. In this paper, a first numerical modeling of a wheel circulating on a crossing, using multi-body tool is presented. It helps to determine the forces applied to the crossing. FEM calculations, whose results are compared to field measurements, followed by fatigue analysis enable to compare different crossing designs and to optimize them. In order to have more accurate numerical results by taking into account plastic deformations of the crossing, in particular in the contact area, and the high frequency dynamical response of the system (>1000Hz), a second full FEM modeling of the wheelset and the track is developed. Using an explicit algorithm, simulations in the time domain are performed to determine the dynamical wheel-rail forces.

Publication Date 2019
Abstract Measurement and theoretical studies on wheel/rail noise have been carried out widely and thoroughly. The wheel/rail noise from 250 Hz to 4 kHz has greater influence on the wayside noise along railway lines. However, when a train runs on a gently curved track, the noise due to wheel and rail vibrations above 10 kHz (referred to as high-frequency noise) has sometimes a greater contribution to the total wayside noise. Such high-frequency noise is observed on both low-speed (160 km/h at most) and highspeed railway lines. Previous studies in Japan found that, on a low-speed railway line, the dominant sources of high-frequency noise are the wheels on the outside rail. But, on a high-speed railway line, the characteristics of the high-frequency noise generated in the curved section remained unclear. In this paper, the contribution of the high-frequency noise to wayside noise on a high-speed railway line is investigated in field tests and static experiments, the latter involving shaker excitation. Furthermore, the source related to the high-frequency noise is localized in the field tests by means of a directional microphone. These results show that wheel noise above 10 kHz is generated mainly by the outside leading wheel of each bogie. And, the noise is found to depend on the axle position of the outside wheel and also on the train speed.
