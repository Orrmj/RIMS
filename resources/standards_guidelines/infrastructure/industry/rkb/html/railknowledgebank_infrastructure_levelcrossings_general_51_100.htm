Title Level with me: human factors in pedestrian and road-user violations at a notorious Victorian railway level crossing
 
Author Naweed, A
Rose, J
Larue, GS
Wullems, C
Affiliation Appleton Institute for Behavioural Science, Central Queensland University
University of South Australia
Centre for Accident Research and Road Safety (CARRS-Q), Queensland University of Technology
European Space Agency
Source ATRI
Journal Road & Transport Research
Publication Date 2016-06
Abstract There are 23 500 railway level crossings in Australia. In these risky environments, it is important to understand what human factors issues are present and how road users and pedestrians engage with crossings. In this study, on-site observations were performed over a 2-day period at a 3-track active railway level crossing with a history of reported issues. This was followed by 52 interviews with level crossing users. Over 700 separate violations were recorded, with representations in multiple categories (e.g. going through flashing lights >2s after starting flashing; stopping on crossing). Time stamping data revealed that the crossing was active for 59% of the time in morning periods with trains taking up to 4 min to arrive following first activation. Level crossing users experienced frustration due to delays caused by the frequency of trains, which was considered to increase the likelihood of risktaking. Analysis of interview data identified themes associated with congestion, safety, and violations. This work offers insight into context specific issues associated with active level crossing protection and provides some empirical context around the level crossing removal program currently underway in Victoria.

Abstract The purpose of this paper is to describe the process used in, and the outcomes from, an assessment for its potential use in New Zealand of a risk methodology from another jurisdiction. ALCAM (Australian Level Crossing Assessment Model), a method for identifying weaknesses in protection offered at level crossings and assigning appropriate priority to remedial measures, was endorsed as a national standard by the Australian Transport Council of State Ministers in 2004. ALCAM was applied to a representative sample of level crossings in New Zealand and the information obtained used to identify issues and their possible resolution, assess likely costs and benefits of applying the model, consider funding options and assist in involving practitioners and decision makers in the process. The major strength of the model was identified as the necessary involvement of, and collaboration between, road and rail authorities in agreeing remedial treatments.

Title Best practises in wheel detection and axle counting: the evolution of level crossing control systems
 
Author Popplewell, N
Rendell, S
Affiliation Rail Control Systems Australia, Melbourne, Vic
Rail Planning Services, Sydney, NSW
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract When considering what is the standard active level crossing arrangement used in New South Wales (NSW), Australia, not a great deal has changed on the technology of the control system in a significant time. Early active systems used simple Direct Current (DC) track circuits for train detection with shelf-mounted relays performing the control of the flashing lights, barrier machines and bell. These systems have evolved to utilise miniature relays in lieu of the shelf mounted type, with installations still relying upon DC track circuits for train detection. The only recent evolution of these systems, is that many newer installations are solar powered to reduce installation costs. The next significant innovation was the introduction of track circuit based grade crossing predictor systems, where the train detection and control system were managed from within a single safety system. These systems utilise audio-frequency track circuits for train detection and speed prediction. It was not until the acceptance of the axle counter train detection systems that level crossings in regional locations could be unshackled from the risks associated with poor wheel to rail electrical contact. More recently there has been a systematic push to implement new technology that will retain a SIL4 safety reliability rating and enable a reduction in the installation and ongoing maintenance costs. A collaborative effort has taken place between the authors to integrate axle counter technology, the Frauscher FAdC, and a modern solid-state control system, the HIMA HIMatrix F35 safety controller. The result is an integrated level crossing control system. During October 2016, a trial system was commissioned just outside Bathurst in New South Wales, Australia. This site's equipment was completely isolated from the live railway, except for the axle counter system that counted every train that passed the location. This system takes advantage of the Frauscher Safe Ethernet (FSE) protocol that allows the HIMA control system to communicate directly with the Frauscher FAdC axle counting system's FSE communication module. The Ethernet communication and system architecture allows greater system functionality and more detailed logging to be achieved. The system is more tolerant to certain types of equipment failure, and intended or unintended interference from the likes of vandals and maintenance workers, respectively. This allows the normal operation of the level crossing warning equipment to continue uninterrupted in many circumstances. The system is also able to tolerate a local method of working where road/rail vehicles, also known as hi-rails, may mount the track at the level crossing and depart the site without be left disturbed, thus requiring a manual system reset. The integrated level crossing system developed can be programmed to recognise the movement and suppress the operation of the level crossing warning equipment whilst monitoring and logging the wheel counts within the system.

Title Identifying an optimal intervention framework for managing safety upgrades to railway level crossings
 
Description The Australian rail industry currently carries out upgrades to level crossings incrementally, according to rankings informed by a safety-risk assessment model. Improvements and upgrades are subsequently implemented using a combination of active and/or passive countermeasures that render them fail-to-safe. However, given the sheer number of level crossings in operation across Australia, implementing upgrades on an incremental and fail-to-safe basis will be costly and very time consuming. Another solution is to upgrade crossings on a system-wide basis using an intervention framework that is less than fail-to-safe. This will deliver risk mitigation on an affordable and much larger scale, as opposed to a much smaller number of crossings in an equivalent time frame. However, the issue of expediting railway level crossing upgrades outside fail-to-safe standards is contentious, and invokes issues of legal liability in the event of future mishaps. This project seeks to explore these issues and undertake risk calculations that establish which of these approaches can optimise railway level crossings intervention in terms of the best societal outcome. This research is likely to meet the identified needs of rail operators and regulators through a cost-benefit justification of a system-wide approach for managing safety at railway level crossings, or through legal clarification of substantive issues.

Abstract Two rail/road level crossings will be removed near Melbourne, Australia via 'rail under road' (rail trench) construction. This paper discusses the potential for groundwater impacts and approaches considered to achieve impact minimisation. Further numerical groundwater modelling, impact and risk assessments and reviews were undertaken, which indicated that at Edithvale, a passive horizontal drain could be installed to minimise project induced changes to groundwater levels. Affiliation University of Costa Rica, San Pedro, Costa Rica
Source ATRI
Journal Journal of Transportation Safety and Security
Publication Date 2021-06
Abstract Injury severity is a key aspect in road safety, especially at railway crossings. Traditional statistical methods such as ordered probit or ordered logit models provide adequate approaches to these analyses when plenty of covariates are available. However, these traditional approaches can be combined with other techniques, for instance, spatial methods and Bayesian analyses to provide better estimations by including the effects of covariates that are not explicitly included in the models, among other benefits. Bayesian analyses are useful when low crash frequencies and small sample sizes are present in the data, which is common for railway crashes. A case from San JosÃ©, Costa Rica is used to demonstrate the use of the proposed methodology. Spatial analyses were implemented through conditional autoregressive (CAR) models up to third-ordered neighbors. A Bayesian approach was used to obtain more accurate estimations for the model coefficients and parameters. The results show how this methodology provides better overall results than a non-spatial ordered probit model. The first-order CAR model was determined to be the best overall model and therefore is preferred.
