Title Formation of rail corrugation and measures to reduce it on curved track
 
Author Tan, L
Yu, T
Affiliation China Academy Of Railway Sciences, Beijing, China
Conference 5th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1993), 6-13 June, 1993, Beijing, China
Abstract Of the features of rail corrugation, the first one is that it may occur on the high rail, or on the low rail of a curve; the second one is that at the earlier stage, wearing will predominate, but gradually the plastic deformation will predominate. One of the characteristics of rail/wheel interaction is that the rail/wheel friction has positive and negative gradients. Based on the above we propose that the frictional self-excited vibration of wheelset expecially the relaxation vibration (stick slip vibration) is the mechanism of corrugation formation. After its formation, the vertical force between rail and wheel, becomes greater, causing the plastic metal flow on rail head to grow grea~er and greater and the development of corrugation to be intensified. The authors conducted experimental study and theoretical analysis. using four-axle freight cars. The comparison between preliminary theoretical analysis and field investigation results proves that the above point of view is tenable. In this paper, the authors also explore the effect on the development of corrugation caused by the rapidly increased vertical force between rail and wheel, induced by wavy irregularities of rail, and by lozenging of three-piece trucks as well. The FAST experiment results show that the high rail lubrication can reduce the low rail corrugation and the longitudinal forces may be explained by the theory discussed in this paper. The most essential measure for reducing or preventing corrugation lies in reducing or preventing frictional self-excited vibration of wheelset, especially the relaxation vibration.

Title Automatic system for checking the wear condition of wheels profile
 
Author Octavian, I
Affiliation Institute for Research and Technological Design in Transport, Bucharest, Romania
Conference 5th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1993), 6-13 June, 1993, Beijing, China
Abstract The present paper refers to an automated procedure and system for checking the wear state of the wheels profile of motor and hauled rolling stock in running during the process of their operation and repair. The procedure is based upon the technique of recording the profile images of all train wheels or of one vehicle by using the video surveying system with close circuit and digital processing of the image recorded followed by the execution of the measurements directly on the image by means of the electronic computer at the same time with establishing the order number of the axle, followed by printing and management of dangerous cases for traffic safety. The automated system is made up of the metering and control device, an assembly of video cameras, an illumination system, guiding device, calculation system for acquisition and digital processing of the image and a terminal for data recording (or transmission). It is capable of performing the checking of the wear state of the railway vehicles wheels profile on the basis of the above mentioned procedure. The automated system mounted in the track (on the railway, tramway or underground network) ensures the checking and metering of all the vehicles wheels that run under the pre-established speed conditions on the track sector on which it is mounted. The automated system performs the checking of all wheels and signalling of dangerous cases for traffic safety.

Title Martensite formation and damage around railway wheel flats
 
Author Jergeus, J
Affiliation Chalmers University of Technology, Goteborg, Sweden
Conference 6th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1997), 6-10 April, 1997, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 1997
Abstract A railway wheel flat is a flat spot on the rolling surface of a wheel caused by its unintentional sliding on the rail. Under certain sliding conditions, martensite, which is a brittle phase of carbon steel, will form around such a flat. The presence of martensite often leads to cracking and spalling of the material in the tread of a rolling wheel having a flat. In Sweden, flat-induced cracks and spalls lead to large costs for wheelset maintenance. It is believed that a better knowledge about the process of formation of martensite around a wheel flat, and about the ensuing spalling, will facilitate money-saving improvements in wheelset operation and maintenance. In this paper, the development of a railway wheel flat is studied by use of the finite element method. The numerical results are compared with data from physical experiments.

Title Investigation into the abnormally high wear rate of AC electric locomotive wheels on the Ermelo - Richards Bay coal line when replacing UIC-a rail with chrome - maganese rail
 
Author Venter, JP
Affiliation South African Transport Services
Conference 4th International Heavy Haul Conference, Railways in action, 11-15 September 1989, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1989
Abstract Shortly after the introduction of S.60 Cr-Mn rail, the rate of locomotive wheel wear began to increase. Within a short period, the wear rate reached crisis proportions with wheel life dropping from 200 000 km to 30 000 km. A comprehensive investigation project was started to establish the reasons for the fast deterioration which was almost directly proportional to the percentage of S.60 Cr-Mn rail laid in the track. The areas identified and investigated were locomotive tolerances, wheel and rail metallurgy, rail and track geometry and wheel rail lubrication. Rail and track geometry, and wheel rail lubrication were identified as the areas where the greatest gains could be made. An advantageous assymmetric track profile was generated and applied to the line, and a mobile lubrication vehicle developed which provided effective lubrication of the wheel/rail interface. With these complimentary actions, South African Transport Services have succeeded in restoring locomotive wheel life to approximately 150.000 km between tyre toppings.

Title Working and engineering hardening of wheel contact surfaces
 
Author Bogdanov, VM
Markov, DP
Penkova, GI
Affiliation All-Russian Railway Research Institute, Moscow, Russia
Conference International Heavy Haul Association STS Conference, (IHHA 1999), 14-17 June, 1999, Moscow, Russia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract The tribotechnical properties of surface layers of wheels and laboratory rollers hardened in service and in production were studied. Hardness of work self-hardened wheel tread is less 450 HVO. I and slowly decreases with depth, approaching initial level 280-300 HV at 5-8 mm. Hardness of conical surface of flanges approaches 900 HVO. I. Depth of hardened layer here is less 0,5 mm. It was found that surface hardness of wheel steel (0.60-0.65% C) laboratory rollers is proportional to slippage in range from 0 to 2% and rises up to the maximum value 750-850 HVO. 1, if slippage is more than 2%, creating frictional layer with the increased wear resistance. High quality thermal hardening of wheel flanges with hardness in the range from 600 to 800 HV minimizes the wear rate of flanges and side wear of rails up to 3 times. Thermal hardening of wheel tread with martensite or tempering troostite with hardness more than 450 HV provokes high wear rate of tread (vertical wear).

Abstract This study presents a quantitative evaluation of the mechanical behavior of counterformal non-Hertzian rail/wheel contacts that have been treated as both non-Hertzian and ellipticized geometries.
