Title Principle theory of single phase feeding power conditioner for AC traction
 
Author Uzuka, T
Mochinaga, Y
Hase, S
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Most of Japanese AC electrified trains such as JR group's Shinkansen use a 3-phase to 2-phase transformation system. In some cases, a 3-phase to single phase feeding system would be better than the 3-phase to 2-phase system. For example, a directional feeding system is not fit for the depot that needs single phase. Different phase directional feeding system has another problem that trains have to run across different phase circuits at every substation (and sometimes at every sectioning post.) The 3-phase to single phase feeding system can avoid this problem and it supports parallel feeding easily. We are trying to apply a new type of 3-phase to single phase transformation feeding system called "SFC: single phase feeding power conditioner." A scalene Scottconnected transformer and a pair of self-commutated inverters constitute the basic configuration of SFC. Proper transformation ratio between Main-phase and Teaser should be even in SFC under the latest traction loads. Inverters are connected to both phases of secondary side of transformer. SFC feeds trains from a slant phase (from Teaser to Main-phase, thus a feeding voltage phase is 45 degrees lag from teaser) of the secondary side of the scalene Scott-connected transformer. Primary side of the transformer is connected to the power grid. A pair of self-commutated inverters is controlled by PWM method so that they can handle active power and reactive power. Inverters compensate reactive power of each secondary side respectively to regulate a 3-phase voltage fluctuation. Further, inverters connected back-to-back in DC side can accommodate an active power in the Mainphase and Teaser of secondary side of transformer each other, so that 3-phase side active power would balance. Thus SFC can feed any type of single phase feeding loads, without unbalance of 3-phase side. Rated capacity of SFC (transformer and two inverters) should be approximately equal to all loads of feeding circuit, if 3-phase side voltage fluctuation should be zero. Actually, desirable capacity depends on relative rate of power source capacitor to traction load and demands of 3-phase side. We have proved a principle theory of SFC using 200V-scale mini-model and variety types of imitation loads, such as resistor (self-commutated car), thyristor phase controlled car. According to the result of this test, SFC can feed any type of traction load and can suppress 3-phase voltage unbalance.

Abstract In order to decrease the headway on the TGV PARIS LYONS Line from 5 to 4 minutes, the electrical traction fixed installations had to be strengthened. To this end, capacitor banks were connected in series with "25 kV" feeders in the meshes of the 2 x 25 kV assembly. Title Effect of rail potential reduction owing to substation controlled by thyristors
 
Author Hase, S
Ito, T
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The rail is a part of the feeding circuit and the voltage of the rail is generated by the load current and the rail resistance. The rail is connected to the earth through a certain resistance and thereby a voltage is generated between the rail and the earth . This voltage is called "the rail potential". The rail potential has a close relation to the electrolytic erosion in DC electric railway system. The railway engineers have addressed to this problem for a long time. The problem is aggravating with an increased load current, high resistivity between the rail and the earth and development of a new rail fastening device . The rail potential tends to be larger recently. Therefore it is necessary to suppress the rail potential from a view point of the electrolytic erosion and safety in maintenance work. In this paper, we describe the existing substation controlled by silicon, the substation controlled by thyristors to keep the output voltage constant, and calculated results of the rail voltage with two types of substations mentioned above. In the calculation, we assume that first, there are four substations at intervals of 10km. Second, track is single or double. Third, the train has a load capacity of 2000A and its location is at the center of the line. As a result, installing the substation controlled by thyristors makes it possible to suppress the rail voltage unlike the conventional substation. The current supplied by two substations located at the edge of the model line causes a rise in the rail voltage.

Abstract Performances of power converters used in railway traction depend on the features of high power semiconductor devices which are employed. At present converters for heavy locomotives employ press-pack devices like fast thyristors and GTO's. Recently the development of high voltage IGBT (Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistor) module [1-2] has been welcomed because this device may operate at high speed switching with limited losses using relatively simple driving circuit that also may be used for an easy protection against short circuit. These characteristics allow an overall reduction in size and wei ght of the whole converter [3]. After an initial use of IGBT for auxiliary power supply and for light traction application (metro, trolleybus, tramway, etc.) now first prototypes of heavy traction converter using these devices are under test [4,7]. Together with the functional and electrical tests an important task is the accurate evaluation of the reliability of these devices; in fact many different technologies are involved in IGBT modules (i.e. soldering, wire bonding, DCB, epoxy insulation) which makes modules potentially less reliable than simpler and well known press-pack disc devices [5]. The european BRITE research project RAPSDRA (Reliability of Advanced High Power Semiconductor Devices for Railway traction Application) started in 1996 as an important contribution to the evaluation and improvement of reliability of IGBT devices [6]. The RAPSDRA consortium involves railway operators, power semiconductor manufacturers, industrial R&D laboratories and universities as shown in Table 1. The major objectives for the RAPSDRA project are: to reach an agreement on reliability tests based on line profile, physical failure mechanism, operation in an AWCO (assembly for worst case operation); to develop alternative assembly technique for IGBT chips; standardizing IGBT module packages; modelling of converter reliability based on devices failure mode analysis, to get tools for lifetime prediction; developing reliability indicators. In this paper the contribution to the RAPSDRA project of italian railway operator FS and of semiconductor devices and power converters manufacturer Ansaldo Trasporti, is reported.

Title Inductive power supply for main-line railways
 
Author Winter, J
Streit, S
Affiliation German Aerospace Center (DLR), Stuttgart, Germany
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Electric rail systems are characterized by a transfer of electrical energy from the fixed installations of the railway power supply systems to the non-stationary loads and vice versa. For main-line railways, the power transmission is realized via pantograph-mounted sliding contacts from the overhead catenary line. This transmission system is characterized by some operational disadvantages. First, the contact system is subject to wear. Second, wear, noise emission and aerodynamic resistance of the pantograph grow with increasing speed. Consequently, the transmission system contributes substantially to the overall system life cycle cost due to contact wear. Furthermore the periphery is impacted negatively by noise emission and additional aerodynamic drag results in higher CO2 emissions. Especially at low temperatures, icy contact wires lead to increased arcing, which has a strong influence on the wear of the system. In general the costs for operating complications are high. For these reasons, it makes sense to replace the existing transmission system by an energy contact- and wear-free one.
The basic technical feasibility of an inductive energy transfer system (IETS) for main-line railway application is proven. The economic viability of an IETS is compared with a conventional pantograph-catenary system in terms of material costs. Based on this a migration strategy is indicated.

A raising mechanism, which is equipped with a pantograph and gives upward force to the pantograph head, enables the pantograph to contact with catenaries. In a resting state, the contact force between the pantograph and the catenary is identical to the force given by the raising mechanism. However, when the pantograph runs along the catenary, it is fluctuated due to aerodynamic force, propagation and reflection of the wave on the catenaries, change in the dynamic characteristics of the catenary systems depending on the position, etc. An excessively large contact force can damage both the pantograph and the catenary or may cause a severe accident like contact wire breaking in the worst case scenario. The state the pantograph and the catenary are in, the non-contact state, is called contact loss. The contact force fluctuation therefore should be kept as small as possible. Basically, small contact force fluctuation of a pantograph at high speed can be achieved by making use of light-weight materials for the pantograph components. Moreover, it is widely known that the pantograph head springs with longer stroke gives better contact force performance. It is also important that the pantographs for Japanese high-speed trains should meet low-noise requirements due to strict noise regulation in Japan. Noise reduction strategies have been taken in terms of reducing the number of the pantograph parts exposed to airflow. The reduction in the number of parts without losing mechanical strength and current capacity tends to make each of the remaining parts larger and/or heavier, thus there is a limit to reduction in the total weight of the low-noise pantograph. In addition, application of long stroke springs to a low-noise pantograph for the purpose of obtaining high current collection performance works negatively in the sense of reducing aerodynamic noise because it is necessary to put a long casing for the spring, which can be another noise source. As pointed out above, it would be difficult for pantographs to achieve both high current collection performance and low-noise property, and much more difficult if it runs at inexperienced high speed. Publication Date 2004
Abstract Much valuable equipment is lost due to voltages induced from power lines and electric traction systems. In the worst case human life can also be put at risk when high potentials occur between adjacent items of equipment. Signalling equipment could malfunction or adopt erroneous and hazardous logic conditions if overall system design does not consider these external influences. The calculation of induced voltages and the design of earthing and bonding systems is well documented but frequently applied inappropriately as the origin of some of the practices and criteria often seems to have been lost in the mists of time, frequently subject to subjective philosophical decisions and much faith is often placed in highly accurate modelling derived from input data of dubious accuracy. This paper aims to demystify some aspects of this cross-disciplinary subject, reviewing past historical practices adopted by different rail authorities. Methods are given of estimating key design parameters such as induced voltages and equipment and personnel hazards resulting from induction and earth potential rise and installation pitfalls to be avoided by a top down approach to earthing and bonding.
