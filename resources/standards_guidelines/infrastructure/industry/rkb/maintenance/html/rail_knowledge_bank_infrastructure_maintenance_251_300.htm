Title Implementation of e-maintenance concept on the iron ore line in Sweden
 
Author Jägare, V
Juntti, U
Karim, R
Affiliation Luleå University of Technology, Sweden
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The Swedish railway is after deregulation composed, of several different stakeholders, each one with individual objectives and strategies to maintain their assets and rolling stock. Although some maintenance data are common between the stakeholders, no greater sharing of these data exists. The insufficient data availability impacts the effectiveness in maintenance decision making. The Luleå Railway Research Center (JVTC) has in more than a decade conducted research in eMaintenance. One of the objectives with eMaintenance research is to develop maintenance decision support tools through enhanced use of information and communication technology for data analytics from various data sources. These tools can also be used to enable condition based maintenance to minimize disruptions and optimise the maintenance cost in a railway system. Another objective this research is to discover how to offer operators, infrastructure managers and system integrators access to central computerised data to support decision making within operation and maintenance. eMaintenance solutions for railway are now being implemented in a pilot project called ePilot119 in the northern part of Sweden on track section 119 between Luleå and Boden. ePilot119 delivers a collaborative platform aimed to enable the development of eMaintenance solutions based on the needs and requirements from various stakeholders. These solutions are expected to enable and transform the Swedish fragmented rail industry to an integrated system, both from an organizational and technical point of view. Some of the objectives are to enhance the sharing of data, developing decision support, define the information and communication technology (ICT) infrastructure and transform ePilot119 to a common natural process for sharing maintenance decision support for the railway. However, implementing eMaintenance solutions to support effective and efficient maintenance decisionmaking related to a complex technical system (i.e. railway) and with a large number of heterogeneous stakeholders is challenging, and requires appropriate tools (e.g. framework, approaches, methodologies, and technologies). Hence, this paper aims to identify some of the significant factors which need to be considered in order to develop an appropriate implementation process and collaboration platform, which aim to facilitate maintenance decision-making through eMaintenance solutions. By identifying these factors that might hinder the execution of the project and implementation of good results, there is a possibility to jointly create the best prerequisites for the operation and maintenance of the railway system.

Title Monitoring of lightning protection’s degradation level to achieve preventative maintenance
 
Author Mashaba, MM
Affiliation Transnet Freight Rail, Johannesburg, South Africa
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Lightning and switching surges propagate through incoming and outgoing (power and signal) cables to reach electrical and electronic equipment, and subsequently may cause catastrophic damages and operational malfunctions. Surge Protective Devices composed of Metal Oxide Varistors (MOVs) are commonly used to protect the equipment against surges, however, over time the MOVs degrade depending on the severity and frequency of surges. As a result, the MOV no longer offer same (or acceptable) protection, and measuring the physical parameters of the MOV to deduce its condition is not always practical, and thus preventative maintenance mechanisms are sought. The purpose of this paper is to propose a mathematical model that characterises the degradation of the MOV with respect to an 8/20 µs lightning impulse current injected. The proposed model can accurately predict the degradation with a maximum error that is below ±2% relative to the pulse rating curves and 25% compared to experimental test results. Further work is recommended in order to refine and improve the model. Since the model can determine the degradation level of the MOV it will aid to achieve preventative maintenance while ensuring that the electrical equipment are always protected and continue to function properly without any disturbances. Subsequently, train delays will be minimal as the signalling and electrical damages caused by lightning and switching impulses will be prevented.

Title Infrastructure maintenance by contract
 
Author Gorman, JE
Affiliation Fessl Pty Ltd, Parkes, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Tenth International Rail Track Conference, 12-14 September 1994, Brisbane, Australia
Publication Date 1994
Abstract Government Railways like many other maintenance dependent industries are currently looking closely at their options In regard to the ways in which they have their infrastructure maintenance carried out. Host Railways wish to concentrate their efforts on their core business of transporting goods and passengers and are looking to alternative means of carrying out essential maintenance necessary to support that core business. Some Railways have expressed the view that all infrastructure maintenance should be contracted auto while others are identifying only certain activities which can be readily separated out for performance by private industry. This paper discusses the relative merits of these approaches with regard to: i) the current capacity and abilIty of private industry to perform the multitude of tasks involved in infrastructure maintenance. ii) the availability or otherwise of comprehensive specifications for all or some of the tasks in infrastructure maintenance. iii) the necessity for the Railways to maintain in-house expertise and to remain an informed client in all areas central to supporting its core business. iv) the industrial acceptability of bringing Contractors into works traditionally performed by in-house staff. Overriding all of these criteria is a fifth implied criteria being cost effectiveness in both the short and long term. I support the view that a gradual and planned transition from full in-house maintenance to Contract involvement in infrastructure maintenance 15 more likely to best meet the needs of both the Railways and the private Contractors with respect to all of the above mentioned criteria. I contend the process should be more one of evolution rather than the creation of a new regime overnight.

Abstract Heavy haul iron ore operations around the world have been continuously increasing axle loads and train lengths. Increased train length allows higher production without compromising the length of maintenance windows. A key disadvantage of longer trains is the difficulty controlling and limiting in-train forces. In order to manage these in-train forces it is first necessary to monitor them. Waiting for in-service failures to highlight problem areas of operation is clearly not a preferred strategy as service failures of coupling equipment (such as couplers, drawbars, draft packs, yokes, and knuckles) will lead to direct and consequential delays of trains. This paper uses examples recorded across multiple rail systems to illustrate the benefits of continuously monitoring in-train forces across all areas of operation, as opposed to conducting periodic trials or no recording at all. Automated recording and reporting enables the identification of areas of operation that contribute most to component damage; provides continuous feedback to drivers; and enables immediate evaluation of operational changes that affect in-train forces.

Title A model-based systems engineering approach to developing an asset management framework for rail organisations
 
Author Hocking, B
Sproston, C
Affiliation Shoal Engineering Pty Ltd, Adelaide, SA
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Successful implementation of Asset Management (AM) principles is key to a rail organisation’s ability to realise maximum value from its assets. The success of this implementation is based on the structure, maturity and useability of the organisation’s Asset Management System (AMS). ISO55001:2014 specifies the requirements for an AMS, including artefacts such as the Strategic Asset Management Plan and Asset Management Plans. These artefacts are based on, and contain, technical and non-technical information that are inevitably linked in a complex manner. The complexity of connected information presents a significant challenge for organisations attempting to develop and manage an AMS and associated artefacts to meet the requirements of ISO55001:2014 and move beyond them towards AM best practice. Abstract Since an enormous number of ground coils are installed along the entire length of a superconducting maglev guideway, efficient maintenance work is required. Therefore, we developed a condition monitoring system. In this system, the ground coils were monitored by the wireless sensors. In order to collect the sensor data efficiently, we used an opportunistic communication in which a communication device on a maintenance vehicle automatically collected the sensor data while moving. In addition, a wake-up receiver that controls the sleep state was embedded in the wireless sensor to save the power consumption and to avoid the communication interference. To confirm the communicational properties of the condition monitoring system in the maglev guideway, vehicle running tests were conducted on Miyazaki Maglev Test Line. When the on-board communication device transmitted the wake-up signal with an identifier every second at a vehicle speed of 41km/h, we succeeded in receiving the heat flow rate data of the selected ground coil without communication interference. Furthermore, to confirm the communicational properties of the condition monitoring system by means of the high-speed trains, demonstration tests were carried out on Kyushu Shinkansen Line. The wireless sensor was set on a station at which the train does not stop, and the on-board communication device was set inside the Shinkansen train. In the sleep state, the power consumption of the wireless sensor when it is waiting for the wake-up signal was 1.24 mW. When the wake-up signal was transmitted every 0.5 seconds from a Shinkansen train running at a speed of 248 km/h, we succeeded in receiving the 3-axis accelerations and the temperature data five times respectively. It is expected that the maintenance work becomes more efficient by using the condition monitoring system.

The outcomes of FR8RAIL project are shown in this paper, which are expected to positively contribute to and support the SHIFT2RAIL goals to strengthen the role of the freight rail transport.
