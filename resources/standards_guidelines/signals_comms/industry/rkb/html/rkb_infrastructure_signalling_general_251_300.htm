Title Broken rail detection without insulated rail joints: literature review
 
Author Sun, YQ
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No HH2
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2015-04
Physical Description 42p. ; PDF
Abstract Some 68 publications were sourced and reviewed. The review revealed that the most widely used and accepted method of broken rail detection was the use of track circuits, made available where electric signalling systems are used. Dating to its original introduction in the late 19th century, the basic concept of the track circuit has changed little. As the use of the signalling system for broken rail detection is a secondary use, it is not optimised for detection specifically, noting that the circuits must primarily detect the presence of trains. In the wider review, it was found that there are two approaches to managing broken rail detection: Reactive – that is detecting breaks when they occur (i.e. track circuits) or Pro-active – detecting cracks that will propagate into full breaks. Detection systems can also be grouped as vehicle based (on-board) and track based (wayside). In terms of vehicle based proactive detection methods, the most common proactive sensor is the traditional ultrasonic device that has been in use since the 1950s. The improvements in ultrasonic testing include using an array of dry coupled ultrasonic piezoelectric transducers deployed around the perimeter of the rail (excluding the underside) for full rail (head, web and foot) detection of flaws or defects. An induced eddy-current device to detect and quantify breaks and surface cracks in the rail head is also promising, and it is also a proactive device. In contrast to pulsed eddy current (PEC) sensors that are required to be placed at a close and constant distance from the inspected surface, a maximum operating lift-off of 5mm is possible with tolerable reduction in the signal when using alternating current field measurement (ACFM) probes. The ACFM technique is also capable of both detecting and sizing (length and depth) of cracks in rail top surfaces. In terms of track based detection methods, whether or not a specific method is identified as a proactive one is dependent upon the sensitivity of the method to detect smaller rail check cracks. An acoustical method exists for detecting broken rails utilising transducers mounted onto the side of the rail. This is a reactive concept that is similar in several ways to track circuit-based broken rail detection in that an acoustic signal is sent from transmitter to receiver. However, the new fibre optics technology is promising for track segment condition monitoring because its length can be tens of kilometres. This new method could be considered as proactive because the method can detect both cracked and broken rails. It also can be used to detect risks of track buckling as an alternative to stress monitoring. Possible directions for future research are discussed.

Title Addressing a shift in network priorities when deploying new train control technologies
 
Author Frost, J
Affiliation Palazzirail, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2021, 28 February-2 March 2022, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2022
Abstract Railways across Australia are in the process of introducing new train control and management technologies, which provide benefits including enhanced safety, reduced capital and operating costs, increased capacity, and others. Deployment of these systems across each relevant network requires careful planning that considers multiple factors, to ensure a manageable transition for the organisation and operators, whilst realising as many benefits as possible in the early stages. The anticipated drop in medium-term demand for passengers and some commodities, caused by the ongoing Covid pandemic in 2020, 2021 & now 2022, has caused many rail organisations to reconsider their priorities. This uncertainty impacts not only the agreed deployment sequence and timing for these systems across the respective networks, but in some cases the overall viability of network deployment may come into question. Several strategies are available when revisiting the system deployment in this new environment, including being clear on the breadth of benefits provided, understanding the new triggers for deployment, and closely engaging with stakeholders to ensure the revised plan closely aligns with the new organisational priorities. By revisiting the deployment plans for the new rail systems and adopting these strategies, these programs can continue to remain aligned with the new direction of the operating organisations and deliver valuable benefits to clients and customers.

Title Setting speeds for ATO: how many metros have thrown away potential operational performance
 
Author McGowan, G
Affiliation Arup, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2021, 28 February-2 March 2022, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2022
Abstract There is a close relationship between track alignment design and train control design, though frequently as there is with discipline interfaces, there isn’t always a full appreciation of the limits that each team designs to. Maximum speeds for track are governed by the horizontal and vertical geometry. These maximum “civil speeds” are dependent on meeting passenger ride comfort criteria, avoiding excessive lateral acceleration, or jerk, while balancing degradation of the asset and maintenance interventions. They are well below the speeds that might result in a derailment due to roll-over or flange climb of the high rail on curves, or the failure of track system lateral restraint. For the purposes of signalling and train control, these ride comfort-based track speeds can be adopted as the maximum that trains can safely operate at. On railways with ATO and ATP, there can be a number of speed profiles, including those that are identified as hard limits that cannot be exceeded. A suite of controls, each with their own margins, is then put in place through the signalling and train control systems to prevent train overspeeding, which in practice means imposing lower speed limits along with earlier braking and more conservative braking curves. The cumulative impact of these factors can be a reduction in the potential operating speed of railways that have ATO/ATP and a missed opportunity to provide the best journey times for customers.

Title A ‘Cascade Protection Scheme’ is the ideal approach to wayside signaling surge protection
 
Author Wen, L
Affiliation nVent ERICO
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2021, 28 February-2 March 2022, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2022
Abstract Damage from lightning and transient over voltages cost railways millions per year. Exposure to the forces of lightning is unavoidable in the rail environment. To protect (and mitigate damage to) critical network equipment, an approach whereby surge protection devices (SPDs) cascade across electrical and electronic components that work in conjunction to provide greater protection and coverage is recommended. This paper will introduce an enhanced protection scheme through a Cascaded Surge Protection (CSP) system, which includes discussion on surge protection device specifications, recommendations for SPD selection and information on wayside signaling applications. The primary goal of the ‘cascaded protection scheme’ is to reduce the voltage experienced at the equipment to be protected during a transient event, by reducing the voltage at each SPD stage. Robust electronic equipment, like motors and switches, can sustain energy behind a first stage of protection whereas more sensitive electronics, like signaling and measuring devices, should be placed behind a second or even third stage of surge protection.

Title Power resilience for signalling systems
 
Author Wimberley, J
Affiliation CommTel Network Solutions, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2021, 28 February-2 March 2022, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2022
Abstract Coloured light signalling systems rely on a suitable supply of electrical power to operate. For most signalling systems the electrical power is delivered by a local power authority and the reliability of the supply is dependent on the type of service and location of the site. Generally there is an on-site genset which supplies power when the mains supply is unavailable. However, there can be a period of time between the loss of the mains supply and the availability of power from the on-site genset. When there is no power to a signal, the signal is blank and is considered to be at ‘stop’. If a train cannot be stopped before the failed signal, it is considered to be a Signal Passed at Danger (SPAD) or a Restored in Front of Train (RIFOT) event. An Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) can be used to prevent the loss of power to the signalling system during the transition from mains supply to genset supply. This paper describes a UPS solution installed for a major Australian rail freight operator, Aurizon, which is successfully helping to eliminate delays to rail services caused by interruptions to electrical power.
