Title Support of operation, service and maintenance work through monitoring system construction
 
Author Arikado, K
Shinkai, J
Affiliation Kyushu Railway Co., Fukuoka, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The limited express trains of Kyushu Railway Company (JR Kyushu) have an information control system that provides information to operators and conductors, remotely controls air conditioning devices and service equipment, and collects data necessary for maintenance work. Limited express trains generally have a fixed composition, but with variations in passenger transportation demand, train coaches must be coupled and uncoupled year-round so that coach number matches passenger transportation demand. Once a train has returned to its depot, the time available for recoupling is limited, as this must happen when other work, such as next-run inspection, coach cleaning, and waste removal, is not in progress. The maintenance of electric circuits, including auxiliary high-voltage power sources, power sources for controllers and service equipment, and air conditioner circuits, was originally carried out via switching boards in each coach. In order to solve the problems associated with a tight working schedule, the authors attempted to automate this complicated maintenance using monitoring equipment, and successfully built a system with necessary functions. The method automatically determines the power feeding sections on the basis of the (1) instructions to the display converter requesting its content, (2) display of control signals and status of service equipment, (3) transmission of information on the content and timing of announcements sent to the automatic announcing equipment, and (4) supply capability of train power source and load conditions. The method has automated power inductive processing during power source equipment failures and brought flexibility and efficiency to train composition modification. The systems were used in the 787 and 883 lines, JR Kyushu's representative limited express trains, and are now bringing lower costs and increased efficiency in train operation.

Title Diagnostic and maintenance of signalling equipment
 
Author Ciampolini, M
Sanzani, P
Affiliation Sasib Railway S.p.A., Bologna, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract This paper describes the new diagnostic and maintenance support system designed for the Milano-Fiorenza station in Italy, which includes the most modem tools for the maintenance operator. Railway companies needs to increase their efficiency by improving the quality of service and by decreasing costs. Better quality could be achieved by increasing safety and punctuality of trains, lower costs by improving maintenability of equipment, by easing the duties of maintenance teams and by preventing possible causes of traffic perturbation. In addition, they have to reorganize maintenance teams in order to operate on the new generation of electronic signalling equipment. Maintenance is a cost but depending on how it is carried out the economic result could radically change. This is why railway companies are expressing great interest in the new diagnostic and maintenance techniques. The system is based on a computer network organized on two levels. The first level includes a local network using a peripheral computer to collect diagnostic information in the peripheral area. The second level connects all peripheral computers in the geographical area to centralise processed data in a supervision control centre which includes an advanced graphical user interface for the maintenance operator.The system manages all diagnostic information regarding signalling equipment (e.g., interlocking systems, signals, track circuits, switch blocks) and other safety- related signals available in field (e.g., video signal, fire detector, anti-theft devices, etc.).

Title Development of automated visibility inspection equipment for obstruction warning signal
 
Author Aida, M
Nagamine, N
Ukai, M
Nakasone, R
Katagiri, T
Oguchi, K
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
East Japan Railway Company, Tokyo, Japan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In a wayside signaling system, a train operation is performed by a driverâs visual observation of the signal aspects. Checking visibility of the signals is an important point in inspection of signal facilities. From this viewpoint, there is a problem that time and resources are required to check visibility of obstruction warning signals. The obstruction warning signal is a signal, whose aspects are normally turned off. It informs danger to the driver by a blinking red light when something goes wrong in a level crossing. This signal has to be recognized from a distance of 800m. Aspects are not observed when everything goes right. For checking the aspects for maintenance, Signal lights have to be turned on to observe the aspects, so that a large amount of time and human resources are required. In this paper, we propose a method to check the visibility of the obstruction warning signal automatically by using near infrared LEDs and image processing. We have conducted a long term monitoring test whose results show the system has high tolerance to daylight and seasonal variation, leading to stable operation of inspection. We have verified the proposed system had no critical interference between former wayside facilities and we have estimated that the signals will be fully operational for more than 10 years.

Title SCEPTICS: A Systematic Evaluation Process for Threats to Industrial Control Systems
 
Author Evans, R
Easton, J
Roberts, C
Affiliation Birmingham Centre for Railway Research and Education, School of Engineering, University of Birmingham, Birmingham B15 2TT, UK
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The rapid pace of development in Information and Communications Technology (ICT) over the last 30 years has changed the way the rail industry operates. Commercial pressures and the need to share operational information between stakeholders to facilitate cross-border services etc. have gradually pushed the industry away from more expensive, bespoke systems and towards Commercial Off The Shelf (COTS) solutions. Nowhere is this more evident than in the area of industrial control, where examples of the move to standard technologies include the European Train Control System (ETCS) in the signalling domain, and the provision of remote condition monitoring via Supervisory Control And Data Acquisition (SCADA) networks. Although the move away from bespoke systems has allowed the industry to become more agile, reduce the risks of vendor lock-in, and deliver âmore for lessâ in terms of underlying investment, it also risks increasing the attractiveness of the railways to cyber attackers; much of the off-the-shelf hardware is IP based, and therefore subject to many of the same attack mechanisms as any other modern ICT system. Furthermore, common platforms share common vulnerabilities, meaning exploits that have been realised in one industrial sector, such as the Stuxnet worm used to damage Iranâs nuclear centrifuges in 2010, could in theory now be used to attack PLCs used on the railways in the same way. While the rail industry in the UK and worldwide recognises that there will be an increased risk of cyber attack in coming years, many railway undertakings are unsure of how to begin building an understanding of the extent of the problem they face, or the steps required to address it. Traditional threat analysis techniques used in cyber security research frequently require large amounts of detailed information on specific systems to be gathered before they can be applied, and cyber security specialists speak a different language to rail industry ICT professionals making it difficult to prioritise available resources. This paper presents outcomes from the SCEPTICS project, an EPSRC funded initiative that is developing a set of common processes that can be applied by ICT professionals within the rail industry to scope their own industrial control systems, allowing them to get a broad understanding of the potential risks of cyber attack, and delivering sets of priority areas / systems to investigate using more detailed threat analysis tools and approaches.

Abstract The purpose of this study is to formalize the existing economic link between maintenance costs and the volume of infrastructure renewal. We want to clarify the tendencies, the influences and the existing economic equilibrium between maintenance and regeneration expenses. Mathematical and data-processing tools dedicated to the optimization of renewal cycles are developed within several studies conducted by the Infrastructure head office of the SNCF. Abstract Since time immemorial, signalling systems have made use of stiff, upright, highly robust and immovable signal posts for good practical engineering reasons. They should be able to withstand environmental conditions like rain, wind and snow without excessive deflection and still remain upright to support that vital signal arm or head with the associated aspect. Back in the good old days, signal engineers didnât even bother with the mechanical or civil engineer to help with the signal post or foundation design! More recently, the mechanical and civil engineers have got involved and we now have good foundations and strong posts that satisfy the various railwaysâ specifications. Times have also changed with regards to Operational Health and Safety (OH&S). Legislation hasnât always been so onerous and signalling personnel were routinely permitted to work two metres above the ground without a safety harness. It was nothing to expect a maintainer to climb a 12.5m tall lattice post to maintain or repair a piece of mechanical equipment such as pulleys or cranks or replace a light bulb which had expired. So in todayâs safety conscious environment and with the widespread introduction of 25kV overhead line equipment above the track, is it really desirable to have maintenance staff working at heights and in close proximity to lethal electrified equipment? Somewhat surprisingly, the answer to this question is âyesâ and the vast majority of new signals continue to be installed on conventional straight posts. Several alternative solutions are available which allow all maintenance activities to be undertaken at ground level using folding, hinged or sliding posts. These solutions also provide significant safety benefits yet the signalling industry is slow to embrace them. Is there genuine justification for this reluctance to change or is the signalling industry simply intolerant to change?

Title Maintaining designing signalling systems for RAM
 
Author Gifford, J
Affiliation Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC)
Source Institution of Railway Signal Engineers (IRSE)
Conference IRSE Australasia Technical Meeting: Sydney, 12 October 2012
Publication Date 2012
Abstract Most of you will be aware of the term Reliability Centred Maintenance (RCM). It is a standardised, defensible Maintenance Requirements Analysis process. The process originated in the military and aviation industries and is now accepted by, and applied across, many engineering organisations throughout the world for the development of system preventive maintenance requirements. The RCM process is derived from the application of Failure Modes, Effects and Criticality Analysis (FMECA) and recognises that preventive maintenance can only enable assets to achieve the inherent level of reliability designed and built into the equipment or system. Identification and selection of preventive maintenance tasks are based on: reliability characteristics of the equipment; operating environment of the equipment; and consequences of equipment failure. In the event no effective preventive maintenance task is identified to manage a particular failure mode then the alternatives are: run the equipment to failure; design out the failure mode; or continually monitor the equipment. Most modern day signalling and control system equipment have undergone Reliability Availability Maintainability and Safety (RAMS) analysis during the development phase. Usually this is a standalone process that does not look deeply into the interfaces, e.g. RAMS analysis for point drive equipment does not go deeply into the track interface, train axle loads, etc. I have observed maintainability, including occupational health and safety aspects of many the signalling systems, comprising a variety of equipment and interfaces that have not been adequately considered. Many opportunities for improvement in asset performance have been lost, largely through blind adherence to entrenched prescriptive standards, paradigms, beliefs and homage to the sacred cows. This paper will focus heavily in this area of opportunity and challenge engineers, designers, constructors and maintainers to question these paradigms, beliefs and sacred cows for the betterment of our railway industry and "keep the trains moving".
