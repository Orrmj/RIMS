Author Boss, JP
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1995: World Best Practice - The What, Why and How, Conference on Railway Engineering, 29 October-1 November 1995, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 1995
Abstract The theme of this conference is 'World Best Practice - The What, Why and How'. This paper focuses on the Why. Specifically, why is one system the 'world best practice'. Given that the nature of railways varies from country to country, indeed state to state, there must be some latitude in the definition of 'world best practice'. This paper proposes that there is a single aspect that can be used to determine if a system is the 'world best practice', that aspect being the degree to which the system reflects the core business of the railway. The Signalling Control Panel is used to demonstrate how development of panels can be viewed as a migration towards a more business oriented operation. The migration is then examined in other panels and a theory of Optimal Migration is developed which is applied to strategic planning and organisational structure.

Author Golda, M
Kumar, V
Affiliation AnsaldoSTS
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2012, Rail's Role in Society, 27-28 November 2012, Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2012
Abstract The role of signalling systems is to increase safety, availability and traffic efficiency of railway lines. These targets are the basis for the European Rail Traffic Management System (ERTMS) becoming the industry standard in most new lines and major upgrades worldwide, and for the adoption of Positive Train Control (PTC) in the USA. The new generation of signalling systems will be that which allows further simplifications of wayside infrastructure and higher performance; and will be tailored for the needs of all kinds of railway operators. To ensure cost-effective train localisation and protection over long stretches of semi-deserted areas (typically freight applications with full automation in regions such as Australia, and on low-traffic passenger lines) satellite-based localisation with SIL 4 accuracy combined with IP-based telecommunications networks based on well-consolidated signalling products and platforms, will be available to railway operators. These technology enhancements will result in a major reduction in investment in ground infrastructure and operational costs while maintaining current safety levels. The radio network plays a very important role and often tips the balance in the optimisation of system costs especially when a dedicated network has to be deployed and operated along a rural/remote area with challenging environmental conditions. New IP-based telecommunications networks which combine wireless, cellular and satellite technologies will provide more efficient, seamless connectivity by minimising the need of dedicated infrastructures. In order to optimise signalling infrastructure further, satellite train localisation also becomes a viable alternative by implementing the concept of the virtual balise (already anticipated in the European Train Control System (ETCS) platform) and as Absolute Position reference points in PTC platforms. Ansaldo STS is contracted to deliver leading-edge next-generation signalling solutions based on proven signalling products with satellite localisation and IP-based telecommunications networks for the Roy Hill rail netwrok in Australia. Ansaldo STS is also associated with new technologies in a Test Site located in Sardinia, Italy. This has the strong support of Rete Ferroviaria Italiana (RFI), which owns Italy s rail network and major satellite stakeholders such as the European Space Agency (ESA) and the GALILEO Supervisory Authority. This paper will give an overview of the role of satellite geolocalisation and telecommunications for signalling systems, outline the components of the Sardinia-based test site and explore the next frontier of improved traffic efficiency: moving block technology.

Title MPLS based networks in railway deployments
 
Author Oliver, A
Affiliation Calibre Global
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2014, Making Making Innovation Work, 11-12 November 2014, Perth, WA, Australia
Publication Date 2014
Abstract Safe and reliable communications are a critical component in operating modern railway networks. Voice radio, signalling, asset protection, CCTV, station services and alarms form the basis of a typical rail system, with telephony, data and internet connectivity between offices shaping corporate requirements. These services are traditionally provisioned through a combination of separate Synchronous Digital Hierarchy (SDH), Plesiochronous Digital Hierarchy (PDH), Ethernet and legacy analogue circuits, resulting in increased maintenance and support requirements. As more railways move towards Communications Based Signalling (CBS) systems bandwidth requirements increase, with a corresponding focus on reliability and resilience. Coupled with growing focus on Ethernet connectivity for hardware, an opportunity exists to explore alternate network arrangements that are more holistically encompassing of all supported technologies and their requirements. Implementation of an IP/MPLS (Multi-Protocol Label Switched) network allows convergence of these disparate networks into a single unified system. Key infrastructure can be shared between different facets of an organisation on a single physical network, whilst maintaining completely independent circuits for the various services being transported. In contrast to an IP-only implementation, where scalability and traffic engineering can be challenging as network size increases, an IP/MPLS solution allows a network to grow as needed, while providing excellent load balancing to make better use of existing network infrastructure. This in turn can lead to savings in both OPEX and CAPEX, as both infrastructure and maintenance costs are reduced. Ideally suited to both green and brown field rollouts, an IP/MPLS based network is a viable solution for any network rollout.

Title Multi-constellation railway SBAS interface: a common platform for advanced signalling compliant with SIL 4 world-wide
 
Author Filip, A
Affiliation University of Pardubice, Faculty of Electrical Eng. and Informatics, Czech Republic
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Satellite navigation represents a strategic infrastructure for advanced railway signalling due to its efficient train position determination - mainly on long heavy haul lines in difficult environments (high/low temperature, sands, etc.). In Europe, the ERTMS Memorandum of Understanding (2012) considers EGNOS and Galileo satellite systems as key components for a new generation of the European Rail Traffic Management System. EGNOS belongs to the family of wide-area Satellite Based Augmentation Systems (SBAS), similarly as US WAAS, Japanese MSAT/QZSS, Russian SDCM, etc. However, SBAS in general cannot be directly employed in signalling with high demands on the Signal-In-Space safety integrity (SIL 4) because it was designed and approved primarily according to aeronautical requirements which are very different from railway requirements. This paper describes a novel safety concept of train Location Determination System (LDS) based on the multi-constellation EGNOS-R interface that was designed for ERTMS. The application potential of the LDS safety concept (so-called the Triple SIL 4”) is outlined using a new innovative method for GNSS multipath error detection. The transferability of EGNOS-R in the form of more generic SBAS-R for efficient signalling is demonstrated by means of an example of the East Asia-Pacific region, where QZSS is available. Benefits resulting from the intended international SBAS-R standardization are outlined as well.

Abstract Track and signalling are perhaps the rnost obvious and the most essential fixed assets of any railway. This is reflected in the high proportion of operating revenue that is devoted to their maintenance and renewal. In 1982, British Rail spent 25% of its income on track and signalling. Both tracf and signalling. occupy the same physical part of the railway, and both are vital to the safe, efficient operation of the system. Both require a high quality of maintenance, the more so as technological development has led to increased sophistication, in order to maintain the standards which are necessary in a modern high speed railway. Yet, despite these similarities, historically the signalling engineer and the civil engineer have always gone their separate ways. This can be seen by permanent way staff as trespass on their domain, and signalling equipment gets treated vdth less than the respect it deserves. In the authors' experience, sometimes as many as 50% of failures of signalling devices (track circuits, point motors, and so on) can be attributable to civil engineering staff at work, and all too often this is because there is a lack of understanding of the signal engineer's problems and needs. This short paper outlines (but not from a Civil engineer's point of view) some of the major areas of conflicting requirements between the civil engineer and the signal engineer, and looks at the methods that are being used to solve or obviate them.

Affiliation Doc Frank Training and Consulting, Perth, WA

Publication Date 2018
Abstract Metro systems are the showcase for smart and efficient railways. They keep commuters mobile in over a hundred cities worldwide. The key enabling technology for smart operation of metro trains is Communications-Based Train Control, or short CBTC. This high-performance signalling technology is not only the de facto standard for metros around the globe. It is also the technology of choice for the Australian metro initiatives in Sydney, Melbourne and Perth. CBTC offers features almost unimaginable for conventional fixed-block signalling. This wealth of possibility can easily lead to an excessive wish list of seemingly indispensable functions, many of which bespoke to the parochial application of CBTC. This introduces a level of complication that can turn out to be detrimental for the expected benefits from this uber-technology of signalling. A number of challenges for high-capacity railways are actually better resolvable with advanced operational processes rather than technology overkill. The main takeaway is that smart railways can be really simple, unless one insists on making them complicated.

Title Best practises in wheel detection and axle counting: the evolution of level crossing control systems
 
Author Popplewell, N
Rendell, S
Affiliation Rail Control Systems Australia, Melbourne, Vic
Rail Planning Services, Sydney, NSW
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract When considering what is the standard active level crossing arrangement used in New South Wales (NSW), Australia, not a great deal has changed on the technology of the control system in a significant time. Early active systems used simple Direct Current (DC) track circuits for train detection with shelf-mounted relays performing the control of the flashing lights, barrier machines and bell. These systems have evolved to utilise miniature relays in lieu of the shelf mounted type, with installations still relying upon DC track circuits for train detection. The only recent evolution of these systems, is that many newer installations are solar powered to reduce installation costs. The next significant innovation was the introduction of track circuit based grade crossing predictor systems, where the train detection and control system were managed from within a single safety system. These systems utilise audio-frequency track circuits for train detection and speed prediction. It was not until the acceptance of the axle counter train detection systems that level crossings in regional locations could be unshackled from the risks associated with poor wheel to rail electrical contact. More recently there has been a systematic push to implement new technology that will retain a SIL4 safety reliability rating and enable a reduction in the installation and ongoing maintenance costs. A collaborative effort has taken place between the authors to integrate axle counter technology, the Frauscher FAdC, and a modern solid-state control system, the HIMA HIMatrix F35 safety controller. The result is an integrated level crossing control system. During October 2016, a trial system was commissioned just outside Bathurst in New South Wales, Australia. This site's equipment was completely isolated from the live railway, except for the axle counter system that counted every train that passed the location. This system takes advantage of the Frauscher Safe Ethernet (FSE) protocol that allows the HIMA control system to communicate directly with the Frauscher FAdC axle counting system's FSE communication module. The Ethernet communication and system architecture allows greater system functionality and more detailed logging to be achieved. The system is more tolerant to certain types of equipment failure, and intended or unintended interference from the likes of vandals and maintenance workers, respectively. This allows the normal operation of the level crossing warning equipment to continue uninterrupted in many circumstances. The system is also able to tolerate a local method of working where road/rail vehicles, also known as hi-rails, may mount the track at the level crossing and depart the site without be left disturbed, thus requiring a manual system reset. The integrated level crossing system developed can be programmed to recognise the movement and suppress the operation of the level crossing warning equipment whilst monitoring and logging the wheel counts within the system.
