Title Merging between telecommunications and signalling
 
Author Manoni, V
Fortuzzi, G
Affiliation Sasib Railway S.p.A., Bologna, Italy
Sasib Railway S.p.A., Verona, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The challenge facing us over the next few years involves two fundamental aspects: the optimization of performances and a reduction in running costs, while maintaining the same levels of safety. Information technology and telecommunications can offer effective economic and technical solutions. Train and wayside systems must be linked via a reliable, high speed, reconfigurable two-way communication systems. This communication system must be entirely integrated with the traditional railway signalling interlocking and ATS/ATO/ATC functions to guarantee maximum safety. To reduce the cost of these systems, it is necessary to set up a communications network, applying the same principles as those defined for public systems, and using standard commercial components wherever possible to enjoy the economies of scale denied to specialised products.

Title Experimental validation of computer-based signalling systems
 
Author Amendola, AM
Impagliazzo, L
Marmo, P
Poli, F
Affiliation Ansaldso-Cris, Napoli, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Several factors are pushing towards the enhancement of methodologies and techniques for the validation of computer-based railway signalling systems. These factors are related to the introduction of new technologies and equipment, the design of interoperable railway networks in Europe, the emphasis placed on safety by the new CENELEC standards, the new strong competition in the market of railway products. A variety of safety-critical architectures have been developed for railway control systems and are being installed more and more, replacing the traditional electromechanical equipment in both railway and metro signalling systems. The validation techniques that are adopted are based on different principles, specific of each architecture. Experimental validation would be however needed for all the architectures to measure the required performances and to give "experimental evidence" of the safety. But replicating the principles of the testing of relay equipment (characterized by well-established failure modes) has been considered for long time prohibitive for computer-based equipment due to the unpredictable failure modes of microprocessors. Most of the problems related with testing the effects of faults are nowadays addressed by the progress made in the field of fault injection techniques. The paper will first summarize the state of the art in the field of the experimental validation of high-dependable systems. Then it will describe the methodological framework and the validation environment LIVE (Low Intrusion Validation Environment), recently developed at Ansaldo T.rasporti - CRIS and presently used for validation of the new families of Computerbased Signalling Systems. Emphasis will be placed in the paper on the main features of LIVE that integrates fault injection and software testing techniques to achieve an accurate and non intrusive analysis of a system prototype, as requested by the new emerging standards.

Title ERTMS control/command: description of the system specification and advantages of the implementation applied to an existing system
 
Author Carganico, C
Bierlein, H
Dupre, J-L
Cesario, P
Affiliation EEIG ERTMS Users Group, Brussels, Belgium
SCIRO ELECTRA, Genova, Italia
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Various systems able to protect, control and in some cases command the run of the train according to the information received from the trackside system, are used in Europe. They can be used to protect the train only (ATP) or also to show continuosly to the driver the speed limits (ATC). The aim of ERTMS (European Railway Traffic Management System) Users Group is to specify, to test and to validate a new European Standard for the Train Control/Command systems based on standard technologies (EUROBALISE, EUROLOOP, EURORADIO and STMs) to allow train spacing, protection and control, with performances and safety targets better or at least the same one of the existing signalling systems. A modular and flexible implementation of ERTMS ControllCommand is necessary to allow the gradual improvement of the performance and the safety of the railway traffic without leaving the preceding investments. Starting from existing Protection, Control/Command or Signalling systems, new ERTMS modules could be added both on-board and/or trackside, to achieve, as a final result, the European Standard ERTMS Control/Command. The ERTMS specification covers the whole system, but the mandatory part regards only the fulfilment of operational interoperability within the European Railway network. Therefore, only the functions and the variables involved in the link between the on-board and trackside ERTMS devices and the functions necessary to provide the driver with standard ERTMS indication via the Man Machine Interface (MM!) have to be defined as interoperable and mandatory. Within the paper the description of the philosophy of the system to be implemented will be made, based on: the operational and technical modes of the on-board part ERTMS Control/Command; the interface language between the on-board and the trackside parts of ERTMS; the three possible levels of implementation: a level I based on a discontinuous transmission link, existing block systems and/or existing signalling systems, a level 2 based on a continuous radio link and existing block systems, a level 3 based on only a continuous radio link. The second part of the paper will be based on the description of the advantages of a gradual upgrade of an existing line equipped only with trackside signal, according to the ERTMS Control/Command.

Title Bidirectional track circuit: metro project
 
Author Carpanelli, M
Giovannucci, A
Hoelscher, J
Affiliation SASIB Railway SpA, Bologna, Italy
SASIB Railway GRS, Rochester, NY, USA
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract SASIB digital Track Circuit with bi-directional feature (DIGICODE) is part of a railway and metro block and traffic control system. It carries out two functions: the first one is the traditional train detection, the second one is a new data digital transmission between train and wayside in both directions. The data communication uses as its channel the rails, the connection cables and the track circuit equipment. When the train occupies a track circuit, the bi-directional channel joins two terminal points: one side the wayside interlocking system. e.g. SASIB ASCV or VPI electronic interlocking; the other side the train Cab signal. The channel capacity allows to combine together both vital block and speed regulation information, and auxiliary infhrmations. Usually the auxiliary informations rely on other systems and other data channels, e.g. balise or loop. In hi-directional track circuit continuos Cab signaling are integrated with the other informations. as speed line restriction, temporary lowering and train diagnostic, that in other system are spot communication. On the train the Cab equipment integrates both movement control functions, auxiliary fixed and variable functions-, and reverse transmission. Moreover the Cab equipment can be configured to decode existing rate coded Cab Signals and new digital Cab Signals. This simplifies the running of new lines together with preexistent ones, to the limited extent of locomotives equipped with the new Cab equipment. Wayside system is formed by electronic interlocking and Track Circuit modules. The latter ones can be concentrated near the former one and connected to the rails via copper cables. or they can be distributed along the track near the joints. Advantages and drawbacks are compared in the two solutions. The rails are continuos and the joints are electrical. The digital transmission from wayside to train is over several FSK modulated audio frequency carrier channels. The reverse digital transmission from train to wayside is audio frequency modulated carriers also. The coupling from Cab coach to the rails is magnetic all, gapped. In this research some reverse transmission techniques on the rails are described, showing advantages and drawbacks for each one. The system is proposed because of its simplicity and high integration level. Without adding more equipment a high capacity for tine tram traffic control is available. So the system is suited to high traffic condition, as for example, but not only in metro application. The system is based on and incorporates the traditional and proven safe techniques of train detection and rail integrity control by means of track circuit.

Title Compatibility of railway cab signalling by track circuits and spread spectrum modulations with variable frequency chopper drive interferences
 
Author Costamagna, E
Favalli, L
Gamba, P
Savazzi, P
Affiliation Università di Pavia, Pavia, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The paper is devoted to analyse the performances expected from a cab signal spread spectrum, frequency hopping system utilising the track circuit in presence of variabl frequency chopper propulsion drives. As known, similar drives have been found to prevent reliable operation of the customary narrow-band track circuits. Considering suitable chopper interference characteristics, in agreement with the data presented in the technical literature, a simulation scheme has been implemented to compare narrow-band fixed-frequency receivers and frequency hoppers, exploiting similar instantaneous bandwidths but periodically changing the carrier frequency. Digital BPSK modulations at a very low bit rate of 5 bit's have been used, with receiver bandwidths comparable to traditional track circuits. It is shown that the performances of the fixed frequency and of the hopping transmissions are comparable, at least in the lower part of the bandwidth allowed by the electrical characteristic of the track circuits, and that frequency hopping at these frequencies cannot help to remove restrictions to chopper operations in sweep mode.

Title The relative performance benefits of fixed and moving block
 
Author Holgate, D
Lawrence, P
Affiliation BR Research, Derby, Great Britain
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The overall performance of a railway network is usually measured in terms of two key parameters: train speed, and line capacity. This paper examines how the design of the signalling system affects the relationship between these two parameters. This provides an insight into the relative performance benefits of fixed and moving block signalling systems. The approach is based on simple theoretical models, and the inferences drawn from these models are supported by results obtained from simulations using the VISION® computer based simulation tool. The reasons for the relatively poor performance of three aspect fixed block signalling are explained in terms of a substantial loss of capacity which occurs when train speeds are well below those for which the signalling system is designed. The rapid improvement in performance as the number of aspects increases is demonstrated, and it is shown that with a six aspect system, fixed block signalling is almost as good as an ideal moving block system. It is concluded that, ultimately, the choice between moving block and fixed block is likely to be made on grounds of system philosophy, technology and costs, rather than inherent performance differences. Whichever system is chosen, simulation provides the only practical way of predicting the performance in detail.

Title A holistic view on the charm and challenge of CENELEC standards for railway signaling
 
Author Ciancabilla, A
Griebel, S
Affiliation Rete Ferroviaria Italiana, Bologna, Italy
Siemens AG, Braunschweig, Germany
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The core aim of this paper is to sketch the profound challenges that CENELEC standardization in railway signaling is faced with and to demonstrate the strategy and the fields in which CENELEC SC9XA will provide valuable input to the railway signaling community. On the one hand, an ever diverse homologation environment is to be found in which the various stakeholders are to manifest their interests, whether national safety authorities, the European Railway Agency, European Railway Industry and Railway Duty Holders Associations or the European and national standardization organizations. This paper will provide an overview of the current state of the art of managing these interests as well as hints at particular aspects. On the other hand, the CENELEC standardization community finds itself burdened with the task of keeping pace with the above-mentioned state of the art in railway signaling. Hence, incorporating new developments that have already been established as well as enabling future innovation by forging non-discriminatory goal-oriented standards is vital to this business of drafting standards. Especially IT security requirements are rapidly growing in various branches of industry and are thus questioning current concepts and demanding answers for yet unsolved challenges. On an international level, a variety of different standards and regulations on IT security already exist, such as for example the so-called Common Criteria or the international standard IEC 62443/ISA99 [8]. With this in mind, this paper will establish and elaborate on how the international standards and terminology about IT security could be adopted for the existing system of railway application standards in CENELEC for communication, signaling and processing systems (EN 50129 [4], EN 50159 [5]). As for safety issues, one of the main outcomes of the safety standards (EN 50126 [3], EN 50129 [4]) is the concept of safety integrity and the measure of the safety integrity level (SIL). Many misunderstandings arise from the fact that these concepts are shared by different standards with various interpretations. This paper will give a simplified overview of the communalities and differences among different standards. As the CENELEC Committee for Railway Signaling is part of the greater community and network of railway standardization in general, be it European or international, this paper concludes with identifying and emphasizing new links that have to be established in order to further the interests of railways in the decades to come.

Abstract Accidents have been the main driver of innovation in the historical development of signalling products. Major milestone advances in technology have also had an effect. Although accidents are still a driver, there are other drivers too:
- Improving operational efficiency and flexibility
- Improving maintenance operations and reducing costs
- Improving reliability and availability of operation
- Overcoming latent safety issues
- Improving project delivery times and reducing costs
Traditionally, most signalling engineers are very cautious and are not known for accepting new and updated products. Rail Authorities do not like to be the first to put a new signalling product into operation. They much prefer to let it be tried and tested by other similar Rail Authorities so that any bugs can be ironed out well before they consider using it. Rail Authorities usually specify that signalling products are required to operate safely and reliably for very long periods of service (eg > 25 years). To introduce innovative signalling products into operation requires the product to be type approved, which involves considerable time, effort and cost. The more the product has safety implications, the more time, effort and cost can become excessive. Suppliers usually need to build a significant business case to pursue a particular innovative development. Innovation has tended to advance more in non-vital products (eg control systems), where the type approval process is less demanding and has allowed these products to keep abreast with the faster new technology advances. Whereas innovation in safety products, such as interlockings, trackside equipment and trainborne equipment, has been slow to change with technology advances, compared with other safety industries. When a new product is brought into operation, there can be unforeseen operational problems which occur and further development is required to rectify these problems. Abstract Some parts of the European high speed line network are near saturation. ETCS-L2, the so called Level 2 of the European train control system, standard for providing signalling and automatic train protection (ATP) on new high speed lines (HSL), offers a pretty good level of capacity. However, even with the mobile block system provided by ETCS-Level 3, it seems quite impossible for the practical capacity to reach 20 trains per hour per HSL track as soon as maximal speed is at 300 km/h or higher. At very high speed, headway between trains is mainly determined by the braking performance of the trains. The paper presents a new running mode using relative braking distances on one hand, and keeping absolute emergency distances in any case on the other hand. The devised running mode is here described within the classical ETCS_L2 context, where signalling still uses track circuits or axle counters. This secure mode of operation could offer a practical capacity more than 20% higher than the today ETCS_L2 Full Supervision mode.
