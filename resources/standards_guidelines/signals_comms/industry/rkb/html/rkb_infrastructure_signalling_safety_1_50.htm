Title Computer-supported requirements analysis for the design of safety-critical systems in railways
 
Author Tsuchiya, R
Arai, H
Uehan, Y
Fukuda, M
Hirao, Y
Nozue, N
Affiliation Railway Technical Research lnstitute, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract This paper describes a systematic approach to the requirements analysis for safety critical systems in which the potential loss is very serious. Formal methods, which are considered promising tool for safety verification, may not be sufficient for ensuring safety of a system which is likely to undergo rapid changes in operating environment. It should also be pointed out that not only safety but also such factors as prospective travelers' benefits, implementational costs and environmental considerations must be taken into account. These complicated requirements must be integrated appropriately into a consistent system architecture. This paper is focused on clarifying some issues inherent in requirements analysis for safety-critical systems and on indicating the importance of environment model, traceability, and well-grounded decisions. We believe these notions greatly contribute to the accountability and adaptability ofsafety-critical system specifications. Also included is the case study of the design of a level-crossing control system, a typical safety-critical system in railways.

Abstract Across the UK Rail infrastructure, a number of alterative approaches to the development of VDU based signalling control systems (VSCS), are being adopted by different companies in order to meet the demands of improved railway safety and performance. The systems incorporate different signaller - system interaction design models; interface functionalities; alarms systems and schematic layout conventions. While Railway Group and Network Rail Company standards largely address the technical and functional requirements to ensure compatibility across the UK network and between its systems, they have historically left important HMI safety critical areas (e.g. alarms, aspects of visual coding) open to designer interpretation. Title Developing a safety case for signalling systems which employ safety critical software
 
Author Aderson, P
Catmur, JR
Affiliation Hong Kong Mass Transit Railway Corporation
Arthur D. Little, Madrid, Spain
Conference CORE 1993: Contracting Railways - Safety, Standards and the Surroundings, 17-20 October 1993, Newcastle, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 1993
Abstract In 1989, Hong Kong Mass Transit Railway Corporation (MTRC) determined the need to replace its existing Automatic Train Control (ATC) System. When contemplating such a major change to a primary safety system, MTRC decided that traditional methods of safety and compatibility assessment, typically theoretical study followed by trials, were insufficient to satisfy the latest safety standards now being applied in non-railway industries. Awareness of these new safety standards and assessment techniques, coupled with the need to maintain the current high degree of safety during the period of change, resulted in the development of an evaluation methodology and procedure termed the 'Safety Case'. The Safety Case is in effect a structured plan which requires evidence from the supplier to support the 'proof of safety' for their system. This is followed by a study of the system in its proposed application. Studies are divided into pre-tender and the post-contact activities. The pre-tender phase includes six main studies: Safety Audit; Safety Analysis; Adaptation Study; Reliability and Maintenance Study; Operational Impact Study and the Performance Study. The post contract award process follows the more traditional route of contractor submissions and approval by MTRC's engineering staff. This paper reviews the methodology and approach adopted by MTRC when faced with the prospect of evaluating a number of possible replacement ATC systems for the existing operational railway.

Author Naweed, A
Bowditch, L
Chapman, J
Balfe, N
Dorrian, J
Affiliation Appleton Institute for Behavioural Science, Central Queensland University, Adelaide, Australia
National Centre for Education and Training on Addiction (NCETA), Flinders University, Adelaide, Australia
Centre for Innovative Human Systems, Trinity College Dublin, Ireland
Behaviour-Brain-Body Research Centre (BBB), University of South Australia, Adelaide, Australia
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Despite the focus on signal passed at danger (SPAD) incidents as a product of interactions in complex rail systems, few studies have identified salient precursors that could lead to SPADs. This descriptive study undertook an exploratory analysis of data collected from an international survey (Australia & New Zealand) to derive preliminary insight into the extent to which different systems factors negatively affect rail driving from a driver perspective, and how these factors compare between SPAD history and type of rail environment (freight; passenger). Rail drivers (N = 751) completed an online survey and rated the extent to which various precursors from the RSSB 10-Incident factor scheme negatively impacted their driving. Five groupings were selected for exploratory analysis (verbal communications; fatigue, health and wellbeing; infrastructure, vehicles, equipment, and clothing; workload and resourcing; teamworking and leadership). ANCOVA analyses were undertaken on SPAD history (controlling for years of driving experience) and ANOVA analyses were undertaken for rail environment. Significant differences between passenger and freight rail environments were found within all five categories. Significant differences between drivers with SPAD history were also found for factors related to fatigue, health and wellbeing, as well as infrastructure, vehicles, equipment, and clothing. Focusing on SPAD prevention addresses concerns linked with the rising complexities of rail, intensification of services, and increased automation. The findings of this exploratory study, while preliminary, hint at the beginnings of a systems and cognitive model to predict SPAD-risk. Further research and analyses of these data will go some way towards filling the research gap on SPAD precursors.

Title The two-train separation problem on non-level track: driving strategies that minimize total required tractive energy subject to prescribed section clearance times
 
Author Albrecht, A
Howlett, P
Pudney, P
Vu, X
Zhou, P
Affiliation University of South Australia, Mawson Lakes, Australia
Source ATRI
Journal Transportation Research Part B: Methodological
Publication Date 2018-05
Abstract When two trains travel along the same track in the same direction it is a common safety requirement with three-aspect signalling that the trains must be separated by at least two signals if they wish to continue following the planned schedule. Under these separation conditions there will always be at least one clear section of track between the two trains. When these conditions are violated the following train must adopt a revised strategy that will enable it to stop at the next signal if required. In this paper the authors find necessary conditions on non-level track to minimize the total tractive energy required for both trains to complete their respective journeys within the allowed time subject to safety-compatible separation constraints in the form of a prescribed set of latest allowed section exit times for the leading train and a corresponding prescribed set of earliest allowed section entry times for the following train. The authors use classical methods of constrained optimization to show that the optimal driving strategy for each train is defined by a unique optimal driving speed on each section and that the sequence of optimal driving speeds is a decreasing sequence for the leading train and an increasing sequence for the following train. The authors illustrate their results by finding optimal strategies and associated speed profiles for both the leading train and the following train in some elementary but realistic examples.

Title Integrating dynamic signaling commands under fixed-block signaling systems into train dispatching optimization problems
 
Author Luan, X
Schutter, B
Corman, F
Lodewijks, G
Affiliation Section Transport Engineering and Logistics, Delft University of Technology, Delft, The Netherlands
Delft Center for Systems and Control, Delft University of Technology, Delft, The Netherlands
School of Aviation, Faculty of Science, University of New South Wales, Sydney, Australia
Institute for Transport Planning and Systems (IVT), ETH Zürich, Zürich, Switzerland
Source ATRI
Journal Transportation Research Record
Publication Date 2018-09
Abstract In railway operations, when a disruption occurs, train dispatchers aim to adjust the affected schedule and to minimize negative consequences during and after the disruption. As one of the most important components of the railway system, railway signals are used to guarantee the safety of train services. We study the train dispatching problem with consideration of railway signaling commands under the fixed-block signaling system. In such a system, signaling commands dynamically depend on the movement of the preceding trains in the network. We clarify the impact of the signaling commands on train schedules, which has so far been neglected in the literature on railway train dispatching, and we propose an innovative set of signaling constraints to describe the impact. The determination of the signal indicators is presented using “if-then” constraints, which are further transformed into linear inequalities by applying two transformation properties. Activation of the train speed limits that result from the signaling commands is the core purpose of the signaling constraints, and this is implemented by using the signal indicators. Moreover, we formulate the Greenwave (GW) policy, which requires that trains always proceed under green signals, and we further investigate the impact of the GW policy on delays. In numerical experiments, the proposed signaling constraints are employed within a time-instant optimization problem, which is a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) problem. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed signaling constraints and show the impact of the signaling commands and GW policy on the train dispatching solution.

Abstract Damage from lightning and transient over voltages cost railways millions per year. Exposure to the forces of lightning is unavoidable in the rail environment. To protect (and mitigate damage to) critical network equipment, an approach whereby surge protection devices (SPDs) cascade across electrical and electronic components that work in conjunction to provide greater protection and coverage is recommended. This paper will introduce an enhanced protection scheme through a Cascaded Surge Protection (CSP) system, which includes discussion on surge protection device specifications, recommendations for SPD selection and information on wayside signaling applications. The primary goal of the ‘cascaded protection scheme’ is to reduce the voltage experienced at the equipment to be protected during a transient event, by reducing the voltage at each SPD stage. Robust electronic equipment, like motors and switches, can sustain energy behind a first stage of protection whereas more sensitive electronics, like signaling and measuring devices, should be placed behind a second or even third stage of surge protection.

Title Power resilience for signalling systems
 
Author Wimberley, J
Affiliation CommTel Network Solutions, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2021, 28 February-2 March 2022, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2022
Abstract Coloured light signalling systems rely on a suitable supply of electrical power to operate. For most signalling systems the electrical power is delivered by a local power authority and the reliability of the supply is dependent on the type of service and location of the site. Generally there is an on-site genset which supplies power when the mains supply is unavailable. However, there can be a period of time between the loss of the mains supply and the availability of power from the on-site genset. When there is no power to a signal, the signal is blank and is considered to be at ‘stop’. If a train cannot be stopped before the failed signal, it is considered to be a Signal Passed at Danger (SPAD) or a Restored in Front of Train (RIFOT) event. An Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) can be used to prevent the loss of power to the signalling system during the transition from mains supply to genset supply. This paper describes a UPS solution installed for a major Australian rail freight operator, Aurizon, which is successfully helping to eliminate delays to rail services caused by interruptions to electrical power.
