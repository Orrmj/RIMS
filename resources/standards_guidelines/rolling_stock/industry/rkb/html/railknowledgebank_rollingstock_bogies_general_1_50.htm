Abstract Components behaviour under alternate loads combined with repeated relative surface motion is one of the open problems in the area of fatigue. The combination of the effects of abrasive and adhesive wear, caused by relative surface sliding and resulting in surface damage, and fatigue, caused by alternated loads in presence of high local stress intensity factors (i.e surface degradation by wear), is called âfretting fatigueâ. A presence of oxidation phenomenon can also be part of the fretting fatigue process. In the case of the railway wheelsets designed after 2003, there are no records of failures at press-fits because the design rules are based on shape of the transitions (D/d = 1.2) that make the axle transition the most stressed point. However, it is important to analyze the acceptability of defects and microcracks at the press-fits in order to correctly interpret fatigue strength considering that micro-scars and micro-cracks are some times detected after fatigue tests for D/d = 1.2. In this paper we analyse the full-scale test results obtained on EA4T axles within the Euraxles project on the basis of detailed FE analyses and a new criterion for prediction non-propagation of cracks under RCF conditions. Predictions are compared with full-scale test experiments on axles with artificial micronotches at the press-fit.

Title Fault diagnosis in railway vehicle suspensions
 
Author Rossouw, CCE
Frohling, RD
Nel, AL
Affiliation Transnet Freight Rail Mechanical Technology, Pretoria, South Africa
University of Johannesburg, Johannesburg, South Africa
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Railway vehicle suspension components deteriorate and may fail during normal in-service operation. To ensure safe vehicle operation and acceptable dynamic performance, reliable and efficient condition-monitoring, fault-diagnosis and performance evaluation techniques are required. Successful suspension condition assessment techniques could prevent or reduce sequential damage to the vehicle suspension and related subsystems by timely reporting of required maintenance interventions, while maintaining acceptable operational safety. In the field of structural health monitoring, damage/ fault detection, specifically the identification of damage presence, location and estimation of severity, can be approached using either a data-driven or a physics-model-driven methodology. In this paper, the feasibility of on-board (datadriven) vibration analysis-based fault diagnosis of the self-steering three-piece MkV bogie is investigated. Sequential faults of the primary and secondary suspension are used as a case study. Fault characterisation is done by comparison of the acceleration response characteristics of the undamaged and damaged secondary suspensions using a non-parametric, statistical time-series-based method. The method makes use of changes in the vibration signal spectrum. A verified dynamic simulation model is used to generate vehicle suspension acceleration response data for the undamaged (healthy) and damaged states. With this method it is possible to detect the presence of damage of the primary, secondary and stabiliser springs but no differentiation between damage severities is possible. Due to the response dominating influence of changing vehicle speed (excitation) on damage detection efficacy, the fault detection methodology in its current form is deemed theoretically possible but impractical for the heavy haul industry.

Abstract In order to decrease the lateral force generated in the railway vehicles in the curving sections, a simple steering system for the bolsterless bogie was developed. This steering bogie system does not adopt complex bogie structure, and considered to be equipped with the function which prevents reverse steering movement. The most important feature of the developed system is that the system controls the steering force by the relative mechanical movement between the bogie and the car body that passes curving sections. This system is not based on the electrical control; the authors have designated this system as a âmechanical pneumatic steering systemâ. This system is composed of pneumatic actuators that are built into mono-links, a mechanical sensing device of the bogie angle made by the relative movement between the bogie and the car body, and a pneumatic valve that works according to the sensing device. The sensing device for mechanical detection of the bogie angle is composed of two universal joints and a connecting rod that is expandable and contractile, but not revolvable. This device is able to transfer only the relative yawing angle between the car body and the bogie, and the other movement can be canceled by the motion of joints and the connecting rod. The mechanical pneumatic steering system with such features has been applied to a test bogie, and fundamental running tests were carried out on the test line in Railway Technical Research Institute. As a result, it is found that the average lateral force in a circular curve section with a radius of 160(m) can be decreased by about 80% compared with the case where the steering control is not applied. The other side, the basic safety against the reverse steering mode which causes the controller failing was confirmed. In addition, we confirmed the reducing effect of the flange squeal noise that arises in a tight curving section by this running test. The other advantages of the mechanical pneumatic steering system have been confirmed by the numerical analysis based on multi-body dynamics (MBD) software SIMPACK. The authors carried out the performance evaluation of energy consumption reduction by estimating the difference in vehicle's kinetic energy consumed when the vehicle passing in a tight curving section. In the same way that was applied to the MBD simulation, the progress situation of the wheel wear was reproduced. The results show that the wheel flange contact is avoided by the effect of steering control in spite of a circular curve section, and the amount of the wheel wear that arose near the flange is reduced.

Abstract Vertical split rim cracks (VSRs) are the most common cause for the removal of broken or cracked heavy haul car wheels in North America. This failure mode is characterized by rapid unstable crack propagation in the hoop or tangential direction, and it predominantly affects the field side of the rim, except in rare instances when the flange side is involved. In order to investigate the ways to prevent VSRs, an understanding of how the service loads correlate to their occurrence is necessary. First, in this study, FE analyses were conducted to evaluate the rim stresses below the tread surface during rolling contact between the wheel tread and rail. Then, Charpy impact tests were conducted to obtain the variation in the fracture toughness of the wheel steel. FE analysis results showed that axial residual tensile stress, which is a stress normal to the rim crack, increases at deeper positions below the tread surface due to cyclic rolling contact. These results indicate that the crack can propagate rapidly below the tread surface, making VSR likely to occur. The Charpy impact test results showed that a Weibull distribution enables an approximate relationship between fracture probability and fracture toughness of the wheel steel to be obtained. Next, the stress intensity factor (SIF) was calculated using the stress distribution in the wheel rim obtained by FE analyses, and the VSR rates of AAR Class-C wheels were estimated by fracture mechanics. In order to evaluate effect of fracture toughness of wheel steels on the differences in VSR crack probability, a comparison between high fracture toughness wheels and conventional Class-C wheels was made. VSR rates could be predicted from SIF and the Weibull equation for the fracture toughness. The prediction results showed that VSR rates of high fracture toughness wheels are estimated to be only 5% of that of the conventional Class-C wheel. Therefore, high fracture toughness steel wheels are believed to have higher resistance to VSR cracks.
