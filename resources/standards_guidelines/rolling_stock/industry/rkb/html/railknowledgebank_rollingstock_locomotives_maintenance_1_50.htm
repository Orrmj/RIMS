Title Using augmented reality for locomotive repair
 
Author Swenson, SA
Affiliation Progress Rail, A Caterpillar Company, La Grange, IL, USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Augmented Reality (AR), a technology that superimposes a computer-generated image on a userâs view of the real world, is moving beyond games and entertainment and is becoming an industrial application used to reduce life cycle costs. In the locomotive business, AR technology for handheld and wearable devices is being used to revolutionize design, troubleshooting, repair, and maintenance processes. When the product is in service, AR applications are being used to visualize, troubleshoot, and repair assets by bringing all system information into a single view. This reduces the time required to find information and perform tasks, and it empowers users to locate parts and wires quickly without the use of paper schematics and diagrams. Precise guidance can be provided to show users where to physically move next in any troubleshooting or repair process. AR technology becomes life size by using Ultra Light Geometry (ULG) 3D models consisting of every single piece part and wire on a locomotive. As we move into the future of digital railways, technicians will adapt quickly to AR technology. For AR to continue to be very effective, designers and users need to work together to maintain living product configuration and maintenance specifications that are updated throughout the life cycle of the locomotive.

Title La verifica strutturale del rotabili ferroviari
 
Author Goracci, C
Bianco, V
Affiliation FS S. p. A., Firenze, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract This paper illustrates the modern techniques for sizing and testing the main structures of railway rolling stock with particular reference to the bogie and body. In the engineering stages, appropriate simulation programs are used, normally based on the finite elements method (FEM), that is generally applied in a linear elastic field. Later, on one the first cars, static load tests are carried out to ascertain the affinity between the results of the calculations and the tension values measured by means of strain gauges. This type of validation of the model also serves to increase confidence in a whole series of calculation results that are used to establish the weld inspection methods. The paper reports on the procedures that are normally used for FS orders and provides an example that gives an idea of the differences that may be acceptable in a case of optimized engineering. In particular, when performing a structural inspection, the possibility of a test containing, among other things, the arrangement of the loads as well as of constraints acting on the tested element is examined. In validating the solutions theorized for the application and distribution of loads and constraints, it is particularly important to ascertain the maximum congruence possible with the analogous hypotheses used in making the FEM calculations. Otherwise, there is no doubt that the results obtained, in terms of tension, can only partially converge, and in any case in an unsatisfactory manner. On the other hand, as regards the selection of the positioning of the strain gauges, it is essential to identify those points that have been shown to be subject to the greatest amount of stress and that are located in zones with relatively low tension gradients, or at least within areas having the same dimensions as those of the single-axis strain gauges that are normally used. The document that describes the test should contain the following elements: quoted position of the centers of the strain gauges, relative orientation, number of node and element of the corresponding calculation model, tension calculated on the position of the strain gauges and drawings illustrating the position of the strain gauges on the tested structures. Upon completion of the tests it should be possible to ascertain that the difference between the calculated values and the values measured remains within a set percentage.

Title A systemic approach for rolling stock maintenance by LCC, maintenance policies and supply requirements in the Italian railways (FS) experience
 
Author Fusarpoli, P
Diversi, D
Affiliation Ferrovie dello Stato S.p.A., Firenze, Italy
ASA Materiale Rotabile e Trazione, Firenze, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The strategic role of maintenance of durable goods, like railways rolling stock, increases day by day: from this point of view, maintenance can play a role of the same level than the one played by design. For the design phase, through RAMS (Reliability, Availability, Maintainability, Safety) requirements, FS complete the supply specifications with the settled experiences and the options for achieving fixed improvement targets. The first evaluation for the customer can be done by the parameter LCC (Life Cycle Cost). Starting from LCC specifications, suppliers can design rolling stock and, consequently, maintenance system. In the exploitation phase, one the most important aspects is organization and consolidation of logistic support for achieving the service targets; the choice straight depends on the maintenance policies applicable for the specific rolling stock. Achieving the hoped targets need an internal adjustment in the maintenance sector, that must be complete, both structural and cultural. Evaluation of the most suitable maintenance policy for a specific rolling stock will be the most correct if relevant technical problems will be completely known and controlled; therefore monitoring activities are fundamental to perform a continuous improvement process. The above mentioned things well comply with what FS have utilized for purchasing the new EMU's ETR460, ETR470 and ETR500 and the electric locomotives E402 and E412. But in a period in which maintenance must become more and more competitive, FS must succeed in redesigning, even in a partial way, the maintenance process of some rolling stocks of previous generations. The studies performed in optimizing maintenance for new rolling stock will be used, where applicable, also for locomotives and vehicles for which the improvement of maintenance targets still count and can be achieved.

Title Experiences with condition monitoring of traction reactors
 
Author Flinders, F
Mathew, R
Zhang, J
Oghanna, W
Stock, D
Tegg, N
Mcleod, T
Stevens, V
Affiliation Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Qld
Queensland Rail, Rockhampton, Qld
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract It is a normal practice to incorporate smoothing reactors in series with the DC motors in thyristor rectifier traction drives. In the case of Queensland Rail's heavy haul locomotives they are housed with the main transformer in a forced oil cooled tank. The reactor construction consists of layers of strip wound coils separated by insulation spacers. This allows the cooling oil to flow between the layers. Due to various reasons these spacers eventually become dislodged allowing the layers to collapse together. The outcome of this is abrasion due to electromagnetically induced vibration and eventual failure of the nomex insulation material. From this time arcing between turns commences. This slowly erodes the copper strip depositing small copper beads into the tank. These copper beads are carried by oil flow into the main transformer resulting in an expensive failure. A non-invasive technique that identifies collapsed reactors before any electrical arcing, has been developed. It utilises frequency response analysis to identify geometrical changes in the winding structure. The total fleet of eighty-six locomotives have been screened. Nine were identified as having defective reactors and have been repaired. The condition monitoring procedure was quicker and much less expensive than actual inspection. The aim of the paper is to present the technical aspects of the reactor condition monitoring program as well as discuss its economic benefits. This testing program has been an excellent example of cooperation between a research centre and a heavy haul railroad to solve a potentially costly problem quickly and efficiently.

Publication Date 2016
Abstract The railway market constantly requires sensors and systems for the monitoring of potential fault conditions that may occur during the normal operations of the systems, hence the issues related to safety and predictive diagnostics become of utmost importance. Traditional wiring systems for on board monitoring imply high installation and maintenance costs as well as increasing train system complexity. This work aims to implement an onboard temperature monitoring system based on wireless sensors providing real-time warnings in case of temperatures over the working bearings conditions: to the best of our knowledge, such kind of systems are not currently available on the market. The sensors form a wireless network up to a main central router which can collect all the data of the train. A detailed propagation study has been performed in order to define the optimum frequency transmission, the most suitable sensor placements and the related antenna design. A full prototype has been built and successfully tested, proving the feasibility of the proposed idea and thus opening the way to industrialization.

Title Repowering of old locomotives
 
Author Buckley, N
Affiliation Rail Industry Service Providers
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract The average age of the locomotive fleet operating in Australia is in excess of 30 years. Improving the reliability, availability and productivity of older locomotives is a challenge for many rail operators. Replacing old locomotives with modern, state-of-the-art units, whilst appealing, may not be economically viable because the returns that these locomotives can generate in many cases are insufficient to provide a reasonable payback on the huge capital outlay. Overhauling the locomotives so that they are brought back to an original specification condition may address reliability issues but the process will not capitalise on productivity advances in fuel efficiency, environmental emissions and tractive effort that have been made over the years. Repowering of locomotives, where the engine, alternator and traction control equipment is replaced with a new upgraded system provides an alternative, cost-effective option for many locomotive applications in Australia.

Title Machine vision using multi-spectral imaging for undercarriage inspection of railroad equipment
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Hart, JM
Resendiz, E
Freid, B
Sawadisavi, S
Barkan, CPL
Ahuja, N
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, USA
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Locomotive and rolling stock condition is an important element of railway safety, reliability, and service quality. Traditionally, railroads have monitored equipment condition by conducting regular inspections. Over the past several decades, certain inspection tasks have been automated using technologies that have reduced the cost and increased the effectiveness of the inspection. However, the inspection of most aspects of railroad equipment undercarriages is conducted manually. This is a labor-intensive process and often requires that railroad equipment be taken out of service so that the inspection can be conducted on specially equipped pit tracks. Machine vision technology offers the potential to quickly and automatically monitor, assess, record, and transmit detailed information regarding the condition of railroad equipment undercarriages and components. Multi-spectral imaging (e.g. visible and infrared range) allows recording of both physical and thermal condition and correlation between the two. This allows precise comparison of any undercarriage component or element of interest using templates, and/or references to previously recorded inspections, of the same or similar equipment and enables other analyses such as trending of component wear and detection of progressive increases in component heating over time. Multi-spectrum machine vision algorithms can determine if a component is outside its normal operating range and if anomalies are correlated across different spectra. The inspection tasks of particular interest for this investigation include disc brake condition, bearing performance, and detection of incipient failure of electrical systems such as locomotive traction motors and air conditioning units. We are also investigating detection of damaged or missing components and foreign objects.

Title Wheel and axle defect detecting technique by using phased array ultrasonic
 
Author Peng, C
Gao, X
Zhang, Y
Peng, J
Yang,K
Affiliation Non-destructive Testing Research Center, Physics Science & Technology School, Southwest Jiaotong University, Chengdu, China
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference 10th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2013), Capacity Building through Heavy Haul Operation, 4th-6th February, 2013, New Delhi, India.
Publication Date 2013
Abstract The quality of wheel and axle is especially important for the safety of locomotive and vehicle. A technique and system for wheel and solid axle defect detection on disassembled wheelset by using phased array ultrasonic method is described in the paper. Advanced phased array ultrasonic technology, ultrasonic transmitting and receiving principle, method for wheel and solid axle defect detection is explained in detail. Special probe arrangement on wheel tread and inner side of wheel rim, end and neck of solid axle is shown. With this method, different types of defects on wheel flange, wheel rim and wheel disk, and transverse crack defects on solid axle can be detected by one system at the same time. The composition, structure, working process of the system is given. The results of ultrasonic detection are displayed in A- scan and B-scan for operator analyzing. With the described technique and system, one wheelset with two wheels and solid axle can be automatically inspected within five minutes and results are well recorded and reported.

Title Refurbishment of class 11E locomotives for Spoornet
 
Author Fornander, P
Affiliation Bombardier Transportation, Sweden AB
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2007: High Tech in Heavy Haul, 11-13 June, 2007, Kiruna, Sweden
Publication Date 2007
Abstract Bombardier Transportation was, awarded a contract to refurbish 45 heavy haul electric locomotives for Spoornet, (South Africa) in October 2000. The objective was to increase the fleet availability from 80% to 90%, reliability from 55 to 25 faults per Million km and reduce the maintenance hours by 1/3. To achieve the Spoornet goals, an investigation was done by Bombardier in 1996. Based on this investigation a complete overhaul and modernisation of the 11E locomotive fleet was offered to Spoornet. The installed new computer control and diagnostic systems and software tools provide to the drivers and maintenance personnel an excellent support to make the operation and maintenance more efficient. The new computer control also improved the load sharing between the six traction motors which gave an extra 5% tractive effort per locomotive. The Bombardier availability target 90% and reliability target 25 faults per million km was reached mid 2006.

Title Mechanization of wagon cleaning process on Vitoria-Minas railroad
 
Author de Oliveira, BS
Affiliation Vale S/A, SÃ£o LuÃ­s, Brazil
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Gondola type wagons are loaded with iron ore in mine to port direction and can carry other products, like coal, in the reverse direction. Due to humidity and vibration the compaction of the iron ore is common. After the wagons pass through the dumper some residual iron ore will still be trapped in the bottom or on the sides of the wagons. These residues were removed by hand where humans entered inside the wagons and removed this material using picks and shovels. This was a slow and unhealthy operation, and at high cost, given that it was performed by a third part contractor. In order to reduce costs, eliminate manual removal and improve working conditions, equipment was developed to perform mechanized cleaning of wagons. In a pioneering project, Vale and the equipment supplier developed a unique machine in the world to run this type of activity. One year after the implementation of equipment, the manual activity was eliminated, the equipment production meets (and even surpasses) the need, all materials (iron ore) which is taken from the wagons is reused, working conditions and health were improved and costs were reduced.
