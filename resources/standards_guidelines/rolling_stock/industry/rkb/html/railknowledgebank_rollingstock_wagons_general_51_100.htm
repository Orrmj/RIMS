Title Running resistance of ore trains in Sweden
 
Author Lukaszewicz, P
Affiliation Department of Aeronautical & Vehicle Engineering, Royal Institute of Technology (KTH), Sweden
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2007: High Tech in Heavy Haul, 11-13 June, 2007, Kiruna, Sweden
Publication Date 2007
Abstract Running resistance of ore trains consisting of Uad type wagons is determined from full scale measurements on Malmbanan. Test are also run in curves with the Uad and an ore wagon equipped with a bogie allowing the axles to align themselves radially. Influence of speed, axle load, curve radii and train length is studied and quantified. The running resistance is parameterised and expressed in a general way so it can be calculated for any Swedish ore train consisting of Uad-type wagons. The study shows that the increase in running resistance is linear due to increasing axle load on tangent track and train length. The increase in resistance due to curves is significant and increases as the curve radius decreases. If the axles align themselves radially the curve resistance reduces by 40%, compared with the Uad. The results show which parameters in a running resistance formula should be paid extra attention to when constructing a train model for simulation purposes. A comparison is made between ore trains and ordinary Swedish loco-hauled freight trains. In the paper a review of the study will be made with conclusions.

Title The Tangara story
 
Author Stoddart, W
Source Permanent Way Institution (PWI)
Conference Permanent Way Institution (PWI) NSW 1988 Annual Convention: Railways in Focus
Publication Date 1988-10-14
Abstract In the early 1960's it was becoming apparent to the then NSWGR that the Sydney suburban train system, then exclusively single decked, was becoming overloaded. The two options for overcoming this were the lengthening of platforms to allow longer trains or the upgrading of the signalling system to reduce headways between trains, both being very expensive. A third avenue was therefore followed with the result that the first double decked cars were introduced in 1964. These were trailer cars which were coupled between existing motor cars. A significant capacity increase was therefore achieved without major infrastructure costs. Passenger acceptance of the double deck cars was quickly gained and work began on creating matching power cars. Although the concept of bi-level cars dated back as far as the last century, the motor cars introduced in 1968 were a world first. The car's designers were faced with the task of relocating the components normally carried below floor level in single deck cars. This was overcome by utilizing roof space over each end of the car and the advantage was taken of the decreasing size of equipment resulting from technological advances. The single deck fleet, the first of which were built in 1927 and the most recent in 1955, thus began to be replaced by double deck cars. In 1984 the suburban fleet consisted of about 800 double deck and 450 single deck cars. By this time the ageing single deck cars were suffering from poor reliability, high maintenance costs, a scarcity of spare parts and a poor public image. The SRA, which had been steadily improving the design of double deck cars through a series of contracts, began preparing to acquire the cars which would allow the remaining "red rattlers" to be retired.

Title Shunting impact simulations for permanently coupled coal wagons
 
Author Skerman, D
Wishart, M
Affiliation Queensland Rail
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1995: World Best Practice - The What, Why and How, Conference on Railway Engineering, 29 October-1 November 1995, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 1995
Abstract As part of an investigation by the Coal and Minerals Group of Queensland Rail into the optimum grouping of permanently coupled rakes of coal wagons, it was considered that a factor limiting the number of slackless drawbars would be the forces induced by shunting impacts. Simulations were done using impact speeds of 10, 15 and 20 kmh in the longitudinal train force computer model. Results showed that for impact speeds of 15 and 20kmh, all cases of slackless coupling produced maximum forces above the design static load as used by Queensland Rail of 1.8 MN. For an impact speed of 10 kmh, the cases of two and four wagons joined by slackless drawbars fell below this limit. In addition to this, for both impact speeds, forces increased significantly for groups of more than four wagons joined by slackless drawbars.

Title Improvements to wagon connection modelling for longitudinal train simulation
 
Author Cole, C
Affiliation Centre for Railway Engineering, Rockhampton, Qld
Source National Committee on Railway Engineering of the College of Mechanical Engineers of the Institution of Engineers, Australia
Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1998 Engineering innovation for a competitive edge Conference on Railway Engineering 7-9 September 1998, Yeppoon, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1998
Abstract The completion of an extensive program of testing six wagons in different train positions in a 102 wagon coal train has yielded an extensive data base of coupler force and draft gear deflection data. Various draft gear behaviours are identified and presented. These include, loading, stiffening, locking and unloading. Draft gear behaviour can be shown to be dependent on the impact severity. There is also considerable difference between shunt type impacts (which are closely simulated by the drop hammer tests) and impacts occurring during normal train running. The test data has shown that the loading stiffness of draft gear units in normal train running can be up to 17 times stiffer than predicted by drop hammer test data. With the response capabilities of draft gear units so variable, incorporating the range of behaviour between the two subsets of known data is a requirement for a reliable wagon connection model. A new wagon connection model is presented which matches draft gear responses for both normal train operation and shunt type impacts. The accuracy of the new model will enable more reliable simulation results which may result in greater confidence to operate longer, heavier trains through improved driving techniques and/or improvements in draft gear design and maintenance. The operation of longer trains will increase throughput and productivity. Improvements to draft gear design will result in improved reliability and a reduction in maintenance costs.vehicle

Title Performance evaluation of improved freight bogie suspension systems
 
Author Tournay, HM
Cakdi, S
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc., Pueblo, Colorado USA
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract North American railways operate wagons at 32.5-tonne axle load and maximum speeds of 120 km/h. Rolling contact fatigue (RCF) has been found to be the root cause of wheel tread damage resulting in high impact wheels (HIW); this is estimated to be a USD$400-million per year problem. RCF on rails in curves and tangent results in rail grinding and premature rail removal. Current remedial actions are: wheel replacement, rail grinding and replacement and top of rail friction control. Research indicates that improved steering bogies will eliminate or substantially reduce RCF. Bogies with improved steering properties have been subject to a phenomenon termed loaded car hunting (LCH). LCH has been found to be a system problem associated with the suspension characteristics of North American freight bogies under high wheel/rail conicities. An improved suspension concept utilizing the structure of a North American three-piece bogie has been proposed as a solution to this problem. Candidate designs have been offered for test and have been evaluated at the Transportation Technology Center Inc. (TTCI), Pueblo, Colorado, USA. This paper will describe the evaluation methods used as well as the results obtained. It will describe the planned way forward to implementation in revenue service.

Title Optimising ECP in Queensland, preventing brake block metal pick up
 
Author Smalley, C
Affiliation Pacific National, Queensland, Australia
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Pacific National Coal in Queensland operates Wabtec stand alone ECP-4200 braking equipment on all wagons. ECP provides a significant operating advantage over traditional pneumatic braking systems, being easier to use and providing real time feedback to the operator. The wagon’s ECP brake cylinder operating range and driver methodology resulted in brake block metal pick up. Brake block metal pick up is the collection of wheel metal in the brake block, which causes wheel tread damage and hot wheels. Investigation into the brake system showed that the brake block type, brake block moulding pressure, and wheel class will affect the level of metal pick up. The primary initiator of metal pick up was found to be low energy braking. ECP control systems allow a minimum brake cylinder pressure to be programmed to a set pressure, 0kPa if requested. If the minimum brake cylinder pressure level is too low, there is insufficient pressure to overcome the internal resistances and provide sufficient braking force on the brake block. By increasing minimum brake cylinder pressure, metal pick up stopped. Additionally, the driving methodology had to change to suit the change in train brake performance. This change in methodology resulted less overall brake use, especially for unloaded trains.

Title Improving the 10" head alliance coupler and knuckle
 
Author Steed, J
Kimpton, R
Affiliation Rail Industry Supplies, VIC, Australia
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The 10" head Alliance coupler has seen extensive use in various regions of the world since its inception a hundred years ago. The increasing demands of heavy haul trains has facilitated the need for improvements to this design and by examining failed in service coupler and knuckles key locations of failure were identified. Through a combination of physical measurements and 3D laser scanning an initial 3D CAD model was created of the standard Alliance coupler and knuckle. Non-linear finite element static strength analyses and strain life fatigue analyses were performed on these models with results compared to failures in service. Physical static tension tests were used to validate these FEA results. An improved coupler and knuckle were designed addressing known causes of failures. The RIS Alliance coupler had an increase in strength, a reduction in localized yielding under typical in service loads and an increase in fatigue life in known failure areas. Improvements to the patent pending RIS HD knuckle included an increase in strength of approximately 20% and an increase in fatigue life by approximately 55%. Thus the improved Rail Industry Supplies Alliance coupler and Heavy Duty knuckle offer significant improvements to fatigue life and strength while maintaining interchange ability with existing Alliance coupler sub parts.

Title Development of 42 tonne axle load heavy haul wheel
 
Author Hemsworth, A

Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia

Abstract Comsteel has provided wheels to Fortescue Metals Group (Fortescue) since construction of its railway began in 2007. Originally operating at 40 tonne axle load, Fortescue ran the heaviest haul railway in the world. Today, Fortescue is hauling at 42 tonne axle load further cementing itself as a leader in innovation. Having successfully moved to 42 tonne, the company will continue to develop its assets and infrastructure to support continued productivity improvements including the consideration of even higher axle loads. This study examines requirements for safe and effective operation of railway wheels at 42 tonne axle loads.
