Title The development of a bogie for predictable performance in heavy haul service in North America
 
Author Tournay, H
Cakdi, S
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc. Transportation Technology Center, Inc. Pueblo, Colorado, USA
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract High impact wheels (HIW) cost the North American railroad industry an estimated minimum of $400 million annually. This is exacerbated by the unpredictable nature of HIW. HIW occur at different rates, depending on the position in the wagon, the direction of loaded operation, route curvature, wheelset alignment within the bogie and the prevailing winter weather conditions (removals accelerate in the North American winters). The unpredictability in wheel removals hampers planned maintenance management and resource allocations. HIW formation has been shown to be directly related to the magnitude of the steering forces on the wheel. These forces are related in turn to track curvature. Improved steering bogies have been shown to reduce the incidence of HIW. These bogies have been shown, however, to be more susceptible to so-called loaded car hunting (LCH); LCH is a body instability and occurs when a resonant body mode couples to the yaw frequency of wheelsets at speeds generally in excess of approximately 60 mph (100 km/h). LCH imposes high forces on the track, induces probable wheel and rail rolling contact fatigue, and results in damage to wagon components, especially the adapter pads used to provide improved steering. Adapter pad failure, in addition to being expensive, is also a function of service speeds and routes. Consequently, replacement is not predictable. Transportation Technology Center Inc. (TTCI) was tasked, under the Strategic Research Initiatives (SRI) Program of the Association of American Railroads (AAR) to develop and prove a bogie design concept to eliminate HIW and LCH. TTCI proposed a concept (the improved freight car truck, or IFCT) and invited the railway supply industry, worldwide, to supply prototypes for test and evaluation at the Transportation Test Center (TTC) near Pueblo, Colorado USA. Five suppliers provided prototypes for test. During the test program, modifications were made to the designs to improve performance. Currently, three bogies have shown appreciably improved curving performance with hunting stability in excess of 75 mph (125 km/h), and one bogie type has shown adequate steering performance (equal to the current AAR M-976 specification) with hunting performance to 75 mph (125 km/h). This paper reports on the overall results of the test program, provides conclusions on the bogie design elements considered essential to providing adequate curving performance and hunting stability, and describes the way forward to implementation of a bogie for improved wheel life and hunting stability with predictable maintenance performance.

Title Enhancements in the curving performance of locomotives equipped with three-axle radial steering bogies
 
Author Goding, DJ
Handley, JH
Rassaian, M
Affiliation General Motors Locomotive Group, Electro-Motive Division, LaGrange, Illinois, USA
Boeing Aerospace, Seattle, Washington, USA
Conference 4th International Heavy Haul Conference, Railways in action, 11-15 September 1989, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1989
Abstract A comprehensive research and development program has been undertaken by the Electro-Motive Division of the General Motors Locomotive Group to design radial steering bogies for six axle locomotives. Aiming at a reduction of wheel and rail wear in curves, these bogies incorporate a self-steering mechanism which minimizes the angle of attack between the wheels and rails. The development program has included extensive math modeling of the curve negotiation characteristics of both conventional North American bogies and radial steering bogies. Comparisons of the curving performance of these bogies is presented. Parameters for comparison include net lateral load, angle of attack, and wheel flange wear index. An experimental field test has been performed on a prototype three axle radial steering bogie at the Association of American Railroads (AAR) Transportation Test Center (TTC) in Pueblo, Colorado and on a major North American railroad in curves as small as 175 m radius (100). This test demonstrated that reductions of 32-46% in the wheel flange wear index can be achieved in 175 m curves under high tractive effort (16-30% adhesion) conditions. Even more significant reductions (67%) can be achieved during low tractive effort (coasting type) operation.

Title Recent advancements in buff and draft testing techniques
 
Author El-Sibaie, M
Affiliation Association of American Railroads, Transportation Test Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
Conference 5th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1993), 6-13 June, 1993, Beijing, China
Abstract In an operational railroad environment, the longitudinal in-train forces, commonly known as buff and draft forces, can have adverse effects on the curving stability of a moving train. With the recent trend of heavier and longer trains, and the resulting larger coupler forces, the lateral stability of any single car can be compromised. The North American railroad industry still suffers derailments in which in-train forces are the likely cause. The problem seems to be greatest when such forces act on light or empty shortllong car combinations. This paper describes a new method for evaluating the curving stability of any freight railway car under buff and draft conditions. A special test vehicle was designed and constructed by the Association of American Railroads (AAR) for the purpose of applying controlled levels of lateral load at the coupler pin of any adjacent car, and while in motion. This test vehicle is used in determining the maximum lateral coupler load that can be safely applied to any candidate car under various developed for predicting the coupler angles and forces generated under any given buff or draft conditions, track curvature, and coupling arrangements The proposed method combines testing with analysis in order to determine the level of buff or draft force at which the lateral coupler load test limits are exceeded. As an example for the proposed method, the developed techniques were used to examine the curving performance of a standard North American 89-foot (27 metre) flatcar when coupled to another relatively short 2-axle flatcar. The results indicated that the coupling of the two cars reduces the curving stability of the long flatcar under realizable buff and draft conditions.

Title Improvement of the operational characteristics of wrought steel wheels
 
Author Pasholok, IL
Sukhov, AV
Affiliation All-Russian Railway Research Institute, Moscow, Russia
Conference International Heavy Haul Association STS Conference, (IHHA 1999), 14-17 June, 1999, Moscow, Russia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract The analysis of the normative documents and literary data has shown, that alongside with the general tendency to increases in the strength characteristics of wheels and rails in responded at increase of traffic intensity there is the interaction between strength characteristics of wheel and rail. Significant (up to 40 % and more) increases in the strength characteristics of rails over the appropriate characteristics of wheels of freight cars result in decreases of serviceability both wheels and rails. Further increasing of rail hard ness leads to a drop of serviceability both wheels and rails. In view of the inefficiency of superficial hardening of wheel's flange and lubrication of contact between wheel and rail for increase of service life of wheels of freight cars, we offer to remove big differences between strength characteristics of wheels and rail by hardening of wrought steel wheel rims on working depth (50 mm) up to a value of the hardness of rails. This can increase the service life of freight car wheels more than twice their normal value.

Title Locomotive radial steering bogie experience in heavy haul service
 
Author Swenson, CA
Affiliation General Motors Corporation, LaGrange, IL, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul Association STS Conference, (IHHA 1999), 14-17 June, 1999, Moscow, Russia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract Since Electro-Motive Division (EMD) of General Motors introduced the 3-axle radial steering bogie in 1993, over 2500 heavy haul freight locomotives have been put in service with this new design. The application of these radial bogies is on diesel locomotives with AC (alternating current) and DC (direct current) traction, with power ranging from 4000 to 6000 traction horsepower. In addition to the heavy haul applications in North America with axle loads of over 30 metric tons, EMD has applied this new bogie technology to international locomotive designs with axle loads of 21 metric tons. Radial bogie designs are also being developed for other track gauges as well. The heavy haul AC traction locomotives are typically dispatched today at wheel-rail adhesion levels of 35% to 38% in normal operation and develop adhesion levels up to 48% for starting trains. In spite of these dramatic increases in adhesion, the wheel wear is reduced with the radial steering bogies. Extended wheel life results from significant reductions in wheel flange wear. Accordingly, even in operations where wheels are typically re-profiled for tread wear, the profound effect of lower flange wear minimizes the amount of metal removed in wheel re-profiling and extends wheel life.

Title Technologies for achieving both reliability improvement and weight reduction of Shinkansen bogie
 
Author Kanamori, S
Otsuka, T
Adachi, M
Sakanoue, K
Affiliation Central Japan Railway Company, Aichi, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract In designing Shinkansen, it is necessary to constantly improve reliability, and it is also important to consider weight reduction. In particular, weight saving of the bogie strongly affects the running safety, the bogie hunting, the ride quality and the ground vibration. For this reason, it is important to achieve both high reliability and weight reduction while giving priority to safety when we design the track. In this research, we focused on bogie frame which accounts for 20% of the bogie weight. We aimed at reducing bogie frame weight by 75kg and reducing welded points of bogie frame while improving reliability by changing its structure, which had no major design change since 300 series Shinkansen. Further, we developed the light weight and compact traction motor. Since the weight of bogie frame and traction motor was reduced, it became possible to use a more reliable double helical gear device, a brake device increasing the plate thickness, and full-active suspension improving the ride quality. As described above, our Shinkansen is characterized by continuous effort for weight reduction while pursuing reliability improvement and long life. We confirmed the basic performance of the new bogie through bench tests and running tests in commercial line in 2017 and we are planning to install it on N700S in 2020.

Affiliation KTH Rail Vehicles, Division of Aeronautical and Vehicle Engineering, Royal Institute of Technology (KTH), Stockholm, Sweden
Tikab Structural Engineers, Danderyd, Sweden
Conference 9th International Heavy Haul Conference (IHHC 2009), Shanghai, China, 22nd-25th June, 2009
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Today link suspension is very common in freight wagons in Europe. In recent years, however, there is a tendency that the amount of wagons especially with link bogie is decreasing mainly due to the quite moderate running behaviour of these wagons on straight track. A severe hunting motion sometimes is observed at speeds as low as 70 km/h. KTH Rail vehicles have investigated the running behaviour of freight wagons for about ten years. Author Simson, S
Publisher or Institution Central Queensland University
Publication Date 2009
Place Queensland, Australia
Source Central Queensland University (CQU) Institutional Repository
Abstract It has been shown that active steering control can improve steering performance in two axle bogies whilst also improving high speed train stability. In freight operations passive steering three axle bogies have been produced in large numbers for reported benefits including improved adhesion performance and reduced wheel wear. These factors have not been modelled for hauling locomotives and published in public domain though commercial testing by locomotive manufacturers has occurred. A gap therefore exists in the knowledge of steering bogie performance in freight locomotive operations particularly for three axle bogies. Bogies with new designs developed as part of this thesis: Actuated Yaw Force Steered (AY-FS) bogies with creep force control; precedence control; and curve estimation control; Actuated Yaw Variable Steering (AY-VS) bogies with precedence control and curve estimation control. Abstract There have been many large rolling stock acquisition projects in Australia in recent years. Since the Rail Industry Safety and Standards Board (RISSB) released AS7501 especially, the use of Independent Certification has become part of the rolling stock acquisition project compliance landscape. It is important that independent certification is not seen as a box ticking exercise. While AS7501 was developed to provide a standard method for certifying rolling stock compliance to facilitate network registration of rolling stock, there is significant value that can be obtained from implementing the AS7501 process. This paper will consider how AS7501 supports the rolling stock registration processes. The Independent Certifier (IC) process will also be shown to be one of the key pillars of the assurance framework for a rolling stock procurement project. It will also highlight the additional value that an organisation can obtain by introducing AS7501 independent certification. AS7501 is not just a box ticking exercise. It has the potential to be an integral part of the risk management process and improve the outcomes of rolling stock acquisition projects.
