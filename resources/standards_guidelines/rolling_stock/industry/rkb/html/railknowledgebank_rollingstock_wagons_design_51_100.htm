Title Impact of local comfort zones in long-distance rolling stock on objective and subjective thermal comfort rating
 
Author Schmeling, D
Hormann, H-J
Volkmann, A
Goerke, P
Affiliation German Aerospace Center (DLR), Institute of Aerodynamics and Flow Technology, Göttingen, Germany
German Aerospace Center (DLR), Institute of Aerospace Medicine, Hamburg, Germany
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract We present first results of a study addressing the impact of local thermal comfort zones on the objec-tive and subjective thermal comfort rating. The study is conducted in a realistic train geometry repre-senting the lower deck of the DLR’s next generation train. Subject questionnaires and calibrated thermal manikins are used to capture comfort-relevant quantities. Cabin displacement ventilation with rather low supply air temperatures is employed as baseline ventilation and up to six individually con-trollable infrared (IR) heating panels enable the creation of local comfort zones. The three main findings are: a) the IR heating panels can compensate the cold-feet effect of low-temperature cabin displacement ventilation. This positive effect is also measurable for reduced IR panel configurations, which have a reasonable energy demand. As a result, a combination of highly effective displacement ventilation with a reduced IR panel configuration provides the potential for re-ducing the energy demand of the HVAC system. b) Activation of the IR panels strongly decreases the amount of dissatisfied passengers compared to the reference case. Further, giving the control of the IR panels to the individual passengers almost doubles the amount of satisfied passengers. c) The tem-perature evaluation of e.g. warm temperatures in the leg room differs significantly between male and female passengers. Solely the latter finding highlights the importance of local, individual comfort zones.

Title A semi-active multi-mode vibration damping technique to study the impact of vertical flexural vibration damping of a railway car-body floor on ride comfort
 
Author Das, S
Gupta, A
Ono, S
Kobayashi, Y
Emaru, T
Ravankar, A
Affiliation Graduate School of Engineering, Hokkaido University, Sapporo, Japan
Faculty of Engineering, Hokkaido University, Sapporo, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Ride comfort is an indicative of the dynamic performance and ride quality of a railway vehicle. The most prominent motion related factor that affects the ride comfort of a railway vehicle is the vertical vibration of the railway car-body. This study aims to suppress the vertical flexural vibrations on a railway car-body floor by using a semi-active multimode vibration damping technique. The technique uses a Piezo-electric Patch Configuration (PPC) bonded to the floor of a scaled car-body model. The PPC terminals were further connected to a multi-mode shunt circuit which was tuned to effectively damp the first three prominent bending modes in low frequency range. The finite element simulation of the 1/12th scaled car-body model with bonded PPC was done in ANSYS 17.1 along with coupled field simulation of semi-active shunt circuit in Ansys Parametric Design Language (APDL). Experimental analysis of the proposed model was carried out to validate simulation results.

Title Efforts to improve riding comfort of the Tokaido Shinkansen
 
Author Okada, Y
Adachi, M
Kato, H
Aoya, T
Okada, N
Affiliation Central Japan Railway Company, Aichi, Japan
Nippon Sharyo. LTD, Aichi, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Improving ride quality is an eternal challenge for high-speed railways. In Tokaido Shinkansen, in order to provide customers with a comfortable ride, we developed various control technologies such as a vibration damping control device to suppress the vibration of the car body and a car inclination device to ease the centrifugal force in the curve. It has been adopted to the latest Tokaido Shinkansen. In order to improve the ride quality by suppressing the vibration in the lateral direction of the car body, the Series 700, which started operation in 1999, is the first to be introduced semi-active suspension system on the Tokaido Shinkansen. We have improved the control algorithm of the semi-active suspension system to further improve ride quality until the Series N700A, which started operation in 2013. In order to provide a higher quality ride, we have newly developed a compact and lightweight full-active suspension system so that it can be effective even with limited space and limited vehicle weight. This device has a simple configuration in which a small motor is attached to a conventional semi-active damper, and by adopting the same AC 100 V as the power supply for driving the motor. That’s why the size and weight of the entire system can be reduced. The developed full-active suspension system will be mounted on the first class car of the N700S validation test vehicle, the latest model of the Tokaido Shinkansen, which has been in test running in 2018, and will undergo many verifications, and the N700S mass-production model is especially equipped on cars with first class cars and large vibration cars (both leading cars and pantograph cars), and contributes to further improvement in the riding comfort of the Tokaido Shinkansen.

Title Is there a future for freight wagon with link suspension?
 
Author Stichel, S
Jonsson, PA
Affiliation KTH Rail Vehicles, Division of Aeronautical and Vehicle Engineering, Royal Institute of Technology (KTH), Stockholm, Sweden
Tikab Structural Engineers, Danderyd, Sweden
Conference 9th International Heavy Haul Conference (IHHC 2009), Shanghai, China, 22nd-25th June, 2009
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Today link suspension is very common in freight wagons in Europe. In recent years, however, there is a tendency that the amount of wagons especially with link bogie is decreasing mainly due to the quite moderate running behaviour of these wagons on straight track. A severe hunting motion sometimes is observed at speeds as low as 70 km/h. KTH Rail vehicles have investigated the running behaviour of freight wagons for about ten years. To our opinion there should be a future for the link suspension. The main argument is the superior curving performance. The problem of vehicle hunting should be possible to reduce drastically with better conicity management. Further the spread of suspension characteristics that can be observed in measurements must be reduced. Finally results are shown which indicate that it should be possible to upgrade freight wagons with link suspension to services at speeds up to 160 km/h by introducing hydraulic dampers.

Abstract As the service demands on journal bearings continue to increase for heavy haul service, it is increasingly important to optimize the journal bearing design to improve reliability and performance. Lower bearing system torque and operating temperatures are the key to achieving these objectives. This paper presents a new AP_2™ style bearing design, configured to fit the "current short Class G" envelope, and integrating the latest non-contacting "EcoTurn TM" seal technology. Title Railway axle design - to be based on fatigue initiation or crack propagation?
 
Author Lunden, R
Vernersson, T
Ekberg, A
Affiliation Chalmers University of Technology / CHARMEC, Goteborg, Sweden
Conference 9th International Heavy Haul Conference (IHHC 2009), Shanghai, China, 22nd-25th June, 2009
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Design and standardization of railway axles from the mechanical-strength point of view is discussed and a brief state-of-the-art survey is given. Traditional fatigue design with the
"safe-life" approach and a design with the more recent crack-propagation / defect-tolerance approach are described and compared. Axles of a standard freight wheelset (22. 5 tonne axle load) and of two heavy haul wheelsets (25 and 31 tonne axle loads) are used for illustration. From the numerical results it is concluded that a crack-propagation / defect-tolerance approach could be a useful complement to the traditional fatigue design method. A result of particular interest is the fairly strong influence of stresses from the press- / shrink-fitting on the residual life of the axles.

Title Recycled rail wagons for rail bridge decks
 
Project leader A/Prof. M. Dhanasekar, (QUT);
Description The aim of the project is to investigate the structural adequacy of the disused flat bottom railway wagons as bridge decks in the low volume high axle load regional Australian road network. It is proposed to develop appropriate design rules and stringent processes to minimise the risks of using the disused wagons on public roads with adequate levels of safety to the road users through materials characterisation, loads analysis, modelling and full scale laboratory testing. It is proposed to demonstrate the developed technology of road bridge deck replacement for a case study in one of the partner investigators' (Shire Councils) sites through field testing and ongoing monitoring.
Conducting organisation CQ University; QUT
Status Completed
Outcomes It is believed that the technology of adopting disused rail wagons will prove better against existing technologies on triple bottom line measures.
First, from the economic perspective, the current technologies of erecting new bridge decks as per existing design rules and practices of construction typically could be achieved within one-third of that cost. Second, from an environmental perspective, the project will find better usage of the disused wagons, thereby providing energy savings when compared to metal recycling and alternate construction solutions. Third, from the social perspective, bridge deck replacement and new bridges could be provided sooner without delays (based on economy of scale) relative to current practice, which provide safer bridges and improved road access and life style for regional and remote communities of Australia.

Title 3D Suspension characterization of a rapid transit vehicle using a multi-body dynamic model
 
Author Demir, E
Affiliation Sabanci University, Istanbul, Turkey
Composite Technologies Center of Excellence, Istanbul, Turkey
Source ATRI
Journal Urban Rail Transit
Publication Date 2016-12
Abstract In this work, suspension characterization of a rapid transit vehicle is performed with a multi-body dynamic model that represents full degrees of freedom of a rapid transit vehicle. The effects of lateral suspension properties on passenger ride comfort and stability are investigated by variation of critical suspension parameters using design of experiment method. The critical suspension properties are obtained for the best values of car body lateral acceleration and car body lateral stroke. The tangent track time response of the car body verified the negligible effect of both lateral viscous dampers at primary suspensions and longitudinal anti-yaw dampers at secondary suspensions on the passenger ride comfort and stability of a rapid transit vehicle.

Title Evaluation of the aerodynamic component of empty train energy costs
 
Author Cole, C
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No HH7
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2015-08
Physical Description 55p. ; PDF
Abstract The project was proposed to evaluate the energy consumption components associated with wagon aerodynamics in heavy haul trains and compare with possible benefits that might be possible from improvements in aerodynamic design. Empty train aerodynamic improvements for heavy haul rotary dump wagons have been discussed. The open top of the ore wagon generates a large proportion of the aerodynamic drag for empty ore trains at speeds of ~70km/hr and above. Innovative methods for reducing this drag are needed that will be compatible with loading and dumping systems.
Research Questions
1. What potential for energy saving exist from improvements in aerodynamics in empty hopper and ore wagons?
2. What savings could be realised in typical Australian heavy haul track routes and rollingstock designs?

Title AS7501 and compliance certification
 
Subtitle Not just ticking boxes
Author Horgan, M
Affiliation SNC-Lavalin Atkins
Source Informa
Publication Date 2022
Abstract There have been many large rolling stock acquisition projects in Australia in recent years. Since the Rail Industry Safety and Standards Board (RISSB) released AS7501 especially, the use of Independent Certification has become part of the rolling stock acquisition project compliance landscape. It is important that independent certification is not seen as a box ticking exercise. While AS7501 was developed to provide a standard method for certifying rolling stock compliance to facilitate network registration of rolling stock, there is significant value that can be obtained from implementing the AS7501 process. This paper will consider how AS7501 supports the rolling stock registration processes. The Independent Certifier (IC) process will also be shown to be one of the key pillars of the assurance framework for a rolling stock procurement project. It will also highlight the additional value that an organisation can obtain by introducing AS7501 independent certification. AS7501 is not just a box ticking exercise. It has the potential to be an integral part of the risk management process and improve the outcomes of rolling stock acquisition projects.
