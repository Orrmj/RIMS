Title Wagon squeal – is noise costing us money?
 
Author Robertson, S
Hanson, D
Affiliation SNC Lavalin, Australia
Transport for New South Wales, Sydney, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Squealing noise resulting from freight wagons that exhibit poor curving ability is a significant issue for railways located within residential areas. Poor curving ability is typically due to a combination of insufficient bogie rotation and low warp stiffness which together lead to a high angle of attack – the angle of the axle alignment of train wheels relative to the rail. Railway operators practice a range of measures to manage this issue, but ultimately, well designed and maintained bogies are the key to optimised performance. This paper considers the problem of wheel squeal and its cause. Exploration of the performance objectives of freight wagon bogies, focussing on the domestic market, but informed by international practices, is used to establish an understanding of current freight bogie design approaches. The main types of intermodal bogies in operation, along with their advantages, limitations and improvements are presented. The capital, maintenance, operational and third party costs for the operation of intermodal bogies in Australia are subsequently evaluated. These findings are finally drawn together to identify if noise arising from the railways as a result of wheel squeal is costing us money.

Abstract An Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) mounted on the bogie of a railway vehicle records inertial data as the vehicle traverses a length track. The system measures movement in six degrees of freedom, as well as recording tachometer and GPS data. Using measurements collected from the system, a range of information about the track profile can be generated. Research is ongoing into the possibility of extracting information about the deflection of the track as the train passes over it. Owing to the large number of unmeasured parameters in the vehicle’s suspension system, such processing requires a large amount of estimation. Title Study of the long term evolution of low-RCF wheel profiles for LKAB iron-ore wagons
 
Author Nia, SH
Stichel, S
Casanueva, C
Nordmark, T
Affiliation KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm, Sweden
Luleå Technical University, Kiruna, Sweden
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The Swedish iron-ore company LKAB is using three-piece bogies with 30 tonnes axle load to transport pellets from the mines in Kiruna and Malmberget to the ports in Luleå and Narvik. LKAB provided a new wheel profile that is expected to perform better with regard to rolling contact fatigue and results in a longer wheel life than the currently used WP4 profile (RCF). A simulation model of the freight wagon has been built at KTH using the multibody simulation code GENSYS. However, in order to study the long-term stability of the new profile, monitoring the evolution of the shape of the wheel profile is inevitable: there is a risk that the optimised profile might wear in an unfavourable way and, after a while, cause even higher RCF or wear than the original one. The current study predicts the wheel profile evolution as function of running distance using the uniform wear prediction tool developed at KTH, see e.g., Jendel and Berg. It includes an analysis of when, during the wheel life, the highest RCF probability occurs. The procedure contains of a number of so-called wear steps with a set of time-domain simulations reflecting the actual rail network. As the wagons are equipped with pneumatically actuated tread brakes, the influence of braking on material removal is also estimated via the calculation of the braking force for each simulation case; this is, considering the topography of the line, the amount of braking by the locomotives and the running resistance from curving, aerodynamics and mechanical resistance. The calculation is first performed for the current wagon wheel profiles, and the results are compared with field measurements in order to validate the simulation procedure. There is a great potential of saving time and money if an optimal wheel-rail profile combination can be selected with the help of computer simulation. The results did not show any major difference between the wear of WP4 and WPX profiles. Although the WPX profile produces less RCF on the tread, it may be at a risk of developing RCF at the flange area. Since there are no major differences observed in the simulation results, LKAB has not yet decided if they use the suggested WPX profile. A field test with the new profiles on wagons would be beneficial to verify the simulation results. This work has been performed and continuously validated in the Malmbanan Wheel-Rail group to try to solve the RCF problems with the wagons and loco wheels.

Title Progress report on heavy axle load test at FAST
 
Author Reiff, RP
Kalay, S
Reinschmidt, AJ
Allen, RA
Affiliation Association of American Railroads, Washington D.C., USA
Transportation Test Center, Colorado, USA
Conference 4th International Heavy Haul Conference, Railways in action, 11-15 September 1989, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1989
Abstract The Association of American Railroads (AAR) and the U. S. Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) jointly operate the Facility for Accelerated Service Testing (FAST) at the Transportation Test Center (TTC) located near Pueblo, Colorado. The FAST program has been in virtually continuous operation since late 1976. An extensive data base has been assembled documentinq the effects of conventional 33-ton (66,000 lb.) axle loads on track components as well as the total track structure. The North American railroad industry has utilized much of the date generated by the FAST program to improve its operating efficiency and safety. Presently, the axle load limit for unrestricted interchange in North America is 33-tons of commonly referred to as the 100-ton (capacity) car. For the past several years, a number of railroads have been operating heavier cars either entirely on their own lines or in limited interchange on an agreement basis with connecting roads. These cars have capacities of 110 to 125-tons, with axle loads of up to 39-tons. As these heavier cars are operated, along with and sometimes mixed into the existing 33-ton axle load car fleet, the effects of the increased loads are masked by the overall wear and degradation of the general track maintenance programs. In other cases where these cars were exclusively operated on a line, unique circumstances prevented the generalization of track performance due to the heavier axle loads to other situations. To date, it has been impossible to identify the direct effect that 39-ton axle loads have on wear, fatigue and overall maintenance to the track structure attributable to the heavier axle load cars. To address this concern, the FAST program in 1988 reconfigured its train to a 39-ton per axle/125-ton capacity consist. A set of 89 cars was loaned to the FAST program by two railroads. These cars are of two different designs intended to operate with 125-ton capacity and 39-tons per axle. With this new train, the FAST Heavy Axle Load Program (HAL) was initiated in August of 1988. The FAST track was rebuilt with a mix of components to allow a number of experiments to be conducted monitoring the effect of the HAL train. As of the preparation of this paper, approximately 22 Million Gross Tons (MGT) of HAL traffic has been applied to the new set of experiments. Data in this paper will reflect only these early results. The majority of the experiment are designed to assess the long term effects of the HAL train on component wear, fatigue and maintenance; thus, additional information will be presented in the conference as it becomes available.

Title Using technologies for higher axle loads and longer trains: to develop market-oriented railways in China and India
 
Author Anderson, EP
Harris, WJ
Affiliation The World Bank, Washington DC, USA
A&M University, College Station, TX, Australia
Conference 5th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1993), 6-13 June, 1993, Beijing, China
Abstract In China and India, railways are today the main mode for long haul freight and passenger transport. Both countries will continue to rely on the railways for movement of goods and people, assigning them a more prominent role than railways now have in the industrialized countries of North America and Europe. Moreover, the future freight transport role for railways in China and India will not be limited to the transport of low-value bulk products. Due to the increasing diversity of transport needs as their economies develop, these railways also will be challenged to efficiently move a greater volume of high-value manufactured products over long distances. Haulage of greater volumes of products will require increases in axle loads and train lengths, which are characteristics of what is identified as heavy-haul operation. Heavy-haul operation has fully proven its effectiveness in North America, Australia, South Africa, Brazil, and in a few cases in China and India, and has enabled the railways to provide their customers with low-cost transportation that has greatly contributed to the local economies. In China and India as transport demand grows and as the need to use existing rail infrastructure more efficiently also grows, the tradeoff is between creating more trackage and infrastructure, more locomotives and freight cars, and more operating personnel, or instead, using heavy-haul technologies to satisfy the same increasing demand with less investment. It is likely that substantial increases in axle load and train size will he the least costly way to increase the railways' capacity to meet expected demand. The hypothesis of this paper is that the performance of heavy-haul operation on North American railways can be and should he achieved by the high-density and rapidly-growing railways of China and India by a plan to introduce new designs of locomotives, rolling stock, and track components; stronger new and rebuilt bridges; advanced signal and train control systems; improved track maintenance systems; computer-based information and commanications systems; improved blocking of loads and empties; and more efficient cargo loading and unloading systems. After making the investments required to run trains that are twice as long with a 50% higher axle load, compared to today's trains, freight throughput capacity would be increased 2.8 times and the cost per tonne carried - including a 12 percent return on the incremental investment -- actually would fall below the current unit cost as volume grows.

Title Development of a semi automatic/ automatic rolling stock wheel ultrasonic testing facility
 
Author Ward, M
Conference 4th International Heavy Haul Conference, Railways in action, 11-15 September 1989, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1989
Abstract Over the years of operation Hamersley Iron Railways have experienced numerous rolling stock wheel fractures some of which have caused catastrophic failure with subsequent derailment. Depending on the severity of the derailment, multi million dollar expenditure can occur with subsequent disruption to the operation. The majority of catastrophic wheel failures are caused by surface opening fatigue cracks either in the wheel tread or wheel flange areas. The surface opening fatigue cracks initiate from gouge marks caused by the retarder shoes on the wheel grippers at the ore car dumping facilities, propagate, and finally attain critical crack size whereupon catastrophic failure results. Wheels which have suffered overheating from either a drag braking or prolonged braking situation are a potential high risk. This is due to the reversion of the original compressive hoop stress from the wheel rim heat treatment manufacturing process, (which resists crack initiation and propagation to a tensile residual stress, (which assists crack initiation and propagation). Abstract It has been shown that active steering control can improve steering performance in two axle bogies whilst also improving high speed train stability. In freight operations passive steering three axle bogies have been produced in large numbers for reported benefits including improved adhesion performance and reduced wheel wear. However, passive steering bogie designs have been shown to perform similarly to straight rigid frame bogies under high traction forces due to the loss of longitudinal creep forces. These factors have not been modelled for hauling locomotives and published in public domain though commercial testing by locomotive manufacturers has occurred. A gap therefore exists in the knowledge of steering bogie performance in freight locomotive operations particularly for three axle bogies. The following thesis explored traction curving operations for hauling locomotives considering, and in particular, the effect of high adhesion levels, rail-wheel contact friction and lateral components of coupler forces. Both passive and active designs were analysed and new designs were proposed. Methods of active control and data measurement were also compared and discussed. The following three axle bogies were evaluated and compared.
- Bogies with passive steering: Rigid, yaw relaxation, self steering, forced steering.
- Bogies with actively controlled designs: Actuated Wheelset Yaw (AWY) bogies with creep force control; steering angle precedence control; yaw angle precedence control. Bogies with new designs developed as part of this thesis: Actuated Yaw Force Steered (AY-FS) bogies with creep force control; precedence control; and curve estimation control; Actuated Yaw Variable Steering (AY-VS) bogies with precedence control and curve estimation control. Title Investigation of the dynamics of railway bogies subjected to traction / braking torque
 
Author Handoko, YA
Publisher or Institution Central Queensland University
Publication Date 2006
Place Queensland, Australia
Source Central Queensland University (CQU) Institutional Repository
Physical Description PDF
Abstract The limitations of current simulation packages in addressing the true longitudinal behaviour of railway bogie dynamics during braking/traction has prompted the development of a Rail Bogie Dynamics (RBD) program in this thesis. The RBD program offers novel features for the calculation of the speed profile as a function of the brake torque as well as explicitly determining wheelset angular velocity. With such capability, the speed profile is no longer treated as an input calculated as a priori as required by most of the current simulation systems. The RBD program has been developed using a formulation that includes the wheelset pitch degree of freedom explicitly with a coordinate reference system that is fixed in space and time. The formulation has made the simulation of the bogie dynamics during braking/traction possible in a natural way using the brake/traction torque as the input and the resulting speed profile as the output without any need for working out the speed profile as a priori. Consequently, severe dynamics during braking such as the wheelset skid and the onset of wheel climb derailment can be modelled and critical parameters investigated using the RBD program. The RBD program has been validated, where possible, through a series of simulations using a commercial software package (VAMPIRE). For cases which cannot be simulated by VAMPIRE such as the wheelset skid, a novel experimental program has been designed and commissioned in the Heavy Testing Laboratory of the Central Queensland University as reported in this thesis. One of the possible applications of the RBD program in examining the effect of asymmetric brake shoe force in bogies equipped with one-side push brake shoe arrangement is illustrated in this thesis. It is believed that the model and RBD program will have significant benefit in understanding the true longitudinal behaviour of wagons in suburban passenger trains that operate under braking/ traction torques for most of their travel. Similar studies will also be useful to freight train wagon dynamics during entry and exit of speed restriction zones and tight curves.

Abstract This literature review covers the areas of fatigue and failure mechanics affecting railway wheels as well as damage mechanics. It highlights deficiencies in knowledge to date in these areas, such as in the assessment of crack growth in freight wheels.
