Title Fast estimation of the derailment risk of a braking train in curves and turnouts
 
Author Burgelman, N
Li, Z
Dollevoet, R
Affiliation Delft University of Technology, Delft, The Netherlands
Source ATRI
Journal International Journal of Heavy Vehicle Systems
Publication Date 2016
Abstract When a train runs through a turnout or a sharp curve, high lateral forces occur between the wheels and rails. These lateral forces increase when the couplers between the wagons are loaded in compression i.e. a rear locomotive pushing a train or front locomotive braking a train. This study quantifies these effects for a train that begins braking when steadily curving and for a train that brakes upon entering a turnout. Our approach allows distinguishing between the effects of braking and the transient effects of entering the turnout. The dynamic derailment quotient is mapped as a function of the vehicle speed and the braking effort. Then the dynamic derailment coefficient obtained from the dynamic simulations is compared to results from quasi statics. This allows determining a dynamic multiplication coefficient that can be used on the quasi static derailment coefficient to obtain a first estimate of the dynamic derailment coefficient.

Title Optimal placement of trackside autotransformers for 50 KV system in relation to track profile
 
Author Cella, U
Tran, T
Nussey, P
Perin, I
Affiliation Aurizon, Brisbane, Qld
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract The voltage available from the overhead traction power system must remain above a certain limit to ensure proper operation of the electric locomotives. As in any power distribution network, also within a traction power system the voltage is susceptible to variation caused by the magnitude, nature and location of the loads, and it is influenced by the construction characteristics of the power system. This paper covers the effect of the placement of autotransformers along the track in relation to the voltage available to electric locomotives and to the power they absorb. Current literature regarding this particular issue is scarce, as the electrical layout of the network is usually among the assumptions rather than the object of investigation. In this paper, a case of a heavy haul railway energised at 50 kV (also known as 2x25 kV) is considered. A manual calculation methodology is presented first, and it is applied to the case study. The autotransformer is moved along the track through a set of locations, and the minimum expected voltage on the pantograph is calculated for each position of the autotransformer. The same set of locations is analysed by using a detailed traffic simulation tool, and the results are compared. The position of the autotransformer is optimised to maximise the voltage within the section. An optimal location is found, and the manual calculation methodology proves to be accurate enough for a preliminary study. The manual methodology provides to the designer results that can be used to narrow down the set of options that are analysed via a more accurate tool, thus reducing computational time and effort.

Abstract LKAB mining company needed to upgrade four T46 terminal locomotives because they had reached their technical life length and lacked the tractive effort to handle the new longer and heavier trains. Title Inertial tractive effort as an explanatory variable in the analysis of locomotive fuel savings
 
Author Oldknow, K
Cotter, J
Eadie, P
Elvidge, D
Kennedy, W
Nedunoori, S
Peters, J
Replogle, J
Ronasi, H
Stevens, R
Weitzel, L
Affiliation L.B. Foster Rail Technologies, BC, Canada
Union Pacific, NB, USA
First Analytics, CA, USA
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The purpose of the work presented has been the development of an explanatory variable that characterizes train handling effects, allowing for the identification of changes in locomotive fuel consumption over a given territory (for example in response to Top of Rail Friction Modifier (TORFM) application) with improved confidence. To generate the explanatory variable, the concept of Inertial Tractive Effort (ITE) was developed. Given the total instantaneous tractive effort (TE) dispatched by a locomotive consist to overcome all sources of resistance, ITE is an estimate of the portion spent overcoming inertial resistance to generate acceleration. Integrating ITE over time yields cumulative ITE (cITE), which is in essence an estimate of total energy spent on acceleration versus a train travelling at constant speed over the same territory. Analysis of data collected in revenue service on several Union Pacific coal routes has shown that cITE acts as an explanatory variable, effectively mitigating variability in the data and allowing for an improved confidence when analyzing the impacts of other variables such as TORFM application. With the incorporation of cITE as an explanatory variable in a multi-variable regression of locomotive fuel data collected over five months of revenue service operation on Union Pacific, the statistical power of the underlying model was improved to yield a P-value of 0.028 versus 0.202 otherwise (the corresponding fuel savings associated with TORFM application were estimated at 6.4%). The approach represents a novel and useful mechanism to handle fuel data variability, with the potential to underpin a broad range of analyses.

Abstract This paper discusses the effect of heavy haul transportation on the indicators of railway operation and heavy haul transportation efficiency, the composition of the economic indicators and the indicator system of heavy haul transportation efficiency from the aspects of transportation capacity, travelling speed, detention time of cars, turn-around time, productivity of locomotives and cars and railway equipment utilization rate. As a result of research, the method of economic benefit appraisal is put forward and the use of profit - cost method described systematically to analyze the economic benefit of heavy haul combined trains. Firstly the computer is used to determine the compensation coefficient of the parallel train working diagram for different pairs of combined trains running on the same railway lines or sections, and the cost and benefit of the combined trains are calculated as per established system cost mathematical model and system benefit mathematical model. The optimum alternatives for operating different combined trains on a line or a section are worked out. Finally, this paper puts forward the way of increasing heavy haul transportation efficiency.
