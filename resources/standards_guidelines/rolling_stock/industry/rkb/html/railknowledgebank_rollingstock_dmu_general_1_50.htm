Title Data based models and machine learning classifiers applied to fault detection and diagnosis of Diesel-electric locomotives
 
Author Carmo, DA
Munaro, CJ
Affiliation Vale S.A., Vitória-ES, Brazil
Federal University of Espírito Santo, Vitória-ES, Brazil
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Diesel-electric locomotives play an important role in railway composition traction. Failures affect operational efficiency negatively and the time required to predict or isolate a fault is the most important factor to mitigate them. Many sensors are available in diesel engine for control and protection. However, alarms are usually provided when a shutdown is required to avoid engine damage. In this work a diagnosis system is developed to anticipate failures in the diesel engine cooling systems. Data from sensors is used to fit data-based models to generate residuals that are used for fault detection. Features of these residuals are used to train three classifiers for fault diagnosis: artificial neural network, decision trees and k-nearest neighbors. Changes in the rotation speed are considered since all signals are affected and may produce false alarms. Different data sets were used for training, validation and test, using data produced with the diesel engine under normal and faulty operation. Two faults were considered: reduction in the pressure of the cooling water and leakage. The tests showed that both faults were correctly diagnosed in less than 10s, which is a very good result since the engine could operate for more than 1h under these faulty conditions.

Title Passive safety concepts applied to TER X72500 diesel railcar
 
Author Cleon, LM
Legait, J
Leveque, D
Tritz, B
Huss, P
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract For several years, passive safety of railways vehicles has been a subject of special attention in France. SNCF (French national railway company) has imposed stringent protection criteria for personal safety in collisions situations on every new item of rolling stock. For this purpose, crumple zones are integrated into train structures to dissipate the impact energy during a crash. The first step consists in determining the required energy absorption capacities based upon a reference collision. The chosen reference accident is a collision at a level crossing at a speed of 110 km/h against a 15 ton truck. The crash scenario is then divided into 4 steps. The first step is situated inside the automatic coupler and designed to absorb energy. Second and third steps absorb medium speed impacts through the deformation of stainless steel or composite absorbers. For more violent impacts, a fourth step absorbs impact energy through plastic deformation of the driver's cab. The three first steps are interchangeable. All four steps are simulated and tested individually, then compiled by sub-assemblies prior to merging them into one single test involving the whole, i.e. both energy absorption area and non deformable passenger compartment area. Numerical simu­lations performed on meshes of 600 000 finite elements, based upon former tests, are essential as regards final test preparations.

Title Operational effectiveness of brake energy recovery systems for rail vehicles
 
Author Hesse, R
Flenker, H
Kettner, J
Seifert, A
Affiliation lndustrieanlagen-Betriebsgesellschaff mbH, Offobrunn, Germany
Deutsche Bahn AG, Berlin, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The use of a brake energy recovery system makes sense for dynamically operated rail vehicles with frequent acceleration and braking phases. The kinetic energy released during braking is stored in the vehicle and made available for the next acceleration phase. The primary aims of the investigations performed were, on the one hand, to select an appropriate storage system and, on the other hand, to quantify the fuel savings achieved with the selected storage system and the reductions of emissions for vehicles with internal combustion engines. For the computer simulation a reference-vehicle with diesel engine of DB AG was defined. Due to the verification of the simulation results the data for the most important operational parameters, such as running speed, haulage power, revolutions of the diesel engine, etc. are recorded under real operating conditions, and made available for the simulation. The simulation model for the description of the vehicle dynamics was verified initially using the measurement data of the conventional vehicle. Thereafter the operational behavior of the vehicle with storage unit was analyzed in detail. The operating results of the storage vehicle were then determined for various drive cycles and compared with the operating result of the conventional vehicle. Varyingly large storage systems were used for the storage vehicle as parameters with varying energy contents at the beginning of the run. The investigations for the real driving cycles show that, by using the reference vehicle equipped with diesel-electric propulsion and a rotation storage unit is advantageous for, compared to operations with a conventionally powered diesel-hydraulic vehicle, fuel consumption savings, highly depended on type of operation, of between 15 and 30% can be achieved. The freely selectable characteristic operations for the diesel engine effects a considerable reduction of the exhaust emissions (CO, HC, NOx and soot), in particular for nitrogen oxide and soot.

Title Comparison of CNG- and diesel-propulsion with reference to flexibility, consumption and emissions and initial experience of operational use
 
Author Schweiger, HP
Flenker, H
Pfretzschner, P
Affiliation Industrieanlagen-Betriebsgesellschaft mbH, Ottobrunn, Germany
Deutsche Bahn AG, Munchen, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The natural gas engine as the propulsion for rail vehicles represents an efficient alternative to the diesel engine, given its favorable exhaust rating, with the reduction of pollutants under operating conditions. In contrast to the diesel engine, the combustion for the natural gas engine is soot-free, whereby the use of a three-way catalytic converter is possible. At DB AG, two lightweight rail cars LVT772. 1 / LVS972.7 have been converted from diesel to natural gas on the basis of CNG technology for test purposes and are undergoing operational trials. To assess the direct axis dynamics, the fuel consumption and the pollutant emission levels, the natural gas vehicle and the diesel-driven reference vehicle were simulated with the help of appropriate computer programs for various routes, compared with one another and assessed. To reach an optimum the drive behaviour of the train, important parameters, e.g. the gear change points of the automatic gearbox, were changed and assessed for the natural gas vehicle. It is shown that the pollution emission levels for NOx, CO. HC and soot can be reduced by about 75% to almost 100% (depending upon components), the CO 2 emission level can be reduced by up to 12%. With respect to the restricted space conditions for the gas storage unit the ranges are looked at in a greater detail. Initial experiences of operational use are described.

Title The development of a diesel engine acceptance test for British railways
 
Author Benyon, JA
Razden, D
Draper, EJ
Affiliation British Railways Board, Railway Technical Centre, Derby, UK
Conference Institution of Mechanical Engineers 1987: International Conference, Diesel Locomotives for the Future, 7-9 April 1987, York, UK
Publication Date 1987
Abstract Diesel Engines in traction use on BR experience a fluctuating loading pattern not normally seen in other large diesel applications. This makes the engine particularly vulnerable to fatigue failures. To ensure that diesel engines which are purchased in the future are better suited to railway environments, British Rail have developed an acceptance testing procedure based on consideration of metal fatigue. The control and execution of the test are the responsibility of the engine manufacturer. The type test is also used to provide a declaration of performance parameters, fuel consumption, noise, and oil carry over. Experience of testing to a fatigue regime has vindicated British Rails user requirement. Several engine types have been tested to date, the defects revealed ranging in severity from "nuisance" through "expensive" to "potentially catastrophic", Quality defects have also been revealed.

Publication Date 2019
Abstract For the purpose of a reduction of environmental burden and an improvement of energy efficiency of railway systems, we (RTRI) have been developing a fuel cell hybrid powered railway vehicle since the year 2001. From the year 2006, we assembled the fuel cell hybrid powered test railway vehicle and have carried out the running tests in our test facilities for over ten years to confirm whether a fuel cell is applicable to a power source for a railway vehicle traction. From the results of the evaluations, it demonstrates that the fuel cell is applicable to the power source for the railway vehicle traction. However, the sizes of the fuel cell and the power converters were large, and they had to be installed inside the railway vehicle instead of under vehicle floor. Also, the vehicle acceleration was as slow as local type diesel railway vehicles. In order to resolve these issues, we developed the compact and light weight fuel cell module with high power and the integrated power converter, and installed them under the vehicle floor. Hereby, we secured the space for passengers in railway vehicles. Furthermore, we increased the capacities of fuel cell power and battery energy, and improved the tractive performance up to electric railway vehicles.

Title Multi-scale evaluation of diesel commuter rail fuel use, emissions, and eco-driving
 
Author Yuan, W
Frey, H
Affiliation North Carolina State University, Raleigh, NC, USA
Source ATRI
Journal Transportation Research Part D: Transport and Environment
Publication Date 2021-10
Abstract Diesel commuter rail emissions affect populations near rail corridors. An approach is demonstrated to quantify mesoscale and microscale diesel commuter rail fuel use and emission rates (FUERs) of CO2, CO, NOx, particulate matter, and total hydrocarbons based on two U.S. systems. A speed trajectory simulator, an energy model, and an emissions model were calibrated, evaluated, and applied. FUERs and potential reductions from eco-driving were quantified based on simulated trajectories. Hotspots were defined as sections with >= 90th percentile of section-average FUERs by species. A few key variables explain 74–80% of variability in mesoscopic and microscopic FUERs. On average, FUERs are 7–8 times greater for hotspots than non-hotspots. Eco-driving was estimated to reduce segment-average FUERs by 3–33% and eliminate 2–11% hotspots. However, mesoscale oriented eco-driving can, at some locations, increase microscopic FUERs. The approach is adaptable to other diesel commuter rail systems.
