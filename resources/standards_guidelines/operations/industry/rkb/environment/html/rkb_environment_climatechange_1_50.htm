Title Studying the impact of freeze thaw cycles on performance of railway tracks
 
Author Roghani, A
Cobo, JH
Charbachi, P
Affiliation National Research Council Canada, Ottawa, Canada
Engineer, VIA Rail, Montreal, Canada
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Freeze-thaw phenomenon is known to have an impact on railway track performance and is expected to increase in frequency over the next 50 years due to the climate change. The frost heave and thaw softening result in degradation of track geometry that may lead to unsafe operating conditions and are more detrimental for rail transit and passenger rail systems as their higher operating speed makes them much less tolerant to deviations in track geometry parameters. There is, however, little publicly available data on their quantitative impacts on railway operations. Such quantitative data is essential for determining when, where and to what extent climate adaptation measures are needed. The National Research Council of Canada (NRC) is working on an Infrastructure Canada funded project, Climate Resilient Building and Core Public Infrastructure (CRB CPI) project, to develop decision support tools, including guidelines and models for the design of resilient new buildings and rehabilitation of existing buildings in key sectors to ensure that existing and future climate change and extreme weather events are addressed. The infrastructure of interest includes railway tracks. The study presented in this paper is part of the CRB CPI project and aims to investigate the impact of freeze-thaw phenomenon on the performance of railway tracks. This investigation consisted of several different measurement technologies that aimed to monitor track behavior during the freeze-thaw cycles. These technologies are being tested over a section of VIA rail’s track in eastern Ontario, which has historically experienced frost heave issues. This paper is focused on introducing the various methods used to monitor track behavior and presents some preliminary results.

Title An analysis of weather related resilience of the dutch network, with a focus on water related risks
 
Author Zoeteman, A
Hazelaar, AHM
Woning, M
Affiliation ProRail & TU Delft, Delft, the Netherlands
ProRail, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Deltares, Delft, the Netherlands
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract This paper presents an analysis of the impacts of climate change on the Dutch rail network, with a focus on the vulnerability to water problems from extreme rainfall and flooding. The number of extreme warm days for the Netherlands is expected to double and the temperature is expected to grow by double the amount of the world’s average. This causes more extreme summer weather, such as extreme rainfall and lightning. The paper focuses on a specific analysis of the consequences of current and future sensitivity of the railway network related to water. This so-called ‘blue areas analysis’ indicated the likely impact on railway tracks and yards related to extreme rainfall, ground water and flooding risk. This has given an insight into the resilience of current railway design in its natural (and built-up) environment, which is a tool to analyse possible mitigation measures. The railway needs to prepare in time for such changes, due to the long cycles of infrastructure renewal and upgrading. The sooner design changes are determined, the better affordable the implementation can be through timely linking them to the planning of track renewals. Also it can be a tool to start discussion with the owners of other infrastructure systems that interrelate with the railway or that determine boundary conditions or cause problems to the resilience of railway infrastructure (e.g. drainage capacity).

Title How railway administrations can work together to address weather and climate change resilience
 
Author Quinn, A
Palin, E
Sanderson, M
Hanlon, H
Dora, J
Affiliation School of Engineering, University of Birmingham, UK
Met Office Hadley Centre, Exeter, UK
John Dora Consulting Limited, UK
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In recent years, extreme weather events have caused substantial disruption to Great Britain's (GB) railway infrastructure. In coming decades this vulnerability is unlikely to subside as the effects of climate change become more intense. Railway stakeholders in GB are strongly engaged with understanding climate change impacts on the railway system and how the industry could adapt to these impacts. Since 2010, Network Rail and RSSB have supported research into these topics under the “Tomorrow’s Railway and Climate Change Adaptation” programme. Under this programme, an analogue study was performed to determine where best practice lessons could be learned from other countries’ weather management. Two types of analogue were used to identify suitable locations. First, climate data from twenty models of the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project phase 5 (CMIP5) were used to identify regions with similar present-day climate to that projected for GB in future, for the mid- and end of the twenty-first century. The analogue locations were found to be largely insensitive to the climate indicators and the methods used to compare climate at different locations. Next, railway networks in many countries were studied to find those with similar physical and operational characteristics to the GB network. Those regions with both climate and railway analogues are France, Netherlands, Belgium, Germany and Denmark. As part of a wider aim to support the GB railway network’s weather resilience and climate change adaptation (WR/CCA) activities, focused stakeholder engagement has been undertaken with representatives of most of these countries’ railways. This targeted approach suggests a partnership method for railway administrations is a useful approach to improve preparedness and disseminate best practice.

Title Resilience of guided transport systems against natural risks
 
Author Gonzva, M
Barroca, B
Gautier, P. E
Diab, Y
Affiliation SYSTRA, Innovation Directoriate, Paris, France
Paris-Est Marne-la-Vallée University, Lab’Urba Laboratory, Champs-sur-Marne, France
School of Engineering of the City of Paris (EIVP), France
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The vulnerability of guided transport systems exposed to natural disasters is demonstrated in particular through the part of incidents on international railway networks due to intense weather conditions. Moreover, in the future, the frequencies of natural hazards will likely be intensified by climate change, increasing the consequences on transport systems and thus on railway mobility. The concept of resilience brings a new way of analyzing the impacts of natural risks on technical systems according to a systematic approach. Resilience can be defined as the capacity, for a system, to absorb changes and to persist beyond a disturbance. This approach offers the opportunity to investigate the complex interactions between a transport system and a natural hazard by analyzing the failures caused by cascade effect within the system. All these failures can be determined by using a combination of dependability and safety methods: the functional analysis, the failure mode and effects analysis and the event tree analysis. This methodology allows to build a tool based on the identification of the scenarios of successive failures that allows to assess related costs and repair times. The Prague subway flooding in 2002 is considered as a case study application of this tool.

Abstract The weather experienced by the British Isles is perhaps unique; influenced by the Gulf Stream and by the European land mass it exhibits significant variations in climate and weather patterns. These patterns are so varied that even the UK's world-renowned Met Office has been known to comment on the 'weird weather", after 2012 saw the driest spring for over a century give way to the wettest recorded April to June. While these recent weather patterns cannot be ascribed to climate change, they illustrate the challenges faced by weather and climate forecasters and by organisations in Britain, including railway operators. This paper will describe the latest learning on the resilience of railway systems to current and potential future weather events, emerging from the Rail Safety and Standards Board (RSSB) 'T1009 Tomorrow's Railway and Climate Change Adaptation' (T1009 TRaCCA) programme. T1009 TRaCCA was authorised in June 2012 with a £2M budget for the Foundation Projects staged over a two year period, within a five-year programme. The project builds upon a previous short study 'T925 TRaCCA', which provided the first insight into how climate change might impact future railway operations.

Title Saving energy in railway management with an evolutionary multiobjective algorithm: application and case study
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Chevrier, R
Marlière, G
Rodriguez, J
Affiliation Université Lille Nord de France, Lille
IFSTTAR-INRETS ESTAS, Villeneuve d?Ascq, France
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract In recent years, concerns due to pollution and global warming led to the development of more eco-aware transportation systems. Energy spent in railway management is used to move trains and if speed tuning is well suited, energy consumption will be lower than in other cases. Since the acceleration phases consume a huge quantity of energy, it is necessary to tune speeds according to distances, slopes and the timetable in order to avoid an unnecessary sequence of brake/acceleration phases. Also, given that the trains have a big inertia, it is judicious to use this physical property by stopping the engine and letting the train advance just thanks to the initial force. Railway management involves multiple objectives which are often antagonist, such as travel duration and energy consumption. Although travel duration had often the preference of decision makers, in recent years the criterion of energy consumption has been considered from an equivalent point of view. Indeed, global energy saving has become the new challenge of transportation systems including railway. Work has been led for analytically computing speed tuning (ST) solutions according to several levels of delay tolerance but to the authors' knowledge, there are, as yet, few multi-objective approaches. These are based on Differential Evolution for mass transit systems or evolutionary algorithms hybridized or not. Nevertheless, to the authors' knowledge, few approaches propose to optimize energy consumption which becomes the new challenge of the decade. Thus, in this paper the authors deal with a multi-objective optimization of ST with energy saving. These concurrently optimized criteria are: the minimization of the travel duration; the minimization of energy consumption and punctuality (minimization of delays, which is quite different to reducing the travel duration). According to a timetable, delays occurring at stations can be worked out and used in a multi-criteria search. Whatever the criteria under consideration, the main goal consists in designing ST solutions diversified enough to help decision makers to choose the solution most adapted to their needs.

Title ERRAC freight roadmapping
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Olsson, B
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Transport plays a vital role within the economy and society: transport systems are of crucial importance for the competitiveness of any nation or regional economy as well as for the mobility of its citizens. However, while they bring significant benefits to society there are also a substantial cost. The current growth of the transport sector is far from being sustainable. Transport has been focused as a main policy area where further environmental and overall efficiency improvements are critical for a sustainable future of Europe. Transportation is responsible for the major part of the increase in oil consumption during the last three decades, a trend that is expected to increase. In the EU the environmental footprint of transport corresponds to 24% of greenhouse gases and almost 30% of CO2. As the sector is to more than 95% dependent on fossil fuels, the environmental concerns are well aligned with efforts to improve energy security (COM(2009) 279). Globally, the transport sector accounts for approximately 60% of the oil consumption and approximately 20% of the global, energy-related emissions of greenhouse gases. During the last 10 years, the efforts to decouple GDP growth from freight transport growth has not succeeded, the demand for freight transport has increased with 2.7% while GDP increased with 2.5%. The whole transport system needs indeed to be restructured and reorganized.

Abstract The Royal Commission on Environmental Protection study 'Adapting Institutions to Climate Change', has identified 'a growing recognition at a global, European and UK level of the need to adapt to climate change'. However, despite commitments to control the effects of climate change, by reducing carbon emissions for example, it is recognised that some impacts are inevitable and action will be necessary to mitigate the negative impacts. In common with other critical infrastructure upon which society depends, the providers of railway transport systems will firstly need to assess the disruptive effects of critical weather on the performance of the existing transport system, in order to inform the development of appropriate policies and standards for mitigation, which take the impacts of climate change into account. Within the UK railway industry, work has been ongoing over the past decade to identify the impacts of critical weather events on the railway, to establish what are the impacts of climate change, and to develop methodologies for adaptation of the railway to mitigate the impacts of critical weather and climate change. The research discussed in this paper, has built on the knowledge from previous research aimed at development of the tools required by the GB railway industry, to provide a reliable railway into the future. The methodology has been developed by the Met Office Hadley Centre.

Title Inhalable railroad particles at ground level and subterranean stations: physical and chemical properties and relation to train traffic
 
Author Gustafssona, M
Blomqvista, G

Journal Transportation Research Part D: Transport and Environment
Publication Date 2012
Abstract The paper compares PM10 concentrations in railroad environments with EU air quality standards and characterizes particle concentrations and particle properties in relation to train traffic. The results show that PM10 concentrations in ground-level railroad environments do not exceed the EU directive 24-h limit value, while on the platforms of the two subterranean stations PM10 concentrations were far above the outdoor limit value. Diurnal and weekly patterns in PM10 concentration could be identified, co-varying with train traffic intensity. The particle mass size distribution peaked at around 2–6 µm. The elemental composition of PM10 at the subterranean stations was dominated by Fe.
