Title Low-frequency environmental vibrations: excitation mechanism
 
Author Andreasson, B
Affiliation WSP Civils Sweden
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Conference Railway Foundations: RailFound 06: 1st International conference on railway foundations: applied research and application to underpin improved railway track design and performance, 11-13 September 2006, Birmingham, UK
Publication Date 2006
Abstract It is a well-known fact that low-frequency vibrations ( < 20 Hz) governed primarily by train configuration/loads often develop in the surroundings of railways. However, the excitation mechanism is not fully understood. This paper describes a likely source of low-frequency propagating waves - irregular sleeper support. As long as successive sleepers have identical support stiffness no propagating waves develop. The moving load successively applied to identical sleepers only produces a continuous rotation motion mode in the ground with no dynamics or wave propagation. However, the support stiffness of individual sleepers in any track varies. These irregular sleeper reactions disrupt the mentioned continuous rotation motion mode thus producing propagating waves. These waves are mainly governed by the train/load geometry producing low-frequency vibrations. Based on this mechanism mitigation measures have been carried out at a site in Sweden in November, 2005. That project, showing effective reduction of vibrations, is presented in a companion paper to this conference.

Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Conference Railway Foundations: RailFound 06: 1st International conference on railway foundations: applied research and application to underpin improved railway track design and performance, 11-13 September 2006, Birmingham, UK
Publication Date 2006
Abstract Since the West Coast Line was upgraded to two tracks in the 1980s environmental vibrations have caused disturbance to people in the city of Halmstad. Various mitigation alternatives have been considered over the years. The chosen solution is based on a new interpretation of the low-frequency excitation mechanism presented in a companion paper to this conference which identified irregular sleeper support stiffness to be a likely source of low-frequency vibrations. Such irregularities disrupt the harmonic deRection pattern/Row around a moving load producing propagating waves to the surroundings. By the use of resilient elements in the form of rail pads and under sleeper pads, a smoother foundation for the sleepers is produced reducing the environmental vibrations. Mitigation works were carried out in November 2005 on an initial section. Measurements carried out so far show good results and the remaining sections will be treated later this year. The soil profile at the test site consists of 4 to 8 m of dense sand over silty clay/clayey silt to some 15 m.

Title A model for rolling noise from train passbys
 
Author Chua, KH
Koh, CG
Lo, KW
Lee, SL
Affiliation National University of Singapore
Conference CORE 1993: Contracting Railways - Safety, Standards and the Surroundings, 17-20 October 1993, Newcastle, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 1993
Abstract The noise generated by rolling comes from the vibrations of the wheels and rail, due to wheel and rail roughness interaction. A lumped-parameter model for the wheel/rail interaction is employed to determine the velocity power spectra of the wheels and rails during train passbys. The effect of amplitude modulation due to non-stationary train loading has been included in the model. The driving point impedances of the rail and wheel for the model are derived through impact hammer tests of the respective components. These tests are relatively easy to perform without any elaborate set-up, requiring only an inverted bogie set and the tracks at the test yard. The rolling noise spectra can then be readily determined by treating the wheel and rail as random point and line sound radiation sources, respectively. Results obtained from the model compare well with field measurement records. The model may be reasonably employed in studying the effects of changes to the track support system on sound pressure levels. By adopting a softer pad in this case, very little difference in sound pressure would be detected. The driving point impedance of the rail for this instance was obtained via a finite element model of the track support system.

Title Impact noise at insulated rail joint
 
Author Koh, CG
Chua, KH
Lo, KW
Lee, SL
Affiliation National University of Singapore
Conference CORE 1993: Contracting Railways - Safety, Standards and the Surroundings, 17-20 October 1993, Newcastle, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 1993
Abstract This paper presents an investigation study of impact noise generated by train wheels running over rail discontinuity at an insulated rail joint. An analytical model of rigid wheel on a flexible rail is proposed. The equivalent stiffness and lumped mass of rail are computed by finite element analyses of a cantilever beam subjected to static load and impact load, respectively. A dynamic analysis is carried out to determine the separation time of wheel and rail and the resulting impact velocity. The acoustic power is assumed to be proportional to the change in kinetic energy before and after impact. The proportionality constant is obtained by calibration against published results. Field measurement was conducted to measure rail response and sound pressure level of impact noise at an insulated rail joint on a viaduct. The analytically predicted SPL is found to agree well with measured SPL.

Title Predicting the effectiveness of rail dampers on noise emissions: the first Australian trial
 
Author Zoontjens, L
Croft, B
Teng, L
Affiliation SLR Consulting Australia Pty Ltd, Perth, WA
SLR Consulting (Canada) Ltd, British Columbia, Canada
Public Transport Authority, Perth, WA
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Rail dampers are a rolling noise mitigation measure developed in Europe. The first field trails of this track component have previously been undertaken in Australia (Sydney). There, rail dampers have been shown to offer a moderate noise reduction of around 4 dB for passenger trains on slab track and resilient fasteners. On typical ballasted tracks in Sydney rail dampers are less effective and have not been found to be a cost-effective noise control measure. The benefit achievable through rail dampers varies considerably between different systems, depending on the design of the wheel and track. STARDAMP is a modelling tool to predict the noise reduction achievable by rail dampers (or wheel dampers) in a particular situation. This paper presents a study predicting the noise reduction provided by rail dampers on highly resilient slab track and on ballasted track, and comparing model results with field measurements in Perth. Both model and measurements demonstrate that noise from the Perth metropolitan passenger train network can be effectively attenuated using rail dampers. In terms of overall A-weighted levels, a rolling noise reduction of up to 5 dB was identified for passenger trains on ballasted track. In a tunnel situation with slab track (Pandrol Vanhuard), a rolling noise reduction of up to 8 dB was achieved. The effectiveness of rail dampers as a noise control measure in Perth is attributed to two main factors. Firstly, the Perth rolling stock wheel design is relatively quiet. Modelling indicates that in the undamped base camp rolling noise is dominated by rail emissions. If the wheel contributions were higher, the benefit of the dampers would be less. Secondly, both the ballasted and slab tracks tested in Perth utilise resilient elements with relatively low dynamic stiffness. As a result of these two factors, the noise control benefit of rail dampers is greater for the Perth passenger network than is the case on the Sydney passenger network. The paper outlines the modelling and field measurement approaches taken and the results of the Perth rail damper trials. The methods represent an advance in railway environmental impact assessment and forecasting noise control performance that could be applied to rail systems elsewhere.

Title Enhanced rail noise mitigation and management approaches for the Australian rail industry
 
Description The rail industry needs to manage a range of noise issues, most of which can be tackled without the need for research. However, there are two rail noise aspects for which research and industry adoption are crucial to achieving progress: Curve noise, an acute noise phenomenon that is yet to be fully explained by theory, and Rolling noise, for which the adoptiong of existing theory can enable industry to move beyond reliance on noise barriers as the only practival option for noise mitigation, particularly for rail growth projects.
Both of these issues represent significant problems and substantial cost for the Australian rail industry, in addition to the wider community. Direct costs for the industry involve: Protracted effort to mitigate and manage curve noise via monitoring, wayside detection systems, rolling stock inspections/maintenance, friction management and community interaction, The high cost burden of noise barriers for rail development projects (typically over $3m per km and 5 to 10% of total project cost).
There are also significant indirect costs. These include delays to projects owing to community resistance, reputational damage and the undermining of rail's otherwise impressive environmental credentials. But there is also emering evidence of the costs of the health impacts of transport noise in the community (estimated at $250m per annum, based on a recent European Study). Given the above, the proposed project is conservatively estimated to deliver economic benefits exceeding $12m per annum.
The project builds on the preceding CRC project (R1.105 Rail Noise Stage 1) to arrive at validated methods of noise management tailored to the Australian rail operating environment. Stage 1 established the current state of applicable knowledge and treatments for noise both for existing infrastructure and for new developments. The overriding objective of this project is to drive industry adoptiong of the research outcomes.

Abstract This paper proposes a method for separating rail and wheel noise contributions via sparse regularization of microphone array data.
