Title Measurement and presentation of wheel and rail
 
Author Dings, P
van Lier, S
Affiliation NS Technical Research, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Rolling noise is one of the main sources of railway noise. It is generated by the surface roughness (irregularities) in the contact patch between train wheel and rail. This roughness induces vibrations and subsequent sound radiation from wheels, rails and sleepers. The direct relation between wheel and rail roughness and rolling noise is addressed in this paper. By differences in the measurement, analysis and presentation methods of railway wheel and rail roughness, differences in the spectra of 10 dB or more can occur, which seriously frustrate a relevant comparison of roughness results presented in the literature. The main parameters of influence are discussed here and a standard procedure for measurement, analysis and presentation of roughness is proposed. The major findings of the research are summarised here. First, very narrow and deep holes and peaks in the signal that are not 'felt' by the wheel/rail interface, called spikes, have to be removed. Different methods can cause differences of 10 dB in the spectra. In this paper, a spike filling criterion and algorithm are presented that are recommended for general use. Second, the presentation of roughness spectra in third-octave bands of wavelengths (in decreasing order) corresponds to the presentation of sound spectra in third-octave frequency bands. Standard centre wavelength values for third-octave and octave bands are proposed. Third, several roughness indicators (single value indicators) are applied to experimental data and the most promising has been found to be the ),CA -weighted roughness, introduced in this paper. This roughness indicator correlates well with the A-weighted pass-by sound pressure level. However, for a definitive proposal more experimental data is needed. It is recommended that other researchers use the described methodology for future roughness measuring campaigns, in order to facilitate a relevant comparison of roughness results and to give a quick estimation of the produced rolling noise level.

Title R&D efforts help railways to deal with environmental restrictions
 
Author de Vos, P
Affiliation NS Technical Research, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Environmental regulations restrict the new infrastructure from being used to the full extent of its physical capacity. In the Netherlands, mainly noise limits (and to a certain extent also risc and vibration limits) fully determine the capacity of surface lines and depots. Noise and vibration related research and development used to be aiming at a better understanding of the phenomena of noise generation. Succesfull cooperation between European railways (both within and outside the ERRI) has contributed significantly to this objective. Today, not only do we understand the basic phenomena of noise and are we able to design and shape solutions, but also have we achieved an infrastructure of knowledge, where railway r&d bodies and external research institutes throughout Europe are working in close cooperation on a wide range of topics. The re-organisation of railways has introduced an uncertainty about the future of their research bodies. After all, which of the many different units resulting from the former railways being split up is now responsible for maintaining a large infrastructural capacity ? Indeed this would only create possibilities for new competitors on the existing lines! What will motivate a freight line operator to buy quieter vehicles as long as the infrastructure controller puts up noise barriers in order to increase the capacity of his line ? And how will the lack of capacity be distributed amongst the possible users: is it still wise to run noisy freight trains particularly during the night, the period of the day where the limits are the most stringent and thus the capacity is the smallest ? More than ever, research efforts should be lined up with such strategic questions. If traditional customers are no longer willing to provide r&d resources, then the installation of a more substantial research fund, either per individual railway or common, should be seriously considered.

Title Relationship between the performance level of railway drivers and particular environmental variables
 
Author Olivo, A
Cecere, E
Coni, M
Affiliation University of Cagliari, Cagliari, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Recent research studies show that there is a negative effect on the visual function of railway drivers, which is attributable to the noise level. More precisely in the subjects examined a particular incidence has been found on stereoscopic vision, the capabilityof recognising colours, twilight vision, night vision and visual function. It has also been proved clinically that tiredness due to noise can be directly associated with loss in the general performance of drivers. As regards visual performance, it has been experimented that particular occipital areas associated with vision are negatively affected by noise, and that at 70-75 dBA examined subjects generally show a phenomenon called midriasis, that has a negative influence on their visual function. It is also true that this phenomenon is significantly manifest with lower but longlasting noise levels. This disturbance affects the accommodative faculty, the perception of depth and the evaluation of relative distances between two objects moving at different speeds. It has clearly emerged, therefore, how important it can be, in designing safe railway infrastructures, to take into account the level of performance of drivers. This paper first presents an analysis aimed at a preliminary assessment of the different causes of inside cabin noise. Subsequently the effects of this noise on the behaviour of drivers and consequently on their performance are studied. Among the main sources of noise are the wheel/rail contact, the pantograph/line contact, aerodynamic penetration, and the engine. Their incidence varies with vehicle speed. Noise is also affected by several other parameters that depend on the unballasted permanent way, the superstructure, traffic, the vehicle itself and environmental and secondary considerations. Other situations like vibrational stress, shaking and microclimate conditions, that are not always optimal, determine a decrease in the level of performance of drivers. Particularly time is a critical factor in the evaluation of human exposure to whole-body vibration. While the effects of high frequency vibration on visual acuity are not time-dependent, those of lower frequency motion have been shown to vary with vibration exposure time. Other effects on vision, and especially visual tasks, that are greatly affected by attention, may well depend on exposure duration. Studies of this type have been carried out but no substantial proof of their effects is available yet. The level of attention, fatigue, and presence of competitive stimuli interfere with this complex system, and the reaction tends to strengthen the necessity of a more detailed study on the precise nature of these phenomena, as well as the need to search for a law that should be able to quantify, or at least explain, the relationship between noise and vibration and their effect on the visual ability of the drivers.

Title Sound measurements as a means of condition monitoring in light railways
 
Author Lilley, DM
Affiliation University of Newcastle, Newcastle upon Tyne, UK
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract A preliminary investigation has been made of sound levels generated by passing light trains within an underground section of the Tyne and Wear Metro system which serves the city of Newcastle in North East England. Measurements were taken over a period of several hours and have shown significant variation in the maximum values of sound pressure level generated by individual trains. The equipment used for taking measurements is essentially a sound level meter and a digital voltmeter under the control of a portable computer. Special software was written to facilitate analysis of the results. Values of sound pressure level naturally increase as trains approach the site of the equipment and decrease as they continue onto their destination. The total time taken for measurement of sound generated by a single passing train varied between about 45 s and 61 s. One train which twice generated high values of sound as it passed the measurement site was later found to have missed its regular check of wheel condition and maintenance; this work had been re-scheduled to take place shortly after the day when measurements were taken. This finding provides support for the validity of the method of condition monitoring described in the paper. The effects of other factors (such as train speed) are to be investigated in the near future, when track possession and dedicated trains become available during a normal overnight closedown of the Metro system.

Therefore we have a numerical FEM model through which it is possible to establish the acoustic behaviour of a railway tunnel, starting from any sound event located in any point inside the tunnel.

Abstract Much efforts are now spent to optimise the geometry of high-speed trains and trackside environments under aerodynamic constraints. The mastering of the train near-field air flows in steady conditions (drag reduction, flow stability) and of the unsteady I transient air flows clearly appear as major issues to increase the speed of trains. Several Computational Fluid Dynamic software packages oiler now alternatives to study parts of trains in steady aerodynamic conditions.
