Title The literature review: current and emerging issues in skills recognition as relevant to the Australian rail ndustry
 
Subtitle Skills recognition framework
Author Maher, K
Morrison, A
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Report or Manual No P4.111
Publisher CRC for Rail Innovation
Place Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2011
Physical Description 67p. ; PDF
Abstract This literature review contributes to the activities of the CRC for Rail Innovation’s Education and Training Program by discussing current and emerging issues in Skills Recognition (SR) in Australia and internationally to inform the development of a national skills recognition framework for the Australian Rail Industry. To open the review of the SR literature, the various terms used to refer to SR are discussed, most notably recognition of prior learning (RPL). The review argues that SR is a valuable aspect of assessment, training, human resource management and workplace development, and has a place beyond programs designed to accredit pre-existing learning. The relationship between SR and assessment is discussed, and the possibility of conducting SR for learning purposes rather than limiting its objective to one of gaining formal accreditation. More established forms of assessment, such as the portfolio, are considered with reference to innovative adaptations of these methods that may enhance the depth and integrity of assessment. Discussion on dialogue-based assessment methods follows with attention given to emerging methods, including e-portfolios and the use of ICT in SR assessment.

Title Rail and climate change: a scoping paper
 
Author Sheehan, P
Symons, J
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Report or Manual No R1.106
Publisher CRC for Rail Innovation
Place Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2008-11
Physical Description 17p. ; PDF
Abstract The starting point for this project is the hypothesis that there would to be major economic, social and climate benefits from a substantial modal shift of both passenger and freight traffic from road to rail, together with an extensive modernisation of rail transport and a reduction in its energy use and greenhouse emissions. Over the next decade price effects are likely to support such changes – fuel prices will remain high, and the introduction of a price for carbon through an Emissions Trading Scheme (ETS) will increase the competitiveness of rail relative to road. Yet this hypothesis that a modal shift towards an enhanced rail sector will be important is nowhere to be seen in the climate debate in Australia, which focuses mainly on electricity generation and use, on the efficiency of road and air transport and on other related matters. Nor are the relative price changes arising out of higher energy prices and an ETS alone likely to be sufficient to generate either extensive modernisation of Australia’s rail sector or such a modal shift within transport. Especially in this sector, prices do not operate in a vacuum, but in a context heavily influenced by infrastructure constraints, the sunk costs required for track and equipment, the diverse incentives facing companies, agencies and governments, and other factors.

Abstract This report presents the complete work of the CRC for Rail Innovation Project R1.115 Future Power Technologies. The main aim of the project was to evaluate existing and emerging locomotive technologies and suggest environmentally and economically more efficient technologies that can be implemented by the Australian rail industry to deal with an increasingly carbon-constrained environment. Firstly, an energy usage analysis of existing and hybrid technologies is conducted using numerical simulation for two typical heavy haul track routes (Track #1 and Track #2) and one freight heavy haul operation on coastal undulating track (Track #3). Title On-train experience
 
Author Newby, L
Skinner, V
Anastasi, J
Reimer, W
Toft, Y
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Report or Manual No R1.121
Publisher CRC for Rail Innovation
Place Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2011-02
Physical Description 32p. ; PDF
Abstract Rail operators have traditionally relied on customer satisfaction surveys to understand the factors that impact on customer experience and shape investment decisions. Our research shows that these techniques are often flawed and should not be exclusively relied on for operator investment decisions. This report examines the state of knowledge regarding factors that influence customers’ perceptions of satisfaction and service quality in the on-train component of urban rail travel. The report highlights limitations with existing studies and discusses how on-train factors influence rail travel decisions. Importantly, differences in customer profile and journey profile lead to qualitatively different service expectations among customers. National sustainable development aims for a clean physical environment and an inclusive society requires a modal transportation shift toward the use of public transport. There is evidence that such a shift is slowly occurring, particularly in major cities across the world. Research indicates that accessibility, convenience, service quality and personnel are the primary determinants of the uptake of public transportation. Additionally, affordable fare structures (cost), accessibility, reliability and frequency of service (convenience) and comfort form the basis for primary customer expectations. Hence, direct improvements to on-train factors (e.g., seating, cleanliness etc) may only be beneficial if primary considerations (e.g., pricing, convenience, reliability) are of a high standard, or when there is strong competition between competing transport choices. To encourage potential passengers with varying individual characteristics and journey profiles to travel by train, rail networks need to develop differentiated products and adopt a segmented marketing strategy. One potential solution to this problem may be the examination of the value of operating different carriage designs for different journey profiles (e.g., different seating configurations at different times of day, or for short versus longer commutes). The report concludes that rail operators should 1) be cautious when making investment decisions based on customer satisfaction ratings on account of the flaws and inconsistencies in reported data, and 2) that customer perceptions need to be managed. Currently, improvements in service quality and safety are not accompanied by corresponding increases in customers’ perceptions of service quality and safety. Communication technology available to rail network management within the controlled rail environment needs to be utilised as a cost-effective means of providing information to existing customers about the fulfilment of their transportation wants to encourage satisfaction with the product and repeat purchase. Broader dissemination of information through media that reaches potential customers is also worthy of consideration (e.g., via mobile technology). Rail operators need to investigate ways in which information delivery and perceptions about rail services can be improved.

This project canvassed current Australian practice and international best practice to develop and estimate time series and cross-sectional models of rail patronage for Australian mainland state capital cities. Abstract There is currently limited scientific knowledge of the human factors that contribute to pedestrians’ risky behaviour at level crossings, despite their elevated risk of being fatally injured if struck by a train. This program of research aimed to conduct an in-depth analysis of pedestrians’ decision making processes that contribute to (and have the greatest impact upon) errors and deliberate violations at railway crossings e.g., human factors. The research employed mixed methods including a literature review, focus groups (n = 44), a quantitative questionnaire (n = 619) as well as in-depth interviews (n = 40).
