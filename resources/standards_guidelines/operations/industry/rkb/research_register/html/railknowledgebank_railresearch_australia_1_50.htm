Research Project
 
Title Understanding urban rail travel: behaviour for improved patronage forecasting
 
Description Over the last decade, Australian rail operators have been confronted with unexpected increases and changing patterns with respect to urban rail patronage. These changes have placed considerable stress on capacities and service levels in some areas and have resulted in excess capacity in others, all of which presented new challenges to rail managers and have resulted in negative community feedback to state governments. Existing travel models and forecasting approaches failed to predict adequately or explain these changes in rail patronage and thus rail managers, while planners were not adequately supported in their endeavours to plan for changes in patronage or manage these changes when they emerged. This project seeks to significantly improve capabilities in understanding and forecasting urban rail trip making behaviours and provide clear guidelines for the development of better forecasting and analytical models. Current state of technology and research to date:
-Current trip forecasting models have evolved from methods with their basis in the 1950s. Although considerable progress has been made in the underlying estimation methods, the basic framework of most approaches still embodies a linear four-step process reflecting 1970s thinking.
-In addition, many Australian models are inherently 'transit' forecasting and estimation models rather than 'rail trip making' models, and they embed generalised empirical parameters rather than ones specific to urban rail travel. As a consequence current Australian models are inherently limited in their ability to forecast and explain urban rail trip making behaviours. Research has also indicated that a significant proportion of the recent increase in urban rail patronage might be explained by factors outside those contained in models currently in use in Australia. Benefits of the project for industry
-better understanding and modelling of trip generating explanatory factors will assist urban rail operators to forecast changing demands on the system and better manage for these changes. This will lead to better planning and utilisation of rail operating and capital works budgets, higher levels of service delivery, and enhanced financial performance and viability of rail operators.
The project will provide these benefits through:
o Development of a comprehensive multi city database of rail and non-rail user attributes perceptions and attitudes.
o Documentation of the relative importance and influences on rail patronage of macro, micro and behavioural factors.
o Provision of best practice guidelines for the development of comprehensive rail patronage analysis and forecasting models, potentially with the development of a pilot model.
Industry will be able to incorporate these project outcomes into current strategic planning and management processes.
An industry priority
- Industry representatives have indicated the need to develop a finer grained understanding of factors that influence urban rail trip making decisions, both from a geographic and time-frame perspective. In particular, a better understanding is needed of the factors critical to target groups in the community.
-Industry has for some years also been working with travel planning models that do not have sufficient power to adequately support urban rail planning and management decisions. This shortcoming handicaps planners and managers alike in their allocation of capital works and operational budgets, thereby resulting in lower levels of service delivery than desirable.

Research Project
 
Title Strategies to address future urban passenger rail growth
 
Description This project aims to: identify priority strategies to address the expected growth in urban passenger rail, in particular approaches to sustain the viability of rail network services and build/increase operating ratios. The problem: In response to the projected growth in the urban passenger task, increased use of urban passenger rail is being planned by Governments in most major cities across Australia. A major challenge is provision of government funding for passenger transport services, as the current operating ratio of passenger rail fare-box revenue to operating costs for Australian capital cities is generally in the range of 30-40%. Comparable cities in Europe and North America operate in the range of 70-80% cost recovery. The scoping research project suggests that there is scope for improvement in both the effectiveness and efficiency of rail network operations around Australia. The approach: This project aims to identify current and emerging strategies, in both the domestic and international contexts, to deliver sustainable growth and to achieve government and community expectations. These include a range of complementary strategies such as building customer satisfaction, improving inter-modal integration, building patronage on lower utilised services, reviewing fare structures, improving the efficiency of the operation of rail network and services, broadening business strategies, and improving institutional arrangements. The approaches most appropriate for the Australian context will then are collated ranked and developed in collaboration with industry partners. The potential benefits to industry include: identifying practices in international and Australian jurisdictions that can potentially improve the effectiveness and efficiency of urban passenger rail services and help sustain patronage growth, thereby satisfying a broad array of community expectations and reduce the demand for public sector subsidy. This project is considered a priority for industryas it aims to address key challenges being faced by all rail transit agencies and operators.

Title Strategic information for building an effective light rail network in the Australian context
 
Description Many Australian metropolitan regions are facing substantial traffic problems. The provision of an effective public transport system to facilitate desirable urban and economic growth looms as one of Australia's greatest challenges. Since 1990, several new light rail systems have been built or proposed in Australia, at a cost of billions of dollars in public and private money. There has, however, been incomplete discussion of either existing or proposed light rail systems. Light rail systems have the potential in the right settings to offer urban Australian communities a new mode of public transport. There are also many barriers hindering the wider take up of light rail and a range of issues that need to be tackled for the application of light rail technology in Australia?s urban environment. This project represents the first step in revitalising the potential for appropriate public transport infrastructure networks in Australia and provides directions for further investigation. It focuses primarily on recent information drawn from examples of international best practice relating to light rail networks, and examines the critical success factors for light rail in the Australian context.

Research Project
 
Title Station access: new evaluation and implementation tools
 
Description This project aims to: determine measures and strategies for improving access to urban passenger rail stations and hence customer experience, increasing access and egress capacity and hence overall ease-of-use for urban passenger rail transport. The problem: Station access is a key component of customer experience of an overall rail journey, which is determined by what occurs the beginning of the trip (getting to the station) and at the end of the trip (from station to end destination). Encouraging walking, cycling and using public transport to rail stations has the potential to increase rail patronage while reducing the need to provide extensive car parking facilities in the longer term. There is limited detailed understanding of rail station access planning on how to best accommodate each access mode, how to enhance access by preferred modes, or how to manage conflicts between them. The approach: To develop new tools for assessing access to urban passenger rail stations and to improve access through proactive planning and design. Potential analysis approaches and metrics for application in Australia will be developed in collaboration with industry, to provide cost effective approaches. The potential benefits to industry include: enable better customer experience and attraction for overall journeys involving rail; provide faster, more competitive overall journey times for users through better access and egress; enhance cost effectiveness in capital works; improve scoping and project definition for station redevelopment and have evaluation techniques for assessing quality-of-service in station access. This project is considered a priority for industry as it aims to address key challenges being faced by all major urban rail passenger agencies and operators.

Research Project
 
Title Contestability between road and rail for non-bulk freight
 
Description Both road and rail play important roles in realizing Australia's diverse freight task as each mode has attributes that render it more suitable, and generally less costly, for particular tasks. For example, the flexibility of road transport for urban goods distribution is readily accepted. Equally, the scale economies of rail over longer distance and bulk commodities advantage it over road for these tasks. There is also a middle ground where both road and rail are used for the same goods, in some cases competing for freight. Inter-state non-bulk freight is a prime example where road and rail compete for freight traffic. The majority of this freight when carried by rail is containerised in some form. Estimates and projections for freight movements and data on freight transport contestability between rail and road are notoriously unreliable, with cohesive sets of meaningful data from across the entire contestable freight sector being unavailable at present. As a result of the difficulty of carrying out much needed modelling work, the rail industry is keen to better understand the key variables affecting demand for non-bulk freight, and ultimately the potential for non-bulk intermodal rail freight movements into the foreseeable future. This project represents the first step in examining the generation and attraction of freight flows on the freight corridors and examining the underlying economic drivers behind freight movement in Australia. It focuses primarily on developing a profile of industry and business activity in the catchment area and reviewing the methods to be used in identifying the size of the freight market which road and rail can contest. It then completes the proposal for the full study and provides building capacity to undertake the full study.
Project number or code R1.135
Status Completed

Title Analytic tools for human factors evaluation of new in-cab and train control technologies
 
Description Background:
Rapid advancements in train control and in-cab technologies provide opportunities for rail operators to improve efficiency and enhance operations. However, new technologies are a significant form of risk. Neglect of human factors issues can mean that new technologies have unintended consequences in the work environment (particularly in the more challenging situations) leading to either rejection by users or to situations where the job is made more difficult rather than easier.
Objective:
This project aims to develop a toolkit of methods that human factors specialists can use to evaluate new technologies before they are implemented in trains and other safety critical train operation related environments.
Outcomes:
This project will provide the Australian rail industry with a standardised method to evaluate the human factors issues associated with introducing new technology. There is a high probability that this project will reduce the costs associated with introducing technology with significant usability issues.
Benefits:
Developing such a toolkit of methods has the potential to reduce the risk associated with introducing new technologies. It is expected that the outputs of this research will become an industry standard for the assessment of new technologies.

Title Safety case for driver only operations
 
Description Currently, two driver operations are the most common way to operate freight and coal trains in most parts of Australia. However, (Driver Only Operations; DOO) are becoming more frequent. Moving from the traditional 'two-up' driver system to DOO may result in increases in single driver workload as well as changes in likelihood of error and the types of errors made. Therefore, the aim of the proposed research program is to examine the decision-making, error profile and performance of single-driver operations compared to two-driver operations. Specifically, this study will examine the circumstances under which DOO operations are likely to be appropriate (i.e. depending on safeworking system, track characteristics, train consist etc.). Indeed, there may be conditions where DOO may represent a safety improvement relative to dual driver operations. The proposed project will involve a mixed-methods approach, consisting of three complimentary phases:
(1) Focus groups will be conducted to explore the factors that drivers feel may be related to safe operation of the train in single and dual driver systems;
(2) A series of in-cab observations of ?two-up? and DOO operations will be conducted to evaluate driver workload and error tolerance; and
(3) Informed by Phases 1 and 2, scenarios will be developed and tested in the simulator to compare 'two-up' and DOO.
Systematic examination of the differences between 'two-up' and DOO in rail will allow informed implementation of DOO, which is already an increasing trend in the Australian rail Industry. In particular, understanding of the differences in workload for the single driver, and overall error tolerance of the system is critical for safe implementation of DOO. This research will generate evidence-based guidelines for a standard national safety case for single driver operations in Australia.
Project number or code R2.117
Status Completed

Title Understanding pedestrian behaviour at level crossings
 
Description There is currently limited scientific knowledge of the human factors that contribute to the risky behaviour of pedestrians at level crossings, despite pedestrians being considered a high risk group most likely involved in a fatality. What is currently known is that pedestrian collisions are more likely to occur at active crossings and that younger males are at an increased risk of violating crossing rules. Apart from this generic information, little is known about the situational and psychological factors that influence pedestrians' decision making and corresponding behavior at crossings.

Research Project
 
Title Track worker protection technology
 
Description The challenge of ensuring the safety of track and workers when working under traffic on track remains for rail owners. Accidents can easily occur, particularly if the track work is noisy, workers cannot hear the approach of trains on the live track. Supervisors watching the work can 'step back' close to or on to the live track on duplicated track sections. Modern electric locomotives and railcars can also be quieter, but all locomotives are relatively quiet when coasting. Industrial safety standards, legislation and community standards continue to increase the duty of care needed. The idea exist that personal safety devices could be developed for rail bridge and trackside workers. The technologies certainly are available and there are already off-the-shelf solutions. There are however implementation issues, fail to safe issues and human factors all to be considered.
Current state of Technology and Research:
Trackside worker protection devices are available in the form of train detection devices and personal alert systems that are either worn by the trackside worker or placed near the worker. Current products are available that use proven magnetic inductive technology to detect approaching rail vehicles but research throughout the world is also investigating the use of other detection methods such as rail vibration to detect approaching traffic.
A couple of CRC for Rail Innovation industry partners have and are in the process of trialling trackside worker protection devices. Where possible, it is the intention to collaborate with these partners to maximise the outcomes of the project.
Benefits for the Rail Industry
Benefits to the rail industry and community in this area could be very high in reducing accidents and deaths of track and bridge workers.
Priority
Workplaces in Australia are adopting zero harm policies to ensure the safety of their employees. This project will provide up-to-date information and assessment on new products and technology that aim to improve trackside worker safety in the Australian rail industry.
Project number or code R3.120
Status Completed
 
 
 
Research Project
 
Title High speed detection of broken rail, rail cracks and surface faults
 
Description One of the unintended consequences of increased levels of automation and new innovations in signaling, such as the Norfolk Southern Digital Railway and TPC systems that do not use track circuits (e.g. ATMS) is the increased difficulty of detection and reporting rail track conditions and broken rails. The system of track circuits has provided for many years a second function, that of identifying cracked rail. If new generation signaling and TPC systems are to be considered, these associated issues will also need solution. This area of research was recognized as strategic in future planning of AAR. On other hand, the potential threats associated with the undetected broken rail in the rail industry can be very severe in terms of public safety, economy and the environments. Hence, there is a need for development of accurate and reliable technology that can be efficiently used for detection as well as for continuous monitoring of broken rails in high speed scenarios. Therefore, this projects aims to address the following urgent research questions: (i) Can on-board instrumentation (on locomotives), or suitable trackside technology be developed to report poor rail surface conditions and cracks in non-circuited track? (ii) Is the technology accurate enough for rail crack reporting? (ii) Are there flow-on uses for monitoring RCF etc (rail grinding maintenance programs and/or rail tribology on curves).
Project number or code R3.124

Research Project
 
Title New affordable level crossings protection systems for crossings in regional areas and occupational crossings in areas with high speed passenger trains
 
Description Background:
Railway level crossing crashes are one of the most serious safety issues faced by the rail industry in Australia. There are more then 9,400 public railway level crossings in Australia, of which approximately 2,650 (30%) have 'active' protection, 6,060 have 'passive' protection and the remainder have other control or protection. The major limitation of providing active level crossing protection is the high cost of providing track-circuit-based boom gate systems. Increasing the affordability and cost-effectiveness of level crossing protection will allow a greater number of sites to be protected.
Objective:
This project will examine new technology options for level crossings that are cheaper to install and maintain than current systems. Combinations of non-vital technology such as GPS will be considered in conjunction with vital systems and human factor system elements. The project will address the safety vitality issues associated with signalling principles design.
Outcomes:
The project will exclude non-starters and avoid replicating existing products already tested in Australia and overseas. The deliverables will be in the form of an evaluation of applicability of candidate systems and recommendation of a prototype for further evaluation.
Benefits:
The industry-wide benefits of this project have been estimated at 10 percent reduction in collisions. It should be noted that the real savings are difficult to assess as it is difficult to place an accurate value on the lives potentially saved and property protected from the successful completion of this project.
Project number or code R3.111

Abstract This research is conducted under Rail CRC Project 82 to investigate bogie rotation friction management. Existing centre bearing surfaces include flame hardened AlSl 1030 steel against un-greased Bisplate 320 steel, AlSl 1053 steel against un-greased Hadfield steel, and steel against polyethylene. The wear of the longitudinal rim wall of AlSl 1053 steel, Hadfield steel, and polyethylene centre bearing components is observed. Research Project
 
Title Development of integrated wear-fatigue-lubrication model for assessment of rail-wheel degradation and operational risks
 
Project leader Gopinath Chattopadhyay
Description Enhancement of rail and wheel life is extremely important to the rail industry. Rail defects, rail breaks and derailments cost huge amounts due to cancelled / delayed traffic, emergency maintenance, loss of assets, loss of revenues and liability compensations due to down time. It is important to study the interaction of factors behind these problems, monitor those factors and find out technological solutions to reduce or eliminate those problems for enhancing rail and wheel life and reducing operational risks. The project aims to develop a decision model based on predicted operational risks,with a view to establish economic strategies for lubrication and grinding, inspection and rail replacements.

Outputs The outcomes of this research will be useful for informed strategic decisions in inspection, maintenance, rail grinding, lubrication, speed limit, axle load, traffic levels, wear limit and rail replacements. Estimated savings to the Australian rail industry could be of the order of $5 million per year.
Outcomes The main objectives of this project are the development of integrated wear-fatigue-lubrication models for predicting degradation for various curves, tonnage and traffic conditions, and the development of economic models and risk analysis for informed strategic decisions related to rail/wheel maintenance decision and operational risks.
