Abstract Some 68 publications were sourced and reviewed. The review revealed that the most widely used and accepted method of broken rail detection was the use of track circuits, made available where electric signalling systems are used. Dating to its original introduction in the late 19th century, the basic concept of the track circuit has changed little. As the use of the signalling system for broken rail detection is a secondary use, it is not optimised for detection specifically, noting that the circuits must primarily detect the presence of trains. In the wider review, it was found that there are two approaches to managing broken rail detection: Reactive â that is detecting breaks when they occur (i.e. track circuits) or Pro-active â detecting cracks that will propagate into full breaks. Detection systems can also be grouped as vehicle based (on-board) and track based (wayside). In terms of vehicle based proactive detection methods, the most common proactive sensor is the traditional ultrasonic device that has been in use since the 1950s. The improvements in ultrasonic testing include using an array of dry coupled ultrasonic piezoelectric transducers deployed around the perimeter of the rail (excluding the underside) for full rail (head, web and foot) detection of flaws or defects. An induced eddy-current device to detect and quantify breaks and surface cracks in the rail head is also promising, and it is also a proactive device. In contrast to pulsed eddy current (PEC) sensors that are required to be placed at a close and constant distance from the inspected surface, a maximum operating lift-off of 5mm is possible with tolerable reduction in the signal when using alternating current field measurement (ACFM) probes. The ACFM technique is also capable of both detecting and sizing (length and depth) of cracks in rail top surfaces. In terms of track based detection methods, whether or not a specific method is identified as a proactive one is dependent upon the sensitivity of the method to detect smaller rail check cracks. An acoustical method exists for detecting broken rails utilising transducers mounted onto the side of the rail. This is a reactive concept that is similar in several ways to track circuit-based broken rail detection in that an acoustic signal is sent from transmitter to receiver. However, the new fibre optics technology is promising for track segment condition monitoring because its length can be tens of kilometres. This new method could be considered as proactive because the method can detect both cracked and broken rails. It also can be used to detect risks of track buckling as an alternative to stress monitoring. Possible directions for future research are discussed.

This research specifically focuses on evaluating the effects of this new signage on pedestrians distracted by a mobile device (visual or audio). This project aims to assess how visual scanning and looking for train behaviour changes with the introduction of the illuminated in-ground pads as a way to confirm whether the activation of the lights are effective at attracting the attention of pedestrians distracted by mobile devices or headphones. It uses a mixed approach methodology, combining objective data measurements from an eye tracking system (where pedestrians are looking when approaching level crossings), with qualitative information to understand and contextualise gaze behaviour. In order to ensure that such intervention is likely to be effective before conducting this field testing at live level crossings with distracted pedestrians, it was decided to conduct a laboratory-based study.
This report presents the findings of the laboratory-based study. Sets of flashing LEDs were installed on the floor at various distances to the participants (1, 2 and 4 meters), as well as a set of lights mounted on a wall. Participants had to detect the activation of the lights, which were randomly activated. They performed this detection task while not being distracted, while being visually distracted, and while performing an auditory distractive task.

Participants did not need to focus on the lights to detect their activation, suggesting that such lights should be effective even when pedestrians are looking at their mobile device. The findings of the study provide good support for conducting the field trial, and this knowledge will be incorporated in the ethics application for the field trial. The findings show that the field testing design will also require a mixed approach to evaluate whether pedestrians effectively detect the activation of the lights, and to be able to compare the three different interventions installed in New Zealand.
