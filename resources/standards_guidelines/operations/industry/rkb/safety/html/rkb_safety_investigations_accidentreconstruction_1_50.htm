Title A systematic approach for extracting performance, operation and safety indicators from engine recorded data
 
Author Righetti, P
Affiliation Fretless Idea, Verona, Italy
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract According to UIC Safety Database Report 2014, in the last 5-6 yeats the trend of railway accidents related to human factors has stabilized around a threshold of 144 acceidents per year. The report highlights fine-grained statistics regarding the causes of accidents detected at second level, showing a variety of sources, from trask & switch maintenance issues to railway undertaking's staff. Causes of accidents are almost equally balanced between the railway undertaking (RU) side (45%) and them infrastructure manager (IM) side (55%). As the trend of railway accidents is stabilizing, we envisioned that finer actions must be put in place to lower the accident report baseline. Surely working on each of the accident causes would result in lowring accident count, nevertheless our attention has been caught by the increasing trend of collisions between trains and derailments. We analyzed the report of the causes of collisions between trains and derailments, and we spotted that the train driver had been identified as the major root of accidents. Regarding collision of between trains, the train driver has been classified as the cause of more than half of collisions. So we identified the train driver factor as the most interesting aspect to focus our research on. Actually we looked away from track, switch and signalling causes because of higher costs and longer implementation timeline, while focusing on the train driver would certainly produce quick wins. Having no doubts about it, in the UIC report we found confirmation that the major part of accidents is caused by involutary acts. That was the confirmation that quick and economic solutions had to be sought within the awareness-of-risks sphere. Measures should be taken towards raising the perception of risk of the train drivers respect to cause-effects of their decision while driving. Before that, an accurate detection of what each sigle person needs to rehearse must be put in place in order to prevent the perception that rehearsing is a waste of time. Clustering results by demographic criteria can also reveal differences of performances, strategies and training needs between age groups.

Title Fatal level crossing accident, Cairns tilt train
 
Author Matters, L
Affiliation Australian Transport Safety Bureau (ATSB)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2010, Rail - Rejuvenation and Renaissance, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wellington, New Zealand, 12-15 September, 2010
Publication Date 2010
Abstract This paper summarizes the outcomes of an investigation that was conducted into a fatal collision that occurred between a diesel powered passenger train and a B-double truck at a level crossing in far north Queensland in November 2008. The paper examines the factual information associated with the occurrence, the key areas analysed during the investigation, an overview of level crossings in Australia and the use of higher mass limit road vehicles in Australia. The risks posed to the rail industry at level crossings are highlighted, particularly in regard to higher mass road vehicles, the effect of high transverse loading on a locomotive cabin and the importance of monitoring higher mass road vehicles.

Title Case study: derailment of freight train 7AD1 at Edith River near Katherine, Northern Territory
 
Author Hampton, T
Affiliation Genesee & Wyoming Australia Pty Ltd
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract The derailment of the Genesee & Wyoming Australia Pty Ltd (GWA) owned and operated mixed freight train 7AD1 occurred at the Edith River rail bridge, near Katherine, on the Adelaide-Darwin railway on 27 December 2011. Significant damage was sustained by the bridge and rollingstock as several wagons derailed into the flooded Edith River. The train driver was uninjured but the co-driver suffered back injuries. “The Australian Transport Safety Bureau (ATSB) determined that the derailment of train 7AD1 was caused by the wash-away of the south-eastern embankment, associated sub-grade and ballast on the approach side of the Edith River rail bridge. The magnitude of the wash-away meant that the track could not support the weight of train 7AD1 as it passed over the affected track. The track collapsed under the train, initiating the derailment. The wash-away was the result of a severe flood event caused by torrential rains that fell within the Edith River catchment area in the aftermath of cyclone ‘Grant’.” This paper is a case study considering the contributing factors of the derailment, infrastructure restoration and site remediation, operations recovery and management during the restoration period and initiatives implemented to manage risk associated with extreme weather events to reduce likelihood of recurrence.

Title Test and study on chinese freight wagon derailment on straight track
 
Author Qian, L
Affiliation China Academy of Railway Sciences, Beijing, China
Conference International Heavy Haul Association STS Conference, (IHHA 1999), 14-17 June, 1999, Moscow, Russia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract On the main track of Beijing-Shanghai Railway, systematic freight wagon derailment test has been conducted to find out the cause of wagon derailment taken place on straight track. 32 trains have been tested in three types of classification. During the test, derailment coefficient, rate of wheel load reduction and other dozens of parameters have been collected from wayside and on-board. Wheel/rail contact status has been recorded by photographic method. The test shows that safety level of derailment coefficient for freight wagon on straight track can not base on the Formula Nadal only, but should take the lasting time of the maximum derailment coefficient into consideration. The test also shows that derailment is related with wheel/rail friction coefficient, technical status of wagons, loading status of wagons, position of wagons in a train, whether the train is under traction or idle operation and magnitude of braking compressive force. And curve section and elevating one side of rails of the straight track can both restrain snake movement of wagons to lower the possibility of derailment.

Title Methods for accident investigation and human factors: what determines their uptake, use (and sometimes abandonment) by practitioners?
 
Author Waterson, P
Affiliation Loughborough University
Source Martin Baggott
Conference Seventh International Rail Human Factors conference, 23-25 June 2021, live virtual and on-demand
Publication Date 2021-06
Abstract After several decades of development and the publication of numerous scientific studies it still remains the case that many human factors (HF) methods for accident investigation are not used by practitioners. Methods such as STAMP, FRAM and Accimap are well-established and although there is some anecdotal evidence that they are also used by accident investigators, they remain largely a preoccupation of the research community. In this presentation I want to offer some speculations about the gap between research and practice. Part of the paper will draw on personal experiences of using HF methods, as well as conversations with accident investigators and human factors engineers which I have had over the years. A second source will be a series of studies conducted by the author which have sought to determine the factors which lead some methods to be taken up by practitioners and other factors which leads to their non-use or abandonment (e.g., Underwood et al., 2016).

Title Washington Metro train crash 22 June 2009: it could never happen here?
 
Author Szacsvay, P
Affiliation Interfleet Technology
Source Institution of Railway Signal Engineers (IRSE)
Conference IRSE Australasia Technical Meeting: Perth, 18 October 2013
Publication Date 2013
Abstract At 4:58 pm on Monday, June 22nd, 2009, in the middle of the afternoon rush hour, approaching Fort Totten station, Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority (WMATA) Metrorail train 112 ran into the rear of train 214 at close to line speed. The impact caused the rear car of train 214 to telescope into the lead car of train 112, resulting in the death of nine people on board train112, including the train operator (driver). 52 people were transported to local hospitals, and a further 28 people with minor injuries were treated at the site and allowed to home. Initial investigations by the National Transportation Safety Bureau (NTSB) focussed on human error and the possibility that the operator of train 112 may have been using her mobile phone at the time of the crash. As the investigation progressed it became clear that the crash was wholly attributable to the unsafe failure of a track circuit to detect train 214, and that this failure mode was far from being a one-off incident. The accident was largely attributable to failures of the signalling equipment and by the signalling discipline. This paper describes the history of an unsafe failure mode dating back over 20 years, and the equally long chain of events and actions which not only failed to prevent the accident, but also made it almost inevitable that something like this would eventually happen. Each individual incident, response and subsequent action or failure to act has parallels in the author’s experience, and undoubtedly the reader will be able to relate the issues to their own experience. Far from being impossible in our own rail environment, it is evident that similar events could well have combined in our own working environment to produce equally dire outcomes. It may be only a matter of good fortune that we are now in a position to draw lessons from others’ misfortunes, rather than our own.
