Title A study of railway vehicle derailment mechanism based on swallowtail catastrophe theory
 
Author Dai, H
Li, Z
Affiliation Traction Power State Key Laboratory, Southwest Jiaotong University, Chengdu 610031, China
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Based on the elementary catastrophe theory, the swallowtail catastrophe model of the railway vehicle derailment accident is established in this paper mainly considering the derailment coefficient, the angle of attack and the overtime of the derailment coefficient, which are three factors having a great influence on the vehicle derailment. Combined with the swallowtail catastrophe model, various situations were discussed. With the help of the bifurcation set of the model, the critical area and derailment area were given. The vehicle dynamical model was set up in the software Simpack to verify the validity of this method. The simulation results show that the railway vehicle will derail as long as the derailment coefficient, the attack angle and the overtime of the derailment coefficient satisfy certain condition at the same time.

Title Improving rail safety through occurrence investigation skills training
 
Author Grabe, PJ
Affiliation University of Pretoria, Department of Civil Engineering, South Africa
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract It is an established fact that railway incidents can be reduced through proper investigation and safety training. The University of Pretoria has developed a programme in Railway Occurrence Investigation in collaboration with Transnet Freight Rail and the Railway Safety Regulator to address this need. The aim of the programme is to provide occurrence investigators with a detailed understanding of all the major aspects of railways (infrastructure, operations and rolling stock) as well as their interrelationships and interdependencies within the railway undertaking. The paper evaluates railway safety in South Africa and demonstrates how the programme aims to address the most critical issues. Virtual reality simulations and incident reconstruction are used to change the learning environment from a class room to an immersed experience. Responses from the investigators that have completed the programme are used as part of a qualitative process to measure the impact of the programme on railway safety and how it supports the railway business. The paper concludes with the most important learnings of this development and recommendations regarding future training methods and skill sets required for thriving in the challenging future of the digital railway.

Title Railway safety: someone once asked 'are the lunatics running the asylum'?
 
Author Mash, I
Affiliation Interfleet Technology
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Conference CORE 2006, Rail achieving growth, Conference on Railway Engineering, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia, 30 April-3 May 2006
Publication Date 2006
Abstract This paper provides experience of safety risk management, safety cases, and approvals processes appertaining to railway rolling stock introduction and operation in the UK, and more recently in Australia. The paper initially provides the context by presenting an overview of UK Railway, outlining its structure, the organisations involved, and the safety approvals processes created in the years since privatisation. An appropriate Safety Approval Process can and should add value to all railway projects, including Rolling Stock delivery. Further, the contractual imperative to apply the same diligence, and project management skills to this aspect of contract delivery as to the manufacture of the hardware is highlighted. The content of a Safety Case, and the concept of ALARP for Safety and Risk Management are then discussed. ASINZS 4360 and AS 4292.1 are shown to already allude to the ALARP principle, and the continued confusion in its application discussed. The concept of absolute safety is then discussed, and then discredited through the exploration of a rail accident near Selby in the UK. The paper then draws conclusions that might assist in ensuring the Safety Approvals Process delivers the required levels of safety within an appropriate timeframe and cost to all the Stakeholders, and that that process itself does not become an inhibitor to 'Rail Achieving Growth'. Colloquially, how can the Australian Railway Industry avoid commencing a journey towards a future point in time whereby the lunatics are running your asylum?

Author Heavisides, J
Thompson, K
Affiliation Rail Safety and Standards Board (RSSB)
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2013, Driving the Costs out of Rail, 26-28 November 2013, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2013
Abstract In 2007, the Baker Report was published, which documented the findings of the independent safety panel that investigated the causes of the BP Texas City oil refinery explosion of 2005. One of the key learning points from the report was that ‘the passing of time without a process accident is not necessarily an indication that all is well and may contribute to a dangerous and growing sense of complacency’ – a sentiment that could apply to the rail or any other safety critical industry. The report also included a number of recommendations. One in particular struck a chord with an RSSB member: BP should develop, implement, maintain and periodically update an integrated set of leading and lagging performance indicators for more effectively monitoring the process safety performance. Recognising the potential learning opportunities from another industry, the member submitted a research idea to RSSB. After scrutiny of its potential merits, the research project T852: Investigating the application of leading and lagging indicators to the rail industry in Great Britain (GB) was developed and progressed. Its main objective was to produce guidance representing good practice in the development of safety performance indicators (SPIs) for passenger train operating companies (TOCs).

Title Surviving derailments, investigations, and court proceedings
 
Author Mitchell, R
Affiliation HATCH Associates
Source Permanent Way Institution (PWI)
Conference Permanent Way Institution (PWI) NSW 2008 Annual Convention: Breakdowns and Unexpected Glitches Generating Emergency Responses
Publication Date 2008-10-31
Abstract The traditional railway world has changed. Those of us engaged with operating railways have survived by working with the changes in various roles, with different and changed organisations, and in operating environments that have changed significantly. One significant, and little talked about aspect of this change emerges when things go wrong and damage or loss occurs. In this situation the spotlight can be turned on the permanent way practitioner to be involved in determining the cause, a reasonable repair and recovery process, and the amount of loss occasioned. This will then be used to determine the damages that one party can claim of another, with regard to relevant commercial contracts, and common law. Where there are differences between the reports of experts in relation to causation, loss and damages, settling damage claims can involve protracted legal processes which can lead to a court trial. In circumstances where significant losses have occurred, the permanent way practitioner can get be exposed and challenged when providing evidence and opinion. Once provided this can be debated and challenged. Where court proceedings are involved, judgements can be published for all to read. These judgements can and do contain the court’s assessment of a witnesses’ honesty, reliability and credibility. Many would be surprised at what can be written, and prospective witnesses would be better prepared by doing some research in this area. In looking to provide something useful for others in this paper I am mindful of the fact that whilst I have some experience with investigations and court proceedings, I am by no means putting myself forward as a model or as an expert in what I have done, seen, or experienced. I can advise that at the time of writing this paper, I believe I have no such matters in the process of being investigated, argued, mediated, arbitrated or judged. Notwithstanding the relief provided by that recent circumstance, I, from some experience, am aware that seemingly closed matters can unexpectedly restart and develop into a full on dispute with parties equipped with experts and lawyers “at ten paces”, and that new incidents resulting in my involvement can arise at any time.

Title Systemic factors in the investigation of railway occurrences in South Africa
 
Author Hutchings, J
Affiliation Transnet Centre of Systems Engineering, University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg, South Africa
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Railway occurrences in South Africa remain high, despite occurrence investigations. The primary aim of an investigation is to uncover the events at all levels of the system and to identify remedial actions to prevent a recurrence. If accidents are being investigated, why then do the number of occurrences remain largely unchanged? It is proposed that perhaps there are aspects or factors within the railway system that influences the investigation process and therefore its effectiveness. The objectives of this research are to understand what systemic factors (challenges / pressures / frustrations / obstacles) influence the investigation process of railway occurrences. A qualitative multi-method approach was adopted in this research. The results illustrate that systemic factors do influence the investigation process. Examples of systemic factors identified include: no National Rail Policy for the country, financial constraints, resource issues and inadequate training. It can be confirmed that constraints and challenges higher up in the system influence the actual process of conducting investigations, influencing the effectiveness of occurrence investigations. This research contributes to a deeper understanding of the nature of accident investigations.
