Title Exploring the safety performance of tram roundabouts
 
Author Currie, G
Reynolds, J
Naznin, F
Law, J
Affiliation Institute of Transport Studies, Monash University, Clayton, Vic
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 96th, 2017, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2017-00
Abstract Previous research has identified that roundabouts are a major risk factor in French light rail systems. While there are currently few tram roundabout in the USA their numbers are likely to increase in the future. To better inform international light rail practices this paper explores the road safety performance of Melbourne’s 18 tram roundabouts using observational studies, conflict point analysis, and crash analysis. Previous research and design guidelines have emphasized the importance of visibility and alignment, and described typical treatments to improve safety. This study has found that most of Melbourne’s tram roundabouts have colored pavement treatments and static or dynamic/flashing signs, but only one tram roundabout is fully signalized. While roundabouts without trams have less conflict points than roundabouts with trams, other tram intersection treatments typically used in Melbourne have many more. There were 120 crashes between 2010 and 2015 at Melbourne’s 18 tram roundabouts, with no fatalities. Interestingly only 6 of these crashes involved trams. 25% of all crashes at tram roundabouts in Melbourne occurred at a single intersection, the Haymarket roundabout. However, since this site was fully signalized in 2011 the crash rate has decreased. The presence of a roundabout in a section of a route has been found to be associated with a 40% increase in crashes involving trams. Overall the two crash analyses in this paper suggest that while crashes at tram roundabouts do not often involve trams, crashes that involve trams often involve roundabouts. Limitations and areas for future research are also discussed.

Title New methods and perspectives in bus bridging theory and practice
 
Author Pender, B
Currie, G
Shiwakoti, N
Delbosc, A
Affiliation Phillip Boyle and Associates
Institute of Transport Studies, Monash University, Clayton, Vic
RMIT University, Melbourne, Vic
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 96th, 2017, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2017-00
Abstract In a time of increasing road congestion, passenger rail networks provide superior performance to commuters in terms of capacity and speed. Although their technical complexity greatly assists in delivering these improved service levels, it also makes them vulnerable when unplanned disruptions occur. Such unexpected occurrences can be due to infrastructure malfunctions, accidents and extreme weather conditions. These incidents can often result in the rapid degradation of existing service levels given that the options for alternative internal solutions are limited. This is unlike the road network, where when one link fails, traffic can be diverted to neighbouring roads. Unfortunately, passenger railway networks are not as resilient and often a common solution to addressing disrupted commuters’ travel needs is to provide alternative transportation modes such as buses. Bus bridging or the provision of buses to replace rail during service disruptions is a common response. This paper presents a synthesis of findings from a research program seeking to improve responses to unplanned passenger rail disruptions. Component parts of the research described, seek to understand current international practices, explore the significance of track crossovers to bus bridging, explore ways to optimise bus bridging depot location and the economic viability of a bus bridging reserve. Currently the management of unplanned service disruptions and the resultant initiation of bus bridging services are viewed reactively. This research paper demonstrates that it is possible to proactively plan for such incidents. Unplanned disruptions by their very nature are unexpected events; however, preparations can be made to minimise their impacts.

Author Lin, CY
Barkan, CPL
Saat, MR
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, Illinois, USA
Association of American Railroads, Washington D.C., USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Adjacent track accidents (ATA) primarily refer to train accident scenarios in which derailed railroad equipment intrudes upon ("fouls") adjacent tracks and is potentially struck by another train on an adjacent track. ATA has been identified as one of the most important safety issues on shared passenger and freight railroad corridors. Various infrastructure, equipment and operational factors affect the probability and consequence of an ATA. The research described in this paper presents a comprehensive approach to identifying and evaluating various factors affecting the probability and consequence of ATAs. ATAs are divided into three sequential events: initial derailment, intrusion, and train presence on adjacent tracks. Each event is associated with a probability component. Factors affecting each probability component and consequence are identified and their effects are discussed. This research intends to depict a high-level overview of ATA risk and provides a basis for future quantitative risk analyses and risk mitigation measures.

Title Standards, safety and responsibility - from the contractor's viewpoint
 
Author O'Grady, D
Affiliation Fluor Daniel Pty Ltd., Melbourne, Australia
Source RTAA
Conference Twelfth International Rail Track Conference, 9-12 October 1999, Melbourne, Australia
Publication Date 1999
Abstract There is a need for a single regulator to set and be the owner of railway maintenance, construction and operating standards, to maximise the safety and efficiency of our railways around the country. Or is there? There is a need for uniformity in operating and safeworking procedures, in particular on interstate lines and where maintenance or construction personnel are moving from region to region. There is a need for the establishment and implementation of uniform operating and safeworking rules, for uniformity in such areas as radio voice procedures. But Railways have their own peculiarities. Though track standards can be used as guidelines countrywide, it is considered the responsibility of the relevant railway owner to develop specific standards that are relevant to the peculiarities of its system. The contractor must accept responsibility for performing to these specific standards, and limited liability for any failure resulting from non compliance. Safety is the responsibility of all. Risks vary significantly throughout railway systems, and must be recognised and controlled by realistic practical on site means to maximise the safety and efficiency of our railways, not by countrywide standards imposed by regulation.

Cremer & Warner, Ltd., UK
Conference CORE 1993: Contracting Railways - Safety, Standards and the Surroundings, 17-20 October 1993, Newcastle, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 1993
Abstract The concept of a Safety Case (namely to demonstrate the safety of the design and operation of a facility) arose from investigations into the Flixborough and Seveso disasters in Europe. Both the application and scope of safety cases were expanded as a result of Lord Cullen's report into the Piper Alpha offshore disaster. Lord Cullen recommended that the concept be extended to include offshore installations and that the cases primary objective should be to demonstrate firstly to the owner/operator themselves that their safety management systems were adequate to prevent or contain a major accident event and secondarily to demonstrate this to the regulatory authority. There are moves within the UK to extend the requirement to prepare safety cases to the railway industry as part of the UK Government's thrust to privatise its railways. London Underground's efforts to prepare a corporate level safety case in advance of these regulations and some of the challenges we faced as we moved down that path are described.

A useful reference for the predicted risk level for a new railway design can be established using the RSSB Safety Risk Model. The risk acceptance target is only the starting point for a system design to achieve due to the required further application of the safety risk reduced SFAIRP approach.

Description Three key drivers of safety within organisations are equipment, plant and infrastructure, safety systems, and the cultural environment. Cultural factors have been implicated as causal variables in most of the major rail accidents in Australia. Title Ensuring the continuing validity of work-related fatigue modelling tools: expanding the database to cover additional work groups and tasks (scoping)
 
Project leader Sally Ferguson
Description Fatigue models are increasingly used by rail operators and regulators to manage and regulate work-related fatigue in Australia and overseas. In Australia, fatigue models were initially introduced in the early part of this decade as a decision support tool used in the design and evaluation of rosters for freight drivers. More recently, the use of these models by rail operators and regulators has been extended to include many other groups of rail workers. The original data sets underpinning the fatigue modelling software were derived from a relatively narrow selection of freight rail drivers and did not include the diversity of work groups now defined as Rail Safety Workers and increasingly managed using fatigue modelling software packages. The project aims to improve the reliability and validity of the data used to inform fatigue models.

Title Development of algorithms for earthquake early warning system using ocean bottom seismic network
 
Author Korenaga, M
Miyakoshi, H
Yamamoto, S
Aoi, S
Affiliation RTRI Tokyo, Japan
Source Dennis Schut (UIC)
Publication Date 2018
Abstract We have developed new algorithms to stop the Shinkansen quickly and reliably by utilizing the real-time data observed at the ocean bottom, and put them to practical use. For securing the safety of running trains at the time of earthquake, the earthquake early warning (EEW) systems that detect tremors of an earthquake and rapidly issue warning signals have been installed for Shinkansen throughout Japan. The original EEW system for railways uses data from inland seismometers. On the other hand, the world's largest ocean bottom seismometer (OBS) networks have been recently developed by governmental organizations in Japan. Since the OBSs are installed close to the epicenter of earthquakes which occur beneath the sea, it is expected that the OBSs are able to detect tremors of the earthquakes more rapidly than seismometers installed on the land. From the above background, so as to issue warning signals for earthquakes beneath the sea as soon as possible, we have developed new algorithms for EEW system using the data of the OBS networks. We have proposed a judging algorithm for EEW by using the data from OBS networks, a monitoring algorithm to prevent false alarms, and a communication algorithm to transmit data from the OBS networks to railway EEW systems. By using the actually observed data, we confirmed that the new EEW system with new algorithms can issue a warning signal about 10 sec faster than the present system. The new algorithms for OBS have been installed in some EEW system of Shinkansen line and now in operation. It is expected that EEW systems with new algorithms will increase in number to secure safer operation against earthquakes.
