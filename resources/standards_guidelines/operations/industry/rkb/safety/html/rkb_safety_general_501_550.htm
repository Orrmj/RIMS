Title A framework for incorporating JESIP into railway operations
 
Author Smyth, J
Hook, H
Affiliation SYSTRA Scott Lister
Source Martin Baggott
Conference Seventh International Rail Human Factors conference, 23-25 June 2021, live virtual and on-demand
Publication Date 2021-06
Abstract A Rail Operating Centre (ROC) is a mission critical control facility, designed to centralise signalling and control operations for a specific region or route on the United Kingdom's main rail network. Each ROC co-locates teams of Network Rail signallers and controllers together with Train Operating Company (TOC) control teams and technical support staff who work together to resolve operational issues and restore normal service on the network as quickly as possible. A ROC must be capable of dealing with a wide range of operational states ranging from quiet off-peak periods with comparatively little traffic, to handling the most extreme incidents such as terrorist actions and multiple-fatality accidents. Even minor inefficiencies at these operational sites can exacerbate problems, incurring further delays and exposing passengers to increased risk. The concept of operations for the ROC design intended that a co-located teams’ model for Network Rail and TOCs within Route Control would help to break down organisational silos and improve efficiency of communications and incident resolution. However, it is recognised that these benefits are not being fully realised at some locations and a need was identified at one ROC for a new operating strategy. This paper describes the theory that was applied to develop a ‘whole system thinking’ approach for organisational change within a ROC to create a clearly defined ‘command and control’ structure across multiple organisations to improve organisational resilience, increase efficiency and reduce delays. Joint Emergency Services Interoperability Principles (JESIP) (Pollock & Coles, 2015) were used as a frame of reference to develop a mechanism for creating lasting change within the organisation at a strategic, operational and individual level. A taxonomy of Railway Performance Shaping Factors (R-PSF) designed specifically for railway-related tasks (Kyriakidis et al, 2015) was used as a tool to identify key issues and problems affecting performance in the control room. These key themes were further explored and tested via interviews and engagement surveys with a representative group of control room staff. N-squared (N2) analysis (Simpson et al, 2014) was used to map the workflow, representing inputs and outputs across interfaces as ‘in-flows’ and ‘outflows’ to indicate how each of the roles and system elements interrelate. The N2 model provided an invaluable tool for mapping a revised ‘one team process’ for incident management across multiple organisations, which also includes the means by which information is passed between individuals.

Title A study of human performance and mental workload of a railway control room operator
 
Author Byrne, A
Affiliation National Safety Authority for the Republic of Ireland
Source Martin Baggott
Conference Seventh International Rail Human Factors conference, 23-25 June 2021, live virtual and on-demand
Publication Date 2021-06
Abstract People undertaking observation tasks, such as air traffic controllers or railway signallers, are at an increased risk of vigilance decrement given the likely repetitiveness of their duties. Moreover, factor in shift work and long shifts, both of which are contributory to fatigue, and the likelihood of a reduction in performance is increased. These tasks are also safety critical, meaning should the individual make an error, be that a slip, lapse or mistake, then a serious accident with loss of life could result. In such roles, task design and workload are important factors, wherein the Operator/Controller is sufficiently stimulated to minimise the chance of fatigue, boredom or restlessness setting in.
This project report details the study of a specific cohort of Train Control-Room personnel, whose task is repetitive but also intermittent, assessing whether the introduction of a secondary task can improve the performance of the primary task. The secondary tasks introduced were simple visual puzzles to be completed alongside the primary task. After a set period of time the individual’s reactions, using a simple reaction time test, were tested and compared against a period of time when only the primary task was being completed. The empirical study results have proved inconclusive in terms of significance, possibly due to the simplistic testing regime employed. However, employing a mixed method approach, whereby the quantitative study was explored further using a qualitative survey has revealed some interesting findings. Good management of working time, i.e., shift pattern and shift duration, as well as the effect of breaks at work, are likely to improve performance and delay the effects of monotony and fatigue.

Title Engineering non-technical skills: a case study for implementation of NTS in a maintenance environment
 
Author Russell, AJ
Affiliation Rail Training International
Source Martin Baggott
Conference Seventh International Rail Human Factors conference, 23-25 June 2021, live virtual and on-demand
Publication Date 2021-06
Abstract Non-Technical Skills (NTS) are the cognitive, thinking and social skills that underpin and enhance technical tasks. They improve safety by helping people to anticipate, identify and mitigate against errors. Research has consistently shown NTS failure to be a major contributory failure in accidents and incidents. In addition, links are now beginning to be made between NTS and other risk reduction concepts, such as Dynamic Risk Assessment. In the railway industry, much of the application of NTS has been in the train driving community, with the corresponding natural focus on safety. This paper provides a description of how the recognised three-phase method (awareness, practice and feedback, reinforcement) for the introduction of NTS is being implemented into a diverse engineering maintenance environment. The paper will outline approaches that have been used to overcome the challenges of culture and readiness to embrace the concepts of NTS by engineering staff. In addition, solutions have been explored to the pragmatic difficulties of releasing engineering staff in a highly production-driven environment, in sufficient numbers to support meaningful NTS training events. As a result, training effort has been prioritised and targeted into areas where the most benefit from the introduction of NTS is expected. The challenges of providing opportunities for delegates to practise and gain feedback on their application of NTS in a ‘live’ engineering environment are explored, along with the significant challenge of achieving trainer buyin and competence in delivering NTS-oriented material. Approaches to the transfer of knowledge from trainers with a higher-level of NTS competence to ‘in-house’ training and coaching resource are also outlined. The paper describes how Risk-Based Training Needs Analysis (RBTNA) has been used as a basis for developing the positive customised NTS behavioural markers that are critical as a means for providing clear and consistent skills development and assessment. A method of analysing and mapping the safety risks associated with each task is proposed, along with a method for linking this back through the training materials into training validation. The results and learning from the project are discussed and model used for implementation presents a pragmatic approach for the integration of NTS alongside technical engineering skills in a range of engineering disciplines, improving safety and business performance.

Author Kaul, C

Affiliation RSSB
Systra Scott Lister

Conference Seventh International Rail Human Factors conference, 23-25 June 2021, live virtual and on-demand

Abstract The standard of route knowledge competence processes for guards varies across the industry. It is often seen as a simplified version of driver route knowledge. However, guards manage platform-train interface (PTI) safety risks, and with growing passenger numbers and busier timetables, the guards’ route knowledge requirements focusing on PTI risk are growing. In contrast, guards’ route knowledge requirements may be reducing in the areas between stations which is where driver route knowledge typically focusses. This is due to legacy systems being replaced and updated. In the context of other industry activity and research, this project aligns with T1108 Achieving a step change in route knowledge management. T1108 sets out a new approach to route learning called the route story approach. The role of guards was included in the scope of T1108, however the scientific trials and the examples focused on the driver role. This paper will discuss the findings from an operational trial that aimed to test the route story approach with guards. An operational trial was carried out to test new guard route knowledge requirements and the route story approach with a train operating company. The trial monitored the route knowledge competence of a control group and an experimental group of guards as they underwent route learning for one route. The control group received the company’s existing route knowledge training. The experimental group received an updated version of the training aligned with new requirements which incorporates the route story approach. The trials provided data on the participants’ competence over the route and provided qualitative data on the usability of the updated learning materials. The route story approach provided better learning outcomes than traditional methods, including more thorough understanding of the route risks and more efficient route learning durations. The route story approach allowed guards to take their assessment when they were ready, as opposed to after an arbitrary number of trips. This resulted in a third of participants opting to take their assessment earlier, and passing. The route story approach produced documents which were rated as more usable by guards. Finally, the route learning content was risk-based and more aligned to the competence needed to work the route, leading participants to report feeling more prepared to work the route with the new approach.

This will be achieved by testing common approaches between European practitioners such as Law Enforcement Agencies (LEAs) and First Responders. These will be evaluated and validated against the requirements of civil society, including vulnerable groups of citizens, as reflected in the European Security Model. Indeed, a key principle of the project is that CBRNe incident management needs to be studied from the perspective of citizens, which has rarely been done before. Based on the collected preliminary data, project PROACTIVE will help update the rail crisis management plans with practical recommendations concerning a threat which is less conventional, but which can have a very high impact on public security.

Publication Date 2021-06
Abstract Following decades of research which has demonstrated the impact of insufficient sleep on performance and health, there is now a growing public awareness of the importance of sleep. Insufficient sleep and long periods of being awake are known to be two key contributors to fatigue, which has been identified as a factor in a fifth of high-risk rail incidents in GB. To quantify the sleep and wakefulness of railway workers, this paper presents data from a recent survey which was carried out in the rail industry in GB. There were 7807 responses to the survey, which were primarily collected online, though paper copies were made available in some organisations. The performance of people who have been awake for 17-18 hours has been compared to performance at the legal drink drive limit. Almost 30% of respondents to our survey reported going 16 hours or more between waking and finishing work, with 8% going for 18 hours or more. These data therefore suggest that some railway staff remain awake for long periods. It was out of the scope of this study to look into organisations’ approaches to managing risk in relation to fatigued employees carrying out tasks at the end of their shifts or during their subsequent commutes. The data collected also highlights a feast-and-famine pattern when it comes to sleep, with respondents sleeping less when working nights, more when working days, and attempting to recover on days off. While it is commonly known that sleep requirements vary between individuals, the patterns that shift workers reported in this survey suggest that many are not achieving the sleep they need. As a result, these individuals are more vulnerable to fatigue when working nights, and to a lesser extent, when working days. Other than sleep need, sleep and wakefulness are influenced by a range of factors relating to the individual and their work. The data presented in this paper shows that there is work to be done to address the causes of sleep deprivation. To prevent sleep deprivation or prolonged wakefulness affecting safety, RSSB is carrying out work to trial and explore the validity of fitness for duty decision aids, which are based on models of sleep and wakefulness. It is hoped that staff will use these tools to predict when they are likely to become sleepy, and by using these tools they will gain an appreciation of the importance of adequate sleep and rest for optimal health and performance.
