Title Exploring causes of tram-involved crashes using a random effects negative binomial model
 
Author Naznin, F
Currie, G
Sarvi, M
Logan, D
Affiliation Monash University. Institute of Transport Studies)
Monash University. Accident Research Centre (MUARC)
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 95th, 2016, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2016-01
Abstract Safety is an overriding concern in design, operation and development of light rail systems including trams or streetcars. The aim of this study is to identify key traffic, transit and route factors that influence tram-involved crash frequencies along tram route sections in Melbourne. A random effects negative binomial (RENB) regression model was developed to analyze crash frequency data obtained from Yarra Trams, the tram network in Melbourne. The RENB modelling approach can account for spatial and temporal variations within observation groups in panel count data structures by assuming that group specific effects are randomly distributed across locations. The results identify many significant factors affecting tram-involved crash frequency. They are, in order of affect; tram stop spacing (-0.43), tram route section length (0.31), tram signal priority (-0.263), general traffic volume (0.17) and tram lane priority (-0.148). Platform stops (-0.09) and service frequency (0.004) also influence crash frequency. Findings provide useful insights on route section level tram-involved crashes in an urban tram or streetcar operating environment. The method described represents a useful planning tool for transit agencies hoping to improve safety performance.

Title Functional safety of computer systems in railway operations
 
Author Anderson, KJ
Affiliation VRJ Risk Engineers Pty Ltd, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1995: World Best Practice - The What, Why and How, Conference on Railway Engineering, 29 October-1 November 1995, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 1995
Abstract This paper discusses the application of international safety critical software standards to computer systems for railway operations in Australia. The use of risk engineering philosophy is described including the estimation of equipment reliabilities and human error probabilities. It is concluded that the more practical approach to validation and verification of complex computers systems is not to designate them as 'continuous safety control' systems but rather, to regard most train operations as 'equipment-under-control' supported by 'external risk reduction' (human systems of safeworking) and formal 'safety-related' protection systems, each designed to respond to particular identified unwanted events.

Title WESTECT ATP: protects infrastructure and rollingstock
 
Author McDonald, W
Affiliation Westinghouse Brake & Signal Co. (Aust.) Ltd
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1995: World Best Practice - The What, Why and How, Conference on Railway Engineering, 29 October-1 November 1995, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 1995
Abstract Cost pressure demands are causing railways to minimise operating costs while reducing the risk of accident or damage to infrastructure. These functions are often not mutually compatible; for example, the move to Driver Only Operation reduces the opportunity to observe trackside signalling. Computer based safety systems can bridge that gap. This paper reviews the operation of Ibe WESTECT Automatic Train Protection system. This system operates in the background to the normal driver functions to prevent equipped trains from exceeding the safety authorities generated by vital trackside interlockings. The WESTECT ATP described in this paper, like other safety systems, must be carefully designed and built to safety standards to ensure that no systematic design error or equipment failure can possibly reduce the level of safety provided. The safety design approach is overviewed in the paper. Safety standards such as RIA23 and IEC 65A, together with some IEEE development standards, supported a safe system design and adherence to world's Best Practice. A safety culture was built in the development team and an Independent Safety Assurance Auditor appointed to oversee the system safety. The testing approach that has been applied to ensure that actual operation meets requirements is discussed. Development testing was conducted using a specially designed train simulator and field testing was performed with trains on both test track and main line.

Title Performance based design of the fire safety systems for the New Southern Railway, Sydney
 
Author Bustos-Ramirez, A
Miller, DW
Affiliation Transfield Bouygues Joint Venture
Source National Committee on Railway Engineering of the College of Mechanical Engineers of the Institution of Engineers, Australia
Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1998 Engineering innovation for a competitive edge Conference on Railway Engineering 7-9 September 1998, Yeppoon, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1998
Abstract This paper describes the methodology adopted by the Transfield Bouygues Joint Venture (TBN) for the performance based design of the fire safety systems of the tunnel and stations comprising the New Southern Railway (NSR) in Sydney. As distinct from the prescriptive design method, the performance based approach utilises scientific and engineering principles for the evaluation of total fire safety system effectiveness and the achievement of pre-identified fire safety objectives. Our research found that there were no specific Australian or International Standards dealing with fire safety system performance requirements of direct application to an underground suburban railway system, but rather a collection of prescriptive Standards limited to particular aspects such as egress and access to buildings. As a consequence, it was necessary to undertake an integrated, systematic and engineered approach to the fire safety design of the New Southern Railway. The paper describes how this methodology was used to demonstrate, on a quantitative basis, that the design of the fire safety systems for the New Southern Railway would achieve a level of safety which was equal to, or exceeded, the level of safety which would be achieved by compliance with the relevant prescriptive codes.

Title Crashworthiness of rail car structures
 
Author Anic, I
Zhong, Q
Davis, T
Wiltshire, R
Affiliation Compumod Pty Ltd, Australia
Source National Committee on Railway Engineering of the College of Mechanical Engineers of the Institution of Engineers, Australia
Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1998 Engineering innovation for a competitive edge Conference on Railway Engineering 7-9 September 1998, Yeppoon, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1998
Abstract Mass transit systems are one of the basic forms of public transport and are popular in many cities today. Collision loading is the most severe type of loading that a rail car structure will experience during its operating life. Knowing the response of rail car structures to such loading can be used to both maximise passenger safety and to better optimised structures. Damage repair costs can also be reduced. Collisions, by their nature, occur over a very short timescale and involve extremely large deformations. Simulating such phenomena using traditional numerical analysis techniques (stiffness matrix formulation) can be cost prohibitive when complex structures, with a large number of degrees of freedom, need to be solved. This paper discusses the Explicit Method of analysis, a method optimised for complex simulations, such as collision or impact. Items covered include the analysis procedure, shock wave propagation through the structure (importance to solution stability), contact between impacting surfaces and other influencing factors. An extensive presentation is made of a case study involving the determination of responsiveness of a rail car structure to high speed collision loading, the prime objective being the evaluation of passenger safety under such a loading condition, in order to meet technical specification requirements.

Title Innovation and railway safety management - a new paradigm for achieving a competitive edge
 
Author Miller, DW
Hammer, RW
Affiliation Transfield Bouygues Joint Venture
Source National Committee on Railway Engineering of the College of Mechanical Engineers of the Institution of Engineers, Australia
Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1998 Engineering innovation for a competitive edge Conference on Railway Engineering 7-9 September 1998, Yeppoon, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1998
Abstract This paper is derived from the hypothesis that future innovation in railway safety management will be driven increasingly as a result of competitive pressure to continuously inprove operational performance and, through the involvement of the private sector, in the development of new-or existing railway services. The paper considers the organisational interface issues that come with private sector involvement in a restructured railway industry. It examines the need for the establishment of qualitative standards for operational railway safety, considers the question of what quantitative standards should be set, and how these should be measured. It assesses the relevance to these issues of the current Australian Standard for railway safety management and the accreditation process. It then demonstrates, using the private sector involvement in the New Southern Railway Project in Sydney as a model, how an innovative approach has been applied to interface management and operational safety. It proposes a new paradigm by which such innovation could be assessed and managed in a quantitative, systematic and engineered manner to achieve acceptable levels of safety without the need for prescriptive regulations.

Title A systems approach to enhancing railway safety assurance
 
Author Hollywell, P
Affiliation Railways Division, Mott MacDonald Limited
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2014, Making Making Innovation Work, 11-12 November 2014, Perth, WA, Australia
Publication Date 2014
Abstract This paper describes how the quality of Safety Assurance can be enhanced through a full and proper understanding of (a) how the complexity of major rail projects can be managed successfully, (b) how human and organisational failures can impact railway safety, and (c) how that understanding can improve using contemporary Safety Assurance processes, methods and tools. This is done by exploring the synergistic relationships between Systems Engineering (SE), Human Factors & Ergonomics (HFE) and Safety Assurance. SE promotes structured design and delivery processes which reduce risk and improve the efficiency of design, delivery, operation and maintenance of complex engineering projects. HFE manages the human behaviour contribution to system safety through understanding a person’s capabilities and limitations and evaluating human performance. Safety Assurance provides confidence as a project progresses through its lifecycle that the delivered system will be safe to operate and maintain. This paper explains how the quality of Safety Assurance can be enhanced by applying SE and HFE principles when using contemporary Safety Assurance processes, methods and tools on major rail projects.

Title Rolling stock fire safety – Australian practice in the global context
 
Author Henderson, L
Affiliation Metro Trains Melbourne, Vic, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2016, Rail - Moving the Economy Forward, 21-23 November 2016, Adelaide, South Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Approaches taken in the specification of rolling stock fire safety design solutions vary greatly around the globe. The approaches taken differ not only across types of rolling stock but also in the overall level of fire safety provided. In respect to passenger rolling stock, the vehicle fire safety solution is one part of a greater package of measures required to ensure fire safety for the rail system. In some jurisdictions there is a preference for elevating rolling stock fire safety levels as part of the whole of rail system solution. The European Rail Agency, for example, favours the use of preventative rolling stock fire safety measures over mitigating fire safety measures applied to the rail infrastructure. Similarly, many of East and South East Asia’s leading metro operators also mandate a very high level of rolling stock fire safety to ensure they deliver their intended level of fire safety across the greater rail system. This paper reviews these and other approaches taken globally for rolling stock fire safety design and comments on how these compare to that defined within Australia’s latest Rail Industry Safety and Standards Board (RISSB) standard, AS 7529. The four part standard represents Australia’s first ever set of national, harmonised requirements and leverages best practice from Europe, within an Australian specific performance based framework. This review demonstrates the holistic nature of the RISSB standard, and illustrates how it will benefit the Australian rail industry by providing an excellent tool for managing the safety, asset damage and service disruption risks that rolling stock fires pose.

Title Risk management through systems assurance
 
Author Griffiths, A
Affiliation Foxboro Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2000, Railway Technology for the 21st Century, Conference on Railway Engineering, Glenelg, South Australia, May 21-23, 2000
Publication Date 2000
Abstract To show that systems are acceptably safe for deployment into an operational railway, a program of systems assurance is necessary. What is acceptably safe is dependent on the risks associated with system operation. As such, a supplier's systems assurance program must evolve in response to the railway authority's risk management requirements. The basis for expression of a railway authority's risk requirements has typically been a set of risk tables. These tables provide a mechanism for classifying: (i) accident severity; (ii) accident frequency; (iii) risk; and, (iv) risk tolerability. To achieve risk reduction, there has been a growing reliance on the use of Safety Integrity Levels (SILs). This paper discusses our experiences in developing an integrated systems assurance program, in response to the risk management requirements of our customer base. We consider the risk tables that form the basis for risk classification and risk tolerability, and recount from our experience cases where risk tables and rules about safety integrity level allocation have led to anomalous results. In relation to SIL allocation, the problem of commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) software is specifically noted. As a way forward, we suggest that more detailed risk models should be developed and maintained by rail authorities, with the suppliers of safety-related systems being asked to contribute to and support those models.
