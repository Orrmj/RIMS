Title Development of crashworthy composite bodyshells
 
Author Robinson, M
Carruthers, J
Kettle, A
Affiliation The University of Sheffield, Sheffield, UK
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The objective of any crashworthy rail vehicle design is to ensure that, in the event of a collision, the kinetic energy of the impacting bodies is dissipated safely so as to minimise the risk of injury to the vehicle's occupants. Research into the use of composite materials for crashworthy structures has demonstrated that they can be designed to provide collision energy absorption capabilities which are superior to those of metals when compared on a weight-for-weight basis. However, there are still a number of technical challenges which must be resolved before composite materials can be routinely employed for crashworthy rail vehicle bodyshells. This paper reviews these challenges, and describes how one project in particular, HYCOTRANS, is addressing them.

Title Collision damage analysis and optimization for the new transrapid maglev system
 
Author Diekhoff, H-J
Borrmann, M
Becker, I
Kinscher, J
Affiliation IABG mbH, Ottobrunn, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The increasing speed of transportation systems demands superior safety requirements during operation. Besides the investigations of the dynamic behaviour of the structure, the analysis of crash scenarios becomes increasing more importance in the evaluation of risk and safety. Crash tested cars with crash elements and airbags are things we take for granted today. For aircraft or railways these kinds of tests are a little bit more difficult and often very costly. But in recent years tests and quite a number of numerical simulations have been carried out to analyse the crash behaviour for example of local trains and in some aspects also for high speed trains. With respect to superspeed Maglev Systems the situation is the following: a) The collision of Maglev trains with each other and end of guideway is excluded by an automatic train protection. b) The collision of trains with unexpected obstacles and road road construction vehicles the guideway are to be considered. Nowadays complex computer programs for the analysis of structural dynamics are available, which have been verified for numerous collision scenarios. The application of these numerical simulation programs are state of the art in automotive industrie and provide an invaluable tool to meet this task. The presented paper reports on some numerical simulations for assessment of crashworthiness of the new TRANSRAPID system. Different scenarios have been modelled and analysed to evaluate the damage risk for the vehicle and the passengers. The complete system consisting of the carriage body and the corresponding support structure were modelled with Finite Elements in high resolution for the dynamic crash simulations. Unexpected accidential collisions have been considered with obstacles on or beside the guideway. The local and global damage is evaluated as well as the effect in terms of accelerations on passengers. Numerical simulations are used step by step to evaluate and to optimize the crashworthiness against inevitable collisions for the new TRANSRAPID during the development phase.

Title A study of a new earthquake early warning method against
an epicentral earthquake
 
Author Taya, S
Affiliation Politecnico di Torino, Engineering, DIATI-Transport Systems
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In order to detect an earthquake quickly and immediately stop trains, “the Tokaido Shinkansen Earthquake Disaster Prevention System” was installed on the Tokaido Shinkansen. This article outlines a new earthquake early warning method that reacts faster than the conventional system. An analysis of 11,987 pieces of seismic wave data (from 155 earthquakes that occurred inland and in the surrounding waters of the Japanese archipelago between 1998 and 2011) shows a correlation between vertical maximum acceleration and seismic intensity, and that vertical acceleration increases faster than horizontal acceleration immediately after the arrival of a seismic wave. Based on the results, the following new earthquake early warning method was developed: (1) observe vertical acceleration, (2) calculate “Predicted Seismic Intensity for Alarm (Iap)” from vertical acceleration for every sample, (3) issue an alarm when Iap exceeds 4.0 (the threshold at which a train must be stopped). Out of 169 pieces of seismic wave data (from 63 earthquakes that occurred within 30km or less of tracks), it was found that Iap exceeded 4.0, and alarms were issued within 1 second or less after arrival of a seismic wave for 64 pieces of seismic wave data (from 38 earthquakes). This method is especially effective for epicentral earthquakes that occur near railway tracks, and has already been installed in the Tokaido Shinkansen Earthquake Disaster Prevention System in 2013.

Title Nuclear and radioactive materials detection on trains running through portal monitors
 
Author Angarano, MM
Bisceglie, E
Falagario, G
Francescangeli, C
Perrino, D
Affiliation SITAEL S.p.A. Via S. Sabino, 21 - 70042 Mola di Bari (BA), Italy
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Unauthorised cross-border transport of nuclear and radioactive materials has been monitored since ‘90s, when a specific programme was started by the International Atomic Energy Association (IAEA) [1]. The amount of incidents observed, involving nuclear species, showed that close attention must be paid to this subject. As a consequence, preventing illicit traffic and inadvertent movements has become a sensible task in order to stop any possible illegal profit, nuclear proliferation or terroristic threat, as well as the chance of accidental contamination, like for example from metals meant for recycling. Radioactive materials have been studied for long by physicists, who developed techniques to detect and identify them. One of these techniques exploits the properties of scintillators, materials sensitive to gamma rays emitted by the decaying atoms contained in radioactive materials. Scintillators are normally coupled to a Photomultiplier Tube (PMT), which converts light into electrons and generates a signal large enough to be read by front-end electronics. Therefore, scintillators can be used to trigger an alarm whenever an emitting source approaches the detector. Different kinds of scintillators can be used for this purpose. In this paper we propose an inorganic crystal, namely CsI, that provides good energy resolution and high light emission. Moreover, it is rugged, which makes it suitable for railway applications, that usually imply taking measurements in a harsh environment. With respect to common standards, this specific design increases the light conveyed within the crystal while lowering the noise, in particular the common mode noise, improving the overall efficiency. The front-end electronics is accordingly optimised in order to further improve the energy resolution of the detector. Algorithms have been developed to analyse the energy spectrum and provide information about the alarm and identification capabilities of the detector. The paper shows the result of a large amount of acquisitions performed in laboratory and in a benchmark test. Good results have been achieved with the most common radioactive sources. For railway applications, we expect to be able to perform monitoring on trains travelling at least at 10 km/h. A higher speed can be achieved by proportionally enhancing the system size.

Title Pedestrian and vehicular safety at light rail stops in mixed traffic
 
Author Currie, G
Reynolds, J
Affiliation Monash University. Institute of Transport Studies
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 89th, 2010, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2010
Abstract A major flaw in light rail safety research is the omission of mixed traffic (or streetcar) environments despite evidence these are a major safety challenge. This paper reviews the safety impacts of new tram stop designs in Melbourne, one of the world's largest streetcar systems. A major finding is that 82 per cent of safety incidents associated with streetcars were auto-pedestrian conflicts. Previous research does not consider these and has identified poor monitoring of such incidents. Before and after analysis shows that new platform stops reduced collision rates - auto-pedestrian (6 per cent) and auto-tram stop (12 per cent). Tram-pedestrian collisions did not change except at the busiest stop, where total incidents reduced by 53 per cent but tram-pedestrian rates increased. However patronage growth has increased exposure at this stop. An alternative, poorer quality, incident database also showed a decline in tram-pedestrian (10 per cent) and auto-tram stop (25 per cent) incident rates after platform stops were introduced. These findings also suggest that auto-pedestrian incidents may have increased, however data quality and inconsistency with other findings suggests caution about this result. A road safety audit of tram stops showed that new designs have reduced intolerable safety risks compared to older designs. The new easy access stop is particularly attractive due to speed reduction of passing autos. Ironically the older, and inappropriately named, safety zone stop had the largest number of serious safety risks. Overall findings suggest new tram stop designs have resulted in significant safety benefits. Implications for planning and research are also suggested.

Source Informa

Publication Date 2013-11
Abstract Track workers work in a potentially dangerous environment. Being struck by a train or another rail vehicle can be fatal and only requires a momentary lapse in attention. With advances in technology there is the possibility of using new technology to increase the safety of rail workers. Current devices and systems are available that provide an additional layer of protection to track workers. These range from Automatic Track Warning Systems (ATWS) that inform workers of approaching trains, to systems that incorporate signalling and locomotive control systems where trains are automatically stopped if workers are still on track. With a wide range of technologies available there are a number of questions such as: what are the safety benefits, will they improve safety or is there a potential for a reduction in safety? The analysis of new track worker safety technology needs to investigate both the technology and its integration with track workers. This paper discusses the methods and results from a Human Factors - Hazard and Operability (HAZOP) study into a range of track worker safety technologies. It is possible that the analysis method could be reused and also encourage international collaboration into track worker safety research.

Publication Date 2013-11
Abstract Millions of carloads of hazardous materials are transported annually throughout the North American railway network. The risk of hazardous materials release incidents makes railway transportation safety a high priority. A number of strategies have been implemented or are in development to reduce the risk. Each risk reduction strategy has a particular effect on safety and different risk reduction strategies may have interactive effects. Little prior research has addressed these interactive effects among different risk reduction strategies, nor how elements of them should be compared and/or combined to achieve the maximum risk reduction in the most cost-effective manner. This paper develops an integrated risk management framework to estimate the levels of risk reduction due to prevention of major accident causes, modification of tank car safety design or/and changes in operational practices such as train speed reduction. The model has the potential to be implemented into railways' risk management system for local, regional and system-wide safety improvement.

Author Gollogly, KS

Affiliation Interfleet Technology Pty Ltd
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Electronically Controlled Pneumatic (ECP) brakes commenced operating in Australia in 2005 for captive unit freight trains and the technology is currently used by three rail operators. The potential benefits of this technology include increased network capacity and improved train safety. The barriers that apply to ECP brake implementation in Australia are almost identical to those faced in the domestic US rail industry. The significant barriers include two cost penalties for the transition of existing rail fleets to a future state where the ECP electronic brake is used for normal train operation. The first issue is the cost of train service disruption during the transition of operations between the two incompatible braking systems. The second issue is the high cost of fitting ECP brakes to the fleets of existing wagons. Progress towards fleet wide use of ECP brake will be limited where the driving factors for its adoption are based on the disparate needs of separate stakeholders. In this regard, the joint endorsement of an agreed implementation policy and a standard for ECP interoperability is essential if we are to avoid creating the 21st Century equivalent of a ‘break of gauge’. This paper examines the barriers to investment in ECP train brakes by Australian rail operators and the issues we face if differing standards are adopted.

Title Formal validation method and tools for French computerized railway interlocking systems

Abstract The SNCF is responsible for putting into service all interlocking systems of the French railway network. This is why, especially for the computerized SIL4 system, the SNCF wishes to check that new interlocking systems are always correct and safe, in the context of the French network, with its own approval process. Checks and tests before putting safety facilities into service as well as the results of these tests are essential, time consuming and may show great variations between each other. Economic constraints and the increasing complexity associated with the development of computerized tools tend to limit the capacity of the classic approval process (manual or automatic). A reduction of the cover rate could be stated in practice. This is not compatible with the French national plan to renew the interlocking systems of the national network. The method and the tool presented in this paper make it possible to formally validate new computerized systems or evolutions of existing French interlocking systems with real-time functional interpreted Petri nets. The aim of this project is to provide the SNCF with an operating method for the formal validation of French interlocking systems. The empirical result indicates the positive safety culture has certain effects upon the awareness in safety behavior of the railway driver. In addition, the positive safety culture was also found to have a positive effect on the positive perceived safety performance. Safety behavior was found to have a positive effect on the perceived safety performance. Several policies suggestions were included and could be useful for the decision makers of rail system’s operators and the government regulators when designing and regulating the safety management system.

Title European harmonised approach for risk assessment in railway sector
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Duquenne, N
Antova, M

Abstract In order to promote the harmonisation of railway safety performance and railway regulation across Europe, the EU Legislators approved on April 2004 the railway safety directive 2004/49/EC. That directive allocates, amongst others, the task of defining a Common Safety Method (CSM) on risk evaluation and assessment to the European Railway Agency. The objective of this paper is to summarise and highlight the framework put in place for the dissemination of the associated "Commission Regulation on a Common Safety Method (CSM) on risk evaluation and assessment" (EC) N° 352/2009 published in the Official Journal of the European Union on 29th of April 2009. The purpose of the CSM on risk assessment is to contribute to the overall objective of the EU. It facilitates the access to the market for rail transport services and enables the mutual recognition of results from risk assessment, by harmonising:
the risk management processes used to assess the safety levels and the compliance with safety requirements;
the exchange of safety relevant information between different actors within the rail sector in order to manage the safety across the different interfaces which may exist within this sector; It specifies only what requirements must be fulfilled without specifying how to fulfil them. To help with improving the understanding, the European Railway Agency proposed the following two step approach for disseminating the CSM and collecting feedback on its applications: 1st step: start the dissemination of the CSM shortly after the translated and checked versions of the CSM guides are available in all EU languages. This activity should be completed by the end of June 2010. The purpose of this first step in the CSM dissemination is therefore to explain further in more detail the requirements in the CSM Regulation, with the support of the associated CSM guides 2nd step: continue the CSM dissemination with the review of and feedback based on real case examples of changes to the railway system where the CSM process was applied. The railway sector organisations and national safety authorities shall support the Challenge H: For an even safer and more secure railway Agency in this task. The purpose of the second step review and feedback phase will be, among others:
to collect the experience and difficulties encountered by different actors who apply the CSM in order to be taken into account in the future revision of CSM;
to analyse the experience of proposers on difficulties in evaluating the significance of changes;
to analyse the cases and encountered difficulties where codes of practice and reference systems were used to control hazards and the associated risks;
to analyse the overall effectiveness of the CSM on risk assessment;
to examine the cases where the risk acceptance criterion for technical systems (RAC-TS) has been applied;
to obtain feedback from independent safety assessments.

Title Obstacle detection on railway track by fusing radar and image sensor
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Ukai, M
Nassu, BT
Nagamine, N
Watanabe, M
Inaba, T
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
The University of Electro-Communications, Tokyo, Japan
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Novel technology to recognize situations in distant places is necessary to develop a railway safety monitoring system by which human beings having fallen onto the tracks from a platform and obstacles in the level crossing can be detected. In this research, the authors propose a method for detecting a stationary or moving obstacle by the technology which employs the super resolution radar techniques, image recognition techniques, and the technology to fuse these techniques. The method is designed for detecting obstacles such as cars, bicycles, and humans on the track, in a range up to hundreds of meters ahead, by using sensors mounted on a train. In super resolution radar techniques, novel stepped multiple frequency radar is confirmed to provide the expected high resolution performance by experimental study using software defined radar. The parameters of the stepped multiple frequency radar were designed. The signal processing processor which employs A/Ds, D/As of high-sampling rate and the highest performance FPGAs has been designed and developed. In image recognition techniques, the main algorithm which detects a pair of rails is based on information about rail edges and intensity gradients. As the obstacle exists in the vicinity of rails, all the detection processes can be limited to the region in the vicinity of rails. As an example, an obstacle is detected by estimating image boundaries which enclose a group of feature points exactly on the obstacle. In order to determine the group of feature points, feature points of the whole image are detected at each frames and tracked over image sequence. For robust estimation of boundary, radar is used so as to detect the obstacle’s rough position in an image region where an obstacle would exist; the motion segmentation technique is applied to the tracks that is located in the region. Note that an obstacle's position detected by radar is remarkably rough because radar has low transverse resolution. Nevertheless, the position detected by radar contributes to rapid and robust estimation of satisfactory image boundaries. To allow for monitoring in large distances, a prototype for an onboard pan/tilt camera platform with directional control was designed and manufactured as required in a railway situation. To detect obstacles in real time, a high-performance test machine with a GPU and an image processing environment has been built.
