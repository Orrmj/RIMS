Title The weighting of non-fatal injuries. Fatalities and weighted injuries
 
Author Jones-Lee, M
Loomes, G
Yates, D
Mitchell, E
Spackman, M
McQuaid, J
Thomson, T
Source ATRI
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Many railway safety measures are not aimed exclusively at reducing the risks of death: often they will be concerned with reducing non-fatal injuries, or some mix of injuries and death. The question then is: how much weight to give the prevention of an injury relative to the value assigned to preventing a death. The objectives of this report are 1. to determine and recommend the weights to be assigned to major and minor injuries to give equivalent fatalities across all groups; 2. to provide a robust justification for the equivalent fatality weighted injury ratios recommendation; 3. to assess the impact the new weightings would have on risk assessments.

Title Track worker safety: investigating the contributing factors and technology solutions
 
Author Noorudheen, N
McClanachan, M
Toft, Y
Affiliation CRC for Rail Innovation
Centre for Rail Engineering, Central Queensland University (CQU)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2012, Global perspectives, conference on railway engineering, Brisbane, Queensland, 10-12 September 2012
Publication Date 2012
Abstract The maintenance of tracks and track side infrastructures is considered to be one of the most hazardous jobs in the rail industry. Track worker safety is a major concern for the rail industry as track worker fatalities and incidents tend to occur every year despite the improved safety measures that are implemented. The CRC for Rail Innovation project ‘R3120 -Track Worker Protection Technology’ is looking into the available track worker protection systems and analysing them for adoption in Australia. Part of the research includes investigating the common contributing factors that lead to a track worker fatality or incident, other human factors and procedural issues. It is essential these topics are analysed in order to understand the nature of protection that is required for the Australian rail environment and the effectiveness of introducing additional track worker protection systems into the Australian rail network. Some of the identified factors and technology solutions are discussed in this paper.

Title Innovations in worksite protection
 
Author Langron, J
Hickey, M
Affiliation RailCorp
Transport for NSW
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2012, Global perspectives, conference on railway engineering, Brisbane, Queensland, 10-12 September 2012
Publication Date 2012
Abstract In January 2010, RailCorp commenced a Worksite Protection Program of more than 20 projects to improve the protection of track workers. RailCorp implemented the program to respond to concerns about the number of worksite protection incidents occurring on the RailCorp network. This paper outlines RailCorp and Transport for NSW innovations to improve worksite protection and reduce the risk of injury to track workers from being struck by rail traffic. The paper provides an overview of some of the projects in the Worksite Protection Program, which fall into three categories: rules and procedures, staff skills and competence improvement and new tools and technology. It outlines the status of the program – what has been completed and what is planned over the next 18 months to significantly reduce the number and severity of worksite protection incidents on the RailCorp network. The paper also discusses how RailCorp has been working closely with the broader rail industry to maximise the opportunity for improvement in this area. Transport for NSW’s perspective is outlined, through its expert developer and deliverer of major infrastructure projects, the Transport Projects Division. Details are provided of innovations undertaken in the delivery of Transport Projects Division’s projects, particularly Alliance projects, to improve worksite protection during the construction of new rail infrastructure. These initiatives include the introduction of an Automatic Track Warning System on the Kingsgrove to Revesby Quadruplication project, improvement in skills and competence of staff, and alternative work methods to provide a safe place for work within the danger zone (within 3 metres of the track).

Title Zero potential for zero potential: shocking results from track circuits
 
Author Phay, YL
Affiliation Public Transport Authority of Western Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2012, Global perspectives, conference on railway engineering, Brisbane, Queensland, 10-12 September 2012
Publication Date 2012
Abstract Tests were conducted using track circuit shorting-out devices (TCSODs) in order to ascertain if an electrical hazard was present in the use of these devices. In some cases it was found that users may be exposed to brief voltage spikes in excess of 500 V. Using AS/NZS 60479.2(2002) it was suggested that exposure to such an impulse could result in painful sensations or, in rare cases, ventricular fibrillation. Despite the low chance of occurrence, the impact was deemed to be an unacceptable. Further tests were conducted to examine methods which may prove to be effective in reducing the risks posed to users of TCSODs. It was found that the surge arrestors and transorbs were very effective at suppressing the voltage spikes. This provides an engineering solution to the electrical hazard which exists in the use of TCSODs in areas using AC-immune DC track circuits.

Title World’s best practice train car maintenance, process automation and safe working practices
 
Author Grose, J
Affiliation Parsons Brinckerhoff (PB)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2012, Global perspectives, conference on railway engineering, Brisbane, Queensland, 10-12 September 2012
Publication Date 2012
Abstract PB were engaged by Fortescue Metals Group (FMG) to participate in a tour of the United States of America’s locomotive and car (wagon) maintenance facilities and process automation equipment suppliers. The purpose of this tour was to observe the world’s best practice maintenance procedures, focusing on process automation and safe work practices. FMG are expanding their current iron ore car fleet size to increase the quantity of iron ore they are exporting from 45 million tonnes per annum to 155 million tonnes per annum. In order to support and maintain this increased iron ore car fleet FMG are building a new state of the art ore car maintenance workshop. This workshop is located at Port Hedland, a very remote area of Western Australia where it is extremely difficult to obtain maintenance staff. This facility must therefore utilise process automation wherever possible to reduce personnel requirements. In addition, this facility must demonstrate safe working practices to provide a workplace goal of zero harm to working staff. This paper highlights some of the maintenance practices observed in the United States train maintenance facilities whilst on tour with FMG. These include both positive practices to be implemented into FMG’s ore car maintenance workshop and practices that must be avoided in addition to maintaining FMG’s existing policy of positive isolated through tagging and locking out. A concept design for the new ore car workshop and accompanying report were completed by PB at the completion of the tour that incorporated some of the better practices witnessed on this tour. This concept design, as well as a detailed description of its workings, has been included in this paper.
