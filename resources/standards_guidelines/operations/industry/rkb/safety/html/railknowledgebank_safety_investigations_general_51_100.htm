Title Final report Advisory Council on Rail Safety Working Group on locomotive voice and video recorders
 
Corporate Author Transport Canada
Publication Date 2013-06
Abstract A study by the Advisory Council on Rail Safety’s Working Group on Locomotive Voice and Video Recorders (LVRs) calls for the voluntary installation of voice/video recording devices on locomotives by railway companies. The working group, comprised of representatives from the railways, unions and Transport Canada, undertook a comprehensive study of the issue of locomotive voice and video recorders at the request of the Minister following a VIA Rail train derailment in Burlington, Ontario on February 26, 2012. VIA Rail has committed to installing voice recorders on all its locomotives. LVR practices in other countries are included in the report.

Title Success factors in the reduction of highway-rail grade crossing incidents from 1994 to 2003
 
Author Horton, S
Carroll, A
Chaudhary, M
Ngamdung, T
Mozenter, J
Skinner, D
Corporate Author Federal Railroad Administration (FRA)
Report or Manual No DOT/FRA/ORD-09/05
Publisher Department of Transportation, Federal Railroad Administration
Place Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2009-04
Physical Description 46p. ; PDF
Abstract Between the years 1994 and 2003, incidents at highway-rail grade crossings declined by 41.2 percent. The reasons for this decline were unknown. The John A. Volpe National Transportation Systems Center was tasked by the Federal Railroad Administration to identify the salient success factors in highway-rail grade crossing incident reduction. The success factors were analyzed and investigated using various qualitative and quantitative methods. Ten factors were identified as the most influential safety factors. The ten factors are: Commercial Driver Safety, Locomotive Conspicuity, More Reliable Motor Vehicles, Crossing Closure and Grade Separation, Sight Lines Clearance, Warning Device Upgrades, the Grade Crossing Maintenance Rule, the Section 130 Program, Operation Lifesaver, and Railroad Mergers. Commercial Driver Safety, Locomotive Conspicuity, More Reliable Motor Vehicles, Sight Lines Clearance, and the Grade Crossing Maintenance Rule were quantitatively analyzed with data from the Railroad Accident Incident Reporting System; they impacted 54 percent of the incidents and accounted for 79 percent of the reduction in incidents.

URL https://railroads.dot.gov/elibrary-search

Publisher Australian Transport Safety Bureau (ATSB)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Abstract The Australian Transport Safety Bureau (ATSB) is an Australian independent Commonwealth Government statutory Agency. The ATSB is governed by a Commission and is entirely separate from transport regulators, policy makers and service providers. Its function is to improve safety and public confidence in the aviation, marine and rail modes of transport through excellence in independent investigation of transport accidents and other safety occurrences; safety data recording, analysis and research; and fostering safety awareness, knowledge and action. Rail incident reports commence from 1997 and are ongoing.

Abstract The Switching Operations Fatality Analysis (SOFA) Working Group was formed to analyze the factors contributing to fatalities in switching operations. Title On good form? Analysis of Rail Signal passed at Danger pro formas and the extent to which they capture systems influences following incidents
 
Author Naweed, A
Bowditch, L
Chapman, J
Dorrian, J
Balfe, N
Affiliation Appleton Institute for Behavioural Science, Central Queensland University, Australia
National Centre for Education and Training on Addiction (NCETA) Flinders University, Australia
UniSA Justice and Society, University of South Australia, Australia
Centre for Innovative Human Systems, Trinity College Dublin, Ireland
Source ATRI
Journal Safety Science
Publication Date 2022-07
Abstract Through systems thinking it is recognised that human error is not a matter of personal control, but an emergent property of overall system design. In the context of rail, Signal Passed at Danger (SPAD) incidents are a weak link in an otherwise safe system and one of the few points where a single failure can have catastrophic consequences. One area overlooked in SPAD research is the investigation process itself, specifically in terms of what investigators do and do not look at when investigating incidents. The aim of this study was to examine the document (i.e. pro forma) investigators use to capture information and attribute SPAD causation, in order to determine the extent which systems thinking is reflected in the information captured. A qualitative cross-sectional multiple-case design was used to categorise a large dataset comprising completed SPAD pro formas (n = 208) from 10 rail organisations across Australasia. Conventional content analysis identified two types of SPAD pro forma (modulated, narrative) varying in length (long, short), and a comprehensive hierarchical description revealed shallow methods of analysis, and the tendency to emphasise and attribute SPAD causation to the individual-level (human error, personal factors) without identification of deeper underlying factors and other system influences. Findings are discussed in relation to the idea that SPAD pro formas are educative tools shaping the focus of investigations, and the observed deficiencies limit the learning that can be drawn from incidents and real change that can be achieved. SPAD pro forma development is needed, scaffolded by further research.
Physical Description 18p. ; PDF
Location Online
URL https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ssci.2022.105726

Title ATSB contribution to rail industry: milestone report
 
Author Biggs, H
Dovan, N
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No P4.113
Publisher CRC for Rail Innovation
Place Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2012
Physical Description 11p. ; PDF
Abstract This aim of this report is to highlight the contribution of the Australian Transport Safety Bureau to the Australian rail industry. Currently, the training of rail accident investigators is varied, with each state and territory having its own rail safety regulator. With the creation of a national safety investigator for the rail industry - proposed to be implemented by 2013 - it is likely that the Australian Transport Safety Bureau will become the sole national safety investigator of the future. In doing so, it is anticipated that a standardised competency framework and training for accident investigators can be established.

Title Market demand, supply and capabilities for rail accident
investigators: interim report
 
Author Biggs, H
Dovan, N
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No P4.113
Publisher CRC for Rail Innovation
Place Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2011
Physical Description 15p. ; PDF
Abstract The aim of this milestone 2 report is to highlight the market supply and demand as well as the growing need for rail accident investigator training. With the increasing number of investigators entering the rail industry, the need for competency based training has never been greater. Whilst currently, it is acknowledged that competencies for investigators tended to be highly specific to the organisation, the development of a standard competency framework is hoped to create a larger pool of investigators with the new regulations slated to be introduced in 01-01-13. This report will examine the needs and current capabilities and training options for investigators in rail organisations within each State jurisdiction.
