Title Multimodal transport competitiveness: a case study of freight movement on the UK-Greece corridor
 
Author Beresford, AKC
Savides, K
Affiliation Department of Maritime Studies & International Transport,
University of Wales, Cardiff, UK
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract For the international movement of high-value cargoes in Europe, rail not only has to compete effectively with other modes, notably road, but it also has to link efficiently with them to provide a combined or multimodal service. Operational constraints, as well as lack of investment, infrastructure weaknesses and physical barriers, have meant that rail has suffered a loss of traffic in many countries and on many routes over the past several decades. As a mover of high volumes of goods over specific routes, however, rail is very price-competitive, especially if distances are long. This paper examines the role of rail as potentially the key link between the UK and Southeast Europe for the movement of freight and, at the same time, it identifies the bottlenecks which reduce the effectiveness of some of the routes. Transport of whisky in unitised form between the UK and Greece is taken as a case study to highlight capacity provision, routeing, modal alternatives and logistics requirements. Road haulage via Italy is shown to be very expensive, though flexible, and routes via Eastern Europe are prone to delays. But intermodal transport via the Channel Tunnel and Bari is at least as cheap as shipping. However, there is a need for careful co-ordination of train, truck and ferry schedules to maintain the reliability and competitiveness of the intermodal options. If intermodal transport via Ban, or potentially via Venice, is tightly organised, it is not only time-competitive against road and cost-competitive against sea, but it also makes great savings in inventory costs.

Affiliation Korea Railroad Research Institute, Uiwang, South Korea, 437-757
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The freight volume between Korea and China has gradually increased, and especially the bulk cargo demand between two countries would be increased. The trade quantity was 76.6 mil ton in 2010, which was approximately increased by 33 % compared to 2009. Due to the increasing demand, the effective logistics transport system is required through marine transportation route between Korea and China not land via North Korea. Recently, due to the increases in container traffic between Korea and China, the concerns on train-ferry operation between Korea and China through the Yellow Sea have been increased. As compared with the land transportation between Korea and China by way of the North Korea, the train-ferry system between Korea and China provides effective ways in terms of cheap transportation costs, mass transit, etc., with the combined advantages of maritime transportation and railway transportation. However, in order to initiate the operation of train-ferry between Korea and China, several substantial problems have to be considered including the improvement of existing ports facility, design stability of ferry boat for stable operation, the management of freight cars between borders at each country, the design of floating ramp to overcome tidal range in Korean side, etc. In this investigation, an innovative container loading/unloading system using smart lifter was suggested to solve the technical problems on operation of train-ferry system between Korea and China. Moreover, the new system consists of smart lifter and battery operated self-driven wheels which make possible for an unmanned operation. In this investigation, the conceptual design, operation logic, and detailed function description of new container loading/unloading system will be introduced and discussed with a scale model system. In summary, the proposed an innovative train-ferry system could vitalize the transportation system between Korea and China with commercialization of train ferry. Annual freight is expected to reach 2.4 mil ton and 270,000 TEU in 2025. The transportation cost reduction is possible by no movement of freight cars between two countries with employment of the new system. If annual freight cars needed are 200,000, the cost is reduced by approximately 0.12 billion dollars. The cost is also reduced on maintenance expenses of freight cars. The total cost is reduced over 400,000 dollars if non-withdrawal freight cars from other country are utilized.

Title Conceptualisation of an innovative rail freight vehicle for transporting LDHV cargo in an EU context
 
Author Jackson, R
Matsika, E
Zunder, TH
Mahler, G
Affiliation NewRail Railway Research Centre, Newcastle University, UK
Railistics GmbH, Germany
Source Informa
Conference 10th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), keeping ahead of the curve through the sharing of knowledge, 25-28 November 2013, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2013-11
Abstract In the global competitive market, shippers and consignees require a logistics service that offers reliable, consistent and precise movement and storage of goods. Regarding customer requirements, Mangan et al. (2008 p.9) states that 'logistics involves getting the correct product, in the correct quantity and quality, at the right place, at the right time, for the right customers, at the right cost'. These service requirements become even more crucial for time-sensitive, lower density and higher value goods (LDHV). Such LDHV cargoes are generally transported by non-rail modes of transport, for example road. Road transport is cost effective for shorter distances and air transport is effective when time critical premium products justify a higher transport cost. In many cases, road and air offer multimodal services for such LDHV cargoes, where road serves the pre- and post haulage, while air transport provides the main trunk haul. In the European context, road transport is inherently favoured for the transport of LDHV goods, as the origins and destinations are densely populated areas. As a result, the major road networks are now congested and, in many cases, road transport has become unreliable. It is also generally recognised that road transport is less environmentally friendly than transport by rail and/or inland waterways, despite significant improvements in vehicle emissions and other measures. This has been recognised by the European Commission who present a number of modal shift targets in their 2011 transport white paper. In doing so the White Paper aims for a 30% modal shift from road, to more sustainable modes such as rail, short sea shipping and inland waterways, by 2030 and a 50% shift by 2050, for distances greater than 300 kilometres (European Commission, 2011). This research therefore proposes an innovative rail freight concept (and the steps taken to conceptualisation) for the transport of LDHV goods.

Title Contestability between road and rail for non-bulk freight
 
Description Both road and rail play important roles in realizing Australia's diverse freight task as each mode has attributes that render it more suitable, and generally less costly, for particular tasks. For example, the flexibility of road transport for urban goods distribution is readily accepted. Equally, the scale economies of rail over longer distance and bulk commodities advantage it over road for these tasks. There is also a middle ground where both road and rail are used for the same goods, in some cases competing for freight. Inter-state non-bulk freight is a prime example where road and rail compete for freight traffic. The majority of this freight when carried by rail is containerised in some form. Estimates and projections for freight movements and data on freight transport contestability between rail and road are notoriously unreliable, with cohesive sets of meaningful data from across the entire contestable freight sector being unavailable at present. As a result of the difficulty of carrying out much needed modelling work, the rail industry is keen to better understand the key variables affecting demand for non-bulk freight, and ultimately the potential for non-bulk intermodal rail freight movements into the foreseeable future. This project represents the first step in examining the generation and attraction of freight flows on the freight corridors and examining the underlying economic drivers behind freight movement in Australia. It focuses primarily on developing a profile of industry and business activity in the catchment area and reviewing the methods to be used in identifying the size of the freight market which road and rail can contest. It then completes the proposal for the full study and provides building capacity to undertake the full study.

Title An optimisation model for multi-modal container terminals
 
Author Kozan, E
Affiliation Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2002, Cost Efficient Railways through Engineering, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wollongong, New South Wales, November 10-13, 2002
Publication Date 2002
Abstract Today's multi-modal container terminal (MMCT) systems are very complex and consequently require large amounts of computation time for sequencing and scheduling of containers. At the same time, due to the dynamic nature of the environment a large number of timely decisions have to be continuously reviewed in accordance with the changing conditions of the system. When a container vessel calls to a port, the containers on board must be unloaded and stored at the port until they are transported further by rail or road. The containers must be stored in a manner so as to minimise the amount of handling needed to place a container in the storage area and to remove it when needed. Terminal layout is influenced by the transfer techniques employed and the number of containers handled. Therefore, the problem being investigated is to minimise the total throughput time, which is the handling time for all the containers from ships at berth and the transferring time of the containers to the MMCT. If dealing with export containers the problem is reversed. The following major activities in the MMCT are considered in this paper: the re-sequencing and balancing of the multi-stage container transfer process; operating strategies; physical layout; and storage processes. The best algorithms to schedule and sequence container transfers and locations of ship to/from road and/or rail inter-modal terminals are modelled in a "Time Window".

Title Increasing rail mode share in Fremantle inner harbour
 
Author Turner, S

Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract Intermodal terminals form a critical part of the overall freight system in Western Australia. Rail freight can be an efficient means of transporting goods where market conditions are suitable and adequate infrastructure is available. The Western Australian government has set a series of strategic priorities to ensure the freight system is developed towards maximising the amount of freight that is transported on rail. In 2002, a target was set of 30% rail mode share (versus road) for the Fremantle Inner Harbour. To date, this target is yet to be achieved, although solid progress has been made. The overall planning strategy for the Inner Harbour has included close consideration of the integration of land use and transport logistics. Land use planning for the expansion of the Inner Harbour has linked future development of logistics operations to the planned transport infrastructure, maximising the efficiency and attractiveness of future transport operations in the Inner Harbour. The North Quay Rail Terminal, located within the Fremantle Inner Harbour, is the primary rail link between inland freight precincts and the container port at Fremantle. Rail transport into the port has evolved since the port was first constructed, and the current North Quay Rail Terminal was completed in 2005. As part of a suite of infrastructure upgrades aimed at increasing the rail mode share, the Federal and State governments jointly committed to extending the existing North Quay Rail Terminal sidings from 400m to 690m in length. The planning for the terminal included two primary stages; an “immediate” configuration and an “ultimate” configuration. This planning allowed for the current operator’s operating preferences to be respected whilst allowing for infrastructure upgrades which would be required in future to reach the proposed ultimate throughput of up to 360,000 TEU/annum. Following planning, detailed design was subsequently completed. This design included close consideration of a number of design aspects, including constructability of the works and staging. The construction contract for the upgrade was awarded on 23 October 2013 at a cost of $AUD31 million. This project will increase the terminal capacity dramatically and will improve rail’s efficiency and competitiveness as a transport mode. Following upgrades to other system bottlenecks and improvement in market conditions over time, it is planned that the terminal will achieve its goal of handling a 30% mode share of all containers transported through the Fremantle Inner Harbour. Achievement of this goal will provide overall system efficiency benefits and also benefits to the community surrounding the Inner Harbour.

Title Aerodynamic analysis of intermodal freight trains using machine vision
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Kumar, A
Rickett, TG
Vemula, A
Hart, JM
Riley Edwards, J
Ahuja, N
Barkan, CPL
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Intermodal freight transportation is one of the largest sources of revenue for North American freight railroads and has experienced considerable growth over the past few decades. However, intermodal trains use rolling stock that generates significantly greater aerodynamic drag compared to other types of freight trains. The increased drag results in greater fuel consumption, which increases annual operating expenditures. There are opportunities to improve intermodal train aerodynamics by strategically placing the intermodal loads within the train's consist. A machinevision system is being developed to automatically monitor and analyze an intermodal train?s aerodynamic efficiency based on the container/trailer loading pattern and the gap lengths between them. This system's main components are train detection sensors, a digital camera, video acquisition software, machine vision and analysis software, and a communication network. An automated system coordinates these components for the video capture of in-service trains. The machine vision algorithms separate the train from the background in the video and assemble a panoramic image of the entire train. Using this panorama, the containers, trailers, and gaps between the loads are identified and measured. Following the successful development of a prototype system at an intermodal terminal in Illinois, USA, a fully automated machinevision system is being developed along the BNSF Railway's intermodal corridor from Chicago to Los Angeles in the USA. The outputs of this system include load pattern monitoring, gap length information, and aerodynamic scoring, which are used to evaluate the loading efficiency of each train. In addition to the machine vision system, research is being conducted to determine how intermodal terminal managers can improve their decision making so that intermodal train loading can be more energy efficient.

Title Robustness assessment of multimodal freight transport networks
 
Author He, Z
Navneet, K
van Dam, W
Van Mieghem, P
Affiliation Delft University of Technology, The Netherlands
Rijkswaterstaat, The Netherlands
Journal Reliability Engineering & System Safety
Publication Date 2021-03
Abstract Multimodal freight transport allows switching among different modes of transport to utilize transport facilities more efficiently. This paper proposes an approach on network modeling and robustness assessment for multimodal freight transport networks, where the nodes represent junctions, terminals and crossings, and the links represent pathways. The network model captures the features of interconnection and interdependency. Freight can switch between different modalities at interconnected terminals, while disruption of a single interdependent node (e.g., bridge, tunnel, railway crossing) affects multiple modalities. Considering disruptions of infrastructure elements and capacity degradation of pathways as perturbations, the network robustness is evaluated as the increment of the total travel time caused by these perturbations. Title Road and rail freight: competitors or complements?

Publication Date 2009

Abstract Up until the early 1960s, railways dominated all but the shortest land-based freight task. Since then, vast improvements in road vehicle productivity and road infrastructure quality, the gradual removal of regulations restricting road freight carriage and the exponential growth in interstate trade has broadened the range of freight tasks for which road is better suited than rail. Yet both road and rail and also sea and air continue to play important roles in transporting Australia's diverse freight task. Each mode has attributes that render them more suitable, and generally less costly, for particular transport tasks. For example, the flexibility of road transport for urban goods distribution is unassailable; equally, the scale economies of rail over longer distances and for bulk commodities advantage it, over road, for these tasks.

This includes the derivation of closed-form expressions for quantifying the expected crane cycle time and its variance under completely randomized operations of gantry cranes within its dedicated region. We also provide a method to measure the expected truck waiting times under cranes considering priority rules for truck service. Our results show that non-synchronized operations have a notorious impact on transshipment yard performance, handling capacity and congestion.

Abstract This report examines why freight carriers and shippers choose one transport mode over others. It analyses the main determinants for using road, rail, inland waterways, coastal shipping or pipelines to move goods and assesses government policies to influence it. The study also reviews how shifting freight to more sustainable modes could reduce the contribution of goods transport to climate change and provides recommendations for more effective policies. The role of mode choice in alleviating congestion and making goods transport safer is also addressed. Three case studies from China, Canada and the Netherlands highlight modal-shift policies.
