Title Operational benefit and maintenance assessment of new tubular axles
 
Author Antunes, RF
Bachour, MTC
Fonseca, ASM
Winiarski, S
Affiliation Vallourec Soluções Tubulares do Brasil S.A., Belo Horizonte, Minas Gerais, Brazil
Rumo S.A., Curitiba, Parana, Brazil
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract There is a new light wagon axle produced from seamless hot rolled and forged tubes in a new steel grade which have been applied regularly in two Brazilian iron ore railways since 2010. A local railway company that transports fuel, industrial and agricultural goods decided to carry out practical evaluations of these axles at their operation because this lever could improve their efficiency by reducing 700 kg wagon weight. Six new hopper wagons using 6 ½” x 9” x 1600 mm tubular axles, which are fully interchangeable with equivalent AAR M-101solid axles, operate regularly since 2017 on a 1600 km long track. Both types of axles provided at same wagons lots and with similar amount of travels were removed for comparative evaluations in the wheelset maintenance shop after one year operating. Parameters evaluated were wheels and bearings conditions, visual and dimensional inspection of wheel seat and journal, axle bending and ultrasonic inspection. All axles fulfilled the requirements and went back to rail after wheelsets assembly. Operational results were also computed after two years carrying corn and soybean and the average loading compared to analog wagon with solid axles demonstrated a real average payload increase of 500 kg per wagon.

Title A decision support system for passenger and freight vehicle scheduling
 
Author Lee, K-S
Huck, A
Kuchenbecker, K
Schang,
Schoch, M
Schulldorf, H
Affiliation DB Mobility Logistics AG, GSV, 60329 Frankfurt, Germany
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract We developed an optimization based decision support system (DSS) for the railway vehicle scheduling problem. This is a joint development for both the rail freight and rail passenger divisions of Deutsche Bahn, and it is required that both freight and passenger constraints are included in the model. However, the requirements and the problem structure are very different. The passenger trains usually run very stable and periodically but complex maintenance requirements must be considered. In rail freight service, there are many different locomotive types and it is possible to change train times to get a better loco schedule. We developed an extremely flexible model which can represent all practical requirements for both problems. For example, required seats in passenger trains and required power in freight trains can be modelled by the same abstract structure. The DSS is now used by more than 80 planners within Deutsche Bahn. It is used on regular basis for calculating the best vehicle mix in call for bids for regional passenger traffic as well as for short-term planning of the chartered freight trains next week. Our DSS helped the planners not only to increase the vehicle productivity for their respective divisions of Deutsche Bahn, but also to get new insights and ideas from the others. Seeing the different applications helps the planners to understand the underlying model and the algorithm which increases the acceptance of the results. As a bonus, Deutsche Bahn could save much development costs compared to individual software development for each division. With this example, we show the benefits and the importance of a flexible and easy-to-extend model. Not only for monetary reasons, but also for ad-hoc calculations within projects when there is simply no time to develop a new model and algorithm. We conclude that applying the same model and algorithm for different practical problems not only saves time and money in development, but also helps people of different background and education to understand mathematical concepts – which are essential in a digitalized world.

Title A system for monitoring and managing wagon dynamic stability in long trains
 
Author Cole, C
Spiryagin, M
Wu, Q
Bosomworth, C
Affiliation Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Australia
Insyte Solutions
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract A system, envisaged as a cabin instrument, to provide real time and future predictions of train dynamics and vehicle dynamics and derailment risks is proposed. Using synchronised simulation of longitudinal train dynamics to give coupler forces and accelerations, the system will provide predictive information of wheel unloading that is derived from the effects of coupler forces on curves and from wagon pitching and lifting motions derived from coupler impacts. The risks of vehicle derailment for a predetermined future time period in response to driver control and track inputs can also be determined. The assessment of derailment risk is based on the estimation of wheel unloading caused by the train’s longitudinal and lateral force components, impact forces, centrifugal forces, vehicle speed, and track geometry including irregularities. With this information made available as a cabin instrument, risk in ‘difficult to operate scenarios” can be assessed in real time and operational safety improved. The paper presents simulation examples and a discussion of the proposed device.

Abstract During the last fifteen years the development of the clients demand in freight railway transport has taken the railway societies to a total reconsideration of the characteristics of their offer. The wagon load offer has become less interesting compared with the train load offer. Block trains between two main railway shunting yards or, better, between two private terminals allow the reduction of railways costs and the reduction of the delivering times. To be competitive with the road transport, the railway societies need solutions to reduce transportation costs. Economical studies have demonstrated that an improve­ment may be achieved through a more accurate examination of the logistic chain, an improvement of management, an augmentation of the profitability of the freight cars and of the trains. The new generation of freight cars has to permit a better utilisation of the train length, the improvement of the payload/tare ratio, the transport of high cube loading units and the augmentation of the speed of the combined transport trains. The transport of goods is both a major problem and one of the directions of progress taken by the modern society. In Europe the correct an balanced development of the various countries and the consolidation of their position into the world market are depending, to a large extent, on the development of fast, low cost and reliable goods transport systems. These systems need to be cost and quality efficient, both for the customers and the products. They must reduce the impact on the environment and ensure minimal use of energy. The globalisation of the market and the emergence of new industrial products have strongly impacted freight transport in recent years. The efforts to reach shorter lead times, increased frequency of transportation, the increased volumes, the longer distances have resulted in even more demanding requirements for integrated and cost efficient door to door transport solutions. From a system point of view, railway freight transport may be split into the three following production systems: Train load, i.e. complete trains from an individual site to another; Wagon load, i.e. transportation of single wagons or group of wagons between two stations; Intermodal, i.e. transportation of swap-bodies, containers and adapted semi-trailers using block trains.

Publication Date 1997
Abstract The Deutsche Bahn AG starts the reorganisation of its freight wagon maintenance. Instead of the present rigidly periodical preventive maintenance, a system of conditionrelated condition-related maintenance will be introduced. In this way, the efficiency of the use of railway freight wagons will be noticeably increased by means of better use of existing wear reserves. To reach this aim, it was necessary to find a dimension for the condition of wear. Following experience gathered by SNCF, the material wear of critical sub-assemblies was defined as the quantity of evaluation. Wear can be evaluated with adequate accuracy by means of a combination of the operating data of the running distance and of the load, i.e. a specific definition of the work performed W (tkm). Presently, the data on the work performed by each railway freight wagon are received from a special information system. In selected types of railway freight wagons, such data will be recorded in future by a sensor system and will be storable and retrievable by on-board computer and communication system.

Title Conceptualisation of an innovative rail freight vehicle for transporting LDHV cargo in an EU context
 
Author Jackson, R
Matsika, E
Zunder, TH
Mahler, G
Affiliation NewRail Railway Research Centre, Newcastle University, UK
Railistics GmbH, Germany
Source Informa
Conference 10th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), keeping ahead of the curve through the sharing of knowledge, 25-28 November 2013, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2013-11
Abstract In the global competitive market, shippers and consignees require a logistics service that offers reliable, consistent and precise movement and storage of goods. Regarding customer requirements, Mangan et al. (2008 p.9) states that 'logistics involves getting the correct product, in the correct quantity and quality, at the right place, at the right time, for the right customers, at the right cost'. These service requirements become even more crucial for time-sensitive, lower density and higher value goods (LDHV). Such LDHV cargoes are generally transported by non-rail modes of transport, for example road. Road transport is cost effective for shorter distances and air transport is effective when time critical premium products justify a higher transport cost. In many cases, road and air offer multimodal services for such LDHV cargoes, where road serves the pre- and post haulage, while air transport provides the main trunk haul. In the European context, road transport is inherently favoured for the transport of LDHV goods, as the origins and destinations are densely populated areas. As a result, the major road networks are now congested and, in many cases, road transport has become unreliable. It is also generally recognised that road transport is less environmentally friendly than transport by rail and/or inland waterways, despite significant improvements in vehicle emissions and other measures. This has been recognised by the European Commission who present a number of modal shift targets in their 2011 transport white paper. In doing so the White Paper aims for a 30% modal shift from road, to more sustainable modes such as rail, short sea shipping and inland waterways, by 2030 and a 50% shift by 2050, for distances greater than 300 kilometres (European Commission, 2011). This research therefore proposes an innovative rail freight concept (and the steps taken to conceptualisation) for the transport of LDHV goods.

Abstract The Australian Government in undertaking a series of significant corridor strategies under the AusLink policy. The study assessed the viability of potential upgrades to the existing congested rail route as well as new inland route options. Title Australian freight railways for a new century
 
Author Laird, P
Michell, M
Stoney, A
Adorni-Braccesi, G
Affiliation University of Wollongong
Samrom Pty Ltd
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2005, 22-24 November 2005, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2005
Abstract After a decade of reform, Australian rail has demonstrated that it can be cost-efficient in moving bulk freight such as coal and iron ore along with the lion's share of the non-bulk freight market on the east/west corridor. However, on the north-south corridor, rail has less than 20 per cent market share of contestable freight. This paper looks at one particular aspect, rail infrastructure, which can be improved to give faster transit and reduced rolling stock inventory and fuel costs to contribute to reduced train operating costs, while at the same time reducing the ongoing cost of infrastructure maintenance. This paper considers track alignment, and explores the aspects of cost and benefit that are relevant to the justification of such work. By way of example, construction of a 67 km Karuah Valley railway could replace the existing 91 km Hexham - Stroud Road section with its poor alignment plus halve transit times and reduce fuel use by 40 per cent. A new 67 km Grandchester - Gowrie crossing of the Toowoomba Range, replacing the existing 107 km section, would cut freight train transit times from about three hours to one hour and almost halve fuel use. Appreciable reductions in external costs would also result in both cases. The paper concludes with some commentary on the issue of funding where there are benefits to the community in general as well as specifically to the project participants.

Title Benchmarking track maintenance on Queensland coal network
 
Author Tetther, C
Tunna, J
Affiliation Worley Parsons Services Pty Ltd
Transportation Technology Center Inc
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Over the past few years, there have been a number of major advances in terms of increased speeds, heavier axle loads, longer trains and increased traffic density for many international and national heavy haul railway operations. Australian railways can learn through observation and review of other heavy haul railways across the globe, both through the identification of where efficiencies can be gained and through confirmation that some of the processes implemented in Australia may already be considered leading edge on a global platform. A known and accepted method of accumulating this information is to conduct a benchmark survey. However, previous benchmarking has tended to concentrate on European and UK operations and has tended to concentrate on passenger transport lines. These conditions found in these operations can vary greatly from infrastructure, conditions and operational factors encountered in Queensland, and from the Queensland environment which poses additional challenges for heavy haul maintenance with periods of very heavy rainfall and flooding, black soils, and extreme heat conditions.. For this reason benchmarking as a comparative exercise has often been criticised. Hence a benchmark survey was conducted for major coal networks in Queensland which identified specifically those criteria which impacted their operations. Railways from all over the world were selected only if they fitted into the criteria, in order that a “best fit” assessment could be made. The results of the benchmarking defined a diversity of practices despite similar working conditions although some general trends and common practices were identified. Many areas were identified where Queensland coal operations are implementing and developing innovative engineering and sustainable solutions with efficient results. These are discussed in relation to relative performance, possible future research and application in international operations.

Both rail systems have developed different train designs to meet their specific haulage needs. The reasons for these differences are examined using a combination of simulated and measured data. Title Rail freight for regional development
 
Author Gray, I
Laird, P

Conference SEGRA Conference, Bathurst NSW, 21st October 2015
Publication Date 2015
Abstract The potential of rail freight to grow regional economies by boosting local primary and secondary industry is now well documented in research and commentary, as is the desirability of increasing rail freight's modal share. Branch line railways can contribute. North American experience, where small railways contribute 25 per cent of the freight moved on the main lines, and attempts at reviving branch line freight here, have revealed what could be done with the right model. The case of the Blayney-Demondrille (Cowra) line in the Central West of New South Wales shows the promise. However, its ongoing process of revival also shows the necessity for a simple and effective model of a way to make it happen. The overall economics of branch line operation is not what is getting in the way. People trying to develop rail freight are hampered by misconceptions among potential rail customers, governments and even parts of the rail industry. These misconceptions include ideas about distance breakeven points for road and rail, the capital and operating costs of railways, the potential for 'first and last mile' railways and relations among large and small rail operators. There also appears to be little appreciation of the benefits of railways which focus on local and regional development. The paper discusses these issues with an eye on potential developments in regional New South Wales and proposes some principles for a simple and reliable process of branch line development.
