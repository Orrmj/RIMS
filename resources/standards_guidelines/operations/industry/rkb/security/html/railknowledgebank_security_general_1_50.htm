Title IT security – Blessing or curse of digital railways?
 
Author Braband, J
Affiliation Siemens AG, Braunschweig, Germany
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract This paper discusses a practical way forward towards the digitalization of railway automation without compromising safety. It starts with a discussion of the necessary changes in the normative and legislative background, including an innovative approach towards the integration of IT security aspects in risk analysis for railways. A reference architecture in terms of zones and conduits is proposed, which aims to separate IT security and safety requirements as well as certification processes as far as possible. Two solutions are sketched which allow software patch management for safety-related systems and further centralization, miniaturization and virtualization of interlocking and operations control functions.

Title RESTRAIL Toolbox: The ongoing development of a tool for safety and security risk management
 
Author Havarnmeanu, G. M.
Affiliation International Union of Railways (UIC), Security Division, Paris, France
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Railway suicides and trespassing behaviour determine 88% of all fatalities occurring in the European railway system, resulting in important delays, post-traumatic stress to the railway staff and discomfort to passengers. RESTRAIL (REduction of Suicides and Trespasses on RAILway property) was a three-year EU FP7 collaborative project coordinated by the International Union of Railway’s Security Division. The project aimed to reduce railway suicides and trespassing fatalities as well as the costly service disruption caused by these events. It provided the rail industry with a toolbox, which has become both an applied and theoretical research input for any railway safety and security management system. This contribution presents the first part of this toolbox, specifically a systematic process to structure the analysis of a suicide or trespass-related problem on the railways. The method used to design, evaluate and improve this systematic process included collecting information from literature reviews and surveys in the railway industry, evaluation workshops with railway experts, and reviews inside and outside the RESTRAIL consortium. The systematic process provided by the RESTRAIL toolbox comprises six steps with several subsequent actions, and for each action a checklist of prompting questions and possible answers. The process can help decision-makers, railway undertakings and infrastructure managers to analyse specific problems and to select the optimal intervention(s) from the range of available preventative and mitigation measures. We discuss the added value of this systematic process for decision makers and its usability in the safety and security management system. In the context of the RESTRAIL toolbox, the systematic process described here is a good example of exploitable ongoing results from EU-funded projects: it was developed during the project, it has been openly available online at www.restrail.eu/toolbox since the end of the project, and will continue to be updated and improved by the International Union of Railways (UIC) for the benefit of the entire railway community and society. The RESTRAIL toolbox with all its components will continue to develop as a practical risk management framework at the border between two fundamental values of the railways: safety and security.

Title Delivering effective & affordable train protection
 
Author Chilver, S
Affiliation Thales
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2005, 22-24 November 2005, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2005
Abstract The design and implementation of an effective yet affordable Train Protection System should be underpinned by a robust safety assurance methodology. In the UK, once it became clear in the early nineties that the intended British Rail Automatic Train Protection (ATP) was not affordable, a project was undertaken that led to the development of the Train Protection and Warning System (TPWS). TPWS, rather than attempting to provide full protection for all situations, was focused on the statistically highest causes of train accidents – Signals Passed At Danger (SPAD’s) and over-speeding. At the same time, the safety culture in the UK rail industry was maturing and the development of the TPWS product was one of the early projects to travel through the industry’s ‘Yellow Book’ safety proving process. Very similar processes are now contained in European standards which at first sight may appear to provide projects with a daunting set of onerous requirements which threaten to result in extended project timescales and increase project cost significantly. The temptations with safety systems in the rail environment is to demand excessive safety integrity levels – often those of the primary signalling system for everything connected with train control. Whilst this produces system safety requirements which are easily defendable it often leads to overly engineered and unaffordable systems which are not implemented and therefore fail to deliver the primary intention, a safer railway. The TPWS project demonstrated that it is possible to deliver an affordable product implementation developed using the now almost universally recognised safety proving processes applied in an appropriate way. This enabled cost-effective yet robust development and proving and demonstrated that the application of such standards need not add an excessive overhead to the development life cycle. After all, unless implemented, safety systems provide no safety benefit at all. Today TPWS is a testament to the success of the ‘Yellow Book’ process and is a clear demonstration that ‘safe enough’ can be appropriate in a rail environment. The benefits of TPWS in preventing and mitigating serious accidents can clearly be seen from publicly available reports. It is also clear that delay in implementation of the system would have probably resulted in further loss of life on the UK railway. This paper describes the practical application of the processes used; the issues faced and tips for success. It uses the UK experience to provide recommendations for others faced with the challenge of implementing world-class safety standards on an existing rail network.

Abstract This paper introduces the Railway Risk Assessment Information Management System (RAIMS) developed by the Korea Railroad Research Institute (KRRI). This project is one subject of the Railway Safety Technology Development project which has been supported by Government since 2004. The purpose of RAIMS is to make the risk management process more systematically effective, by recording the accident in a form of valuable information. Title Developing a computer-aided system for analyzing human error in railway operations
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Kim, DS
Baek, DH
Yoon, WC
Affiliation Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology, Daejeon, Korea;
Hanyang University, Ansan, Korea
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract As human error has been recognized as one of the major contributors to accidents in safety critical systems, there has been a strong need for techniques that can analyze human error effectively. Although a large number of techniques have been developed and used to analyse human error in accidents, much room for improvement remains. As human error analysis is a cognitively demanding and time consuming task, it is particularly necessary to develop a computerized system supporting this task. In this paper, representative techniques for human error analysis are briefly reviewed and the underlying model and procedure of CAS-HEAR, a web-based support system developed in this study, is then described along with its major aiding features. The support system was developed to reduce the cognitive demand in human error analysis and to improve the quality of the analysis. It supports analysts to find the multiple levels of the causes of an error and their causal relationships using predefined links between contextual and causal factors as well as links between causal factors. In addition, the support system is based on a complete accident model; hence, it helps analysts to conduct a thorough analysis without missing any important part of the human error analysis.

Title Fire scenarios modelling for the safe design of a passenger rail carriage
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Andreini, A
Giusti, A
Facchini, B
Da Soghe, R
Caruso, L
Luconi, G
Troiano, D
Affiliation Engineering Department, University of Florence, Italy
Trenitalia S.p.A, Florence, Italy
Italcertifer SCpA, Florence, Italy
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Effective fire safety design of rail infrastructure is underpinned by the application of realistic design fires representing credible train fire incidents. One of many objectives for passenger train designers is to minimise both the probability of a large interior fire occurring and the maximum fire size and duration. However, current understanding of fire development in passenger train interiors is limited and existing methods for design fire estimation applied to trains are based on crude assumptions and, therefore, appropriate design fires for passenger trains are uncertain. In this paper a summary of the results of a research programme sponsored by the Italian national railways will be presented. The programme involves university as well as experimental laboratories and fire suppression systems manufacturing companies. The project is focused on the development of numerical tools for the reliable fire scenarios simulation and on the definitions of standards for the fire safety design of rail carriage. The programme experimental campaign has considered full scale and maquette tests and have provided valuable data in terms of exhaust gases concentration, temperature, heat flux measurements. The experimental data have been used to validate the advanced design tools used by Trenitalia (CFD codes and correlative formulations). One of the main goal of the research is to define a reliable procedure for the design of a fire extinguishing system for railway passenger train by the use of the code FDS (Fire Dynamic Simulator) developed by the NIST (National Institute of Standard and Technologies). In order to reach the programme objectives, several aspects have been deeply analysed. More in detail attention has been paid to the combustion and pyrolysis modelling and a best practice for solid fuel’s burning properties evaluation has been defined. CFD has proved to be an excellent tool for this kind of analysis as it provides a reliable evaluation of the interesting physical quantities that are analyzed to develop a fire safety design of rail carriage. The methodologies defined are a novel and potentially industrially useful contribution to rail infrastructure design.

Title A review of the literature on social, non-technical deterrents for vandalism in the rail industry
 
Project leader Kirrilly Thompson
Description Vandalism incidents include malicious damage, arson and graffiti. Vandalism is an endemic and difficult to manage aspect of the rail industry and costs the rail industry millions of dollars each year. The identification of effective practices to reduce the significance and impact of vandalism will save significant financial burdens and create an improved public perception of affected railways. This project conducted a review of international best practice in vandalism reduction and provided strategic short and long-term strategies suitable for preventing rail vandalism. The focus was on the social, non-technical deterrents of vandalism and graffiti.
Project number or code R2.106
Conducting organisation University of South Australia
Project leader contact details Ph: (08) 8302 2611
Kirrilly.Thompson@unisa.edu.au
Project commencement 1 December 2008
Expected completion date 29 May 2009
Status Completed
Outputs The project provided an improved understanding of rail vandalism prevention strategies that will feed into a collaborative workshop to determine the best way forward to: improve understanding of rail prevention strategies; reduce vandalism incidence and severity; reduce cost of vandalism removal; improve appeal of rail through more aesthetically pleasing rolling stock and platforms; improve perception of rail as an efficient manager of vandalism; and to provide a direction for anti-vandalism initiatives in the Australian rail industry.
Outcomes This project reviewed the literature on social, non-technical deterrents for vandalism in the rail industry, identified best practices for input into short and long term strategies for reducing the scope and scale of vandalism and graffiti on rail property in Australia, and determined the need for multi-disciplinary and collaborative interventions to counter the problem of graffiti and vandalism.

Title Improving passenger security on urban rail networks
 
Author Schubert, P
Affiliation Public Public Transport Authority of Western Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2008, Rail; the core of integrated transport, Conference on Railway Engineering, Perth, Western Australia, 2008
Publication Date 2018
Abstract In recent years and particularly since the catastrophic events of Sept 11th 2001 and the London Tube terrorist bombings the installation of Closed Circuit Television Systems (CCTV) on mass transit systems has become a key element for the prevention and proactive response to anti social behaviour. Some large installations did exist prior to these times, primarily for security purposes. Many of these systems were installed at great cost and have proven to be less effective than expected. This has largely been the result of adopting a simplistic approach of “trying to provide remote visibility of problem areas” and was adopted without consideration of a risk based approach and human behaviour patterns and perceptions. This paper recommends a holistic framework for best practice CCTV installation on fixed infrastructure in the passenger rail context. It does not consider “on-train” systems. For those contemplating installing CCTV systems for passenger security purposes this paper suggests some design considerations based on the Public Transport Authority of WA’s (PTA) experience with its multi - award winning system.
