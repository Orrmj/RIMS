Title Assessing cognitive underload during train driving: a physiological approach (CUPID)
 
Author Waters, S
Whitmore, A
Basacik, D
Reed, N
Affiliation Transport Research Laboratory (TRL)
Report or Manual No PPR749
Publisher Transport Research Laboratory (TRL)
Place Crowthorne, UK
Publication Date 2015-11
Physical Description 77p. ; PDF
Abstract TRL and RSSB are involved in joint research investigating the effects of very low mental workload (also known as cognitive underload) on train driver performance. This is a particularly important area of research because certain train driving scenarios involve periods of very low task demands, requiring the driver to continuously monitor and respond to repetitive stimuli in the environment, whilst at the same time maintaining a preparedness to react appropriately to infrequent critical events (Larue et al., 2010). Such conditions are associated with a reduction in arousal and attention, and reduced vigilance with increasing time on task. The purpose of the current research was thus to understand whether it is possible to establish a set of reliable, objective physiological measures that could detect the presence of underload in a sample of train drivers.

Title Remedial actions to prevent suicides on commuter and metro rail systems
 
Author Sherry, P
Corporate Author Mineta Transportation Institute
Source ATRI
Publisher Department of Transportation
Place Sacramento, CA, USA
Publication Date 2016-03
Physical Description 99p.
Abstract The present study reviewed current efforts of commuter railroads to reduce or prevent suicide on railways and discussed preventative activities affecting rail related suicides. It also sought to provide information about community awareness and attitudes toward suicide on the railroads and survey data collected from 498 respondents demonstrate that community members feel a strong sense of responsibility for assisting those who are suicidal. Pre-post surveys of railroad personnel involved in workplace suicide prevention training programs examined the effects of an employee training program with a large commuter railroad designed to identify and prevent rail related suicide. Results demonstrated that people who completed training obtained higher levels of self-efficacy with respect to understanding, identifying, and assisting people who appeared to be at risk for suicide. Program participants also demonstrated knowledge of the signs and symptoms and risk factors for suicide. Recommendations for prevention strategies include continued efforts to identify hotspots and to erect barriers to reduce access to the railroad right-of-way, and for installing signage with warnings and contact information for crisis services. The use of drones equipped with video monitoring systems working in tandem with trespasser intrusion alert technology could be one way of dealing with more remote locations. Training programs for railroad employees designed to increase their confidence and skill intervening with suicidal individuals is also needed. Additionally railroads should maximize their prevention efforts by partnering with other groups devoted to preventing suicide as well as with government agencies. Suicide is a community-wide concern, community residents feel some responsibility for prevention, and railroads should not be expected to be the sole source of preventive activities for intentional fatalities by rail.

Title Progressive addition lenses: mitigating signal colour- misconceptions from prescription lenses worn by train drivers
 
Author Larue, G
Wood, J
Black, A
Atchison, D
Corporate Author Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No PF27
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2020-05
Physical Description 38p. ; PDF
Abstract Red signals are typically used to signify danger in a range of situations including train travel and must be clearly identified as different from yellow/orange, given that the braking response to red and yellow signals are very different. The potential consequences of misjudging signal colour have serious safety and economic costs in terms of the increased occurrence of Signals Passed at Danger (SPAD). On 21 April 2016, train 3MP5 derailed at Rawlinna, Western Australia. The train driver reported observing that the point light enhancer viewed through his tinted prescription glasses appeared yellow, but when his glasses were removed, the point light enhancer appeared red. An image provided in the report indicated that the spectacles worn by the driver were likely to be progressive-addition lenses (where the power of the lens changes gradually across the lens surface). While there were many other contributing factors, it was suggested that the tinted prescription glasses may have further contributed to the derailment. Previous research has demonstrated that small amounts of optical defocus, such as those experienced when looking through the intermediate corridor of progressive addition lenses or with sub-optimal refractive correction for a single vision distance correction, can cause such colour misperception of train signals. This report describes an experimental evaluation conducted to further assess whether prescription spectacles with progressive-addition lenses might alter the perception of signal colours and whether selected spectacle lens tints might also influence signal colour perception. The project involved experimental field observations and laboratory testing to answer questions regarding the potential effects of progressive-addition lenses and sunglass tints on the perception of signal colour.

Journal Scientific Reports

Abstract Train drivers work long hours on 24 h schedules and many factors impact their fatigue risk at work, creating a clear imperative for good rostering practice. Adopting a systems approach, this study investigated the relationship between multiple interrelated factors (train driversâ schedule, sleep, wellbeing, and fatigue) and the perceived infuence of these factors on train driving performance and safety using an online survey distributed in Australia and New Zealand. In addition to demographics and work schedule, passenger and freight train drivers (n = 751) answered questions about: (1) Sleep duration; (2) wellbeing, including physical and mental health, the extent to which shiftwork causes sleep, social, domestic, and work problems, and (3) the extent to which ten fatigue, health and wellbeing factors in the work and home environment negatively impact their driving performance. As shift variability and schedule irregularity are often poorly represented in existing industry guidance, these results provide evidence for increased reflection on current fatigue management guidelines for train drivers and suggest a need for greater focus on schedule irregularity through the lens of a systems approach.
