Title Foresight, visioning and roadmapping towards a 2050 vision for rail transport in Europ
 
Author Doll, C
Donitz, E
Mejia-Dorantes, L
Kuhn, A
Affiliation Fraunhofer Institute for Systems and Innovation Research ISI Competence Center Sustainability and Infrastructure Systems, Karlsruhe, Germany
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Even thought there is an extensive literature on foresight in many sectors (12; 13), and though a good number of transport related publications use titles like “foresight” or “roadmap” “visioning” (21; 20; 14; 16; 18; 15; 17; 19), to our knowledge, there is a lack of scientific reports on the combination of sophisticated foresight, visioning and roadmapping on the one hand, and quantitative scenarios and simulation applications on the other hand in the transport sector. In this paper our intention is to demonstrate and discuss the usefulness of the joint application of these techniques for achieving robust policy recommendations. The demonstration case for this exercise is nothing less than the European railway sector in total and is based on the results of the LivingRAIL research project funded by the European Commission. The LivingRAIL project develops a vision for how a modern European railway system could look in the year 2050 and a roadmap for carrying it out. Within this vision, electrified rail dominates the transport market, both for freight and passenger transport, and cover long-distance transportation as well as metropolitan regions. The vision for rail in 2050 is embedded within existing megatrend demographic, climate change, peak oil trends -. It seeks a better quality of life in a sustainable Europe.

Affiliation ENGIE Ineo
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract The construction of a first High-Speed Line in a country presents challenges that need to be anticipated by all parties to ensure a successful completion. ENGIE Ineo has recently delivered two high-speed line projects, which lessons learnt can be helpful to understand how these challenges would apply to Australia, and how they could be overcome so that Australia could embrace this technology. The South Europe Atlantic High-Speed Line is a 340 km double track infrastructure with a commercial speed of 320kmph, which cumulated a lot of Firsts which resonates with what Australia might be faced with: first High-Speed Line (HSL) in France to be undertaken as a PPP; first HSL in France to be delivered and commissioned by the private sector while all precedent lines have been delivered by SNCF, the French national Railways operator. Looking back on those projects, it appears that building a High-Speed Line in Australia would present some comparable challenges, which the industry can anticipate on to better address them.

Title Integrated strategic planning: how do we take advantage of a golden age in rail infrastructure investment to deliver on the future of mobility?
 
Author Tonges, B
Young, N
Hodgson, G
O’Doherty, P
Pringle, R
Affiliation Monitor Deloitte
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract Investment in transport infrastructure is currently reaching record levels as governments seek to unlock economic potential, reduce congestion and improve liveability in our cities, all of which are key goals of the future of mobility. Over the next few years Australia will spend approximately AUD 15B a year on transport infrastructure, with rail consisting up to ~50% of this spend. This level of investment carries significant expectations from the community on what will be delivered by rail infrastructure to realise the benefits of the future of mobility. It also poses significant questions of rail executives to effectively deliver and manage growing rail and transport networks that are increasingly complex, technologically more advanced, digitally enabled and multi-modal in nature. Juxtaposed to these expectations and questions, rail executives continue to battle to deliver the growing rail networks today as evidenced by the major disruption events occurring over the last few years. We believe that an integrated approach to planning is required to effectively deliver, integrate and operate new rail infrastructure that has increased frequencies, real time information, and seamless integration between modes. This approach brings together long term network growth with the short term day of operations, and considers them independently and collectively as a whole. We define that as Integrated Strategic Planning, a critical capability required to take advantage of a golden age in rail infrastructure investment to deliver on the future of mobility, and to navigate the challenges faced today.

Title A prospective view on railway innovation. The contribution of the task force and railway systems of the future
 
Author Blonk, WAG
Affiliation Directorate-General VII - Transport, Brussels, Belgium
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract To get the best value from the Community's R&D activities, the Commission instituted in 1 995 the concept of Task Forces. One of these addressed the theme "Trains and Railway Systems of the Future". It constituted an element of a broader policy framework, bringing together the previously separated strands of railway related transport, industrial and research policies, towards effectively contributing to the revitalization of the sector. The main aim of these congregated effort is to contribute to a radical structural and cultural change of railways, based on a market orientation, quality and cost-efficiency perspective, enabling the rail mode to play a greater role in tackling the transport challenges facing the Community. This paper gives an overview of the main results achieved so far within the framework of the Task Force. Notably, these address the assessment of the priority research clusters with the potential to contribute to the development of effective responses to the most pressing business and social goals facing rail transport. The identification and quantification of these crucial goals evolved from a constructive dialogue and active collaboration between the stakeholders in the sector, creating the backcloth for the establishment of a pro-active innovation strategy that can respond to the manifold challenges of implementing a modern, interoperable, high performance railway system that can respond to requirements for mobility of the future.

Title Railroads and public policy: a research agenda for the twenty-first century
 
Author Perl, A
Turrittin, A
Affiliation University of Calgary, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
York University, North York, Ontario, Canada
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Following decades of rigid economic control, governments are increasingly leaving the job of running railroads to their management. Public policy changes toward market management have taken various forms, but by the 1990s, railroad executives moved to innovate and adapt their activities to the world's competitive multi modal transportation markets. Such a transformation also requires a redesign of railroad research activities, to better meet the opportunities of an entreprenurial operating environment. Railroad executives have long appreciated the importance of first-rate technical and operational research to improve their efficiency as a transport mode. But in the rapid reorientation to market forces, executives must broaden their research activities to create new strategic alliances with governments, universities, and other transport modes, much the way that aerospace and automotive research have done. Interdisciplinary teams of investigators must address the commercial, organizational, and technical aspects of the rail mode's contribution to environmental and energy conservation, to industrial diversification, and to urban and regional redevelopment. In this paper we propose three strategic themes to guide railroad research in these new directions, and yield big dividends for the industry in the twenty-first century.

Title RFI application of RINF (Register of INFrastructure) for checking compatibility between rolling stock and infrastructure before planning operation
 
Author Galli, A
Genovesi, P
Re, R
Schillaci, M
Affiliation Rete Ferroviaria Italiana S.p.A., Rome, Italy
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The Decision 880/2014/EU, based on the Interoperability Directive, introduced the common specifications for RINF “Register of Infrastructure”, that every NREs (National Register Entities) at Member State level has to implement and update by collecting all the relevant technical features of ENE, CCS and INF subsystems of the railway network. ERA (European Railway Agency) is responsible for joining and connecting all the registers of the Member States throughout a web Common User Interface (CUI), that is publically available. The Decision 880/2014/EU stated that RINF purposes are: ensuring technical compatibility for fixed installations, monitoring progress of interoperability of the EU railway network, ascertaining route compatibility for proposed train service and designing Rolling Stock subsystems. Similarly for rolling stock, according to the specifications of the Decision 665/2011/EU, ERA shall make publicly accessible the “European Register of Authorized Types of Vehicles” ERATV, which identifies in a one-to-one manner all types of vehicle authorized for placing in service on the EU rail network. ERATV is meant to serve some technical and strategic purposes, among which there is allowing any organisation interested in running a railway related business to asses which vehicle types might be compatible with a particular part of the EU railway network (by means of comparing the characteristics of the vehicle with the data of RINF). In an open market vision at European level, ERA intends to interface ERATV and RINF to make the process of checking compatibility between rolling stock and infrastructure for future operation open, faster, easier and more efficient, reducing impact of human factor and time consumption in checking technical documents, verifying compatibility conditions, exchanging data. The contribution will compare the method based on the checks stated in the current RFI procedure for the authorization to operate on Torino-Milano high speed line issued by RFI for the vehicle type “V300ZEFIRO” with a new method involving an IT tool based on RINF specifications. The study checks the coherence and the consistency between these two methods, analyzing the critical items, eventual limitations or missings, the “nice to have”, the benefits.

Author Evans, R
Blumenfeld, M
Roberts, C
Schmid, F
Affiliation BCRRE, University of Birmingham, England
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In recent years, there has been a worldwide effort to determine long term visions, strategies and policies for railway systems. There is a common acknowledgement that the long lifecycle of railway systems requires nations to look beyond shorter political cycles. Usually, future aspirations and plans for the railway are set out in published documents, such as white papers and technical strategies. However, engineers and stakeholders often find these documents to be opaque, ambiguous and difficult to understand. Previous work has shown that this can increase the risk of misalignment between engineering projects and high level strategic aims. This paper presents the results of a systems engineering (SE) evaluation of four influential strategy documents. The documents are scored against the SE Competency Framework to determine how well they align with systems thinking. In some areas, the documents are very poorly aligned, suggesting that there is potential for better systems thinking in railway strategy development. The work contributes to the development of a systems approach to communicating strategy.

Title Best practice in procurement of commuter rail services
 
Author Meija, T
Lind, B
Affiliation Independent Transport Safety and Reliability Regulator, New South Wales
Source ATRI
Conference Australasian Transport Research Forum (ATRF), 32nd, 2009, Auckland, New Zealand
Publication Date 2009
Peer reviewed Yes
Abstract This paper examines evidence from international research into how governments can effectively procure commuter rail services for achieving their transport goals including growing patronage. The research initially surveyed 21 cities in Australia and overseas on their arrangements for procuring rail services and monitoring performance. The second stage focused on the effectiveness of purchaser-provider arrangements with a view to identifying best practice. This stage consisted of a literature review and in-depth interviews of transport authorities, rail operators and rail experts in Australia and Europe. The research identified successful practices in articulating transport goals into transport policies and strategies, governance and procurement arrangements, and noted trends toward: 1. formal arm's length contracts for both publicly and privately delivered transport services; 2. integrated transport and single transport authorities; 3. clear accountability for strategic, tactical and operational roles between purchasers and providers, and collaborative relationships between the two. While there is no template for an 'ideal contract', the research established key elements and principles in developing and implementing effective contracts and performance measurement. In addition to a contract, the research identified other effective ways of increasing public transport use.
