Title Rail costs, profitability and infrastructure in a competitive environment: The case of the Greek railways
 
Author Athanassakopoulos, DT
Affiliation Centre of Planning & Economic Research, Athens, Greece
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In analyzing railway performance in a competitive environment it is useful to take into account the technological abilities of railroads to satisfy the different requirements of users, which are described in the transportation models in terms of technological variables. Since these technological variables are closely related to costs, the analysis of the impact of their changes upon costs is of extreme importance for policies aimed at improving rail efficiency. This paper, by applying monthly time-series data of the Greek railways, attempts to evaluate the impact of railway infrastructure changes on rail costs and profitability in a competitive transportation market. This analysis is based on short-run marginal costs for the existing stock of railroad way-and-structure capital. Since Greek railroads, like most railroads, have allowed their maintenance expenditure on way-and-structure to decline, this stock of capital is unlikely to approach the optimal level. Thus rail costs might be reduced and efficiency improved by policies aimed at rationalizing the railroad infrastructure.

Title Optimizing the railways-operation by route-pricing?
 
Author Siegmann, J
Affiliation Technical University of Berlin, Berlin, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The reorganisation and privatisation of the former state-owned railways divided the railway companies into business units and network operation units. In order to fulfil the EU-regulations, the operators of train traffic have to pay a route price for using the tracks. Generally these route pricing systems can be very simple, e.g. to pay for train-km at the end of a period. On the other hand the network operation unit can add some factors and aspects to the price model to regulate the traffic. The aims are: to exhaust the whole network capacity, to optimize the operation quality and; to minimize the network operation costs by minimizing the maintenance and the detail parts of the network (switches, sidetracks etc.). This paper will show the general aspects and will discuss, what kind of factors can be implemented. One of the key-factors are the database, which gives the correct number of train-km on the parts of the network with a different price per km. Another question is the price margin for different kind of trains. How should the axle load be priced? What kind of cost/price-aspects are in dependence of the speed of the trains? It is necessary to define a very detailed route-pricing-system for railways in Europe with special parameters to optimize the railway operation.

Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy

Abstract The new concession was contracted, starting in 2013 until 2024, in a framework of procedural, financial and performance requirements. Metro and tram are the backbone of the transport system and are very much synonym for the image of the company. Management realized that both the financial and performance requirements needed a solid base in professional Asset Management. To get a clear understanding of the state of the organization a SWOT analyses based on several reviews, audits and benchmarks was made. One of the SWOT analyses targeted on the maintenance of trams and metro’s. The overall conclusion was that the maintenance organization was reactive, degraded and had availability as the only performance indicator (which was unstable and on a low level). Next to the day-to-day business a number of projects connected to a major infrastructural project had a big impact on the organization. New Metro’s, an expanded and renovated workshop for running maintenance and a new signalling and control system in the metro’s. A challenge to the organizations operational, logistical and technical skills. Although the maintenance organization is in Asset management terms the manager and the service provider, the standard PAS55 [1]/ISO55001 [2] has been very helpful in identifying and mapping the necessary changes. Since mid-2013 three approaches were followed; almost on a daily basis managing major disruptions, day-to-day running the business in regard to the operational performance and a program to structurally improve the organization into a high performance organization. The effect of these approaches was that it created a sense of urgency in the organization and it gave time and resources for the structural improvement. The improvement program was based on the SWOT analyses and the comparison with ISO 55001. The organizational change was performed in a structured approach with heavy involvement of management, staff and a limited number of external consultants. By the end of 2014 the program has ended and the results are integrated into the organization. A moment to map the organization against the standard. The results are visible in culture (proactive) and a more stable performance on a higher level.

Title The economic service life of track
 
Author Veit, P
Affiliation Graz University of Technology, Institute of Railway Engineering and Transport Economy, Graz, Austria
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Analysing life-cycle-cost of track depicts (re-)investment causing more than 50% of total track cost. Thus the identification of the economic service life is of major importance for reducing track costs. In principle the economic service life is reached as soon as the increase of maintenance costs overshoots the decrease of depreciation. As the decrease of the depreciation can easily be calculated, forecasting the maintenance demand is a precondition for service life optimisation and the real challenge. Based on time sequences of track data trends of degradation for the specific track section as well as the single components can be analysed and used for predicting the entire maintenance demand. As this methodology is already used for identifying the optimal point in time of major track re-investment projects of Infrastructure Company of Austrian Federal Railways (OeBB), the preciseness of the methodology and thus the maintenance predictions is already verified for a time span well above the period of six years defined for project decisions at OeBB. Furthermore the economic evaluation allows ranking projects in case of budget restrictions.

Title Calculating and modulating direct costs Track maintenance costs, vehicle quality, and track access charges – still the same engineer’s approach
 
Author Marschnig, S
Affiliation Graz University of Technology, Institute of Railway Engineering and Transport Economy, Graz, Austria
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Econometric models are used as a standard solution for estimating the marginal cost level of track access charges (TACs). This is a sufficient approach as long as charges are calculated on a network-wide basis. If detailed cost allocation systems and engineering knowledge about track and its maintenance needs are available and documented, it is possible to calculate the costs directly incurred by a train run bottom-up. The latter methodology enables the infrastructure managers to separate different effects in order to make access charges more transparent, closer to cost reality, and fair. Averaging the cost level leads to in-transparent, network-wide charges – a methodology that is proper enough as long as only one railway undertaking is operating trains in one market segment, as the sum of payments is constant. Most European infrastructure managers are facing a mixed traffic system showing much more effects than a top-down cost calibration can bear. The principles, now defined for the calculation and modulation of direct costs, give the possibility to re-design TACs from a scratch: modulation of direct costs on marginal cost level should be executed on a four-level methodology: Modulation 1: Separating points of exceptionally high track costs (curves, turnouts) Modulation 2: Separating costs due to higher speed levels (on lines exceeding speeds of average freight trains) Modulation 3: Adjusting costs due to vehicle characteristics Modulation 4: Adjusting costs due to market segment demands Modulations 1, 2, and 4 focus on different market segments on operator level. Most high-cost lines are operated with regional (national) passenger trains, while the international freight services and the interregional or international long distance passenger trains are generally more concentrated on the – cheaper – high capacity lines. On the latter lines, freight trains are obliged to pay for high(er)-speed standards if averaged charges are billed. Modulation 3 goes directly to one major task, missing in most charging systems. As long as track friendly vehicle concepts are not addressed by TACs, new rolling stock will be purchased due to other principles furtheron. Costs directly incurred by a train run vary due to the vehicle quality, of course. The methodology proposed is more complex in extracting different prices, but this is the task of the infrastructure manager. The charging itself is slightly more challenging, but thanks to modern computer-aided systems still manageable.

Abstract The economic regulation of railway infrastructure in Australia covers all links which are part of the interconnected rail system in Australia, since such infrastructure represents a natural monopoly. However, railways form part of logistics chains and the extent to which monopoly power can meaningfully be exercised is dependent on the nature and extent of rents available within the final consumption market each logistics chain serves. This paper identifies three distinct types of rents and analyses the incentives of a railway infrastructure owner in the presence of each. It then examines the major haulage tasks of Australian railways to ascertain whether the relevant railway infrastructure owner has both the incentives and the ability to make anticompetitive gains from the exploitation of its natural monopoly position. In most cases, it finds that they do not.

Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2002, Cost Efficient Railways through Engineering, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wollongong, New South Wales, November 10-13, 2002
Publication Date 2002
Abstract One of the most important attributes of train running is the capacity to slow or stop. The downside of actually doing so en route between traffic nodes is that transit times are extended and train operating costs are increased. Despite this there does not appear to have been much attention paid to costing the process of slowing or stopping trains. This paper attempts to redress this situation by investigating the cost that would be incurred by a typical freight train as a result of slowing or stopping from otherwise achievable running speeds - hence the title notation of Smooth Running. A range of cost factors were considered and built into a model. The variability of the cost to these factors was then considered, indicating that the significant variables associated with train stopping are train gross mass, normal running speed at the location concerned, and the dwell time when stopped. Similar factors were found to be significant for train slowing (such as for a temporary speed restriction or for a signal check) but with the restricted speed, train length and speed restriction length replacing dwell time. The cost of stopping and re-starting a typical east coast Superfreighter train is estimated to be in the order of $106 , while slowing the same train by as little as 20 km/h from 100 km/h is estimated to cost in the order of $30 with other degrees of slowing being between these two extremes.
