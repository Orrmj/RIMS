Title Digital route maps as an aid to GNSS railway applications
 
Author Hutchinson, M
Zheng, Y
Nasioula, Z
Lowe, D
Dumville, M
Affiliation Nottingham Scientific Ltd, Nottingham, UK
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract NSL are currently engaged in the Map3 demonstration project sponsored by the UK Future Railway organisation. Future Railway is a collaboration between Network Rail and the Rail Safety and Standards Board (RSSB), working with industry and the supply chain to deliver the UK Rail Technical Strategy. The project seeks to develop and demonstrate two Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) based train location concepts which make use of a Digital Route Map (DRM) as a means to enhance absolute positioning performance in terms of integrity, accuracy and availability. The motivation for this comes from the emergence of a prototype UK network model, developed by Network Rail, including geospatial and logical components. The first train location concept applies Map-Matching, most commonly used in the automotive sector within “SatNav” products, at a back-office to enhance GNSS-based train position solutions received within tracking reports. Such reports may originate from a variety of on-train systems depending on the train operator, e.g. passenger WiFi, driver advisory systems and passenger information systems. The process of Map-Matching brings consistency to these reported positions, which typically vary in quality, through identification of the correct rail track along with the provision of a confidence metric. This development has multiple potential non-safety-critical applications in the near-to-medium term. Some involve real time processing e.g. customer information systems while others are based on an offline approach e.g. “equivalent mega gross tonnage”. As a by-product of Map-Matching the capability to find errors within a DRM has been identified and this is being investigated as part of an additional activity. The second positioning concept seeks to utilise a DRM within an on-train locator alongside GNSS raw measurements and data from other sensors e.g. odometers and compasses. This is known as Map-Aiding. It targets safety-critical train control applications such as European Train Control System (ETCS) levels 2 and 3 where there is a need to move train location onboard without compromising the stringent safety performance of conventional signalling and ETCS Level 1. Benefits come through a reduction in trackside equipment which reduces costs in terms of both CAPEX and OPEX. This is currently a subject of much research within Europe and will be included in Shift2Rail demonstration activities. Finding the means to use a DRM in a safe, assured way may enable the cost of an on-train locator to be minimised.

Title Passenger demand-based train regulation for CBTC
 
Author Leveque, O
Affiliation Alstom - Australia New-Zealand
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract Traffic regulation aims to support the performance of the transport system in efficiently and reliably satisfying the service requirements and passenger experience. Current transport systems utilise a theoretical timetable which includes operational margins allowing the mitigation and recovery of some delays. But theoretical timetables are designed based on an average estimate of the passenger demand which cannot accurately reflect the reality of the passenger density on platforms over time along a line. As a matter of fact, traditional traffic regulation systems do not allow for any adjustments of the timetable in real-time with the actual passenger demand at stations but only regulate station dwell-times and travel times to recover the evaluated delay according to the target schedule or headway depending on the regulation strategy in use. This paper presents an innovative automatic train regulation solution for CBTC systems, which provides a real-time regulation according to the actual density of passengers waiting on platforms while at the same time controlling train departure and arrival times to avoid resulting delays. The proposed solution offers an efficient way to optimize the transport offer through significant enhancements of the service capacity, operational fluidity and reliability without major upgrades of the existing infrastructure.

Abstract All railway companies try to attain more regular and reliable train services, in order to be able to offer their customers a higher reliability and flexibility. The present, expensive, strategy is increasing "physical" capacity. A more cost effective alternative is to first, as far as possible, improve quality in the train control process itself. The most important factor in this process is the train dispatcher, who from a control centre supervise (monitor and control) the movements of trains. The dispatchers chances to, in an active way, plan and control the train traffic is often crucial for how different disturbances effect train delays. Within this research area, one focus has been on development of algorithms and systems, which support optimal computer-aided train dispatching in real-time (CATD-RT). We present a brief overview of existing knowledge and research and some important findings from a survey based on a set of interviews with dispatchers and train traffic experts. Also indicated are further studies necessary in order to, at the end, be able to successfully implement a system of this kind.

Abstract We can now obtain various kinds of and big volume of data concerning train operation. Analysis of these data is very useful to enhance punctuality and robustness of timetables. On the other hand, because the amount of the data is huge, it is strongly desired to establish an efficient approach with which we can get useful insights. In this paper, in order to detect causes of delays from train traffic records, we introduce an approach based on the association rule, which is a very popular technique in the data mining community. Abstract Although information overload is a widely-observed phenomenon, the provision of useful, accurate and timely information to railway users remains a challenge. The need and opportunities for improvement are illustrated by the recently-published Operational Philosophy for Britain’s railways, which sets out the agreed industry vision for the next 30 years and beyond, including the requirements for an operational system that can make use of intelligent information, and for the interactive provision to railway users of personalised, up-to-date information, as epitomised by Network Rail’s vision of a digital railway. This paper draws upon the experiences of railway customers and operators to establish the current status and deficiencies of information provision prior to and during travel/transport. It considers the findings of reviews undertaken of the provision of Passenger Information During Disruption (PIDD) by Britain’s railways It reviews the information currently and prospectively available within railway industry systems, the extent to which it is made available to users, and how it could best be provided. Based on these findings, the gaps between available and required customer information are identified, and the possibilities for making additional, existing industry information available to customers are assessed. Consideration is also given to how real-time travel/transport information should best be delivered to passengers and freight customers, and to customer-facing railway staff. The delivery of the proposed improvements is essential to the improved functioning of the railway industry in the 21st century, and, if implemented correctly, will provide improved customer satisfaction and increased patronage.

Title From data to information: provision of railway data to passengers in the information age
 
Author Morris, C
Easton, J
Roberts, C
Affiliation Birmingham Centre for Railway Research and Engineering, University Of Birmingham, Birmingham, United Kingdom
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract A series of rapid developments in ICT over the last 30 years have fundamentally changed the ways in which we work & spend our leisure time. The advent of affordable personal computing (mid 80s), the growth of the World Wide Web (late 90s), & the ability to receive / digest information on mobile computing devices including tablets & smartphones (last 5 years), has allowed us to make decisions more effectively based on up-to-date real-time information on the state of the world we live in.In the transport industry arguably the greatest impact of the new information-driven technologies has been in the area of passenger information, & in particular in journey planning. The railways, in common with other transport modes, have made good progress in the delivery of online timetables / journey planners, real-time services status reports & electronic ticketing to customers, but the real value of these tools will not be realised until a customers’ entire journey can be captured across transport modes; information services of this type will enable the impact of disruption to service on one transport mode to be viewed in the context of knock-on effects to the rest of the journey, allowing the passenger to understand what the disruption means to them, & opening up new possibilities for use of the multimodal transport network as a single system. The integration of data from across a number of transport modes is a challenge from the perspective of data modelling, as the meaning of terminology is often subtly different in each mode. As a result, large amounts of developer time can be spent ensuring that data is being used in the correct context, & reflecting that context in application code. The hard wiring of data context in this way means that applications frequently need to be updated in response to changes in one or more of the domain-specific data models. This paper presents work on a new family of data models (ontology) designed for use with the next generation of the Web (the Semantic Web) that capture the context & meaning of data in a machine interpretable form, allowing information to be used without ambiguity across transport modes. It shows how, in combination with linked data architectures, ontology allows data resources to evolve over time without the need for substantial changes to the applications using them. Finally, it describes ways in which railway undertakings can publish their data to ensure maximum reusability in next-generation web applications.

Abstract JR East Shinkansen is a representative of high-speed railway lines of Japan. To provide high quality service for passengers, JR East employees are expected to guide passengers with up-to-date and accurate information about train status. Conventionally, station staffs and train conductors have two basic approaches to obtain current train status; one is direct communication between employees, the other is to go to the station office and check the dedicated terminal. However, when it comes to traffic disturbance, relying on this conventional method makes it difficult for employees to acknowledge train status in a timely manner. To improve this situation, we developed a system called “S-Way” which transmits real-time train status to hand-held tablet device, processing data obtained from “COSMOS”, JR East’s existing train traffic control system. This is the first time to utilize public cloud computing for Shinkansen related systems. Now, with S-way, station staffs and train conductors can acknowledge train status information in realtime through hand-held tablets wherever they are. We expect that S-way further improves employee job performance and contributes to meeting higher satisfaction of passengers.

Source ACRI

Abstract At the time of growing demands on population mobility, increasing the attractiveness of transport systems while maintaining the effectiveness of their operation is a prerequisite for ensuring the sustainable development of railway transport. The increasing demand for travel comfort shall be satisfied through the development of information systems, which will provide railway customers with an integrated end-to-end solution for their travel needs – from transaction support to en-route assistance. Starting from May 2015, the IT²RAIL activities will be executed by the consortium of partners for the next 30 months. Their main goal is to implement solutions that respond to customer needs and thus meet the requirements mentioned above. The works will be carried out under the MG2.2-2014 ‘Smart Rail Services’ open call, within the ‘Mobility for Growth’ call of the Horizon 2020 work plan. The IT²RAIL approach aims to give the traveller full control of the door-to-door travel experience across transport modes and services. Both before and during the journey, travellers currently have only a limited amount of information regarding their travel plans (accommodation, weather conditions, disruptions, etc.). In case of sudden traffic irregularities they are not provided with actual information and usually have no tool to reschedule the travel plan in response to the current situation they faced. Journey planners are often searching for the optimal connection based only on planned timetables. Thus, lots of wrong decisions are made. Trip Tracker, which is an integral part of the IT²RAIL project, introduces a new set of functions, that shall allow users to cope better with travel disruptions. Access to their actual position together with regular monitoring of unforeseen irregularities enable Trip Tracker to identify relevant events that could affect traveller’s journey. Matching these events with user‘s preferences and door-to-door itineraries, stored in the Travel Companion, Trip Tracker aims to provide the user with a shield against disruptions or other travel conflicts by enabling seamless re-arrangement. To this end, Trip Tracker may automatically invoke building of suitable alternatives to travel plan, and, through the Travel Companion display alerts, offer alternative routings and trigger re-accommodation where applicable or desired.

Abstract Powerline technology is widely used in home application to extend an Ethernet network using already existing power cables thereby enhancing audio and video multimedia services and intranet connectivity. Ethernet is nowadays also one of the main standards for coach and train communication networks (TCN), as defined in IEC 61375-2-5 (ETB) and IEC 61375-3-4 (ECN). In trains, however, building an effective Ethernet LAN requires solutions for train coupling, i.e. linking individual coach networks together in a single integrated trail LAN. This is also where Powerline technology comes into play as a real alternative solution against wireless or standard Ethernet cables and connectors, both in refurbished and in new trains. Integrating Powerline technology in Passenger Information System devices allows to provide new services to comply with new passenger needs or security rules on existing trains. A real case of a Passenger Emergency Intercom subsystem with Passenger Intercom Units and Train Driver Handsets with integrated Powerline modules is presented. The paper also describes as well some applications on high speed and regional refurbished fleets and profiles some standard topologies for Point-to-Point train coupling and Bus PIS services. A special focus is on the characteristics of railway existing cables, connectors and train couplers, with the purpose to identify maximum communication data rate (Mbps) in different scenarios of train length and number of coaches. In addition to the hardware part, also the software side is examined, within the network OSI model layers, and different implementations are suggested. The aim is to show the benefits of Powerline solutions for rolling stock applications on existing trains and how to assure its compliance to railway standards.

Abstract Railways connect people, cities and regions which are increasingly being instrumented with ICT capabilities, where everything and everybody is a sensor, a data producer and consumer, has local storage and processing capabilities, and is interconnected in an open-ended network of Railway Operators, Retailers, Travel related Service Companies, Public Agencies, Businesses and Individuals which expands driven by market dynamics. In this paper, we describe “passenger-centrality” as a statement of the fact that passengers are consumers of ‘network products’ that exhibit network externalities. We describe the specific dynamics of the markets for these products, showing that their self-sustainability is maintained only beyond a minimum threshold and below a maximum limit of their size, both of which depend on the cost of compatibility between complementary products for a given network size. We describe a semantic interoperability approach to the challenge which leverages open data policies and semantic web technologies for the creation of an “open web” of transportation data, illustrating it with an example.

– GoA 3 is driverless train operation (DTO) where starting and stopping are automated but a train attendant operates the doors and drives the train in case of emergencies.
– GoA 4 is unattended train operation (UTO) where starting and stopping, operation of doors and handling of emergencies are fully automated without any on-train staff.

Abstract Considering the high speed rail (HSR) activities in the world, the International Union of Railways expects the worldwide HSR network to grow from about 10,000 km in 2008 to almost 37,000 km in 2025. Apart from Western Europe and Eastern Asia, where HSR has been operated since decades, countries like China and Turkey started to build new HSR lines.
To improve the economic and environmental performance of HSR operation, the German Aerospace Centre (DLR) develops a new concept for high speed trains within the project 'Next Generation Train' (NGT). Apart from being technically innovative, the NGT is expected to service entirely new HSR routes and to substitute short haul and feeder flights. For quantifying the potentials for operating the NGT, we developed a gravity model that predicts rail passenger movements. The model takes into account population size of two places, their gross domestic product and travel time. As basic input we used German data on rail travel demand and calibrated the gravity model using regression analysis. We further applied the model on western Europe. This allows verifying the results of the gravity model by comparing the calculated most frequented relations to the HSR lines planned within the Trans-European Network (TEN) proposed by the EU. Based on this reference scenario, we applied the gravity model on other regions in the world, in order to predict potentially new HSR lines for the NGT. Further we studied HSR routes for the identified lines. Based on data on orography, population density and surface waters, we evaluated the feasibility of possible routes using a geographic information system (GIS). Combining the GIS data with information on construction costs for HSR lines allows assessing the profitability of such HSR routes. By applying this new method, we conclude on worldwide potentials for the new NGT concept.
