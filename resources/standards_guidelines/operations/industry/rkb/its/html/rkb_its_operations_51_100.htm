Title Power line communication: a new approach for train passenger information systems
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Russo, D
Gatti, A
Ghelardini, A
Mancini, G
Verduci, A
Amato, D
Battani, R
Affiliation Trenitalia S.p.A., Firenze, Italy
TSF S.p.A., Bologna, Italy
Sadel S.p.A., Bologna, Italy
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Standard train communication between different railway vehicles usually works on Wire Train Bus (WTB), with physical layer commonly on line 17-18 (TCN) or 7-8 (TCN used by Trenitalia on older 13 poles train bus) of UIC cable. Devices transmitting information on TCN or TCN lines can be divided in two different categories: critical systems (driver desk, motor control, brakes, track signals, power electronics), mainly used for command and control systems and non critical systems (passenger audio, passenger video, vehicle diagnostics) that usually have no real-time requirements. In this paper an alternative approach dividing critical devices and non critical devices and defining a new physical and communication layer for non critical devices is considered. In this alternative approach, the physical layer is deployed either on coaxial cables (commonly used for passenger audio on trainset) or UIC cable (on other trains). In the last case, communication lines are placed on already used wires, like line 1 and 2, used for voice and general audio information to passengers, or line 10 and 11, used as remote control of train lights. The transport of information on these lines is performed with a power line communication module (PLC). Each PLC provides an interface with a band-pass filter to avoid interference with existing audio and light systems. The main parameters of PLC communications are the transmission bandwidth (normally expressed in ranges of kHz or MHz) and theoretical bits per second rate (bps) reached during the communication. This network architecture can be used both at train or vehicle level, providing two separated layers like the architecture adopted for WTB and MVB communication. In this system a PLCNODE is defined as the transceiver allowing bridging from train to vehicle networks and vice versa. This architecture is currently under test on some Italian commuter trains, equipped with an OBoE system which broadcast updated journey information to DOVE6 video devices connected with a PLC based network.

Mori, M
Affiliation East Japan Railway Company, Saitama, Japan
Daido Signal Co. Ltd, Tokyo, Japan
Hitachi, Ltd., Hitachinaka, Japan
Toshiba Corp., Tokyo, Japan
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract This paper describes a new signalling system which controls signalling field devices of automatic block signal between stations through an IP network. The system improves the method of the system already installed at Ichikawaono station on the Musasino line in February 2007. The Logic Controller (LC), placed in a signal house, exchanges the command and feedback data with the Field Controller (FC), placed near each automatic block signal, through the Ethernet Passive Optical Network (EPON). Following the command data, the FC electrically controls signalling field devices such as signals, track circuits, transponders of the Automatic Train Stop (i.e. Automatic Train Protection) system with Pattern (ATS-P), transponders of the S-type of ATS (ATS-S), and output relays. Only optical fiber cable is required between the LC and the FC. The system has high reliability because the LC, the FC, and the data paths of E-PON, are all duplex. The system provides sufficient maintenance information through the IP network. The system can realize higher reliability, less wire-connection work, less amount of cable, cost cutting, and faster troubleshooting. A prototype system was under evaluation on the Joban Rapid Service line between Mabashi and Kitakashiwa from August 2006 to January 2008. Evaluating the results of the field test, it was concluded that the prototype system is technically suitable for signal control.

Title SAT.engine: a new satellite based development tool for train control systems
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Schütte, J
Jurtz, S
Naundorf, A
Wenzel, B
Affiliation Dresden University of Technology, Institute for Transport Systems Engineering, Germany
Ingenieurgesellschaft für Schienenverkehrstechnik mbH, Berlin, Germany
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract With the introduction of the European Train Control System ETCS), the requirements for precision of input data, in particular with respect to positions and distances, are substantially higher than for conventional control and signalling installations. Precise distances between balises and other relevant locations in the track are mandatory for the safety, functionality and operational availability of ETCS. Determination of functions and parameters such as the length of Movement Authorities (MA), balise linkings or speed or gradient profiles are also mandatory requirements. SAT.engine is a measurement and engineering platform that has been developed specifically for ETCS projects. The tool determines distances between signalling components in the track area with respect to the track centreline. The measurement and engineering process is highly efficient and represents therefore an economically beneficial alternative to conventional measurement systems. The SAT.engine measurement components are installed within hours on any rail vehicle and can operate at speeds of up to 100km/h. First objective is the position determination on the base of satellite measurements (DGPS), complemented by an inertial navigation sensor and by correction services. A synchronized recorded video stream correlates the measured positions with individual track video images that may be used offline for further engineering applications. The precision of the distance measurements are compatible with the practical requirements in ETCS projects (<1m). A particular SAT.engine SW-environment serves for a variety of offline project engineering tasks. Thus, SAT.engine finds utilization in multiple phases during the engineering life cycle, in particular for system planning and validation (and verification). The performance of the SAT.engine tools environment had been proven in recent ETCS-projects. Recorded and generated data are stored within an ETCS data base and may be interfaced to other planning and project engineering tools through multiple interface standards.

Title Adapting the train for use of COTS (commercial off the shelf) ICT equipment
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Boom, P
Bos, B
de Grauw, JT
Affiliation Lloyd's Register Rail, Utrecht, The Netherlands
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Increasingly, train operating companies and other stakeholders within the railway industry are looking at incorporating ICT (information and communication technology) as well as internet technology in trains. Be it to offer wireless internet to passengers, getting real-time travel information to the train, or to improve internal processes, the amount of secondary (meaning non-safety critical) ICT on trains is on the rise. Generally speaking, two things may be done with regard to ICT components on trains: adapt the component to the train (meaning train-proof equipment)and adapt the train to the component. Up until now, most if not all components used have had to conform to train norms and standards, greatly reducing the free choice of components and increasing costs and time to market. These factors prevent flexibility for train operating companies (or other stakeholders) with regards to exchanging components, or adding new services. In this light, consider the lifecycle of the train, versus ICT equipment, versus ICT services. Trains usually have a lifecycle of thirty to forty years, ICT equipment tends to be outdated in five years due to, for instance, processor upgrades or software requirements. ICT and internet services offered to the general public have an even shorter shelf life. What's in vogue right now can be obsolete in 6 month's time. Consequently, requirements for secondary ICT to be used on trains can be quite a challenge leading to a natural desire to use standard, Commercial Off The Shelf, or COTS for short, components as much as possible, while maintaining acceptable RAMS scores. Especially for customer-facing and/or image building equipment, failure rates need to be low. This last factor, reliability, has historically led to having to use train-proof components.

The speed management project has been launched to enhance capacity in bottlenecks and to reduce overall train delays. Once the strategic constraint points have been determined, it is necessary to cope with the train order. When potential conflicts appear between two trains, the system automatically sends a speed advisory to one of the train drivers. The modulation of the speed well before the node allows for trains to arrive at the right time and to pass through it at full speed. Abstract The trend across the world is for more interoperable signalling solutions: European Train Control System (ETCS) on mainline railways in Europe, Positive Train Control (PTC) on the American class one railroads, and limited interoperability within the metro systems being introduced in Paris and New York. Up until now, the heavy-haul railways have considered that, due to their closed nature, interoperability is not applicable to them. Their systems do not need to be compatible with those of any other railway. However, solutions with some additional interoperability are now available, but will such solutions reduce overall costs? With standard gauge locomotives being significantly cheaper (per horsepower) than narrow gauge locomotives, and all modern telecommunication systems having commercial-standard interfaces, one might expect interoperable solutions to become the norm. The IT industry would suggest that this is the case as product lines mature. This paper specifically looks at the heavy-haul signalling industry and the functionality that is important for such operations, such as managing in-train forces for long trains. One subsystem is now being supplied in purely “soft” form to be run on the hardware platform of another. This widens the range of interfaces that must be considered for system compatibility. The benefits of additional interoperability, other than the initial capital cost of the system, are harder to quantify. This paper lists some of these, and describes the impact that greater interoperability may bring. These include operational flexibility of rolling stock, standardisation of driver/operator/maintainer training, and standardisation of business systems. Given that the asset being controlled by the signalling system has a value of 20 to 100 times that of the train control equipment, any asset utilisation improvement that interoperability can bring is extremely valuable. Overall, this paper makes the case for interoperability for heavy-haul signalling solutions. It shows that the cost savings are in the total cost of ownership of the railway, rather than directly in the train control system. Whilst interoperability may not be a project goal, it is a means of achieving lower total costs of ownership.

Title Cost reductions through telecommunications

Abstract Rapidly increasing demand for rail services requires the use of technology to ensure current and future infrastructure is used efficiently and that operating costs are reduced. The leading technology in achieving this efficiency is information and communications, with wireless telecommunications allowing information to be easily transitioned between the office and the field. The increasing presence of wireless telecommunications is opening up opportunities to strip costs out of rail operations through automating many basic functions that involve the accessing, provision and processing of information. The current predominant use of wireless telecommunications is voice to manage the many issues and incidents that occur on a daily basis. However, through the use of wireless data and applications there can be significant reductions in the cost of many daily rail operations. Significant numbers of rail entities have already embarked on the use of wireless data to support communications based high capacity signalling systems that greatly increase the efficiency of existing rail infrastructure and assets and defer the significant costs associated with constructing additional infrastructure in existing corridors. Yet, it is not always necessary to spend millions of dollars to achieve reductions in operational costs once reliable data communication capabilities are established. While there are a myriad of initiatives that can leverage telecommunications, this paper considers just two examples, which may be known to the reader, but are still to be adopted into rail operating environments. Maintaining the safety of resources working on the rail lines is paramount for all rail operators. However, the current manual labour intensive systems used to ensure safety can mean that the time and resources on site is disproportionate to the time and resources required for the actual task. The system maintaining safety could be transferred to a smart telecommunications based application that would significantly reduce the time on site while maintaining safety. As new rolling stock is brought into service, the ability to extract and analyse large amounts of information from data loggers and other sources, which can provide powerful measurement tools, allowing a tailored proactive approach to operational and maintenance activities. To date, the gathering of information about rolling stock condition has been limited to locomotive units rather than the more passive carriages and wagons. Though, of the things that are more likely to interrupt the progression of a train, the locomotive unit is in the minority. Any monitoring of carriages and wagons has typically been of the condition of the freight itself rather than the condition of the rolling stock. Small, low power, self building, self healing communication based sensory nodes could allow management of the whole train at all times rather than only a part of the train or only in specific isolated locations. Wireless telecommunications, intelligent applications and the increasing processing capability to support higher levels of automation can be leveraged by the Australian Rail Industry to turn constrained reactionary activities into planned, informed, predictive and automated activities and processes that will positively impact the industry’s efficiency and reduce its cost.

- Network resilience and maintainability
- Network security or Cyber Security
- Critical and non-critical data on the same network
This white paper will focus on how the change can be managed and how data can be sent in a timely, secure and sustainable way in the challenging environment of the trackside.
