Title Unattended vehicle/track interaction (V/TI) monitoring systems: applied approaches for improving track safety and maintenance planning
 
Author Stevens, J
Brown, J
Maldony, M
Affiliation Ensco
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract With increased demands of rail transport, the challenge of finding and fixing poor track conditions before they cause vehicle damage or derailments is becoming more difficult every day. Railroads throughout the world are turning to new technologies to meet this need—new technologies that enable poor track conditions be detected by revenue service vehicles and reported to the Maintenance of Way (MoW) managers or rail operations managers, indicating the location and severity of problem spots. Over the past five years, North American railroads have rapidly embraced one such technology that provides unattended monitoring of the interactions that take place between the rail vehicles and the track. This paper presents a summary of unattended Vehicle/Track Interaction (V/TI) Monitoring technology employed in North America across high speed, inter-city passenger and freight railroad operations. The paper addresses the technology benefits and provides a profile of how some of the largest railroads in North America have established internal procedures for using data from unattended measurement systems to streamline maintenance and safety initiatives.

Title GSM-R for non-European railways and the migration from an existing analogue system
 
Author Saul, J
Affiliation Siemens
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract GSM-R is a technology developed for the Railways in Europe, yet it is now also being chosen by railways in other parts of the world as the medium through which they can update their communications systems. This paper looks at the background that led to the GSM-R specifications being developed and how those specifications are being managed today. When choosing GSM-R all railways, and particularly non-European ones, gain the immediate advantage of implementing a proven technology which has already accumulated significant operational experience in the railway arena. However they need to be aware of the implications and consequences of any deviations from the specifications that they require their suppliers to provide. The paper discusses typical national deviations and will highlight the consequences of such specification changes. Once a railway has decided to adopt a GSM-R based system, how do they migrate their communications safely and effectively from what they currently have to the new system? This question is addressed by examining how some of the railways in Europe are going about this task, or in some cases how they have already achieved it. This paper identifies the possible strategies that exist and their strengths and weaknesses so that railway operators can make their own informed decision.

Title Adapting the train for use of COTS (commercial off the shelf) ICT equipment
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Boom, P
Bos, B
de Grauw, JT
Affiliation Lloyd's Register Rail, Utrecht, The Netherlands
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Increasingly, train operating companies and other stakeholders within the railway industry are looking at incorporating ICT (information and communication technology) as well as internet technology in trains. Be it to offer wireless internet to passengers, getting real-time travel information to the train, or to improve internal processes, the amount of secondary (meaning non-safety critical) ICT on trains is on the rise. Generally speaking, two things may be done with regard to ICT components on trains: adapt the component to the train (meaning train-proof equipment)and adapt the train to the component. Up until now, most if not all components used have had to conform to train norms and standards, greatly reducing the free choice of components and increasing costs and time to market. These factors prevent flexibility for train operating companies (or other stakeholders) with regards to exchanging components, or adding new services. In this light, consider the lifecycle of the train, versus ICT equipment, versus ICT services. Trains usually have a lifecycle of thirty to forty years, ICT equipment tends to be outdated in five years due to, for instance, processor upgrades or software requirements. ICT and internet services offered to the general public have an even shorter shelf life. What's in vogue right now can be obsolete in 6 month's time. Consequently, requirements for secondary ICT to be used on trains can be quite a challenge leading to a natural desire to use standard, Commercial Off The Shelf, or COTS for short, components as much as possible, while maintaining acceptable RAMS scores. Especially for customer-facing and/or image building equipment, failure rates need to be low. This last factor, reliability, has historically led to having to use train-proof components.

Affiliation KiwiRail Network
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2010, Rail - Rejuvenation and Renaissance, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wellington, New Zealand, 12-15 September, 2010
Publication Date 2010
Abstract By 2013 the Auckland Metropolitan Rail Network (AMRN) will be electrified with a 25kV AC electric traction system. One major hurdle to achieving this goal has been the signalling system in Auckland which was predominately in excess of 30 years old and not immunised against the effects of electric traction. To remedy this, and the other operational deficiencies of the signalling, a major project to completely re-signal the AMRN was initiated. An important requirement of Auckland’s new signalling system is that it provides some form of Automatic Train Protection (ATP) to mitigate the growing risk of a signal being passed at stop resulting in a dangerous situation. This requirement was born from an extensive risk analysis study that was undertaken by KiwiRail Network (formerly ONTRACK) and included involvement of all Operators on the AMRN. The ATP system selected for the AMRN is the European Train Control System (ETCS) Level 1. With this decision, and the allocation of Country ID 600 (NID_C:600) by the European Railway Agency (ERA), New Zealand took its first tentative steps towards implementing what is becoming the ATP system of choice around the globe. This paper gives an overview of how we determined the need for ATP, how we decided that ETCS Level 1 was the right technology for the AMRN, and how we have chosen to implement it. The paper will briefly describe New Zealand’s ETCS application principles and provide some insight into how we developed them. The paper will also discuss some of the problems encountered and lessons learned thus far. Finally, the paper will provide an update on how the AMRN resignalling project and ETCS implementation is progressing.

Title Vital-positive train control in the United States and its relationship to Australia's advanced train management system
 
Author Groves, B
Affiliation Lockheed Martin
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2010, Rail - Rejuvenation and Renaissance, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wellington, New Zealand, 12-15 September, 2010
Publication Date 2010
Abstract Lockheed Martin has demonstrated the viability of safety critical, centralised train control with the Vital – Positive Train Control (V-PTC) project. V-PTC does not rely upon track circuits, balises, or trackside signals, but uses a centralised server for interlocking functionality and a trainborne system to provide operational context to Train Drivers and enforcement functions to ensure no unsafe conditions may occur. This significantly reduces maintenance cost, allows potential for expansion of network capacity, and improves safety over conventional safeworking systems. The tests conducted in 2008 and 2009 have shown the ability to locate the train with an along-track accuracy of 12 ft, 99%, in areas with no GPS visibility. Additionally, the ability to remotely monitor track circuits, remotely monitor and control points, and centrally distribute safe authorities on an electronic block basis to trains was demonstrated. If the trains violated the speed or authority limits, the trains were automatically enforced to a stop. V-PTC is closely aligned with the Advanced Train Management System (ATMS), currently in development under contract by the Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC). The two programs have similar goals and operating environments, identical system architectures and share inter-element interfaces.

Title Does interoperability reduce the costs of heavy-haul signalling systems?
 
Author Terry, N
Affiliation Aecom
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2013, Driving the Costs out of Rail, 26-28 November 2013, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2013
Abstract The trend across the world is for more interoperable signalling solutions: European Train Control System (ETCS) on mainline railways in Europe, Positive Train Control (PTC) on the American class one railroads, and limited interoperability within the metro systems being introduced in Paris and New York. Up until now, the heavy-haul railways have considered that, due to their closed nature, interoperability is not applicable to them. Their systems do not need to be compatible with those of any other railway. However, solutions with some additional interoperability are now available, but will such solutions reduce overall costs? With standard gauge locomotives being significantly cheaper (per horsepower) than narrow gauge locomotives, and all modern telecommunication systems having commercial-standard interfaces, one might expect interoperable solutions to become the norm. The IT industry would suggest that this is the case as product lines mature. This paper specifically looks at the heavy-haul signalling industry and the functionality that is important for such operations, such as managing in-train forces for long trains. One subsystem is now being supplied in purely “soft” form to be run on the hardware platform of another. This widens the range of interfaces that must be considered for system compatibility. The benefits of additional interoperability, other than the initial capital cost of the system, are harder to quantify. This paper lists some of these, and describes the impact that greater interoperability may bring. These include operational flexibility of rolling stock, standardisation of driver/operator/maintainer training, and standardisation of business systems. Given that the asset being controlled by the signalling system has a value of 20 to 100 times that of the train control equipment, any asset utilisation improvement that interoperability can bring is extremely valuable. Overall, this paper makes the case for interoperability for heavy-haul signalling solutions. It shows that the cost savings are in the total cost of ownership of the railway, rather than directly in the train control system. Whilst interoperability may not be a project goal, it is a means of achieving lower total costs of ownership.

Title Cost reductions through telecommunications
 
Author Harris, P
Affiliation Public Transport Victoria (PTV)
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2013, Driving the Costs out of Rail, 26-28 November 2013, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2013
Abstract Rapidly increasing demand for rail services requires the use of technology to ensure current and future infrastructure is used efficiently and that operating costs are reduced. The leading technology in achieving this efficiency is information and communications, with wireless telecommunications allowing information to be easily transitioned between the office and the field. The increasing presence of wireless telecommunications is opening up opportunities to strip costs out of rail operations through automating many basic functions that involve the accessing, provision and processing of information. The current predominant use of wireless telecommunications is voice to manage the many issues and incidents that occur on a daily basis. However, through the use of wireless data and applications there can be significant reductions in the cost of many daily rail operations. Significant numbers of rail entities have already embarked on the use of wireless data to support communications based high capacity signalling systems that greatly increase the efficiency of existing rail infrastructure and assets and defer the significant costs associated with constructing additional infrastructure in existing corridors. Yet, it is not always necessary to spend millions of dollars to achieve reductions in operational costs once reliable data communication capabilities are established. While there are a myriad of initiatives that can leverage telecommunications, this paper considers just two examples, which may be known to the reader, but are still to be adopted into rail operating environments. Maintaining the safety of resources working on the rail lines is paramount for all rail operators. However, the current manual labour intensive systems used to ensure safety can mean that the time and resources on site is disproportionate to the time and resources required for the actual task. The system maintaining safety could be transferred to a smart telecommunications based application that would significantly reduce the time on site while maintaining safety. As new rolling stock is brought into service, the ability to extract and analyse large amounts of information from data loggers and other sources, which can provide powerful measurement tools, allowing a tailored proactive approach to operational and maintenance activities. To date, the gathering of information about rolling stock condition has been limited to locomotive units rather than the more passive carriages and wagons. Though, of the things that are more likely to interrupt the progression of a train, the locomotive unit is in the minority. Any monitoring of carriages and wagons has typically been of the condition of the freight itself rather than the condition of the rolling stock. Small, low power, self building, self healing communication based sensory nodes could allow management of the whole train at all times rather than only a part of the train or only in specific isolated locations. Wireless telecommunications, intelligent applications and the increasing processing capability to support higher levels of automation can be leveraged by the Australian Rail Industry to turn constrained reactionary activities into planned, informed, predictive and automated activities and processes that will positively impact the industry’s efficiency and reduce its cost.

Title Railway technology and innovation: driverless trains
 
Author Trestour, A
De-Reuck, A
Affiliation Alstom Transport
Source Permanent Way Institution (PWI)
Conference Permanent Way Institution (PWI) NSW 2015 Annual Convention: Fifty Shades of Perway: the Allure of the Rail Industry
Publication Date 2015-10-30
Abstract The notion of Automatic Train Operation (ATO) defines the operational safety levels against functional requirements needed to help automate train operations. The International Association of Public Transport (UITP) has defined five Grades of Automation (GoA) for trains. The GoA’s are defined according to basic functions of train operation and split in operational responsibilities whether it is for humans or the system itself.
– GoA 0 is a train driver on-sight operation, similar to a tram system running in street traffic, reliant entirely on train driver to manage the system safety.
– GoA 1 is a train operation where a train driver controls starting and stopping, operation of doors and handling of emergencies or sudden diversions, but with an Automatic Train Protection (speed and signal past at danger protection control).
– GoA 2 is semi-automatic train operation (STO) where stopping is automated but a driver in the cab starts the train, operates the doors, drives the train if needed and handles emergencies. Many ATO systems are GoA 2.
– GoA 3 is driverless train operation (DTO) where starting and stopping are automated but a train attendant operates the doors and drives the train in case of emergencies.
– GoA 4 is unattended train operation (UTO) where starting and stopping, operation of doors and handling of emergencies are fully automated without any on-train staff.

Title Satellite technologies and IP telecommunications at the forefront of the ERTMS/ETCS evolution of cost effective solutions
 
Author Golda, M
Kumar, V
Affiliation AnsaldoSTS
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2012, Rail's Role in Society, 27-28 November 2012, Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2012
Abstract The role of signalling systems is to increase safety, availability and traffic efficiency of railway lines. These targets are the basis for the European Rail Traffic Management System (ERTMS) becoming the industry standard in most new lines and major upgrades worldwide, and for the adoption of Positive Train Control (PTC) in the USA. The new generation of signalling systems will be that which allows further simplifications of wayside infrastructure and higher performance; and will be tailored for the needs of all kinds of railway operators. To ensure cost-effective train localisation and protection over long stretches of semi-deserted areas (typically freight applications with full automation in regions such as Australia, and on low-traffic passenger lines) satellite-based localisation with SIL 4 accuracy combined with IP-based telecommunications networks based on well-consolidated signalling products and platforms, will be available to railway operators. These technology enhancements will result in a major reduction in investment in ground infrastructure and operational costs while maintaining current safety levels. The radio network plays a very important role and often tips the balance in the optimisation of system costs especially when a dedicated network has to be deployed and operated along a rural/remote area with challenging environmental conditions. New IP-based telecommunications networks which combine wireless, cellular and satellite technologies will provide more efficient, seamless connectivity by minimising the need of dedicated infrastructures. In order to optimise signalling infrastructure further, satellite train localisation also becomes a viable alternative by implementing the concept of the virtual balise (already anticipated in the European Train Control System (ETCS) platform) and as Absolute Position reference points in PTC platforms. Ansaldo STS is contracted to deliver leading-edge next-generation signalling solutions based on proven signalling products with satellite localisation and IP-based telecommunications networks for the Roy Hill rail netwrok in Australia. Ansaldo STS is also associated with new technologies in a Test Site located in Sardinia, Italy. This has the strong support of Rete Ferroviaria Italiana (RFI), which owns Italy s rail network and major satellite stakeholders such as the European Space Agency (ESA) and the GALILEO Supervisory Authority. This paper will give an overview of the role of satellite geolocalisation and telecommunications for signalling systems, outline the components of the Sardinia-based test site and explore the next frontier of improved traffic efficiency: moving block technology.

Author Eisenbach, S
Palmer, C
Affiliation UBIMET (Institute for Ubiquitous Meteorology) Pty Ltd, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2017, Rail's Digital Revolution, 21-23 November 2017, Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2017
Abstract The digital revolution has reached wayside monitoring systems. The monitoring of natural hazards and critical meteorological conditions can be a challenge, particularly in large countries with long track sections through uninhabited areas. Using new approaches from meteorology and big data analysis allows for significantly improved monitoring and planning for an entire network, including real time monitoring of remote track sections. Installing and maintaining sensors at very close intervals can come with high costs. For this reason UBIMET (Institute for Ubiquitous Meteorology) has developed a special analysis method for the realtime monitoring of rail networks using all available data. This method has already been adopted by European national railway infrastructure companies and has now been adapted for the special requirements and natural hazards of Australia, in part by implementing three-dimensional lightning data for flash flood warnings or using simulations from aviation for the calculation of the track temperature. Implementation of advanced techniques for remote monitoring has several advantages. The meteorological analysis data and forecast data can be digitally implemented in existing monitoring systems or signaling systems and the data can be used as well for “big data” analysis, such as maintenance monitoring, predictive maintenance methods and identifying patterns in weather related patterns in passenger travel behavior.

Title City railway capacity increase through in-cab signalling
 
Author Heibel, F
Affiliation Doc Frank Pty Ltd, Perth, Western Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract The metropolitan rail systems in all major Australian cities developed as suburban commuter networks with low to medium traffic density, lines intertwined by junctions for operational flexibility, and partly mixed traffic with interstate passenger and freight trains. The unprecedented recent growth in patronage requires those rail systems to increasingly resemble urban mass transit metro systems in the inner city areas, while the outer network areas retain their suburban traffic characteristic. To accommodate more frequent services of a growing train fleet, building additional tracks is usually prohibitive due to lack of available city real estate and associated costs, e.g. for building underground. It is comparably inexpensive to increase line capacity by enhancing existing signalling systems with modern incab technology. Communications Based Train Control (CBTC) is the system of choice for high-capacity metro rail systems, but does not necessarily fit the historically grown Australian suburban networks. The most popular in-cab signalling technology for mainline applications is the European Train Control System (ERTMS/ETCS) which however does not match the potential capacity provision of CBTC. Based on current case studies, this paper discusses ways to identify the most suitable in-cab signalling technologies for Australian city networks.

Title Machine learning approaches for tamping effectiveness prediction
 
Author Tan, CW
Webb, GI
Petitjean, F
Reichl, P
Affiliation Faculty of Information Technology, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Tamping is a maintenance procedure which repacks ballast particles under sleepers in order to restore the correct geometrical position of ballasted tracks. Tamping is often used when geometrical issues are first identified. However, historical data often shows that tamping is not always the most appropriate, or effective, form of maintenance to restore track to its nominal condition. Furthermore, ineffective or unnecessary tamping tends to reduce the lifetime of existing track assets. The objective of this work is to develop a form of pattern recognition system to predict the likely tamping effectiveness ahead of time. In this work, the tamping effectiveness is predicted using a machine learning technique. The training data set consists of multivariate sensor data from Instrumented Revenue Vehicles (IRVs). The data between the previous and current tamping dates form time series. These are labelled with the tamping effectiveness based on the responses recorded before and after tamping. The labelled time series data is then used to train a time series classifier. In this case, the state of the art time series classification algorithm, k-Nearest Neighbour (k-NN) is extended to the case of multivariate data. To label a new time series at another potential tamping location that is not in the training dataset, the algorithm uses the expected tamping effectiveness from the time series in the database that is the most similar to the target. This allows the algorithm to identify locations where tamping is likely be ineffective. This work was able to achieve high accuracy in the prediction of tamping effectiveness and it is hoped that the results will help in assisting decision making for maintenance planning activities.

Title Computer-aided maintenance and renewal of track
 
Author Esveld, C
Conference 4th International Heavy Haul Conference, Railways in action, 11-15 September 1989, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1989
Abstract Every year large amounts of money are spent on track maintenance and renewal. Cost reduction and better control of maintenance processes therefore remain actual issues. The paper describes an approach to analyse the deterioration of track components and suggests methods for helping the engineer decide when maintenance is necessary and when is the best time for renewal. The general concept of the computer system described here is that it stores data about the track condition, first of all as a monitoring exercise, showing all relevant data together as a function of time. The data should come not just from automatic measurements, but also from visual or manual inspection. The data bank contains current and historical information which gives the possibility to average the data over periods of time and look at trends with time. The system can provide priority lists for activities such as tamping and grinding and these can include geographical information by putting the information on a map. In addition the system allows for information on the effectiveness of these maintenance operations. Also attention is given to analyse the data for making service life predictions.
