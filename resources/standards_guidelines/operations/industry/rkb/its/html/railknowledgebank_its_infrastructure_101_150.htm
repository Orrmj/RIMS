Abstract Historically, the risk of cyber-attack against operational railway systems has been considered unlikely because traditional Industrial Automation Control Systems (IACS) systems were of bespoke design and isolated from external influence. However, the appearance of Stuxnet in 2010 and the proliferation of other IACS malware since then has meant that the risk to IACS has increased and must be revaluated. The increase of interconnected digital assets in the railway sector in parallel with an increase of cyber-crime targeting IACS has driven the need for a coordinated approach to deliver a cybersecure infrastructure to the railway sector. The approach includes the assessment of IEC 62443 cybersecurity framework against the requirements of the railway sector, the definition of a generic cybersecurity context and high-level architecture, together with the specification of a common approach for security risk assessment. Based on results of the risk assessment applied to generic railway signalling architecture, common protection profiles for railway signaling specific equipmentâs have been specified in order to build a common cybersecurity reference for railway components. In parallel, security-by-design concepts are investigated to specify a common quality framework for cybersecurity development in the railway sector.

Abstract The non-ballasted technology has yielded numerous new railway tracks, especially in tunnels. The âlow vibration trackâ (LVT) is a system developed in 1980s, and it today covers more than 1100km of track around the world, including the Channel tunnel. This system contains a concrete block based on a micro-cellular pad all integrated in a rubber boot embedded in the slab of unreinforced concrete. It is difficult to determine the block degradation because it is hidden by the surrounding. This article presents a fast and efficient detection method for the block monitoring. Basically, the block damage occurs in two types: vertical and horizontal cracks. Finally, the detection method has been developed to identify block damage level by using modal analysis of vibration response. A robot called COBRA has been developed for the Channel tunnel which automatizes the block monitoring at a high speed.

Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Visual inspection of railway infrastructure elements is typically a slow and dangerous task, performed manually by track workers or special measurement trains. Siemens Mobility and Strukton Rail teamed up to improve this process and make it safer, combining data analytics and artificial intelligence knowledge from Siemens with the operational maintenance expertise of Strukton. In this paper, the approach of the so-called Video Track Inspector (VTI) will be explained in detail and its benefits for railways will be outlined. The VTI allows for the automated fault inspection of insulated rail joints: essential safety-critical components in track circuitries that control signaling, and which prevent the train from derailing. Video cameras from measurement trains or in-revenue trains deliver input for data analytics: at first, the rail joints are automatically identified, secondly typical failures are identified and classified by an algorithm. On this basis, work orders for preventive maintenance purposes can automatically be created and transferred to a Maintenance Management System. By using the VTI, numerous benefits can be achieved: the automated defect detection allows savings for network operators, as well as higher reliability of fault detection due to exclusion of human error. Furthermore, the safety of the staff and for rail transport increases because of the elimination of foot patrols by track workers. By in future using camera data from in-revenue trains, there is no need for track closures anymore - this leads to higher availability of the track for regular operation trains and higher passenger comfort.

Abstract Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC), referred to as Positive Train Control (PTC) by freight railroads in the USA, was originally conceived to be a safer, more productive and more cost-effective system of train control than conventional signalling systems. One aspect of the added safety was to be attained by fail-safe enforcement brought about by a locomotive-borne system that knows where the train is within the limits of the authority provided to it and is able to apply the brakes in time to prevent violation of limits in the event that the train crew fails to respond. Another aspect is to use PTC enforcement of speed limits. In practice, however, the use of enforcement has decreased capacity and slowed the operation in many situations. The reason is that braking distance can vary substantially from one train to the next because of factors which are not known precisely-factors such as train weight, the rate at which the train brakes will apply or the overall braking efficiency of the train. Enforcement braking is usually based on a full service penalty brake application that is an action of last resort when the onboard system determines that the train will overrun a target if the penalty application is not invoked. Because of the uncertainty of the factors affecting the braking distances, enforcement algorithms are almost invariably based on worst-case braking characteristics in order to assure not to overrun the limits of the authority. The problem is that there are many circumstances when the system calls for enforcement sooner than the train crew would start normal service braking. This is particularly acute for trains operating at slow speeds or on down-gradients. Very often, capacity and efficiency are affected when trains are forced to stop well short of the authority limits or to start slowing earlier than normal operating practice. This paper will investigate the elements that contribute to the problem of the variability of braking distance, describe the issues associated with using a static enforcement algorithm and review techniques being analyzed and tested at the Transportation Technology Center, Incorporated (TTCI ) to change a static enforcement algorithm into one which is dynamic and adaptive. The paper will conclude with the potential improvements to the operation that an adaptive algorithm can produce.
