Title Improving information provision for railway passenger and freight customers
 
Author Armstrong, J
Preston, J
Hood, I
Affiliation University of Southampton, Southampton, UK
Arup, London, UK
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Although information overload is a widely-observed phenomenon, the provision of useful, accurate and timely information to railway users remains a challenge. The need and opportunities for improvement are illustrated by the recently-published Operational Philosophy for Britainâs railways, which sets out the agreed industry vision for the next 30 years and beyond, including the requirements for an operational system that can make use of intelligent information, and for the interactive provision to railway users of personalised, up-to-date information, as epitomised by Network Railâs vision of a digital railway. This paper draws upon the experiences of railway customers and operators to establish the current status and deficiencies of information provision prior to and during travel/transport. It considers the findings of reviews undertaken of the provision of Passenger Information During Disruption (PIDD) by Britainâs railways It reviews the information currently and prospectively available within railway industry systems, the extent to which it is made available to users, and how it could best be provided. Based on these findings, the gaps between available and required customer information are identified, and the possibilities for making additional, existing industry information available to customers are assessed. Consideration is also given to how real-time travel/transport information should best be delivered to passengers and freight customers, and to customer-facing railway staff. The delivery of the proposed improvements is essential to the improved functioning of the railway industry in the 21st century, and, if implemented correctly, will provide improved customer satisfaction and increased patronage.

Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The railroad blocking problem is categorized into the tactical level of freight rail transportation. The objective of this problem is to determine the optimal paths for each shipment provided that the railway limitations are satisfied. The quantity of demand is usually assumed known and certain in this problem. Since, a time horizon is varied from one to three months, the ignorance of uncertainty in demand could incur the clear difference between the optimal solution of deterministic and stochastic models. Due to the size and complexity of the stochastic model, the efficient algorithm has been proposed. This algorithm is developed based on the L-Shaped method and then modified by using the valid inequality. The extensive experiments on the test networks show that the algorithm outperforms the CPLEX software.

Title Freightmiser: helping drivers to save fuel and arrive on time
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Howlett, P
Pudney, P
Vu, X
Yee, R
Affiliation Centre for Industrial and Applied Mathematics, University of South Australia, Adelaide, Australia
TMG Rail Technology, Sydney, Australia
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Fuel costs and CO2 emissions are a major concern for all transport operators, and the rail freight industry must increase its efficiency if it wishes to remain competitive. Freightmiser is an in-cab advice system that helps drivers of long-haul trains save fuel and stay on time. Freightmiser uses optimal control theory to determine speed profiles that minimise fuel consumption subject to completing the journey within the specified time. The recommended speed profile is displayed on a screen in the cabin of the locomotive along with additional advice about track topography and train location that will assist the driver to follow an energy-efficient speed profile and stay on time. The system determines current location and speed from an on-board GPS unit and continually updates the displayed advice during the journey, thereby ensuring that allowance is made for previous departures from the recommended speed profile. Thus the in-cab display always provides an optimal speed profile from the current position to the next target location. Freightmiser technology can ensure that best driving practice becomes the norm and hence consistently reduce fuel costs by as much as 15%.

Title Remnant coal detection system for coal train wagons
 
Author Hargrave, C
Thompson, J
Hainsworth, D
Affiliation CSIRO Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2010, Rail - Rejuvenation and Renaissance, Conference on Railway Engineering, Wellington, New Zealand, 12-15 September, 2010
Publication Date 2010
Abstract The paper describes an automatic system, developed by the Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO) of Australia, to detect and quantify remnant coal (carryback) that is left in coal wagons after dumping at coal terminal facilities. A two-year carryback detection project included software development, sensor selection and testing, pre-site trials at a train yard in Brisbane Australia, and site installation at the R G Tanna coal terminal in Gladstone together with an extended site trial of the technology under normal production conditions. The system uses scanning laser technology to build up a three dimensional profile of the interior of each coal wagon with anomalies associated with coal carryback being automatically detected using algorithms developed by CSIRO. The success of the initial trials is attested to by the fact that the system is now being further developed in conjunction with the port authority in preparation for commercialisation.

Title Railroad assets: information and operational (IT/OT) convergence
 
Author Kraeling, M
Fletcher, D
Affiliation GE Transportation, Melbourne, Florida, USA
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2017, Rail's Digital Revolution, 21-23 November 2017, Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Corporate networks and Information Technology (IT) data centers have revolutionized all aspects of business operations. The heavy freight locomotive is a complex machine, operating in an increasingly restricted track environment, yet is pivotal to revenues. Operational Technology (OT) involves the control or flow of assets in the railroad. The convergence of these technologies drives commonality in the processing and management of data. Locomotives to date have been treated and considered as unique assets, largely outside of corporate IT management and control, requiring specialty personnel and processes to manage and maintain them. Most railways also necessarily operate with significant quantities of diverse locomotive from different manufacturers, of varied vintage and configuration, a barrier to efficient interoperability. By approaching the locomotive with the same modular processing, networking and communications philosophy as the corporate data center, these pivotal assets will simultaneously offer vastly enhanced capability, security, reliability and manageability, with reduced capital and operating cost, in a smaller footprint on the vehicle.

Title Energymiser: past, present, future
 
Author Pudney, P
Howlett, P
Source Institution of Railway Signal Engineers (IRSE)
Conference IRSE Australasia Technical Meeting: Adelaide 22 July, 2011
Publication Date 2011
Abstract In 1982, researchers at the University of South Australia embarked on a project to develop an in-cab system to provide driving advice that would help suburban railcar drivers improve timekeeping and reduce energy use. By 2000, we had developed specialised journey optimisation software that could quickly calculate an optimal driving strategy over hundreds of kilometres with any combination of gradients, curves and speed limits. For the next ten years we worked with TTG Transportation Technology to develop the prototype software into a commercial product, and to demonstrate significant energy savings on trains. The system, called Energymiser, is currently being installed on freight trains in Australia and on high-speed passenger trains in the UK. Meanwhile, we are continuing to enhance the system, to make it easier to adopt, to evaluate, and to integrate with other systems.
