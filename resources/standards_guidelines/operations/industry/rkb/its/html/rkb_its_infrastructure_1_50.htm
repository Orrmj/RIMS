Title Formal validation & verification of software for railway control and protection systems: experimental applications in ANSALDO
 
Author Ciinatti, A
Giunchiglia, F
Mongardi, G
Pietra, B
Romano, D
Torielli, F
Traverso, P
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The ever increasing use of software-based controllers provides flexible and complex functions at a reasonable cost, extremely lower than traditional circuitry. However, it poses crucial problems of safety: even though existing software engineering techniques provide structured methodologies for design, implementation, testing, verification and validation of software, safety in software systems is harder to demonstrate than in traditional relays circuitry. An approach to safety assurance which can provide significant help is the use of "Formal Validation & Verification" (Formal WV). The idea is to use a mathematical model for software specification, design and implementation, and a mathematical algorithm for the validation and verification of such a model. In this paper we report on the experimentation with Formal V&V carried out in ANSALDO. We have devised the possibility of integrating the technology within the traditional development process, and have estimated the costs and benefits of such introduction. After an assessment of the state of the art, Model Checking has been identified as a particularly promising technique for Formal V&V, for its ability to perform validations in frilly automated way and pinpoint, anomalous behaviors at early stages of the design cycle.. Experiments have been carried out on a case study taken from one of ANSALDO safety critical applications.

Title Use of advanced computer models in permanent way life cycle cost reduction
 
Author Hunt, GA
Affiliation BR Research, Derby, UK
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In principle, knowledge of the track structure, geometric characteristics, traffic details and speed of operation permits the calculation of deterioration behaviour, maintenance requirement and useful life of the track. Characterisation of this behaviour is clearly an important element in reducing railway costs, and many years of research into track behaviour, by British Railways and others, has led to the development of predictive models for long term track performance. These models, which cover important aspects such as deterioration of geometry and the life of components such as rails, sleepers and ballast, have already been effectively employed to influence track and vehicle design, and to derive the access charging formulae where track and operations are separately charged. Identifying the critical parameters of the vehicle/track system has enabled new standards for design and maintenance to be implemented, and can permit running at higher speed at minimum cost. Track access charging formulae for privatised railway operation have also been derived in this way. Recent studies have focused more deeply on the track structural behaviour. The important characteristics of the track are now being identified, notably the importance of track structure sti.ffuess, and its relationship with ballast settlement. These new discoveries are pointing the way to producing the next generation of highly durable track forms, which could have major effects on life cycle costs. New track may possibly be of novel construction, but it is likely that the performance of ballasted track can be significantly improved. The paper gives historical background to the model development work and describes the current state of the art. Examples of typical applications are presented, and the key aspects of track structure performance are highlighted and quantified

The system can show those image maps of planned route. The geometrical condition of the route can be easily checked on CRT display. If maps of historical sites and hospitals are employed, these obstacles can be eliminated from route environments. The environmental assessment involves the noise problem and the landscape problem. For evaluation of landscape along the planning route, aerial view is effective. Title Design of a small-sized interlocking system supporting track maintenance work
 
Author Yanagi, H
Fujiwara, M
Kawabata, A
Kikuchi, T
Affiliation Hitachi, Ltd., Ibaraki-ken, Japan
East Japan Railway, Ibaraki-ken, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract We have developed a Small-Sized Electronic Interlocking System which supports track maintenance work. Using the portable wireless terminal from the track side, maintenance workers can request to close tracks. The developed interlocking system consists of an interlocking logical unit and power controlling unit. After this logical unit calculate the relationship among the train position, the rail sifting position and the signal, it decides the aspect of signal. Power controlling unit supplies electrical power to the switch motor and the bulb of signal according to the decision of the logical unit. This paper describes a small-sized interlocking system which uses triplex redundant off-the-shelf controller with 32 bits CPU. Each controller has fail-safe checker. 2-outof -3 voter circuit ensure the safety of parallel output from the interlocking logical unit. This circuit satisfy the condition of "Totally Self-Checking' and also the condition of "High Availability". In other words, any kind of a single fault never leads hazardous state and this circuit has ability of safely functioning after single fault occurring. Maintenance workers can request to close tracks and to set a route for maintenance wagon directly by the portable wireless terminal, as if they operate the main control panel of the interlocking system. This direct operation prevents an accident caused by the misinterpretation between maintenance workers and operators of the station. To prevent erroneous display on the portable wireless terminal, we developed the fail-safe indication tectonic. In the case of transmission error, maintenance workers can recognize it easily. This interlocking system has been tested at Honno station in Sotoboh Line. It will work at a small-sized station in Tokyo metropolitan area and others in 1999.

mistral

Abstract In the management of every Railway Infrastructure Company the principal aim is to combine safety and efficiency. The rollout of ATC/ATP Systems, such as the ERTMS, has brought undisputed benefits in terms of safety together with a remarkable technological revolution, whilst increasing complexity requires efforts for supervision and maintenance. As we know, ERTMS ATC functions require a continuous interaction between on-board and trackside subsystems. Therefore It is necessary, in order to achieve a comprehensive understanding of system behavior, to perform an integrated analysis, including information belonging to juridical data recorded by on-board Data-Loggers. Such integrated analysis becomes even more important and necessary with regards to the introduction of interoperable corridors where several operators compete on an open market, thus rendering fundamental the attribution of responsibilities for delays. MISTRAL is an integrated ERTMS diagnostic platform aimed at collecting, decoding and analyzing information captured by tapping interfaces such as RBC-IXL, RBC-MSC and among in-train subsystems. The platform is projected to verify the ETCS/ERTMS behavior together with system configuration and, by mean of recorded logs, it can replay the train view the same way the Operator Interface Monitor can. An ETCS state-chart Model allows verifying system behavior by comparing recorded log-files with system requirement specifications. Finally, Mistral allows analysing and trouble-shooting anomalies both on-premises (where control systems are located) and remotely by mean of Web Service applications. Outcome benefits include, among others, a sensible reduction of infrastructure-related issues thanks to a quicker troubleshooting and a remarkable improvement of train operations.

Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy

Abstract The traction power supply (TPS) system constitutes a central part of the railway infrastructure and influences considerably the availability of the entire railway system. Approaches to increase the availability of the TPS are typically based on enhanced communication, like IEC 61850, which is considered an enabler for advanced automation and control applications. These ideas will move the traction power supply in the direction traced by concepts such as the internet of things and industry 4.0. These paradigms are characterized by an exponentially increasing number of communication participants and by a strongly growing amount of information exchanged in the system. While the communication problem is solved, turning the exchanged information in a smarter behaviour of the overall system is still an open challenge. Due to the big amount of information and to the enlarged number of actors, programming of advanced automation becomes increasingly difficult. With growing complexity, automation schemes also become more error-prone, thus making their application riskier for infrastructure owners and railway operators. The implementation of the internet of things in TPS seems therefore to be doomed from the start due to the programming complexity implicit to this paradigm. In this paper we compare the evolution of automation schemes in power supply systems with the evolution of computer technology in general. Basing on this, we will give a vision of the future of automated power supply systems, especially discussing the use of automated programming tools as enablers for the creation and application of reliable and secure advanced automations schemes. Programming tools could make the internet of things a viable paradigm in traction power supply, ultimately enhancing the performance of the power supply system and consequently of the whole railway infrastructure.

Title ATMS/ETCS interoperability options
 
Author Stainlay, G
Shier, C
Kitto, A
Affiliation RailCorp
Lockheed Martin
Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC)
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract This paper looks at the issue of interoperability between different rail traffic management systems. It examines the matter of interoperability generally, and the objective of achieving a framework for national interoperability between present and future train control and protection systems, across all rail networks in Australia. It examines interoperability specifically in reference to the interface between ARTC’s Advanced Train Management System (ATMS) and RailCorp’s implementation of the European Train Control System (ETCS). The paper discusses a variety of ways in which interoperability can be achieved, in ways that depend on the relative architectures of the respective systems, and the implications of each method. It concludes that interoperability is achievable, with minimal duplication of equipment and functions, with any translations needed between systems kept ‘under the covers’ and transparent to the operators.

Title Productivity and safety gains from integrated rail yard automation
 
Author Denny, JM
Affiliation RailComm
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Today’s railroads tend to adopt control technologies in a somewhat piecemeal fashion. The discrete application of a single technology solution may be effective within its defined mission, but more consideration must be given to the interaction of applications when defining an integrated systems approach. This paper will describe a layered approach to integrate existing control technologies with enhanced train detection/location capabilities and multiple methods of operator interface to improve classification yard efficiencies, enhance worker safety, and set the groundwork for highly optimized and coordinated Train-Engine-Car-Crew (TECC) resource movement. Recent advances in data communication, sensor networks and mission critical central control systems have contributed to the advent of command and control platforms capable of supporting integrated rail yard automation applications. Command, Control, Communication and information (C3i) systems operate somewhat analogously to the Computer Aided Dispatch systems for mainline train control. Integrated yard automation systems address coordinated control of key locations within the yard, while optimizing and minimizing the movement of all mobile assets, referred to as TECC resources. Control of field devices including switches, derails and any other protection device are coordinated in relation to the location and assigned activity of all TECC resources within the yard facility. A seven layer approach to building an integrated automation infrastructure to support dozens of productivity, safety and efficiency applications is presented.

Title A successful approach to Solid State Interlocking (SSI) modernisation
 
Author Lauthier, J
Affiliation Alstom Transport
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Solid State Interlocking is a British design successfully exported to the rail networks of a dozen countries. In this paper, we review the benefits of improved design, in particular the use of open-standard communication equipment and centralised architectures against maintenance costs and system availability. Through the case study of the Belgian Railway Infrastructure Objectives, we demonstrate how the SSI installed base can be upgraded intelligently and without impact to revenue operations. In particular, we show how re-using the existing interfacing equipment to track products – and benefiting from a backward compatibility with the SSI data configuration – translates into a cost-effective migration path for asset owners and rail operators.

Title Extending the reach of trans European rail traffic
 
Author Thulin, M
Affiliation Bombardier
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract For several years it has been brought forward that there is a need to develop a new traffic control system for regional lines. The introduction of ERTMS/ETCS as a European standard opened the possibility for such a development. The European Rail Traffic Management System (ERTMS) is an EU-sponsored initiative to enhance cross-border interoperability and signalling system procurement by creating a single Europewide standard for railway signalling. A workgroup within UIC has developed a proposal for a system called ERTMS Regional. This development aims at specifying a system that is fully compliant with the standard ERTMS air gap as well as other ERTMS interfaces. The ERTMS Regional proposal specifies the functions for a Traffic Control Centre (TCC). The TCC consists of an integrated interlocking, radio block central and traffic management system. The main purpose is to make it possible to operate regional and rural lines in a cost efficient way. An important argument for introducing a new traffic control system on regional lines is to reduce the cost of traffic management and by doing that, creating better financial conditions for regional lines. The introduction of ERTMS/ETCS creates a possibility to develop a system that both reduces the capital and life cycle cost and at the same time, opens for Trans European traffic to reach even further out on existing networks.

Abstract On 1 July 2008 the Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC) signed a contract with Lockheed Martin to develop and test a new type of Train Management System that will allow for the removal of all conventional trackside signals and install a positive control system in the locomotive of all trains that routinely use the ARTC Managed Network. Title Integrated rail operation management software: IVU.Rail.Suite
 
Author Nagel, F
Affiliation IVU Traffic Technologies AG
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2011, Customer Relations and Innovation, 22-24 November 2011, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Railways have a key role to play in an efficient public transport network. As these rail systems and the relevant infrastructure have expanded over recent decades, there have been growing requirements for efficient and comprehensive IT systems for planning, operation and controlling. System landscapes undergo different aging processes and the consequence is a large number of IT systems and modules. Railway companies may well employ 20-40 different IT- systems at the same time. Also the separation of network operation and rail operation can result in different IT systems being used for security and operational information. Where completely new railway systems are developed, this requires the rapid implementation of a completely new IT system and its integration into existing infrastructure. Because of the privatisation of public transport services and increased competition there is a need to plan and operate these services quickly and efficiently. The control and approval of the service against the contractor also calls for an integrated IT system landscape Higher passenger expectations concerning real time operational information increases the need for integrated public transport systems. Furthermore, the aim of an integrated rail operation management system is to gradually replace existing systems, to build up a consistent IT landscape, and to integrate existing systems through standard interfaces. Finally, the aim is to reduce operational costs, increase operational stability in combination with higher flexibility, and to attract more passengers by improving customer satisfaction.

Title Evaluating the impact of train signalling systems on rail network capacity: a simulation approach
 
Author Corry, P
Smith, S
Affiliation AECOM
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2011, Customer Relations and Innovation, 22-24 November 2011, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Control strategies for trains are a critical part of the design of any greenfield or brownfield rail network. The selection of a control strategy has an impact on the capacity of the network, capital investment required, and ultimately the net present value (NPV) of the project. In this paper, we discuss the use of discrete-event simulation techniques to evaluate the impact of alternative network control strategies on overall capacity. A case study where simulation techniques have been used to recommend optimal network control strategies is also presented. Central to the decision-making process on the optimal control strategy is an accurate representation of the signalling impact on system throughput and capacity. Traditional spreadsheet-type calculations provide a useful starting point; however, these do not adequately capture all delays in the system, particularly delays caused by interference between trains. A useful comparison of the impact of different signalling strategies requires a more robust and detailed capacity evaluation approach, which can be achieved using discrete-event simulation (DES) models. DES models are used worldwide to evaluate the capacity of rail networks. This paper describes a DES based methodology currently being used to support the planning and design for several bulk materials rail projects at various stages of their project life cycles within Australia. The described methodology can be used to assess greenfield or brownfield systems, in some cases with a mix of passenger, general freight and bulk materials traffic. Whilst DES is being used to assess a wide range of infrastructure and rollingstock options, this paper pays particular attention to the selection of a network control system. DES is used to optimise the timing of system upgrades as tonnages ramp over several years from initially low levels to the ultimate forecast tonnages.
