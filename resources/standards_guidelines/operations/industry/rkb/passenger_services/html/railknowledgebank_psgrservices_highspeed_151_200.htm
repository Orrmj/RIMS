Title Why are the wider effects of high-speed rail service so different according to places?
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Delaplace M
Affiliation University of Rheims Champagne-Ardenne Laboratoire OMI 57 bis, Reims Cedex
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract In 2010, 17 594 km of High-Speed Lines are planned all around the world by 2025 (UIC, 2010). Economic and politic stakeholders of the served areas do expect a lot of positive effects in terms of economic dynamism but a recent review of the literature shows that it is not always the case (Bazin, Beckerich, Blanquart, Delaplace, Vandebossche, 2011). No significant effects result from a HSR infrastructure alone (Offner, 1991, Vickerman, 1997, Bazin, Beckerich, Delaplace, 2006b, Blanquart, Delaplace, 2009). Nevertheless, some local economic dynamic indirectly linked to HSR can emerge and have emerged in some areas and in some conditions. The aim of the paper is to propose a theoretical conceptualization of this heterogeneity of the wider effects of a High-Speed Rail Service according to places.

Title TGV at 360 km/h: a research program following a test campaign
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Gautier, P-E
Poisson, F
Barcena, S
Josse, F
Affiliation SNCF Innovation and Research, Paris, France
SNCF Rolling Stock Dept, Paris, France
SNCF Infrastructure Engineering, St Denis-la Plaine, France
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract A comprehensive test campaign was carried out in October 2008 in order to assess, on different ballasted tracks, the ability for the TGV to run at 360 km/h on existing high speed lines in France. The trainsets under tests were commercial double-decker TGVs, selected from recently delivered commercial TGV-Dasye (Duplex-asynchonous motor) series, and equipped with a comprehensive set of sensors for running dynamics, passenger comfort, acoustics and vibration tests, and also detecting potential 'flying ballast'. External noise measurements were also carried out, tests were carried out on ballasted tracks of different qualities, in order to assess its influence on passenger comfort. The latter was assessed both using perceptive tests and with current recommended indicators. An account of the main results obtained from the testing campaign is presented in the paper, with a focus on the main sensitive identified subjects: external noise, and acoustic comfort on platforms, dynamic comfort as a function of the track quality. 'Ballast flying phenomenon' was found under control. From these results, a research program was undertaken by SNCF, focused on the main interest points identified from the test campaign: external and internal noise, passenger dynamic comfort as a function of the track quality, braking specification in order to maintain the same headway whilst increasing the speed and control of flying ballast. The first main results of this research program are presented in the paper.

Yamashita, Y
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan

Abstract Although wear of contact wire has been one of the most serious problems in catenary system maintenance, mechanism of wear progress of contact wire has not been clear yet due to its complexity. The authors have attempted the identification of quantitative effects of a contact force of pantographs and an arc due to contact loss on the contact wire wear from the measurement data obtained on Shinkansen commercial lines. For this purpose, the authors have developed a contact force measurement method by equipping a catenary with sensors. An arc measurement equipment which detects UV ray emitted from an arc at the wayside has also been developed. The contact force of pantographs, the arc due to contact loss and collected current of all the trains which pass through two measurement sections on Shinkansen commercial line have been measured periodically. We propose a wear prediction model of contact wire from data which are measured by these method and equipment. Using this model, the wear of contact wire on the commercial line can be predicted. The predicted contact wire wear by this model agrees well with the measurement data, therefore, this model can be utilized for the efficient maintenance of the overhead catenary system.

Title Advanced active control of a contact force between a pantograph and a catenary for a high-speed train
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Yamashita, Y
Ikeda, M
Masuda, A
Iba, D
Sone, A
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Kyoto Institute of Technology, Kyoto, Japan
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Pantographs are the equipment that provide electric power to railway vehicles by contact with catenaries. A raising mechanism, which is equipped with a pantograph and gives upward force to the pantograph head, enables the pantograph to contact with catenaries. In a resting state, the contact force between the pantograph and the catenary is identical to the force given by the raising mechanism. However, when the pantograph runs along the catenary, it is fluctuated due to aerodynamic force, propagation and reflection of the wave on the catenaries, change in the dynamic characteristics of the catenary systems depending on the position, etc. An excessively large contact force can damage both the pantograph and the catenary or may cause a severe accident like contact wire breaking in the worst case scenario. On the other hand, if the contact force is too small, the pantograph and the catenary easily lose their contact state. The state the pantograph and the catenary are in, the non-contact state, is called contact loss. Occurrence of a contact loss can cause electrical damage both on the pantograph and the catenary due to arcing generated between them or power supply disruptions from the catenary to the vehicle. The contact force fluctuation therefore should be kept as small as possible. Basically, small contact force fluctuation of a pantograph at high speed can be achieved by making use of light-weight materials for the pantograph components. Moreover, it is widely known that the pantograph head springs with longer stroke gives better contact force performance. It is also important that the pantographs for Japanese high-speed trains should meet low-noise requirements due to strict noise regulation in Japan. Noise reduction strategies have been taken in terms of reducing the number of the pantograph parts exposed to airflow. The reduction in the number of parts without losing mechanical strength and current capacity tends to make each of the remaining parts larger and/or heavier, thus there is a limit to reduction in the total weight of the low-noise pantograph. In addition, application of long stroke springs to a low-noise pantograph for the purpose of obtaining high current collection performance works negatively in the sense of reducing aerodynamic noise because it is necessary to put a long casing for the spring, which can be another noise source. As pointed out above, it would be difficult for pantographs to achieve both high current collection performance and low-noise property, and much more difficult if it runs at inexperienced high speed. For the future demand, the authors are working on the project about application of active control techniques to pantographs as one of the method for suppressing the contact force fluctuation.

Title Improving the train diagram of the Tokaido Shinkansen: development of new timetable planning system
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Fukami, K
Komatsu, S
Seki, T
Saito, A
Affiliation Central Japan Railway Company, Tokyo, Japan
JR TOKAI Information Systems Company, Tokyo, Japan
Toshiba Corporation, Tokyo, Japan
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract As the main artery of the Japanese railway network, the Tokaido Shinkansen, operated by Central Japan Railway Company (JR Central), links Japan's principal metropolitan areas of Tokyo, Nagoya, and Osaka. The Tokaido Shinkansen is currently used by approximately 138 million passengers per year and has played an important role in Japanese economic, social and cultural development as a means of high speed and mass transport. The Tokaido Shinkansen consists of three types of trains, all of which have different stopping patterns: from the fastest to the slowest, Nozomi, Hikari, and Kodama. The basic patterned schedule for an hour consists of nine Nozomi, two Hikari, and two Kodama, operating 400 trains a day during the peak period. In spite of a complicated and densely scheduled train operation, the average delay time per train of the Tokaido Shinkansen is only 0.5 minutes (FY2009). One of the key factors to achieve this accurate transport service is the traffic control system of Tokaido Shinkansen, called COMTRAC (Compute-Aided Traffic Control system), which includes timetable planning system. To achieve a densely scheduled train operation, equipment conditions (wayside equipment arrangements, rolling stock performance and so on) have been improved continuously. To meet more increased passengers' demand in the future, the train diagram should be more densely scheduled and equipment conditions also should be improved. However, the equipment conditions improvements have been already made for number of years, and as a result further examination of equipment conditions, which enable even more densely scheduled train diagram, is becoming extremely difficult. To solve this problem, we have developed a new timetable planning system 'Train Setting Simulation system'. This system enables JR Central to examine the equipment conditions exactly and efficiently, which satisfies the optimal train diagram.

Abstract The trend towards a more efficient, cost-effective and performance oriented railway system is strongly pushed by political, economic and environmental motivations, as it represents an important and strategic asset for guaranteeing citizens and goods mobility at reasonable cost, involves a 35 billion Euro/year still growing market and is by far the lower impact transportation mode. Despite some sporadic and non-objective scepticism, railways are the best (maybe the only) candidate to achieve sustainable transport capacity and growth, provided that they can keep pace with increasing customer expectations, evolution of market demand and high safety/security levels. While the railways' impressive technological progress is evident to all, a lot can still be done in system optimisation and efficiency improvement, in order to increase substantially their overall competiveness and market share, not only referring to state of the art high-speed trains, but also to high-capacity conventional trains. An area where improvements are needed and can be achieved using advanced ICT solutions is that related to rolling-stock maintenance and condition monitoring. The principal goal is to minimise the probability of a mission-critical fault occurring during train service, with strong consequences in terms of quality of service and extra costs, while at the same time keeping maintenance costs as low as possible, in order to reduce life-cycle spending and improve return of investment.

Title Studies for an Australian east coast very high speed train
 
Author Thornton, P

Abstract Very high speed trains (VHST's) have been under consideration in corridors on the east coast of Australia for over 15 years. Private consortia have made offers to Government to build-own-operate VHST's for the Sydney - Melbourne (-900 km) and Sydney- Canberra (-300 km) corridors. Ultimately, Government rejected these proposals because of the scale of financial concessions or capital input required. The study described briefly in this paper was commissioned by the Commonwealth Government in 2001 to examine for the first time the issues involved in a VHST from the perspective of Government. Economic considerations of general social welfare and financial returns to investors in general differ substantially with regard to the various project alternatives owing to their having different routes, interconnections, services, operating and fare structures. lndepth feasibility studies are therefore required which formulate various systemic solutions satisfying the basic objectives of the investment and analyze their technical and economic implications. This paper describes a complete methodlogy for carrying out feasibility studies for new railway lines set up in recent years in Italy during research into the new "Alta Velocita" (High Speed) lines. This methodology is based on three principles: configuration of the alternatives as system projects (sets of infrastructures, services and fares), simulation of the effects taking into consideration the characteristics of demand and other transportation modes, and economic and financial evaluation based on a vast range of internal and external effects from various points of view. The proposed methodology is first outlined; in later sections, attention is focused on mathematical models to simulate passenger and freight transport systems, which lie conceptually at the centre of such methodology. Finally, some applications are reported, in particular for the "Alta Velocita" projects in Italy.

Title Service strategy of Shinkansen excelling airlines
 
Author Hidaka, H
Iwamoto, K
Wasada, T
Yagi, E
Affiliation West Japan Railway Co., Osaka, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), 14-16 November 1994, Paris, France
Publication Date 1994
Abstract Inaugurated in 1964, the Shinkansen celebrated its 30th anniversary this autumn. During that time, its greatly reduced traveling time has had a strong influence on the customer shares of the railway and airlines. Research shows that railway has a tendency to surpass airlines where the difference of traveling time between them is within a certain range. Therefore, the railway should bring the difference as close to that range as possible. One and a half years has passed since "Nozomi" started a full-scale commercial service at 270 km/h on the Tokaido and Sanyo Shinkansen Lines. "Nozomi" increased railway's share with airlines in many sections. This supports the above-mentioned relationship, which means "speed" will play an important role in railway services. A recent survey for the Shinkansen passengers shows that "riding comfort" also should be considered in speedup. Since the passenger's sense of values has become diversified today "riding comfort" is an important criteria for many of them. JA-West, whose operating territory is the western part of Japan, earns more than 40% of traffic receipts with the Sanyo Shinkansen Line. So JA-West has been making efforts to speed up this line since it was opened in 1987. In the area this line covers, however, the air and road infrastructure has been improving rapidly, providing increased competition for the railway. Under these circumstances, JA-West decided to develop a new type of Shinkansen car that is faster and more convenient and stable while running. In 1992, JA-West made a high-speed testing train named "WIN350" to clarify and overcome the technical problems involved in a speedup to 350 km/h. In Japan, the toughest obstacle to speedup has been the wayside environment problems such as train noise. There are severe government regulations to limit the levels of noise and ground vibration in residential areas. In the process of development, major issues turned out to be related in many ways to aerodynamic phenomena. After we recorded the maximum speed of 350.4 km/h, we spent over two years performing test runs and research on these subjects in cooperation with research institutions such as the Railway Technical Research Institute. These provided us with many valuable results including a "wing-shaped pantograph" and "active suspension systems". Based on these results, JA-West was able to conceive a safe, comfortable, high-speed train satisfying environmental standards at 300 km/h, and decided to develop a new type of commercial Shinkansen car aiming at its commercial operation in 1996. Introduction of this car will make the traveling time between Osaka and Fukuoka shorter by 10-20 minutes with improved riding comfort.
