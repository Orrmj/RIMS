Title Specification and evaluation of French automatic telephone information systems in the arise project
 
Author Gitton, S
Temen, J-N
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In order to give information and distribute their products, railway companies need large information distribution channels, easy to be used by the general public and, possibly, at low cost. The fact of automatically handling a more significant volume of telephone calls may alleviate congestion and allow new services to be created or information to be provided around the clock. The goal of the Arise project is to create the technical conditions to make the industrial deployment possible, in the field of train schedule information systems, of dialogue systems based on user-independent large vocabulary speech recognition systems over the telephone-network. In this project, SNCF participates to the design and testing of two systems dedicated to inter-city train information, based on Limsi and Philips technologies. The systems specifications are drawn up to include dialogue management specifications aimed at providing sufficient robustness combined with user-friendliness. The freedom that we would like to give to the user has to do with the freedom of action and the flexible approach to expression- rules without disturbing the system. This freedom has a cost, in that the dialogue system controls the conversation-process. A strong evaluation procedure is needed to verify that the options taken at the specification-stage give satisfactory results. The evaluation procedure described here deals with usability characteristics and is based upon well-defined travel scenarios, upon questionnaires, interviews and comments on recorded dialogues. Both systems Limsi and Philips were tested within SNCF. The results were encouraging. Mainly, it appeared that the balance between too much freedom and too many constraints is very important and not easy to strike. Some of the points developed in our specifications remain to be tested. The aim of this project is to bring these systems to a high user satisfaction level in order to ensure that the response provided matches the needs of railway customers functionally and ergonomically. In this case, such systems may be used to deal with a part of the clients'requests. Our short term aim is to experiment these systems with our clients' needs.

Title Forecasting ridership of railway systems by factoring interactions between railway facilities and land-use patterns
 
Author Chien, S-T
Hsu, Y-T
Affiliation Department of Civil Engineering, National Taiwan University, Taipei, Taiwan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Ridership forecasting is a very initial step for railway system development that can consequently influence its financial and investment planning. However, overestimation of ridership has been frequently observed in railway projects. Most existing forecasting approaches are developed based on the assumption that travel demand can be attributed to some socioeconomic factors, such as regional population and income level, which are modeled in a static context. By contrast, the feedback effects resulting from a railway system to regional development can be intricate, manifested as an interactive dynamic process, but neglected or largely simplified in the related theory and practice. This research seeks to explore the dynamics of spatiotemporal interactions between a railway project and the associated land-use patterns, particularly in the areas surrounding stations, thereby providing insights regarding ridership forecasting and even regional development. A case study is made with respect to Taiwan High Speed Rail, which is originally expected to greatly benefit from the station-related/-oriented development in terms of inducing both travel demand and the increment of land value. Nevertheless, limited accomplishments have been attained over years of struggling for the development of its adjacent areas, and Taiwan High Speed Rail currently faces severe financial hardship against the revenue planning based on the original expectation. From the case study, we seek to propose an integrated framework that analyzes the dynamic process of transportation-land-use evolution involving spatiotemporal interactions by using panel data analysis. The process is investigated from the planning stage as the general public and investors may take certain actions in response to the development potential due to expected mobility increase. Then throughout the entire project horizon, the key issues or specific development conditions that may be required to foster the process are identified. Based on more comprehensively understanding land-use modeling related to transportation facilities (railway stations), planners for a railway project are enabled to better factor travel demand and the course of its variation in a big picture of long-term regional development. Hence, ridership of a railway system may be more precisely forecasted, thereby improving the quality of feasibility analysis and the robustness of following decisions on project implementation.

Title Planning for the unplanned: an international review of current approaches to service disruption management of railways
 
Author Pender, B
Currie, G
Delbosc, A
Shiwakoti, N
Affiliation Monash University, Clayton, Victoria, Australia
Source ATRI
Conference Australasian Transport Research Forum (ATRF), 35th, 26-28 September 2012, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 2012
Abstract This research paper explores the manner in which passenger rail transit organisations plan for and manage unplanned service disruptions through an international survey of practices. This included semi-structured interviews of those staff responsible for service disruption management within 48 international passenger rail transit agencies. Results suggest that only 11% of agencies had parallel transit systems which can be used for riders on disrupted services. Most of these were in inner urban contexts. Track intrusions/medical emergencies, weather extremes, track and rolling stock failures were common causes of unplanned disruptions. Bus bridging was the most common response to line blockages whilst transfer of passengers to the next train was the most common approach to individual train rolling stock failures. Track crossovers were widely seen as critical to managing responses to disruptions, however, a small minority in mostly very cold and freezing climates also saw crossovers as a cause of unplanned failures. Most agencies used available spare buses to source bus bridging vehicles, however, just over 40% actively retracted buses from existing scheduled bus services. It is rare for agencies to have a strategic reserve of buses for bus bridging purposes. The paper discusses the implications of the study findings for future research and practice.

Title A network wide study of unfamiliar public transport journey patterns using a major origin-destination survey
 
Author Schmitt, L
Currie, G
Delbosc, A
Affiliation Institute of Transport Studies, Monash University
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 94th, 2015, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2015-00
Abstract Increasing transit patronage is commonly perceived to be vital component of travel demand management programs that seek to reduce car dependence. It is important to understand the context of new transit travel to identify opportunities to attract and retain new users. This paper is the first large-scale, representative study of unfamiliar transit travel on an urban rail network. It uses a major origin-destination survey of nearly 24,000 rail passengers in Melbourne, Australia to determine that 2% of journeys by train is undertaken for the first time (representing some 10,000 journeys per weekday). Compared to familiar travel, a larger share of unfamiliar travel was undertaken in off-peak times and for a large variety of trip purposes whereas familiar travel was more dominated by commuting for work or education. The spatial distribution of unfamiliar travel was very similar to familiar travel, although it was slightly more likely to taken to destinations in the inner city and inner suburbs. Results suggest that efforts to improve the travel experience for new trips should be broadly distributed across the network and consider the broad variation in trip purpose and that unfamiliar travel often occurs at off-peak times. Policy implications and areas for future research are discussed.

Title Modeling transit access stop choices
 
Author Nassir, N
Hickman, M
Malekzadeh, A
Irannezhad, E
Affiliation University of Queensland
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 94th, 2015, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2015-00
Abstract This research aims at a better understanding of passengers’ behaviour when using public transit systems. For this purpose, a logit discrete choice model is developed to investigate the choice of transit access stop (i.e. departure stop). The proposed model incorporates different components of the transit service, between given origin-destination pairs and at given times, in modeling the choice of bus stops, railway stations, and ferry terminals, as access points to the public transit network. The data used in this research was taken from the household travel survey (HTS) of 2009 in Southeast Queensland (SEQ), Australia, which includes travel records on three public transit modes: bus, train, and ferry. A choice set generation algorithm was developed to create the set of access stop choices and to calculate the time-dependent impedances from each departure stop to the destination. The correlation among the choices is treated at two levels: 1) mode of travel; and, 2) route of travel. A nested logit model structure was adopted to account for the dependencies among the choices on the same mode; and a correction term is proposed that captures the correlation among the stop choices that result from the commonality of the routes serving the destination. The correction term takes three different forms, based on the idea of the overlap correction attributes used in C-Logit (CL) or Path Size Logit (PSL) route choice models. All three forms share the same desirable features, such as simplicity in calculation and applicability to large- scale network models. The case study analysis of SEQ shows the effectiveness of the proposed correction by demonstrating improvements in modelling the choice of access stop.
