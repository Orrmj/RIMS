Title Evaluation of delay reduction measures in urban railways using data mining technique
 
Author Yabuki, H
Uchikoshi, K
Tomii, N
Affiliation Tokyo Metro Co. Ltd., 3-19-6 Higashi-Ueno, Taito-ku, Tokyo, Japan
Chiba Institute of Technology, 2-17-1 Tsudanuma Narashino Chiba, Japan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract We can now obtain various kinds of and big volume of data concerning train operation. Analysis of these data is very useful to enhance punctuality and robustness of timetables. On the other hand, because the amount of the data is huge, it is strongly desired to establish an efficient approach with which we can get useful insights. In this paper, in order to detect causes of delays from train traffic records, we introduce an approach based on the association rule, which is a very popular technique in the data mining community. We consider events such as a delay, an increase of a dwell time and so on as an item and analyse relationship between them. Based on this approach, we can identify the causes of delays which very often happen and are widely influential. The obtained results suggest what kind of countermeasures are most effective to reduce delays. We can also apply this approach to know if some countermeasures which were already introduced have been effective or not. In this paper, we show the details of the algorithm together with the results of the numerical experiments which we conducted using actual data.

Publication Date 2016
Abstract This paper presents an approach to cope with the very dense nature of the Transilien mass transit system on the Paris suburban network. Operating Transilien lines on a daily basis is challenging given the multiplicity of actors involved, and the very fast spreading of small delays through the network. We first define the stability of this system through indicators to evaluate the real-time condition of the lines. We then present a visualization method used to diagnose recurring design or operational problems. Finally, we propose a mathematical reservicing model that can delete stops in order to minimize the recovery delay and other indicators. We test our approach with real data from SNCF Transilien; the experimental results show that the impact of a perturbation can be strongly reduced. Moreover, this impact depends on the number of deleted stops. We then detail some perspectives to improve our approach.

Abstract This paper examines the demand for local bus and rail services in the three major regions in New Zealand: Auckland, Wellington and Canterbury. In order to determine the drivers behind the changes in public transport ridership over time, econometric analysis techniques were applied to analyse the time-series data collected for the last decade. Abstract Successive governments in NSW are creating high growth residential areas in South Western Sydney. Title The SA train meeting Auckland's increase in rail capacity needs
 
Author Trout, E
Gabara, A
Affiliation Auckland Regional Transport Authority ARTA
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2005, 22-24 November 2005, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2005
Abstract The Auckland Regional Council (ARC) commissioned the SA (Suburban Auckland) Train concept in July 2003, to address growing rail passenger demand which had outstripped capability of the existing Auckland fleet. The concept for SA Trains was based on re-engineering and re-manufacturing British Rail Mark 2 carriages into world class standard trains which comprises a diesel locomotive hauled four carriage consist. As at August 2005, a total of seven SA Trains, each providing seating for 260 passengers, are operating along the Auckland rail network. This forms part of a wider plan to provide a total of 14 SA trains (56 rail carriages) by the end of 2006. Each carriage has been completely stripped and re-built with a new body frame and body shell, and new floor. The carriages include large, centrally located side doors for easy access, heating, lighting and ventilation systems and European inspired interior styling. The SA train also includes a part passenger compartment and part slave driving cab so that the train can be operated by a single locomotive. An SA train costs approximately $5 million to rebuild compared to a cost of over $15 million to purchase new rail vehicles with the equivalent passenger capacity. The import replacement is comparable to 28 two car multiple units at a budgeted cost of $240 million.

Title Trip generating factors
 
Description A significant surge in patronage of urban rail services in Australia over the last 5 to 10 years has highlighted gaps in a comprehensive understanding of the link between demand for travel by rail and external and internal factors. Traditional approaches have provided some insights into the relationship between urban rail patronage and some of these factors. However, they do not fully explain recent trends and some current issues such as commercialised models, transit-oriented development, urban redevelopment, and peak spreading are much less well understood. The project is a scoping study designed to provide an appraisal of the current understanding of urban rail trip generation factors and how these factors interact, identification of gaps in the current knowledge and an indication of the form and nature of research needed to fill current knowledge gaps. By filling the gaps in current knowledge a better understanding of a complex number of external and internal factors that influence traveller decision-making will provide a superior basis for urban rail system planning and development. The analysis seeks to discover what make rail systems perform successfully and what hinder their success, it provides policy guidance for planners and urban rail operators for future urban rail development and management as well as urban planning.

Title Understanding urban rail travel: behaviour for improved patronage forecasting
 
Description Over the last decade, Australian rail operators have been confronted with unexpected increases and changing patterns with respect to urban rail patronage. These changes have placed considerable stress on capacities and service levels in some areas and have resulted in excess capacity in others, all of which presented new challenges to rail managers and have resulted in negative community feedback to state governments. Existing travel models and forecasting approaches failed to predict adequately or explain these changes in rail patronage and thus rail managers, while planners were not adequately supported in their endeavours to plan for changes in patronage or manage these changes when they emerged. This project seeks to significantly improve capabilities in understanding and forecasting urban rail trip making behaviours and provide clear guidelines for the development of better forecasting and analytical models. Current state of technology and research to date:
-Current trip forecasting models have evolved from methods with their basis in the 1950s. Although considerable progress has been made in the underlying estimation methods, the basic framework of most approaches still embodies a linear four-step process reflecting 1970s thinking.
-In addition, many Australian models are inherently 'transit' forecasting and estimation models rather than 'rail trip making' models, and they embed generalised empirical parameters rather than ones specific to urban rail travel. As a consequence current Australian models are inherently limited in their ability to forecast and explain urban rail trip making behaviours. Research has also indicated that a significant proportion of the recent increase in urban rail patronage might be explained by factors outside those contained in models currently in use in Australia. Benefits of the project for industry
-better understanding and modelling of trip generating explanatory factors will assist urban rail operators to forecast changing demands on the system and better manage for these changes.
