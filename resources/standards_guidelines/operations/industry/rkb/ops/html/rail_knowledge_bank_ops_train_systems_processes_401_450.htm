Title Decision support for train movement planning and control in railroads
 
Author Goncalves, R
Rondon, M
Neto, LE
Gomide, F
Affiliation Applied Soft Computing, Brazil
State University of Campinas, Brazil
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2007: High Tech in Heavy Haul, 11-13 June, 2007, Kiruna, Sweden
Publication Date 2007
Abstract Train movement planning and control is complex task that when properly managed improves railroad capacity, increases rolling stock utilization and fuel efficiency, decreases chances of delays and bottlenecks, and augment operational flexibility. Often, train movement planning, especially meeting and passing decisions are done in real time considering trains moving in a territory and the interchange of trains among territories. Currently, planning and control decisions are done by dispatchers with limited or no support of computational tools designed for this purpose. This work suggests a human-centered decision support approach for freight train movement planning and control. The approach uses computational intelligence techniques and train graph language to assemble an integrated tool aiming at amplifying human intelligence. The may include operational knowledge and has effective mechanisms man-machine interaction. Decision making combines discrete event modeling and decision procedures based on artificial and computational intelligence to develop optimal solutions through cooperation of models, algorithms and dispatchers. The efficiency of the approach has been verified through actual implementations developed with major Brazilian freight railroads.

Title A rolling horizon framework for intermodal loading assignment to improve fuel efficiency
 
Author Lai, Y-C
Ouyang, Y
Barkan, CPL
Affiliation Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2007: High Tech in Heavy Haul, 11-13 June, 2007, Kiruna, Sweden
Publication Date 2007
Abstract Aerodynamic efficiency of intermodal freight trains can be significantly improved by minimizing the adjusted gap lengths between adjacent loads. This paper first develops a static model to optimize load placement on a sequence of intermodal trains that have scheduled departure times. This model applies when full information on all trains and loads is available. Then, a dynamic model is developed to account for the more realistic situation where information on future trains and incoming loads may not be completely available. This paper seeks ways to balance between: (i) the advantage from optimizing multiple trains together; and (ii) the risk of making suboptimal decisions due to incomplete future information. We propose a rolling horizon scheme to address this challenge, where exponentially decreasing weights are assigned to the objective functions of future trains. Numerical results based on empirical data show significant aerodynamic efficiency benefits from these optimization models.

Abstract Optimized Train Management System - OTMS is a complete train management solution designed for heavy haul railroads, combining: General Management (crew, rolling stock, equipment, goods and other kinds of load); Conflict Detection Management (train movement on main lines and crossing yards); Train planning (fleets, travels, maintenance); Train Dispatching and Traffic Control, by the use of vital (fail-safe) and non vital systems. Benefits are the reduction on fleet energy consumption, rationalization of train travels, automatic scheduling for preventive and corrective maintenance and optimization of the overall railroad operation. This paper describes the basic architecture and an application of such a system, which is running on a major railroad in Brazil called CVRD (Companhia Vale do Rio Doce). Its main architecture comprehends an Integrated Control Center (ICC) linked throughout a communication backbone to a Centralized Interlocking (IXL), On-Board Computer (OBC) and also to the Wayside Interface Units. Statistics obtained in CVRD, where the project is being evaluated, demonstrate that just a reduction of fuel consumption in order of 1% would result in an economy of US$ 400.000,00 (four hundred thousand dollars) a year. Step forward is the implementation of train driverless. The intended OBC by Alstom/CVRD will break the heavy haul train operation paradigm of having a human conductor. The Automatic Pilot, one of the main features of the OBC, will implement that function. Heavy haul train operation demands a lot of experience and knowledge of the good practices in train operation, trains are too big (in CVRD case: up to 320 cars with 3 Locomotives with distributed power techniques) and trains are conducted in terrain not easy for train operation (a lot of curves and grades of 2.5 % - worst case). Analyzing all the engineer trips, it is possible to find a trip that was the most economical, the safest and most efficient. The proposal of using on-board computers is to recover that knowledge, applied in a trip, and to make available for a driverless mode system in conjunction with Automatic pilot device built in OBC. CVRD and Alstom are pursuing this close future in heavy haul world and we know driverless techniques to get there.

Title Train safety in NSW: the future and an integrated engineering, risk and human factors approach
 
Author Wong, KK
Hughes, GJ
Long, AF
Affiliation Asset Standards Authority, Transport for NSW, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Historically, train safety in NSW has relied heavily on the progressive evolution of driver safety systems based on lessons learnt from various incidents. This has led to a safe and reliable approach to ensure train safety so far as is reasonably practicable (SFAIRP). In achieving this we are currently nearing the limits of human performance. The introduction of new train control technologies in NSW and the ever increasing demands for higher levels of capacity and safety on the rail network means that a new approach is required to ensure train safety into the future. This paper presents an alternative, integrated approach which encompasses engineering, human factors and risk. It proactively identifies and assesses the safety risks associated with train operations. It also looks beyond the rolling stock and considers train, infrastructure and human centred controls as an integrated system to ensure train safety. The proposed approach provides flexibility for the industry to provide assurance on train safety by either demonstrating how a train safety system address all risks SFAIRP, or alternatively by adhering to the existing train (driver) safety system used on rolling stock currently operating on the network.

Title Exploring the operational boundaries of Australian suburban railway services
 
Author Wardrop, A
Affiliation Railway Operations Researcher and Analyst
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract It is too often forgotten that railways form a system in which its components have to properly mesh in order to obtain the greatest benefit. So it is for suburban passenger train operations. These operations are highly peaked and use expensive infrastructure and rolling stock fleets. Therefore, many of their components are often under-utilised out of commuter peak periods. The best use that can be made of these resources should occur when infrastructure, rolling stock and operations can be simultaneously optimised. However, suburban passenger rail operations need to be constrained by transportation objectives, such as acceptable levels of accommodation, travel times and network connectivity, etc. This is because they compete with alternative modes of transport. If suburban passenger rail operations fail to successfully compete with private vehicular travel then the supporting community will be forced into more highway construction to compensate. This paper thus explores the trade-offs between: passenger car design and layout; the types and levels of accommodation; the interactions between car design and critical station dwells; the interactions between dwell times and train throughput; the influence of timetable construction; and developments that might reduce travel times, raise car capacity, reduce critical dwell times and/or reduce intrinsic signal clearance times. It will provide bounds within which line capacity and suburban passenger flows might be realistically developed while meeting overarching transportation objectives pertinent to Australia. Examples and analyses will be drawn from the author’s knowledge of the Melbourne and Sydney metropolitan railways, their rolling stock and signalling systems.

Title The effect of energy and time efficient driving strategies on longitudinal heavy haul train dynamics
 
Author McClanachan, M
Cole, C
Affiliation Central Queensland University, Centre for Railway Engineering, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Railway operators are continually investigating ways to reduce the cost of heavy haul train haulage operations. Two main components that influence the cost of a haulage trip are energy/fuel usage and journey time. But other costs are more difficult to measure such as train and wagon wear, fatigue damage and derailment likelihood. Past research and train driving training resources recognise that generally energy efficient driving strategies provide the best train handling and therefore the lowest cost of train operation. However, more energy efficient strategies tend to be slower in order to reduce the amount of braking required. While slower journey times result in lower energy usage it has the disadvantage of reducing the capacity of the rail network and rolling stock utilisation and hence increases cost in this area. Evaluating the total cost of a train driving strategy involves many parameters. The paper presents a method used to determine the total cost of a number of train driving strategies used on an Australian heavy haul line. The results are used to show the various cost relationships between energy, time and longitudinal dynamics during typical heavy haul train operation. The paper also highlights ways cost analysis can be used to further reduce train operation costs.

For a Heavy Haul Rail network, telecommunications is present for the operation of control networks for train movement and train communications. Business communications (VOIP desk phones, Business LAN) is not a primary driver of the operational network topology and usage. Title Not just TPH: exploring capability and capacity
 
Author Aiton, J
King, D
Affiliation WSP | Parsons Brinckerhoff, United Kingdom
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2016, Maintaining the Momentum, Conference on Railway Excellence, Melbourne, Victoria, 16-18 May 2016
Publication Date 2016
Abstract A single metric, Trains Per Hour (TPH), is commonly used to describe a railway system’s capacity throughout all planning and delivery stages. However, the capacity delivered is constrained by the choices and actions made at each strategic, operational and tactical level. The planners, designers and deliverers must work to different definitions of capability and capacity to assure the system is configured to meet demand. Currently, the definitions on capability and capacity are inconsistent across the industry internationally. This paper presents four key terms that align with each planning and delivery stage. ‘Ultimate Capability’ is a result of how the system is configured. The operating restrictions enforced results in the ‘Network Capability’. How the system is resourced and timetabled based on operational planning rules presents the ‘Planned Capacity’. The final delivery, accounting for daily system variation, results in the ‘Actual Capacity’. By understanding how the system capability and delivered capacity is impacted by every planning and delivery decision, the future of the railway can continue to drive towards providing services that meet demand while understanding the impact of consuming existing capability and capacity.

Author Schütte, J
McGrady, A
Affiliation Technische Universität Dresden, Germany
ERTMS User Group, Brussels, Belgium
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In 2013, the European Commission granted substantial FP7 funds to the NGTC Project Consortium consisting of numerous representative members from European Railways and associated institutions, the Railway Supply Industry, Urban Mass Transit Operators and associated organizations and railway research institutions. The objective of the NGTC research project is to prepare a set of specifications and analyses for a future Train Control system that shall support better re-utilization of major constituents in mainline railway applications as well as in urban mass transit applications. Significant challenges for the project come from the requirement that the proposed modular architecture shall be on the one side backward compatible to the existing ERTMS Baseline 3, and on the other side introduce the next generation train control system that may be used in urban mass transit CBTC (Communications Based Train Control) applications without reduction of the already demonstrated high performance of equivalent systems, including Unmanned Operation (Grade of Automation GOA4). The paper will focus its report on the already completed Functional Requirement Specification of NGTC including details of functions that are “common” or those that remain specific to either mainline or urban applications. Also, an overview of other activities/results in the NGTC project will be provided including the modular architecture and a new IP based radio communication for both domains.
