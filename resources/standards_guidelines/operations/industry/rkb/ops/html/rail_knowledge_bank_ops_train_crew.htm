Title Non-Technical Skills: The next step change in safety improvement?
 
Author Fletcher, S
Affiliation The Occupational Psychology Centre, Watford, UK
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Non-Technical Skills (NTS) are the personality characteristics, motivations, attitudes and abilities an employee will bring to their work that will help them to undertake their job in a safe and effective manner. Key research from the aviation industry has shown that organisations that use and apply NTS can enjoy additional and in some cases significant improvements in safety. Psychologists from the Occupational Psychology Centre (OPC) have been working with its UK and international rail clients for over 20 years in using and applying NTS to help improve safety performance. However the big question is what benefits could NTS bring to the rail industry? In this paper we will explore what NTS is all about and how rail organisations can use it to bring about a step change in safety performance. We will provide the OPC model of how rail organisations can use NTS across the whole employee life cycle including applying NTS during:Selection – by using NTS as selection criteria at recruitment. We will provide hard evidence linking an applicant’s NTS performance at selection to their subsequent performance in training and on the job, including their safety performance. Training for new driver recruits. We will outline how OPC clients have provided their new recruits with training in key NTS that will be critical to their safe performance on the job. Investigation and employee rehabilitation following a safety incident. OPC clients also utilise NTS with employees who have had serious incidents. The OPC psychologist will undertake an in-depth psychological assessment with the employee to help understand what specific NTS were lacking and what could have contributed to the incident. The OPC has undertaken over 300 of these Post Incident Assessments (PIA). Many of these employees have returned to work and remained safe, for some the experience has been life changing. NTS development with existing and experienced employees. OPC clients have used NTS workshops with existing employees to help them understand how NTS could enhance their safety performance still further. We will share how these programmes have been evaluated and their success determined. The paper will explore when NTS works best. It will examine our vision of rail organisations improving safety by integrating NTS across the whole employee life cycle.

Title L'influenza dell'adattamento sensoriale sui comportamenti del personale di macchina
 
Author Russo, G
del Lavoro, P
Affiliation Servizio Impianti Elettrici, Roma, Italia
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The sensory adaptation consists in the reduction of the receptor's response (i.e in the drop of sensibility) when the stimulus remains unchanged the whole time or, vice versa, in the growth of sensibility after a reduced or lacking stimulation. This is due to exhaustion, or increased availability of the receptor's chemical mediator. For some receptors the adaptation to the stimulus is extremely quick and it causes the sudden stop of a signal if the stimulus remains constant. Obviously this happens also for visual perception, which requires the existence of a temporal alteration in the state of retina's stimulation. As far as this is stimulated homogeneously during time, there are no longer premisses for the movement's perception.It is true, at least partly, that external stimuli impose themselves on the operator indipendently from his needs, provided that they are conditioned to certain features. Studying these latter ones lets one avoid some perception mistakes, such as the non-perception of given information or a wrong perception (perceptive confusion). In case of overloading information, which cannot be dealt with completely, the driver makes a selection. If all the information sources are pertinent in the same way, i.e. if they are all equally interesting for the task, or if the driver has neither information at his disposal to decide their pertinence nor rules to organize them, the selection will be made:a) by chance; b) according to stereotype methods of exploration;c) according to signals' formal features (intensity, novelty, inconsistency, optimum position in perceptive field etc.). On the contrary, if the information sources are unequally pertinent in comparison with the task and as far as the operator knows them, he will make the selection according to the different available information. Because of a certain temporal conditioning, the larger is the quantity of information to be dealt with, the bigger is the risk to do it in an incomplete or wrong way, with the obvious need of a carefully considered choice. In most of working situations the available information is not equally pertinent to the task. In addition to the effects of these factors, that can be defined as primary and whose relative importance varies depending on situations, there are others which are produced by driver's knowledge or specific skills, but also shaped by previous learning. If the operator knows or assumes that a certain information source is more useful than another, he will focus here his attention even if it is not the most pregnant. Many expermental researches in the field confirmed this position.

Publication Date 2016
Abstract Train drivers are known to experience many different psychological burdens in the process of stopping the trains they are driving. These burdens depend on variables such as the type of station, accuracy of stopping at designated stop lines, and attention to passengers on platforms. In order to understand psychological burdens faced by train drivers, methodologies for assessing biological indices, such as heart rate, respiratory rate, and sweating are available. However, measuring psychological burden felt by train drivers while they are operating trains is difficult. Therefore, we developed a novel methodology for understanding the psychological burden felt by train drivers using interviews. The degree of burden for different stations assessed by using the newly developed methods is reported.

Author Balakrishnan, G

Thomas, M
Mildred, T

Dawson, D
Affiliation Central Queensland University, Appleton Institute, Wayville, SA, Australia
Sydac Pty Ltd, Adelaide, SA, Australia
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Recently, adoption of competency-based training schemes in safety-critical domains has increased. This includes rail where performance-based models have steadily replaced the older experience- and time-based curricula. The advancement of technology has catalysed this change by introducing new opportunities for enhancing training delivery. Among these innovations, competency development in sitro (conducting training in controlled simulations of the work setting) has received considerable attention in workplace training. The uses of train driving simulators have been gradually expanding but if these simulators are to contribute more meaningfully within the newly espoused competency-based approach, it needs to be encompassed within a structured pedagogical framework that is more inclusive of the broad variety of learning that can take place at work. This paper reviews recent advances in simulator-based pedagogies and presents a new conceptual framework for the development of train driver expertise with an aim to set the stage for discussions and iterative development of comparable theoretical models. First, the paper will present a cross-domain literature review to synthesize the key findings associated with the development of contemporary simulators that have a pedagogic application. Second, the paper will delve deeper into and review the Multi-Crew Pilots Licence (MPL) simulator-based training scheme, recently established to develop the competencies of trainee airline pilots. Drawing upon these findings and supplementing them with current thinking on constructivist pedagogical theories, a prototype conceptual framework for train driver competency development will be modelled to demonstrate and discuss: (1) new potentialities in simulators' mediation of training & learning; (2) more flexibility in the way that theoretical and practical aspects of training syllabus, and training media are blended; and (3) a general need to foster theoretical and empirical research to continue studying how simulators could be integrated into train driver development curricula more effectively.

Title Effective adult learning techniques for the rail industry
 
Author Stabler, LA
Rudner, G
Cook, M
Irani, F
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc., Pueblo, Colorado, USA
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract America’s freight railroads hire thousands of new employees each year and expect to hire an estimated 15,000 employees in 2015. Across the country, railroads are looking to build teams of skilled, highly motivated people to help safely and efficiently transport the raw materials, products, and finished goods that sustain the nation’s economy and people. To be effective in the rail industry, an employee must understand a great deal of information which is unique and can only be learned from industry sources. Railroads provide a great amount of training to the workforce, and it is essential that the training be thoroughly understood. Students need to use the training they receive to safely work at the many complex jobs the rail industry offers. The key to effective training is recognizing the techniques that work best with adult students, many who have not been in a classroom environment for a number of years. One such area is specialized training in the emergency response techniques used in the event of a hazardous materials incident. Each year, U.S. railroads transport approximately 2 million carloads of hazardous materials, including several hundred thousand carloads of crude oil and ethanol and smaller amounts of a wide variety of other hazardous materials. Training includes techniques such as application of magnetic patches, both the transfer of liquids and gases from a damaged car to an undamaged car, and “vent and burn” to safely relieve pressure buildup inside a tank car. It is essential that students thoroughly understand the approaches they are taught if they are to successfully use the techniques to respond to a hazardous materials incident. TTCI/SERTC utilizes a unique “Four Phased Approach” to adult learning, limiting the time spent in the classroom in favor of a hand’s-on approach. The Four Phases are: “Classroom and Theory”, “Work on Individual Freight Car Components”, “Hands-on Techniques Practice on Individual Railcars”, and finally, “Incident Response to a Full-Scale Scenario”. Students spend most of their time in a field environment that allows the student to safely learn using full size railcars in realistic scenarios. The paper will describe the four phases of adult learning using examples from TTCI/SERTC training courses as well as results from individuals who have utilized the training in the field. The Four Phased training approach should be considered when developing training for other areas in the rail industry.

Title Development of electronic checklists for support in drivers' handling of failures
 
Author Ohkawa, K
Iyama, K
Hirose, T
Kusukami, K
Affiliation East Japan Railway Company, Tokyo, Japan
TOSHIBA CORPORATION, Tokyo, Japan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract In the event that train experiences failure while in operation, drivers must be able to take sure and quick stopgap actions. However, a driver may sometimes take the wrong action due to time pressure and other causes, resulting in greater delays until operation can be resumed. Therefore, we developed an electronic checklist (ECL) system to support sound measures for train trouble, based on that used in aviation. We conducted an experiment to evaluate the effect of ECL on drivers' capacity to deal with emergencies. Thirty-two drivers participated in the experiment and dealt with train failure on a drive simulator both with and without the ECL. Completion rate improved from 86% to 96% when drivers used the ECL. Furthermore, it was proven that fewer misunderstanding of the cause of trouble occurred. Another experiment using a test car confirmed that the usability of the ECL is satisfactory in general, and that the ECL does not add an excessive load to a car control system.

Title Selecting safe and effective train drivers
 
Author Fletcher, S
Affiliation The Occupational Psychology Centre, Watford, UK
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The Occupational Psychology Centre (OPC) was set up in British Rail in 1992 to oversee and improve the selection process of train drivers. Since 1992 the UK rail industry has continued to recruit large numbers of train drivers, and this recruitment has accelerated in recent years. The question posed by UK rail and freight companies is how do we select the very best and safest train drivers as quickly and effectively as possible? In addition rail operators continue to be focused on how to reduce driver operational incidents and the selection of the right individual plays a key role. Over the past 23 years the OPC has undertaken key research to examine the personal characteristics of the safe and effective train driver, and also the outstanding train driver. This has helped rail organisations to redesign their driver selection processes to assess these key characteristics. Another key research area has been to explore the empirical link between a train driver applicant’s performance on the psychological tests used at selection and their subsequent performance in training and on the job including their safety performance. Since 1992 the OPC have undertaken leading validation work in this area culminating in numerous key research studies with both UK and international rail clients. This research has involved 1000’s of train drivers. This presentation will draw on our UK and international research and will explore: The personal characteristics of a safe and effective train driver; The personal characteristics of the outstanding train driver; The power of validating your own selection processes for train drivers; What selection tools predict a trainee driver’s performance in training; What selection tools predict a train driver’s job and safety performance. The presentation will also examine how rail organisations can in the future take these research findings to help them design or redesign their selection processes to help select effective and safe train drivers. The poster will also explore how rail organisations around the world can validate their existing selection processes. It will examine how these rail organisations can take these research findings to improve the selection of the very safe driver by giving more weight to those selection tools that are most effective and give less weight or eliminate completely those selection tools that are less effective, as part of a process of continuous improvement.

Title An algorithm for automatically modifying train crew schedule
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Takahashi, S
Kataoka, K
Henda, F
Souma, S
Kojima, T
Asami, M
Affiliation Mitsubishi Electric Corporation, Hyogo, Japan
East Japan Railway Company, Saitama, Japan
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract In this paper, we propose an algorithm for automatically modifying a crew schedule for use when the regular train-schedule breaks down. The proposed algorithm builds an initial solution by relaxing some of the constraint conditions, and then uses a Taboo-search method to revise this solution in order to minimize the degree of constraint violation resulting from these relaxed conditions. We also show not only that the algorithm can generate a solution that simply satisfies all constraints, but also that the solution will satisfy the experts. That is, we show the proposed algorithm is capable of producing a usable solution in a short time by applying the algorithm to actual cases of train-schedule break-down, and that the solution is at least as good as those produced manually, by comparing both solutions with several point of view.

Title Distraction versus communication: the mobile phone challenge in the Great Britain rail industry
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Luke, T
Pitsopoulos, J
Pitsopoulos, C
Regan, MA
Heavisides, J
Affiliation RSSB, London, Great Britain
Human Factors Risk Management Limited, Melbourne, Australia
INRETS, Lyon, France
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract In September 2008 twenty-five people were killed in an accident in Chatsworth USA. The accident was the result of a train passing a red signal because the driver was distracted by text messaging on his mobile phone. This prompted the Great Britain rail industry to consider whether the risk of mobile phone distraction was adequately controlled. This research relates to the challenge for an even safer and more secure railway. However it is also mindful of the challenge of an even more competitive and cost efficient railway because, although a potential source of distraction, mobile phones offer significant benefits and are relied upon for some operational communication, particularly in the freight and engineering context. This research was conducted in two phases. The first phase has been previously reported1 and concluded that limiting the use of mobile phones to when stationary could reduce the number of signals passed at danger due to mobile phone distraction by up to 62%, a saving of around 0.0054 Fatalities and Weighted Injuries per year. This could be achieved by increasing the driver understanding of the risks surrounding mobile phone use and introducing a common minimum requirement that could be applied across all companies. Therefore, a Rail Industry Standard on mobile phone was developed and further research was commissioned. The subsequent research included a survey of 377 train drivers and 96 train driver managers. The survey measured current attitudes and estimated the prevalence of different types of mobile phone use. Results showed that while 87% of drivers never use a mobile phone when in motion, 4% admit to using a phone in motion once a day or more. The results of the survey were used to contribute to the development of an education programme for train drivers. The programme focuses on the risks surrounding mobile phone distraction and refreshment of knowledge of relevant rules and policy. However it also acknowledges the potential benefits offered by mobile phones and aims to equip drivers with a framework to make risk based decisions about mobile phone use within the constraints of rules. The programme was piloted with two companies, an engineering operator and a passenger operator. Preliminary evaluation of the programme comprised qualitative and quantitative assessment of learning, attitudes (including risk perceptions) and programme content with overall positive results. The programme has been widely distributed with approximately 700 requests from rail industry stakeholders and is currently being delivered across Great Britain.

Title Analytic tools for human factors evaluation of new in-cab and train control technologies
 
Description Background:
Rapid advancements in train control and in-cab technologies provide opportunities for rail operators to improve efficiency and enhance operations. However, new technologies are a significant form of risk. Neglect of human factors issues can mean that new technologies have unintended consequences in the work environment (particularly in the more challenging situations) leading to either rejection by users or to situations where the job is made more difficult rather than easier.
Objective:
This project aims to develop a toolkit of methods that human factors specialists can use to evaluate new technologies before they are implemented in trains and other safety critical train operation related environments.
Outcomes:
This project will provide the Australian rail industry with a standardised method to evaluate the human factors issues associated with introducing new technology. There is a high probability that this project will reduce the costs associated with introducing technology with significant usability issues.

This review identified a number of potential human factors implications of new technology such as fixation on and distraction by the technology, disregard or attenuation to warnings, increased attention and knowledge demands, lack of insight into how the technology is functioning, poor technology feedback on its current functioning, misplaced user trust in the automation, mismatches between the technology and the driver’s understanding of the task, changes to the nature of train driving, and difficulties for drivers transitioning in and out of the technology. Although each of the issues on this list is a complex topic in its own right this list forms a useful starting point of potential human factors issues to consider when implementing any new technology and forms a preliminary list of unintended consequences that will be used as the basis for further research in this project. Generally, the review found that while there has been some peer-reviewed research studies into the human factors implications of new technology considerably more research needs to be conducted.
