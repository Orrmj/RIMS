Abstract A comprehensive test campaign was carried out in October 2008 in order to assess, on different ballasted tracks, the ability for the TGV to run at 360 km/h on existing high speed lines in France. The trainsets under tests were commercial double-decker TGVs, selected from recently delivered commercial TGV-Dasye (Duplex-asynchonous motor) series, and equipped with a comprehensive set of sensors for running dynamics, passenger comfort, acoustics and vibration tests, and also detecting potential 'flying ballast'. External noise measurements were also carried out, tests were carried out on ballasted tracks of different qualities, in order to assess its influence on passenger comfort. The latter was assessed both using perceptive tests and with current recommended indicators. An account of the main results obtained from the testing campaign is presented in the paper, with a focus on the main sensitive identified subjects: external noise, and acoustic comfort on platforms, dynamic comfort as a function of the track quality. 'Ballast flying phenomenon' was found under control. From these results, a research program was undertaken by SNCF, focused on the main interest points identified from the test campaign: external and internal noise, passenger dynamic comfort as a function of the track quality, braking specification in order to maintain the same headway whilst increasing the speed and control of flying ballast. The first main results of this research program are presented in the paper.

Title Advanced active control of a contact force between a pantograph and a catenary for a high-speed train
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Yamashita, Y
Ikeda, M
Masuda, A
Iba, D
Sone, A
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Kyoto Institute of Technology, Kyoto, Japan
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Pantographs are the equipment that provide electric power to railway vehicles by contact with catenaries. A raising mechanism, which is equipped with a pantograph and gives upward force to the pantograph head, enables the pantograph to contact with catenaries. In a resting state, the contact force between the pantograph and the catenary is identical to the force given by the raising mechanism. However, when the pantograph runs along the catenary, it is fluctuated due to aerodynamic force, propagation and reflection of the wave on the catenaries, change in the dynamic characteristics of the catenary systems depending on the position, etc. An excessively large contact force can damage both the pantograph and the catenary or may cause a severe accident like contact wire breaking in the worst case scenario. On the other hand, if the contact force is too small, the pantograph and the catenary easily lose their contact state. The state the pantograph and the catenary are in, the non-contact state, is called contact loss. Occurrence of a contact loss can cause electrical damage both on the pantograph and the catenary due to arcing generated between them or power supply disruptions from the catenary to the vehicle. The contact force fluctuation therefore should be kept as small as possible. Basically, small contact force fluctuation of a pantograph at high speed can be achieved by making use of light-weight materials for the pantograph components. Moreover, it is widely known that the pantograph head springs with longer stroke gives better contact force performance. It is also important that the pantographs for Japanese high-speed trains should meet low-noise requirements due to strict noise regulation in Japan. Noise reduction strategies have been taken in terms of reducing the number of the pantograph parts exposed to airflow. The reduction in the number of parts without losing mechanical strength and current capacity tends to make each of the remaining parts larger and/or heavier, thus there is a limit to reduction in the total weight of the low-noise pantograph. In addition, application of long stroke springs to a low-noise pantograph for the purpose of obtaining high current collection performance works negatively in the sense of reducing aerodynamic noise because it is necessary to put a long casing for the spring, which can be another noise source. As pointed out above, it would be difficult for pantographs to achieve both high current collection performance and low-noise property, and much more difficult if it runs at inexperienced high speed. For the future demand, the authors are working on the project about application of active control techniques to pantographs as one of the method for suppressing the contact force fluctuation.

Title Design of a new full scale test-rig for the calibration of instrumented wheelsets
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Diana, G
Resta, F
Braghin, F
Di Gialleonardo, E
Bocciolone, M
Crosio, P
Affiliation Dipartimento di Meccanica, Politecnico di Milano, Milano, Italy
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Instrumented wheelsets have been used for years to assess running safety of railway vehicles. International Standards (EN 14363) prescribe a complete testing procedure for the approval of a new vehicle. As far as running safety is concerned, the sum of the guiding forces in straight track (EY) and the derailment coefficient (Y/Q) in curve must be measured. Therefore, measuring wheel-rail contact forces is mandatory when the so-called 'normal method' must be applied. In order to use wheelsets as a measuring device, a preliminary calibration procedure is required. Usually instrumented wheelsets are based on strain gauge bridges placed either on the axle or on the wheel web (or both). During the calibration phase forces of known magnitude are applied to the wheelset and the corresponding strain values are measured. In this way it is possible to establish a correlation between the output of strain gauge bridges and the applied forces. The calibration procedure appears crucial in order to assess the accuracy of the measuring system. Rolling test-rig are often used to perform the calibration of the wheelset. In this way, forces at wheel-rail contact interface are usually not directly measured but are determined by imposing the equilibrium of the system, thus reducing significantly the accuracy of the system. In order to improve the accuracy of the device, a new full scale test-rig has been designed at the Mechanical Engineering Department of Politecnico di Milano. A bogie assembly made up of a bogie frame and 2/3/4 wheelsets, depending on the vehicle architecture, or a single wheelset in case of a non-bogie vehicle is mounted on the test-rig. This allows to correctly reproduce the real working conditions of the instrumented wheelset that are of fundamental importance: in fact, measured strains depend both of the contact forces and on reaction forces; hence, an error on the boundary conditions determines a re-distribution of reaction forces and thus a measuring error. Instrumented rail elements are positioned under each wheel of the wheelset under calibration. Each rail element is equipped with 7 load cells in order to measure wheel rail contact force components in each direction (vertical, lateral and longitudinal). Vertical forces are applied to the bogie through the secondary suspensions by means of a load beam, whereas lateral and longitudinal forces are applied directly to the rail elements.

The objective of SNCF is to develop a tool in which the user can easily model the design of each passenger coach, to run simulations and derive statistics about the efficiency of the coach architecture and interior design (as seats arrangement or configuration of the area around the doors) proposed by train manufacturers. This simulator will be used in the future by SNCF to evaluate rolling stock and platform design performance in terms of flow efficiency.
