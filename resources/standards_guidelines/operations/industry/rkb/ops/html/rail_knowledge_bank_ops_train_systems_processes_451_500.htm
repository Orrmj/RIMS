Title Infrastructure control centre (ICON)
 
Author Constantinou, A
Affiliation Sydney Trains, NSW, Ausralia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract On an average day, up to 200 incidents occur on the Sydney Trains/NSW Trains network. The most frequent incidents and those which result in major train delays include: points failure, track circuit failure, customer illness and trespassers in rail corridor danger zones. Many incidents on the train network are to a large extent, unavoidable. However, a rapid and effective incident-response capability is critical to minimise impacts on customers. The Maintenance Directorate of Sydney Trains directly contributes to a quick and efficient response to any infrastructure-related incident. Prior to this project, the Maintenance Directorate had six separate, disciplinebased, Operations Centres and help desks, operating 24/7 in geographically spread locations. The six Centres managed their respective systems and functions independently. It was determined through an organisational reform initiative that better coordination, communication, and management would be required to deliver a step change in incident-response performance, and that this would best be achieved by consolidating the six command and control centres into a single Infrastructure Control Centre (ICON). Within weeks of commencing operation, the new arrangement delivered an improvement in performance. The new ICON has delivered a more rapid and targeted response to infrastructure-related incidents. Incident status updates are more accurate, timely and consistent and utilisation of field-based resources has been optimised.

Title Railway engineering: how to monitor integration of multiple systems
 
Author Chan, S
Affiliation Northwest Rapid Transit, Sydney, NSW
John Holland Group, Melbourne, Vic
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract This paper presents one of the project management tools adopted by Northwest Rapid Transit (NRT) to monitor the successful integration of multiple systems to perform critical and complex functions for the Sydney Metro Northwest project. Railways are designed and built to fulfil the requirements of safety, service performance, and customer needs. These requirements have become highly complex and cannot be satisfied by any single system alone. Multiple systems have to inter-connect and perform together seamlessly in order to achieve these complex functional requirements. Railway engineering deals with the design, construction, testing and commissioning, and operation of a rail transport system. It also involves managing the interface and integration of systems from multiple electrical and mechanical disciplines. Integration of multiple systems faultlessly to perform the required functions is a challenging task, and requires special tools to monitor, report and to take preventive and corrective actions proactively, in order to deliver those critical and complex functions for the railway on time, and within budget. A System Integration Function (SIF) tool was developed by Mass Transit Railway (MTR) in Hong Kong. The tool has been tailored for use in the Sydney Metro Northwest project, to monitor the integration of various electrical & mechanical systems, to meet those critical and complex functional requirements of the project. The SIP tool, although similar to other project programme tools, provides senior management with essential information to focus on problems in systems which are more important and high implementation risk, so that efforts can be prioritised and directed to resolve problems promptly. Apart from the process reporting nature, the tool also links to evidence from design through to commissioning, such that, when problem or change arises, impact on those critical and complex functional requirements can be re-assessed for necessary design change.

Title Virtual coupling of trains
 
Author Goikoetxea, J
Affiliation CAF Spain
Source Dennis Schut (UIC)
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Virtual Coupling of trains is said to be a new paradigm in railway operation, proving much higher flexibility, full interoperability, reduced costs and travel times, and maximizing the capacity of lines and stations. This technology allows trains run closer together without any physical connection, building platoons of trains, where the headway between them is reduced to some meters and is far below the absolute braking distance. The literature contains many articles about virtual coupling of trains and its road equivalent, namely vehicle platooning, but never before a prototype had been implemented in railways. The Spanish rolling stock manufacturer, CAF, decided in autumn 2017 to develop the technology. The system was then implemented, deployed on two rail vehicles and tested during two test campaigns in July and September 2018 on a test track. The results are promising (perfect stability and distance control at all tested speeds, up to 40 km/h, and conditions) and open the door for new implementations on more complex scenarios: Trains of different types, higher speeds, longer tracks, joining or leaving on-the-move...This innovation means a breakthrough in the known railway operation bringing new possibilities and providing, finally, full interoperability between trains.

Title Freightmiser: an energy-efficient application of the train control problem
 
Subtitle Conference proceedings
Author Howlett, P
Pudney, P
Vu, X
Source ATRI
Conference Conference of Australian Institutes of Transport Research (CAITR), 30th, 2008, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Fuel costs and CO2 emissions are a major concern for all transport operators, and the rail freight industry must increase its efficiency if it wishes to remain competitive. Freightmiser is an in-cab advice system that helps drivers of long-haul trains save fuel and stay on time. Freightmiser uses optimal control theory to determine speed profiles that minimise fuel consumption subject to completing the journey within the specified time. The recommended speed profile is displayed on a screen in the cabin of the locomotive along with additional advice about track topography and train location that will assist the driver to follow an energy-efficient speed profile and stay on time. The system determines current location and speed from an on-board GPS unit and continually updates the displayed advice during the journey, thereby ensuring that allowance is made for previous departures from the recommended speed profile. Thus the in-cab display always provides an optimal speed profile from the currentposition to the next target location. Freightmiser technology can ensure that best driving practice becomes the norm and hence consistently reduce fuel costs by as much as 15%.

Author Naweed, A
Balakrishnan, G
Source ATRI

Publication Date 2012-09
Abstract In the last decade, simulation technology has become a familiar feature in the Australian rail industry. This has been driven by a strong organisational intent to improve driver learning, and fuelled by the promise that train simulators enrich training, enhance decision support, and engender skills that transfer positively from the synthetic environment to the actual cab. This paper presents research that sets out to investigate how train simulators are currently being integrated into the rail organisation, and how industry end-users are utilising them to deliver training. Focus groups and observations were undertaken at a number of rail organisations that either owned simulators or were undergoing procurement. The data collected were analysed thematically and showed that, whilst practices for integration were varied, simulators were being extremely underutilised, in spite of their keen uptake. Further, effective application appeared hindered by a plethora of usability issues, concerns over technical reliability and simulator fidelity. The findings revealed one or more disconnects residing between the developer's promise and the organisational intent, which destabilised the process for fully integrating a simulator. This paper disentangles this dynamic by exploring the key obstructions impeding the path of effective simulator application.

Title Improving enforcement algorithms for Communications-Based Train Control using adaptive methods
 
Author Moore Ede, B
Polivka, A
Brosseau, J
Tse, Y
Reinschmidt, A
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center (TTCI)
Federal Railroad Administration (FRA)
Conference 9th International Heavy Haul Conference (IHHC 2009), Shanghai, China, 22nd-25th June, 2009
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC), referred to as Positive Train Control (PTC) by freight railroads in the USA, was originally conceived to be a safer, more productive and more cost-effective system of train control than conventional signalling systems. One aspect of the added safety was to be attained by fail-safe enforcement brought about by a locomotive-borne system that knows where the train is within the limits of the authority provided to it and is able to apply the brakes in time to prevent violation of limits in the event that the train crew fails to respond. Another aspect is to use PTC enforcement of speed limits. In practice, however, the use of enforcement has decreased capacity and slowed the operation in many situations. The reason is that braking distance can vary substantially from one train to the next because of factors which are not known precisely-factors such as train weight, the rate at which the train brakes will apply or the overall braking efficiency of the train. Enforcement braking is usually based on a full service penalty brake application that is an action of last resort when the onboard system determines that the train will overrun a target if the penalty application is not invoked. Because of the uncertainty of the factors affecting the braking distances, enforcement algorithms are almost invariably based on worst-case braking characteristics in order to assure not to overrun the limits of the authority. The problem is that there are many circumstances when the system calls for enforcement sooner than the train crew would start normal service braking. This is particularly acute for trains operating at slow speeds or on down-gradients.
