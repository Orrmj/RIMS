Title 25 years of heavy axle load unit train operation on the Quebec, North Shore and Labrador railway
 
Author Bramwell, S
McElroy, TF
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract The practical effects of heavy axle loads and high gross annual tonnages over a 640 km. long heavy duty railroad with unit train traffic in the sub Arctic region of eastern Canada are discussed. Particular emphasis is given to the disproportionate shotening of rail life which occurs when a relatively small increase in axle loading is introduced into the operation. Major rail problems encountered are corrugation, shelling, and horizontal split heads. The effects of these defects are accentuated during the 6 month long winter when temperatures of minus 50 degrees below celsius are experienced. The question that then arises is, are present day rail steels being operated near to or above their economic loading limits. Particularly in operations such as those on the Quebec, North Shore and Labrador Railway? Constant ultrasonic and electro magnetic induction testing plus two or more major rail grinding campaigns per year are essential features of the very strict and intensive track maintenence programmes so necessary for the safe and efficient operation of such a railroad.

Title Locomotive wheel-rail loading: A systems approach
 
Author Koci, HH
Swenson, CA
Conference Heavy Haul Railways Conference, 18 - 22 September, 1978, Perth, Western Australia
Publication Date 1978
Abstract With the use of heavier and larger cars, more powerful locomotives, increased train lengths, and changes in track maintenance practices, new problems of interaction between rail vehicles and interaction between wheel and rail have been manifested. This has led to intensified research of these interactions as they are influenced by vehicle and track parameters as well as train operating practices. As a manufacturer of locomotives for world-wide markets, Electro-Motive Division of General Motors Corporation (EMO) has conducted experimental and analytical studies of these vehicle and wheel-rail interactions. Koci and Marta (1965) reported results for the basic curve negotiation loads developed between wheel and rail with 2, 3, and 4-axle locomotive trucks, and they described the curving phenomenon as it relates to wheel-rail creep and friction. In the late 1960's, a number of tests were conducted, in cooperation with railroads in the USA and Canada, to further study operating conditions and train handling procedures which were thought to influence derailment tendency. Influences of locomotive braking practice, location of empty and long overhang cars in trains, rail profile, and locomotive maintenance have been reviewed by Koci and Marta (1971). In the 1970's, much of this type of research has been focussed and combined under the Track-Train Dynamics (TTD) Program coordinated by the Association of American Railroads. While contributing to the TTD Program, EMD also continues to conduct its own analytical studies and use its test car and locomotives to conduct full-scale tests. This paper is an overview of EMD's work in locomotive wheel-rail loading, and it is intended to reflect recent developments. These developments are mainly in two areas: use of the modern, high speed digital computer as an analysis tool, and the comprehensive measurement of dynamic wheel-rail loads at sites specifically chosen for deteriorated or special track conditions which were found to maximize wheel-rail loads.

Abstract Modelling and validation of locomotive dynamic behaviour involve very complex multi-disciplinary engineering problems which require coverage of all uncertainties and non-linearities presented in the system. Abstract The integrity of structures and responsible units of railway transport in extreme environment depends on the efficiency materials such as locomotive and rail steels at low temperature. Understanding of materials and structures behavior under the impact loading it is necessary to improve the characteristics and reliability of all railway transport. So the study of locomotive steel fracture mechanism at low temperature has been carried out. The optical and electron microscopy used to study the fracture surface of impact toughness test samples. The analysis reveals the sources of theradial fracture pattern. In case of locomotive wheel bandage it is the globular particle located in the steel grains. It is obvious that the spheroidization of non-metal imperfection happens under the thermal influence during the variable cyclic loading over the operation time. The stochastic modeling shows the high susceptibility of the crack growth velocity to structural size. So the problem of low reliability and lifetime could be solved by improving the impact toughness at low temperature through the thermal and mechanical processing of steel and microalloying with refractory and rare-earth element compositions.
