Title Understanding the impact of high traction hybrid locomotive designs on heavy haul train performance
 
Author Spiryagin, M
Wu, Q
Bosomworth, C
Cole, C
Wolfs, P
Hayman, M
Persson, I
Affiliation Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Australia
Insyte Solutions Pty Ltd, Rockhampton, Australia
AB DEsolver, Östersund, Sweden
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract The analysis of hybrid traction with its application to heavy haul train tasks is a very complex problem and is not well studied. Considering that any locomotive field tests for the verification, validation and adaptation of new locomotive designs involve high costs and disruption to commercial operations, the development of the methodology that is based on advanced simulation approaches and takes into consideration a whole train dynamic behaviour looks very adequate as an alternative for this purpose. The methodology is built on a system engineering approach that requires knowledge from a range of disciplines (mechanical engineering, civil engineering, computer science and electrical engineering) and is strongly oriented on how to design and manage complex systems and predict behaviour of locomotive traction systems under different heavy haul train operational conditions. As a result, the developed methodology will increase knowledge of high traction locomotive design requirements in order to deliver better performances in rolling stock and train operational efficiency. In the developed methodology, the integration of the disciplines mentioned above is achieved through a novel, advanced co-simulation and parallel simulation technique. Advanced locomotive modelling principles have been developed and a significant number of components have been included in order to accurately evaluate locomotive behaviours. The components considered include existing AC and hybrid locomotive designs, full locomotive traction system models, the application of exact wheel-rail contact theory, train dynamic behaviour and in-train forces, comprehensive track infrastructure modelling, rail friction processes and parallel computing. This new simulation methodology represents a Software-In-the-Loop (SiL) testing technique which has been implemented on CQUniversity’s High Performance Computing (HPC) cluster. Confidence in the results delivered through simulations is based on specific validation approaches that include a locomotive model acceptance procedure, and laboratory and field test data.

Title A new tool to optimize the travel time won on tilting trains versus investment
 
Author Quetin, F
Castel, L
Gautier, P-E
Zheng, JS
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The reduction of budget allowed to build new high speed lines led to a stronger need to improve performances of existing infrastructure. The introduction of tilting trains has been reconsidered, even if only minor travel time reductions can be achieved on SNCF lines with the existing infrastructure, because high maximum cant deficiencies are already used (180 mm for a TGV trainset). Thus, a new approach has been suggested, in which a global view of the problem has been considered. All aspects of performance improvement have been studied together, with their influence in global performances: tilting trainsets, traction power, track improvements, signalling adaptations, power reinforcement for catenary, and regulations. A single any improvement does not have major influence on travel time, if taken alone. But the combination of different improvements can enhance significantly performances and could give a new opportunity for train to be competitive. The global optimization of travel time is to be done on some commercial routes. The time won on travel has to be estimated, depending on different scenarios and confronted with return on investment. In order to make it easier to decision makers, investments have to be classified on the cost for a minute won basis. In order to get a deeper insight into the influences of modifications of infrastructures and of the rules on travel time, the TEMPO software has been developed for a PC/Windows 95 environment. This software is intended to determine all potential gains on travel time and to estimate the associated investments. This user friendly tool has been designed in close relation with the users. It takes into account the relevant constraints that presently limit speed. The software is menu driven and results are presented graphically. The effects of any modification on the infrastructure or on the constraints can be viewed at once. A synthesis of the word carried out for global optimization is exposed. The specifications of TEMPO software are described and a practical application is presented for the Paris-Toulouse route.

Author Sagot, J-C
Gouin, V
Chappet, P
Lorinquer, LP
Affiliation Institut Polytechnique de Sévenans, Belfort, France
GEC ALSIHOM, Belfort, France
SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The 21" century will witness a European network of high-speed trains (TGV). This ambitions objective will require higher speeds, more homogeneous driving and safety systems, and lower operating costs. Design of these TGV driving cabs, control panel, informational devices, and control devices will therefore need special attention. In this perspective, a pluridisciplinary workgroup brought together operators, manufacturers, and university researchers to study the ergonomics of the new driving cab. The ergonomic approach, described in this paper, includes three research axes. The first dealt with analyzing the task and activity of train drivers within existing work situations. Knowledge of what exists, the very basis of ergonomic action, led to defining the second axis, what we have called "the field of desirable future activities" in terms of safety, comfort, and efficiency. It was around this definition of the field of desirable future activities that the group was able to specify and (re)design the new driving devices and in particular the new drive-braking master controller and accompanying display device. This last device was the object of the third research axis, validation on a design and research simulator in the presence of twelve drivers.

Title An amphibious train marine-express
 
Author Yoshida, K
Takami, H
Sonoda, A
Affiliation Kyushu University, Fukuoka, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In 1989, the Marine-Express (ME) project, of which the basic concept is an amphibious linear motor (LM) car able to run both on land and under the water, was initiated in Kyushu University. In the 21st century, the ME transportation system would network points on land with offshore airports, offshore bases or cities across the sea. A theory of new combined levitation and propulsion suitable for the ME is proposed which is applied quite independently of train speeds. In the sea, the ME can levitate and travel simultaneously by controlling "space vector" of LSM force in attractive-mode to compensate its buoyancy. On land it does by controlling "space vector" of LSM force in repulsive-mode to compensate the weight itself. Computer simulations with the JR's MLU002 have made simply clear a feasibility of the ME project. The first underwater LIM car MEU I and the second underwater LSM car ME02 have successfully conducted a underwater test in our Laboratory. The third LSM car ME03 is a 1/5-th scale land travelling model train. The ME03 train with three vehicles has been successfully levitated and propelled in repulsive-mode by applying the decoupled-control method proposed. The ME03 train could be controlled to run following very well the demand patterns for shuttle ground-flight and pass through smoothly the section boundaries. A new combined levitation and propulsion from standstill has been verified experimentally as one of the most important features of the method by which the train is levitated independently of speeds.

Title Lightweight potentials in coach body structures of high-speed trains
 
Author Bröcker, W
Rosenberger, RD
Affiliation IABG mbH, Ottobrunn, Germany
Deutsche Bahn AG, Munchen, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Within the scope of feasibility studies carried out for the technology programme ICE 21 a number of different designs for the rail coach body bare structures have been analysed with respect to strength and eigenfrequency and feasible savings of weight have been determined. Metallic materials and fibre reinforced plastics have been considered. It turned out that by individual measures more than 20% of the mass of the central coach body structure can be saved. Combining several useful individual measures the weight saving potential of the central coach body structure is even more than 40%. The mass reduction at this subassembly will cause for other components as couplers, bogies etc. equivalent lower loading and thus opens additional potential for weight savings. The reviewing of national and international standards and regulations showed also some possibilities to influence the mass of rail coaches positively. For example the material strength may be better exploited by updating the design philosophy. Caused by possible problems with fatigue, sophisticated lightweight structures should be treated more differentiated with respect to their life verification philosophy than conventional coach body structures.

Abstract The paper presents a scale-model of a train driven by a locomotive E 402 for Italian rail ways. The scale-model has been designed for simulating different running conditions of the train. It is provided with both a mechanical and an electrical brake. This allow us to take into account respectively the actions of the loading masses, driven on railways with different slopes, and of the air resistance, acting against the motion of the train. Title The reluctance synchronous machine as traction motor
 
Author Kamper, MJ
Affiliation University of Stellenbosch, Stellenbosch, South Africa
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The paper focuses on the merits of using the reluctance synchronous machine as a traction motor. The power density and power factor characteristics of the optimum designed and optimum controlled reluctance synchronous machine are considered. It was found that the power density of the reluctance synchronous machine is remarkably high compared to standard asynchronous machines. This advantage was never realised and was recently found from research, as this paper will show. The advantages and disadvantages of using the reluctance synchronous machine as traction motor are discussed in detail. The paper considers the optimum design of the stator and rotor structure of the reluctance synchronous machine. The optimum design was done with the finite element method together with optimisation algorithms. The optimum control is equally important to maximise the performance of the machine. It is explained how the current space phasor must be controlled with respect to the rotor to maximise the torque or power factor of the machine. The performance of the reluctance synchronous machine in the medium power range is explained by considering the calculated results of finite element optimised machines in the 10 - 300 kW power range. It is found that the reluctance synchronous machine has a good torque density which improves with frame size. The main disadvantage of the reluctance synchronous machine namely the relative low power factor is clearly shown and discussed. An example is given of an optimum designed, high performance traction reluctance synchronous machine. The finite element calculation results of this 640 Nm, 6000 r/min machine are presented and discussed.

Title Evaluation of the gear reliability at the design step for railway applications
 
Author Kouta, R
Guingand, M
Play, D
Affiliation Université Technologique, Belfort, France
INSA, Villeurbanne, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The aim of this study is to evaluate, at the design step, the influence of the pulsatory torques on the fatigue life of the gears in a railway gear box transmission. The reliability calculation takes into account the different known points on the rolling curve. The gear stress cycle around one point is evaluated through a quasi-static study. This analysis is performed with a laboratory software. This numerical tool is able to compute the load sharing between the different teeth into contact, the instantaneous contact pressure along the face width. The 3D stresses in the tooth root filet are then deduced. Load spectra are evaluated with and without pulsatory torques. A reliability index is then obtained by comparison between the load and the strength spectra. Considering the pulsatory torques, an increase of exceed risks of the fatigue strength is also evaluated. The load spectra and the endurance curves permit the calculation of the cumulative damage of the gears. So, the gear fatigue life is evaluated, when the pulsatory torques are present. Introduction of pulsatory torques in a theoretical schedule at the preliminary design steps is new and more realistic for the calculation of the gear stresses. The scripts of exceeding the strength limits has been quantified by a reliability index and an exceeding probability. This study was achieved with the collaboration of the GEC ALSTHOM ACR company.

Title Improvement of damage-dimensioning of railway vehicle structures
 
Author Kouta, R

Play, D
Rabatel, R
Rimbaud, M
Pineau, M

SNCF, Vitry, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract This paper presents a new method able to compute the predictive fatigue life of the T-joint assembly. This method is based on a statistical analysis of the tests. As empirical adjustment of Miner model is specific, the study was based on the corre­lation between the types of loading and test results obtained for fatigue life. The work follows three directions: analysis of the statistical load sharing of extreme values for random load represented by a Markov matrix, statistical analysis of life time obtained when the specimens are submitted to the same random load, design of a set of endurance curves (S-N), called the "random" S-N curve. The S-N curve is shifted comparing with the one obtained when using the sinusoidal loading. The random Wohler curve position in the S-N plane is optimized depending on the time life for the random tests. The Basquin model is used in order to represent the S-N curve. This model is able to take into account the damage due to the small cycles, that are present in actual loading. The use of random Wohler curve for fatigue life calculus gives results fitting with the real fatigue life of a bogie chassis. Different analysis show the robustness of the proposed approach.

Title Multivoltage locomotive design and technological innovation
 
Author Segoni, R
Affiliation DES. TECH., Montecatini Terme, Italia
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Railway design has always been conditioned by the use of technology that was appropriate for the construction of parts in conditions of limited mass-production. A logical consequence of this was the adoption of traditional-type constructive materials and techniques in combination with - inevitably - the use of semi-finished parts that were available on the market (steel sections and sheets, or extrusion sections in light alloys). This was a design policy that allowed wide margins in terms of ease-of-constuction and reliability of the solutions adopted. The unfortunate result of all this however ( despite the enormous technological advances that led to notable increases in performance and high qualitative standards for traction and braking systems and improved safety levels) is that railway design was relegated to a world of rigid and blocky shapes characterised by flat surfaces and sharply angled volumes. In effect, railway design was impeded from keeping abreast of the design development of other industrial products. The use of reinforced techno-polymers (the so-called 'composites') has finally allowed railway design to emerge from the impasse in wich it found itself. Finally, the material used in trains can reach a level of expressive quality that is perfectly in keeping with the styles that have been evident for some time now in the other 'speed shapes': cars, motorbikes, boats and airplanes. The design philosophy behind the construction of the E 412 electrical train represents an example of how it is possible to combine a high degree of both aesthetics and functionality, in order to create a locomotive with an aesthetic quality that fully expresses the shapes of our times.

Title Computational flow simulation for railway configurations
 
Author Huber, T
Schulte-Werning, B
Affiliation Deutsche Bahn AG, Munich, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Fast passenger trains may induce substantial aerodynamic loads on their environment. Namely the aerodynamic forces due to a. passing train experienced by passengers standing on the platform, by another opposing train or constructions like noise barriers must he known in order to guarantee safe transport. Full scale measurements are the traditional technique to prove for airloads lying within accepted limits. As new models of railway vehicles are designed it is necessary to know the mentioned aerodynamic loads in advance. The traditional tool of the a.erodynamicist to shape configurations in order to minimise their aerodynamic impact on the environment is the the wind tunnel. Title Introducing the next generation locomotive to the Australian rail network
 
Author Ramsey, N
Szanto, F
Hewison, P
Affiliation SCT Logistics
Downer
EDI Rail
Halcrow
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2008, Rail; the core of integrated transport, Conference on Railway Engineering, Perth, Western Australia, 2008
Publication Date 2011
Abstract The following paper describes the introduction of what is now the most powerful and efficient locomotive operating across Australia’s Defined Interstate Rail Network. The locomotive was originally conceived by SCT Logistics after analysing the market and commissioning a study from Halcrow to determine motive power configurations that would best suit their expanding rail business. The designer and manufacturer of the SCT locomotive designated the GT46C Ace, is Downer EDI Rail. The paper gives an insight into how the locomotive was conceived, including the planning and modelling that determined SCT’s decision for choosing AC traction and the associated cost benefit analyses. Also discussed are the risks considered by SCT in taking ownership of their own fleet with technology which supersedes present locomotive fleets operating across Australia’s mainline rail networks. The paper details the innovative solutions provided by Downer EDI Rail in engineering and manufacturing a locomotive to meet SCT’s demanding requirements for operation and those of the State Regulators and track access providers. The paper concludes by comparing the operating performance of the new locomotive with the operator’s expectations.

Title QR national AC diesel-electric locomotives for the Hunter Valley
 
Author Haywood, G
Bartlett, S
Affiliation United Group Rail
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Conference CORE 2006, Rail achieving growth, Conference on Railway Engineering, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia, 30 April-3 May 2006
Publication Date 2006
Abstract United Group Rail (UGL Rail) has designed and manufactured, in partnership with GE - Transportation, their first Australian built AC Diesel Electric Locomotives for QRNational (5000 class or C40aci type). These locomotives were designed for heavy haul coal operations in the Hunter Valley of NSW. The haulage task is two 5000 class locomotives hauling 74 coal hoppers at 120 tonnes each from Mt Arthur mine to Kooragang or Port Waratah coal unloaders. Testing has confirmed the locomotive design capability. The new C40aci locomotive design is another significant example of the UGL Rail/GE - Transportation partnership delivering a successful solution to their client. This solution has enabled improved train efficiency with more responsive train brakes, an increased payload (18% increase in trailing load for AC) and reduced maintenance costs for QRNational (less locomotives to perform the haulage task). By maximising the service mass for the AC locomotives from 150 tonnes to 180 tonnes enabled an increase of 20% in trailing load capability but also for this particular application reduced the adhesion demand for a better train reliability. This paper will detail the major challenges associated with the locomotive design, primarily with the minimum of changes to adapt the existing Cv40-9i ('NR' class) DC locomotive design into an AC locomotive configuration.

Title New lives for locomotives in QR
 
Author Rossow, I
Mcleod, T
Affiliation Queensland Rail (QR)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2004, New Horizons for Rail, Conference on Railway Engineering, Darwin, Northern Territory, Australia, June 20-23, 2004
Publication Date 2004
Abstract QR has a fleet of Locomotives which range from new to over 30 years in service. The management and usage of this fleet have been under review for some time and a number of changes have been made to how the locomotives are overhauled and the use they are put to. The QR network is narrow gauge (1067 mm) and is also characterised by a restrictive wine glass clearance gauge. This has made the use of large, heavy, high-powered locomotives very difficult to implement. The track is generally restricted in speed and mass capability, so that any locomotive design has to be optimised to the available track. It has not been possible to bring in standard locomotives from overseas and use them. In many ways QR has led the way with narrow gauge locomotive design features, and the necessary optimisation.

Title Diesel engine development: the combined cycle recuperative locomotive
 
Author Jones, PJ
Affiliation TAFE NSW
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2000, Railway Technology for the 21st Century, Conference on Railway Engineering, Glenelg, South Australia, May 21-23, 2000
Publication Date 2000
Abstract This paper gives a description of a proposed new locomotive called; "Combined Cycle Recuperative". The proposed locomotive is expected to reduce locomotive fuel consumption by approximately 30%. It will incorporate the following features: The adiabatic engine with internal cooling by steam. Combined cycle by steam expanded in the cylinders of the diesel engine. Separation of compression and power strokes by intermediate compressed air storage. Recovery of braking energy by air compression. Turbine compounding The proposed design also seeks to overcome the main cost disadvantage of the locomotive engine compared to marine and aircraft engines. That disadvantage is that the locomotive engine operates at its rated power for only a small part of most journeys. Most of the time the engine is idling or only partly loaded. By separating compression and power strokes with intermediate compressed air storage it is possible to obtain a higher peak power output from the engine. We can then use fewer engines to perform the same transport task. Each engine will have an operating range which can vary from air compression strokes only to power strokes only, with set combinations between these extremes. Thus one large locomotive with one engine could replace the three locomotives with their own separate engines normally required to haul a Sydney to Melbourne superfreighter. The proposed new locomotive would then overcome the uneconomic duplication of components which occurs now with triple heading. Apart from the traction motors, bogies and frames, numbers of most components will be able to be reduced to one third of those used at present.

Title Hybrid active damping for control of resonance in rail traction applications
 
Author Senini, S
Wolfs, P
Affiliation Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University (CQU)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2000, Railway Technology for the 21st Century, Conference on Railway Engineering, Glenelg, South Australia, May 21-23, 2000
Publication Date 2000
Abstract Resonant interactions can occur between the traction supply and the power factor correction equipment on a locomotive. Passive resistive damping only offers a partial solution and results in large power consumption in the damping resistor. This paper proposes an alternative damping approach, which uses an active element to provide damping and control of resonance. This offers superior damping performance under all conditions and reduced power consumption. The concept is verified through simulation studies using parameters from existing locomotive systems, known to have resonance problems.

Title Hybrid locomotive applications for an Australian heavy haul train on a typical track route
 
Author Sun, Y
Cole, C
Spiryagin, M
Rasul, M
Godber, T
Hames, S
Affiliation Centre for Railway Engineering, CQUniversity, Australia
Rio Tinto, Perth, WA 6000, Australia
QR National, Brisbane, Australia
CRC for Rail Innovation, Brisbane, Australia
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference 10th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2013), Capacity Building through Heavy Haul Operation, 4th-6th February, 2013, New Delhi, India.
Publication Date 2013
Abstract A computer simulation technique – the longitudinal train simulation (CRE-LTS) software has been used to study and evaluate the potential of hybrid locomotive applications in the heavy haul operations on a typical track route in Australia. The study envisages a freight heavy haul operation on coastal undulating track rather than typical pit-to-port operations with large altitude change. Based on the simulation results, the train energy usage and the energy from dynamic braking are analysed. The simulation results showed that the locomotives operate at an average power of much less than 100% full power. An energy hybridisation potential factor is used to evaluate the hybridisation possibility, with its value of 54%. It means that there is high potential for hybrid heavy haul locomotive applications. Methods for practical hybrid applications are suggested and discussed.
