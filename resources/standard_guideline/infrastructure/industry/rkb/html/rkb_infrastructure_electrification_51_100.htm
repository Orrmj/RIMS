Electrified railway technology providers were early adopters since high power electrical energy conversion is a key requirement to their operation, particularly for rolling stock traction converters. Today, we are witnessing the equivalent transformation of technology at the grid level – the creation of “smart grids”. Title The impact of rectifier interphase transformer design on stray current levels in DC traction systems: rail: smart, automated, sustainable
 
Author Barr, R
Stuart-Smith, D
Affiliation Electric Power Consulting Pty Ltd, Kiama, NSW
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract This paper identifies how unwanted non-linear voltage-current (V-I) characteristics from some types of transformers/rectifiers incorporating interphase transformers can significantly increase average rail to earth potentials. Such increases can magnify DC stray current levels and therefore risk an increase corrosion rates in utility and other assets. Modelling is provided that shows how interphase transformers that are commonly used to increase the power rating of transformers/rectifiers can have the unintended consequence of providing non-linear voltage-current characteristics which impacts on load sharing between rectifiers at adjacent substations operating in parallel. Non-optimised load sharing can rise the average negative shifts of rail to earth potentials and magnify stray curent emission levels. This work has important DC stray current implications for the design of new traction systems. In addition, this work also provide for low cost strategies for reducing DC stray current levels on some existing DC traction systems.

Title Root cause analysis of harmonic filter component failure in 2x25 KV traction power system
 
Author Cella, UM
Nussey, PF
Affiliation Aurizon Network, Brisbane, Qld
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Aurizon's Central Queensland Coal Network is electrified at 2x25 kV, and has twenty connection points with the electric power transmission grid. The current drawn by electric locomotives has significant harmonic content which must not be transferred into the transmission grid via the connection point at the feeder station. The achieve this, harmonic filters are installed at each connection point. This article focuses on multiple failures of a harmonic filter reaction at one of Aurizon's feeder stations and on the process that Aurizon followed to identify their root cause, and concludes by describing the solution that was implemented. Fault evidence was collected from the field, and fault electrical data were sourced from the recordings of the fault transients. The data were sent to the manufacturer of the reactor and to the supplier of the filter for analysis. However, there was lack of agreement regarding the sequence of events that led to the last fault, which permanently damaged the reactor. Therefore, an in-depth field investigation was conducted, and samples were taken from the reactor. Finally, Aurizon and the manufacturer agreed on a more robust design for the reactor. Because of operational contraints the healthy part of the filter was re-energised after the last field investigation: the process followed and the results obtained are described in the last section of this article.

Title Automated fault waveform analysis in 2x25 kV traction system: development and application to asset management
 
Author Cella, UM
Nussey, PF
Affiliation Aurizon Network, Brisbane, Qld
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Arcing faults due to wildlife and branches are common on Aurizon's overhead traction power lines, and rapid fault clearing by protection devices is essential to limit fault stress to the power system. In order to target maintenance to the areas that are experiencing the highest fault stress, analysis of fault clearing performance is required. However, the multiplicity of factors affecting fault clearing performance makes manual analysis not practical: an automated process is required. This paper presents an automated algorithm that provides a fault stress score based on waveform data of fault voltage and current. The algorithm uses the fault waveform database generated by the Schneider ION meter devices and extracts, via signal processing, key indicator parameters from the waveform. It then scores electrical sections based on the amount of fault stress they are experiencing. Typical fault waveforms are presented first, followed by a description of the empirical correlation between waveform pattern and issues in the electrical section. The development of the algorithm based on this knowledge is then introduced. A practical application of the algorithm to a dataset of traction power system faults concludes the paper.

Title Developments in track feeder protection for AC autotransformer traction networks
 
Author Perin, I
Goffey, J
Cant, C
Grzesiczak, D
Affiliation Aurizon, Brisbane, Qld
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Distance protection is the most commonly used scheme for protecting track feeders in AC electric railways. It operates based on measured impedance, which translates into distance to fault. This enables schemes to be built around protection zones, with progressively increasing reaches and time delays. Unlike transmission and distribution networks, where the feeder impedance is directly proportional to distance, autotransformer rail networks introduce a non-linearity. A particular case, involving asymmetrical earthing of the protected line, resulted in significant distortion of the impedance vs distance function. This scenario prompted a detailed study of the feeder impedance for various network feeding arrangements, with a revision of relevant distance zone reaches. Following the study, a new automated distance protection system was introduced and consequently rolled out for Aurizon's entire traction network, with zone reaches adapting to the network configuration in real time, ensuring maximum possible coverage of the traction network without encroaching into adjacent zones. This paper presents a review of autotransformer track feeder distance protection, together with Aurizon's latest developments.

Publication Date 2018
Abstract We developed a contactless OCS geometry measurement system, which enables onboard measurement of the contact lines at the speed up to 130km/h. This measurement system can measure not only the static height of the contact wire, but also the messenger wire and fittings. This measurement system features compatibility of high resolution and object recognition accuracy by combining cameras and laser sensors. Both sensors are contactless, so the static geometry of the OCS can be evaluated by using contactless measurement. The static geometry data enable to perform dynamic simulation precisely, by which current collection performance of OCS and pantograph under various conditions such as train speeds and types of pantographs can be estimated. To verify capability of our system, we performed on-track test at a maximum speed of 130km/h. As a result, it was verified that the geometry of the contact wire, messenger wire, and fittings is accurately measured. In particular, it was verified that the height of the contact wire can be measured with an error within 20 mm. We are aiming for practical use of this system within 3 years. We expect to bring innovation in OCS maintenance with this system.

Abstract In France and in Japan, the electrical supply of moving trains is provided by a sliding contact between the train’s pantographs and the catenary. This electromechanical interface is composed of the pantograph’s strips – made mainly of carbon or steel – and the catenary contact wire – made mainly of copper. After years of investigations and research the experts are agree, the temperature rising induces the rising of the carbon (or steel) strip and the catenary contact wire wear. Moreover, the heating produced at the interface depends on the surface quality. Indeed, the smaller is the surface contact, the greater is the electrical resistance which implies more heat production because of the Joule effect. The problem is multi-physical; there is a coupling between mechanical, electrical and thermal states. The objective is to define the real contact area with a simple electrical measurement. In many practical or fundamental situations involving contacting solids, the relevant notion of the real contact area is a very delicate one and especially its experimental assessment. The contact area gives the contact resistance which allows simulating the interface in order to find the temperature. Experiments would lead to empirical laws which will allow predicting the contact wire and stripping wear.

SNCF Réseau, Direction de l’ingénierie, 6 avenue Francois Mitterrand, 95574, La plaine St-Denis, France

Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract The present work addresses rail traffic rescheduling in case of electric infrastructure failure. The power available for train traction is restricted and the traffic should be reorganized according to this constraint. The system behavior is analyzed by simulation, using a dynamic multi-physics railway simulator that gives physical quantities such as train’s speed profiles, voltage along the catenary lines, heating’s…The rescheduling problem relies on this black box model, with a large number of input variables, constrained dynamic outputs (typically voltage limits). It involves a complex simulator and a high computation cost. We propose a rescheduling process based on regional sensitivity analysis. Monte Carlo filtering and the two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test statistics are used in order to analyze which rescheduling actions are efficient to obtain an acceptable traffic (respect of operational constraints). Our methodology allows to identify important inputs and reduce the problem’s dimension. Non-influential variables are set to a fixed value, and an optimization post treatment can be performed with the remaining influential variables and performance indicator defined by the operator. This analysis allows the decision maker to choose the solution which best fits his objectives. The proposed approach has been tested on a multi-line heterogeneous traffic. The considered incident is a loss of feeding power substation.
