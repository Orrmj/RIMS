Title Seismic rehabilitation of railway bridge column by CFRP wrapping
 
Author Chen, X
Zhang, X
Ding, M
Affiliation State Key Laboratory of Heavy Duty AC Drive Electric Locomotive Systems Integration, Zhuzhou, China
CRRC Zhuzhou Locomotive Co.,Ltd., Zhuzhou, China
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract With the rapid development of high-speed railway in China, more and more railway bridges have been constructed in the high seismic intensity regions. Damage or collapse of bridges during an earthquake can lead to function failure of the railway network. Quickly rehabilitation technique of damaged bridges is particularly important for post-earthquake rescue, and is cost-effective and more efficient than replacement. In this study, Carbon Fiber Reinforced Plastics, abbreviated CFRP, are used to rehabilitate railway bridge column by wrapping method with whole and partial column height, i.e. 94% and 52% of the column height, respectively. Quasi-static tests of scaled down models are carried out to investigate the failure characteristics and lateral responses of the railway bridge column under cyclic loading. Results show that the seismic capacity of bridge columns can be improved after rehabilitation by CFRP wrapping method with whole and partial height. It can be found that the fracture region of the rehabilitated bridge column with partial height of CFRP wrapping can be shifted to the region without rehabilitation; while for the rehabilitated bridge column with whole height of CFRP wrapping, the ductility of the whole bridge column can be improved.

Title Fatigue of concrete railway bridges - Experimental results compared to calculated results according to Eurocode 2, part 2 (env 1992-2)
 
Author Allaart, AP
Affiliation Holland Railconsult, Utrecht, The Netherlands
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Fatigue of structures in general, and concrete railway bridges in particular, has become increasingly important due to a number of developments, such as heavier axle-loads, higher train velocities and greater traffic density (larger number of load cycles and greater loads), a tendency for slender construction, use of new materials and use of partial pre-stressing in railway bridges. These developments have taken place at almost all railway administrations. The fatigue research carried out during the ERRJ D 183 Study should be seen as a first step to understanding the fatigue behaviour of new concrete railway bridges under current loading. In this paper information is given about the nine test bridges and how they were measured. The results of the fatigue measurements on the test bridges are summarised, evaluated and compared with the calculated results from Eurocode 2, Part 2 (ENV 1992-2) for the reinforcement and (in two cases) the concrete. It should be noted that for these test bridges Eurocode 2, Part 2 (ENV 1992-2) was used for the first time to calculate the fatigue behaviour of structures. It is concluded that none of the measuring points of the test bridges were critical for fatigue. It appeared that Eurocode 2, Part 2 (ENV 1992-2) provides a user-friendly method of checking fatigue, but does not cover every type of structure. In the cases that it does, the measured and calculated values of the A. factor agree to a satisfactory extent. The effect from loading from more than one track is significant. Therefore the percentage of traffic on the bridge needs justification. The influence of the dynamic load factor and the concrete grade appears to be moderate. The research carried out during this study provided a great deal of knowledge on fatigue effects, together with former studies, and shows that the design rules in Eurocode 2, Part 2 (ENV 1992-2), "Concrete bridges" may be successfully used for the common types of concrete railway structures.

Title Innovative designs reduce noise emission of railway traffic
 
Author Bos, JA
Affiliation Holland Railconsult, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Noise emission caused by railway traffic is a serious problem, especially in densely populated areas. Holland Railconsult has succeeded in developing a steel bridge that is even quieter than a ballasted track with timber sleepers. The bridge design consists of a box girder with an optimised plate thickness to reduce vibration, and gutters incorporated in the top surface to accept rails embedded in a cork-rubber emulsion. Noise reduction was also the aim when Holland Railconsult designed a new ballastless track, called Deck Track. Calculations show a reduction of approximately 5dB(A) in comparison to a conventional ballasted track. Besides a lower noise emission, both the low noise bridge and the Deck Track have other advantages like lower construction and maintenance costs.

Title Measures to reduce the noise of existing steel railway bridges
 
Author Dings, P
van Tol, P
Affiliation NS Technical Research, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The Netherlands Railways (NS) network has about 5000 steel bridges, often located in densely populated areas. The noise production of most of these bridges can bc lip to about 15 dB(A) higher than the emission at plain ballasted track with concrete sleepers. Several parties may be interested in reducing this noise production. Over the last 10 years several ways to reduce the noise of these bridges have been tested by NS. During 1996 and 1997, some revolutionary measures have been tested with a surprising result. Sandwich damping has been applied to a pi-girder bridge in Leiden. Two types of viscoelastic materials of two different suppliers have been compared in this test. No technical problems are expected anymore, something that has prevented bridge engineers until now from applying sandwich damping. Depending on the bridge type, this relatively expensive measure can be very attractive if high noise reductions have to be achieved. Big steel blocks of 100 kg have been added as extra mass under each rail fixation point of a plate girder bridge in The Hague, to increase the bridge point-impedance. On these bridges, noise barriers have been applied as well. The result of both measures has been dete mined separately. The direct rail fixation of an existing bridge in Purmerend has been replaced by the NS / Edilon embedded rail system. Over the past 4 years expectations of the application of the embedded rail have been very high, without any proper test to prove the success of the system. This test has now been performed and the advantages are now obvious. On another bridge, in Dordrecht, the under-rail pads of a direct rail fixation have been replaced by two times softer cork-and-rubber pads. The rail has been damped with a Phoenix rail damper at the same time, to compensate for the decreased rail decay rate. With the tested systems, and the ones already known, NS now has a full set of noise reducing measures available, one fit for each existing ballastless bridge type. Depending on the noise reduction needed (or wanted), the most cost-effective measure can be chosen.

Title Engineering assessment of steel railway bridges based on past performance
 
Author Prasad, P
Affiliation Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC)
Source ATRI
Conference Austroads Bridge Conference, 7th, 2009, Auckland, New Zealand
Publication Date 2009
Abstract The Australian Rail Track Corporation (ARTC) is responsible for over 2,200 railway bridges on its rail network which extends from Queensland border through New South Wales, Victoria and South Australia to Western Australia. Of these structures there are 780 transom top steel bridges, most of them built circa 1910. The load carrying capacity of short span bridge elements such as RSJ girders, stringers and cross-girders do not comply with the current Bridge Design Code AS5100 at the ultimate limit state (ULS) in bending, yet these elements are performing without distress due to bending. Performance assessment of a network representative sample of steel bridges was undertaken in accordance with AS13822 Basis for Design of Structures Assessment of Existing Structures , 2005. It was determined that all bridges are deemed safe under current traffic loading and speed. This AS13822 compliance has structural design consequences on dynamic load allowance (DLA) and ULS code factors. The paper discusses in detail the impact of high code DLA for railway traffic loading comprising: Assessment in accordance with AS13822; A comparison of the DLA in AS5100 to the most relevant international standards; The interaction of DLA and Live Load factors; Factors contributing to railway DLA.

Title Probabilistic risk assessment of a PSC box girder railway bridge system considering construction phases using six sigma methodology
 
Subtitle Towards a global railway: proceedings
Author Cho,T
Kim, J
Lee, K
Cha, D
Affiliation Korea Rail Network Authority, Dejeon, Republic of Korea
Conference 8th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 18-22 2008
Publication Date 2008
Abstract Currently the prediction and the control of prestressing force and camber are designed deterministically in the design of cable stayed bridges or prestressed concrete bridges. However, the variation of qualities of materials and external loads have different types of probabilistic distributions, which could make additional errors in the prediction and the control of errors.Therefore, the uncertainties in the resistance and loads should have to be considered in a probabilistic manner. To develop a probabilistic risk assessment technique in prestressed concrete box girder railway bridges, the important random variables are determined by an analytical hierarchy process (AHP) method, which is selected for risk assessment of the target PSC box girder bridge constructed by a MSS method. The limit state functions are determined to investigate the risk of tensile cracks in upper and lower flange concrete, just after the moving of scaffolding, and the risks of the prestressing loss at each construction stage. For composing the implicit limit state function, Response Surface Method (RSM) is selected to evaluate the reliability of the implicit limit states of complex structures. The basic RSM could diverge depending on the nonlinearity of the limit states. For improved convergence, iterations are performed to find the more probable failure points, which are closer to the limit states by updating design matrix, the input data to compose the response surfaces of considered systems. For maximizing the adaptation of RSM, a diagonal weighting matrix is used, which accelerates the convergence of reliability. Accordingly in the target PSC Railway Bridge, the linear adaptive weighted response surface method combined with advanced first order second moment method have been used for the evaluation of reliabilities of the considered limit states. Consequently, risk assessments have been performed for the limit states of the response of the target bridge, as a resistance term using the ultimate stress and prestress loss with the load term using the rupture stress and expected prestress loss, based on the Korean design specifications and ACI specification for each construction stages of PSC box girder bridges built by the Movable Scaffolding Method.
