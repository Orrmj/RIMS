Title Railways in action
 
Author Laver, P
Affiliation BHP Transport Limited, Australia
Conference 4th International Heavy Haul Conference, Railways in action, 11-15 September 1989, Brisbane, Queensland, Australia
Publication Date 1989
Abstract Australian industrial enterprises, the direct and indirect customers of the Australian Railways systems, are facing compelling pressures to become world competitive. To be world competitive the inputs to industry also need to be world class in terms of quality, cost and efficiency. It is essential, therefore, that transport within the country and between Australian producers and their export markets be of a standard equal to or better than anywhere in the world. A frequently repeated misunderstanding is that it is only the export sector that requires high standards of transport efficiency. This is entirely incorrect in a country where wages and taxes reflect the cost-of-living it makes no difference whether the goods are exports, imports or produced for domestic consumption. Inefficiencies in the transport system flow through to the cost structure of the total economy, and are a key ingredient of economic performance. If Australian industries were so much more efficient than their competitors elsewhere, transport inefficiencies could be accommodated and offset by other intrinsic advantages. Unfortunately, other than in the blessing of some mineral resources of a quality and location which makes them inherently world market leaders, most of the rest of Australian industry is struggling to compete. A more efficient transport system is desperately required as Australia seems to lag as far behind in this sector as in any other.

Title Lessons from major rail infrastructure programmes
 
Corporate Author National Audit Office (NAO)
Publisher National Audit Office (NAO)
Place London, UK
Publication Date 2014-10
Abstract A National Audit Office review of five major rail projects sponsored by the Department for Transport since 1998 highlights eleven lessons it should apply to its current and future rail programmes. The review, which draws on published NAO value for money studies, highlights the many challenges the Department has encountered in these projects which are large in scale and complex, with construction either expected to or having taken 9 to 10 years, at a cost of up to £21.4 billion. It concludes that the Department has made progress in its management of rail infrastructure programmes and responded well on programmes such as Thameslink and Crossrail to manage risks and control costs. Overall, the NAO studies indicate an improvement in the Department’s sponsorship of major rail infrastructure programmes. Today’s review shows that the Department has taken action in relation to many issues raised in the NAO studies. However, as current and future rail programmes develop, there remain areas which the Department needs to address or where it needs to provide more focus:- developing clear strategic business cases and scrutinising economic analysis of the estimated benefits of new railways
- economic assumptions also need to reflect changes in real-life behaviour
- more programme management capacity and skills
- beneficiaries of new transport could contribute more funding and finance

Abstract In the wake of two decades of deferred maintenance of track and equipment, massive labor layoffs and declining freight and passenger revenues, in 1990 the Chilean State Railways (EFE) were deeply in debt to the banking system and on the verge of physical and financial collapse. Title Towards an economic argument for subsidising research & development as a community service obligation
 
Author Michael, E
Affiliation La Trobe University, Bundoora, Vic, Australia
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), 14-16 November 1994, Paris, France
Publication Date 1994
Abstract Subsidies have been a mainstay wherever public transport services have been required. For railways, in particular, a subsidy may offset costs whilst encouraging maximum efficient output (so long as the economies of scale continue to generate lower average costs for each unit of output). These "cost" or "producer" subsidies are common in all modes of transport, but have been increasingly criticised by economists and governments, suggesting a trend towards their curtailment. The principal alternative, as this paper observes, is for Community Service Obligations policy to try to effectively subsidise consumers directly. An extension of this proposition is to treat "Research and Development" by the rail sector as unique by nature, and consider it as an appropriate candidate for subsidy. In many cases, the consequences of successful rail research are attributed to the community in the form of externalities, or as public budgetary gains, which are not captured by rail operators. If the research and development programs of railways were more transparent, and their public benefit more visible, they might then be seen (in whole or in part) as clear candidates for Community Service Obligation funding. Rail prices, of course, would reflect this gain.

Title The railways role in the light of the European future transport policies
 
Author Spirito, P
De Lazzari, S
Affiliation Ferrovie dello Stato S.p.A., Rome, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), 14-16 November 1994, Paris, France
Publication Date 1994
Abstract The future of the European transport system can be summed up in a few figures: in the European Union, the market share of railways in passengers transportation was more or less about 11 % in 1970 and 6,8% in 1992. For goods transportation the railways market share decreased sharply: from (more or less) 35% to 17%. Spontaneous development of the transport system will continue to emphasize the role of the road mode. The lack of any intervention by authorities in order to change end user behaviours, will further increase the road transport market share. At the same time, environmental problems (pollution, land use, congestion), which increased sharply in the past, show no signs of important changes in the current trend. The high development of road transport increases environmental problems. In this situation the railways' role seems to be unavoidably more and more marginal within the transport system. The actual guidelines of the EU's transport policy are known: deregulation, internalization of external costs, integration between different transport modes, investments in transport networks. This policy would lead the EU towards an integrated transport system with multimodal or specialized operators offering single or general purpose services competing between one another. In fact, the final aim of this approach would be that of creating the best conditions of choice for the end users (citizens, firms) of transport services. Within this framework each operator has the chance to perform his service by the most adequate transport mode combination, the client has the opportunity of choosing between various services, with different levels of quality and price. Within the railways sector the EU approach is very innovative, upsetting the traditional (institutional and economic) organization. But, whenever the new railway companies achieve the objectives of actual and proposed directives, the goal of a competitive market will still remain distant. In fact, different transport modes are affected by different kinds of external costs, which are not considered in the price of transport services. So, end users of transport services (passengers and goods) pay only a part of the cost of their transport choice. The lack of balance between costs of road transport services and other transport services is one of the most important reasons for the modal disequilibrium in the transport system. Guidelines of European transport policy have been declared many times, but there are important differences regarding its real implementation. Because of these differences, there are concrete risks of failure in European transport policy. The resolve to introduce competition rules for each transport mode added to serious difficulties in carrying out internalization of external costs, will produce a further imbalance to the advantage to road transport. There is clearly a strong need for balance between the realization of each guideline otherwise European transport policy will turn in a heterogeneous sum of single policies which will produce the same results as in the past. Within an "equal speed", European policy, railways may have a leading role because of their specific assets.

Title Measuring benefits of high speed ground transportation: the US northeast corridor: a North American experience
 
Author Stubbs, AD
Ewing, D
Tarajano, M
Affiliation CONEG, Washington, DC, USA
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), 14-16 November 1994, Paris, France
Publication Date 1994
Abstract Effective measurement of the benefits of investing in high speed ground transportation, especially challenging in a multi-juridictional political environment, is critical to the ability to win and retain political and financial support. The selection of methods to identify and measure the benefits of high-speed ground transportation investments may determine the ability to communicate the research finding with policy and political decision-makers, and thus affect the ability of the research to garner political support. Today, the US Northeast corridor faces the challenge of maintaining effective political support for financial and major public investment. While Amtrak is the primary owner and the sole operator of an intercity passenger rail service, the future of high speed rail in the Northeast (and in the nation as a whole) may hinge on the ability of supporters to develop evidence of the benefits of additional investment in the Northeast Corridor which would be persuasive to a multiplicity of local government, states, regional and subregional transportation authorities, and the federal government. The economic rent model is a promising methodology for measuring the benefits of investing in high-speed ground transportation.

Title From research to benefits: how to accelerate the innovation process (organized session 10)
 
Author Moisio, L
Talotte, C
Benox, A
Doherty, A
Hagiwara, Y
Jack, A
Affiliation RSSB, UK
SNCF, France
SBB Infrastructure, Switzerland
Network Rail, UK
Mitsubishi Electric Corporation, Japan
Birmingham Centre for Rail Research and Education, UK
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan

Abstract The difficulty so often lies not so much in developing new ideas, new knowledge, new solutions, as in getting them to be used and deliver benefits. Abstract The European Commission has launched, within the Horizon 2020 (H2020) Programme, several initiatives associated with the development of cross-modal transport infrastructure. Three European H2020 Coordination and Support Actions, namely the REFINET, FOX and USE-iT projects, have been established to support this work. The aim of the FOX (Forever open Infrastructure across all transport modes) project is to develop a highly efficient and effective cross-modal research and development (R&D) environment and culture which meets the demanding requirements of the transport and connectivity of people and goods. The FOX project involves a network of researchers and practitioners from the different modes. It sets the agenda for the further improvement of cross-modal research development innovation, as well as the demonstration and implementation of the results. The overall objectives of the first phase of the project are to: undertake a review of international practice associated with construction, maintenance, recycling and re-use and inspection; and identify state-of-the-art research and best practice methods across road, rail, airfields and waterways. The final phase of the project is to develop a road map for future research and development and guidelines regarding how these initiatives could be implemented across each mode. In this sense, the FOX project seeks to find common methods across all transport modes. This paper describes the outcomes of the first of the three deliverables completed to date and the key next steps of the FOX project.

Title Relocation and transit led development in a new rail corridor
 
Subtitle Conference proceedings
Author Olaru, D
Smith, B
Qiu, M
Taplin, J
Source ATRI
Conference Conference of Australian Institutes of Transport Research (CAITR), 30th, 2008, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2008
Abstract A key factor affecting transport policy is the interaction between urban form and the travel patterns of the community. Compact high-density urban development is thought to lead to decreased travel and thus sustainable mobility. It is expected that compactness and good public transport services result in reduction of travel by car. However, personal attitudes seem to have greater effect on mobility than the characteristics of the urban form. Not all individuals and households are inclined to reduce their travel or switch to public transport, cycling and walking: households with particular lifestyles (e.g., extremely "mobiles") are actually likely to convert the accessibility benefits into increased distances. To some extent self selectivity of households relocating to transit orientated development (TOD) may decide the sustainable transport indicators at these centres. This research investigates the influence TOD characteristics on household's location decisions. The study area is the new Mandurah to Perth railway opened in December 2007 in Perth. Three train-station precincts are chosen. Bull Creek is an established centre with a new station opened in the vicinity. The Wellard and Cockburn Central are relatively new precincts developed around the opening of the new stations. Wellard is a self contained TOD with relatively little development in the adjourning area. Cockburn central, like Bull Creek, acts as a transit interchange for surrounding residential zones. A survey of existing travel patterns and location decisions is combined with a stated choice questionnaire aimed at evaluating the effects of TOD attributes on location decisions and daily activity patterns. The study is longitudinal aimed at uncovering the effect of new railway services. The reported results in this paper draw on the first two waves for the panel.

Title The potential impacts of high speed passenger rail to Eastern Australia
 
Author Devencorn, K
Pey, A
Jacobs, B
Affiliation Aurecon Australasia
Source ATRI
Publication Date 2014-10
Abstract In this paper we consider and reveal the possible impacts - the pros and cons of HSR on eastern Australia including operating high speed freight trains - adding HSR Freight as a viable add-on to Australia’s HSR Passenger. We propose that the Australian Government should consider the establishment of a High Speed Rail Development Office with a clear mandate and strict deadline to define and specify the best and most effective HSR solution for Australia. We suggest that the Government must consider the greater benefits to the community which HSR can yield and formalise its commitment to HSR. It argues that the main concerns of railway reform involve not the separation of infrastructure operators and train operators but some kind of institutionalised separation of strategic authorities and the operating companies, regardless of public or private ownership. Five case examples from the United Kingdom, Germany, France, Sweden and Switzerland have been selected to reflect a large but still comparable variety of optional systems of governance. In its preliminary conclusion, this paper finds that the UK's massive legislative and administrative efforts to pull the railway network out of the disastrous effects of the initial privatisation scheme, are seen as an example of successful bound governance. The same holds true for Switzerland's well-established separation of government strategy and rail operations. The paper highlights successes and shortcomings in each national case, and closely examines the sometimes very different contexts of regional and national networks, infrastructure-wise, as well as concerning freight and passenger operations. The contrasted picture is particularly true of France, where the renowned technical and commercial prodigy of TGV comes along with some of the worst run-down infrastructure still in use in the five countries. France is also the home of a very unique attempt to separate infrastructure management and train operations without touching the integrity of the national railway company, and a series of creative and sometimes successful attempts to commercially revive long-declining secondary and local passenger and freight routes. The paper is highly critical toward the current German rail privatisation plan (now on hold), which includes no clear separation of responsibilities between the government and the Deutsche Bahn. The future development of Germany's extensive railway network with its unique mixture of heavily congested and underused lines, secretive struggle between conflicting priorities in freight and passenger transport, is at stake, but the present institutional framework seem scarcely prepared for the task. Sweden has put a lot of effort into a modestly improved network facing some thorough questions. Its very early ventures into regionalisation and railway reform have inspired all Europe, but their relative success remains heavily debated. Using the governance approach and separation model, the paper attempts to draw a meaningful typology of railway networks as governance systems. A preliminary version of the paper was presented at 2009 MobilTUM and Transatlantic NECTAR Conferences. The present paper has seen significant updates especially concerning sucess indicators, freight operations and crucial recent developments in the five selected countries' railway networks. The author's intent is to extend, once the ongoing PhD thesis is completed, the governance approach and model to comparative examination of railway networks worldwide.

Author Link, H
Merkert, R
Conference European Transport Conference 2010, Glasgow, Scotland, 11th-13th October 2010
Publication Date 2010-10
Abstract An important measure of the 1994 rail reform in Germany was the so-called regionalisation of regional rail passenger services. The background of this measure was i) the attempt to achieve a clear distinction between cost-covering (or even profitable) services to be provided at DB's (incumbent) own entrepreneurial risk (commercial services), and those services which are non-profitable but have to be operated in the public interest and therefore to be subsidised (PSOs), and ii) to guarantee a sound financing for these PSOs. While all long-distance rail services were defined as commercial services being not eligible for explicit subsidies (as a result market entry in long-distance passenger services is very low), regional passenger services were classified as services to be subsidised. Since 1996 the federal states have been responsible for procuring these services from transport companies and for financing them within franchise contracts. Theoretically, this follows the idea of obtaining the most efficient service delivery by introducing bidding procedures for the right to serve lines or networks as monopolist for a pre-defined franchise period. The subsidisation of these services is regulated by the Regionalisation Act and can be considered very sound. In contrast to other countries such as the UK, only regional rail services which are eligible for subsidies are franchised. This paper analyses the development and success of franchising regional rail passenger services in Germany. It is based on an analysis of publicly available sources such as reports of the relevant authorities in the market (Federal Railway Board, Network Agency, Commission on Monopolies), some recent data and a series of interviews conducted by the authors with PTA's and train operating companies. We have found that the character of franchise contracts in Germany varies considerably regarding the question whether they are granted within competitive tendering procedures or not and regarding features such as duration, contract types (net versus gross contracts). Our major conclusion is that the so-called regionalisation with the agreed sound financing of rail-PSOs in Germany and in particular competitive tendering had positive impacts on service provision, patronage, customer satisfaction, service quality and costs. However, the huge diversity across the different states and PTAs hamper a systematic impact analysis as the lack of centrally hold and publicly accessible data does. We have identified several problems of the German approach to rail franchising which are mostly due to failures in the institutional set-up (vertical integration of the DB group and a lack of a sufficient regulation for a long time, as well as the lack of a clear legal rules whether regional rail services have to be tendered or not) and to some extent also due to lack of more experience of PTAs, in particular during the first years after regionalisation. Overall, with improvements in these fields competitive tendering appears to bear still potential for further cost savings and quality improvements (although for already competitively awarded contracts not as much as in the first round of tendering).

- Low socio-economic growth, modest transport market development;
- Modest socio-economic growth, modest transport market development;
- High socio-economic growth, far-reaching transport market development.

These results will be used by ProRail to confront the available rail infrastructure with the future rail freight demand. This way, future infrastructure bottlenecks will be identified and action plans will be developed to solve and prevent these bottlenecks. Finally, the outcomes are used to motivate investments in rail infrastructure.

Title The effects of institutional, environmental and transactional factors on train operating company performance: a Tobit regression approach based on DEA efficiency scores
 
Author Merkert, R
Smith, A
Nash, C
Conference European Transport Conference 2009, NH Hotel Leeuwenhorst, Noordwijkerhout, Netherlands, 5 October 2009
Publication Date 2009-10
Abstract Benchmarking of rail firms has become a matter of substantial interest. Technical and cost efficiency have been modelled for many railways now, indicating that both decrease with vertical separation at the train operation / rail infrastructure interface but may increase with competition (which is seen as associated with vertical separation). Additionally, many authors emphasised the importance of transaction costs in regard to assessing the desirability of vertical separation. Previous authors have asserted that separation of train operation companies and infrastructure managers increases transaction costs (without proving it) and that higher transaction costs means that vertical separation is a bad thing in terms of efficiency. In terms of the previous empirical efficiency studies that have been done, transaction costs have not explicitly been identified within studies. The key reason for this important limitation is mainly that until recently no data on transaction costs was available. This paper builds on latest results in regard to transaction costs measurement (see for example Merkert, 2008) and uses direct indicators of transaction costs in standard efficiency measurement models. We apply two-stage DEA approaches to evaluate the technical, allocative and cost efficiency for 43 British, German and Swedish franchised passenger, open access passenger and freight train operating firms. With this cross-national study we aim to determine the impact of institutional structure, size of firms, degree of competition, type of operation (for example franchised versus open access train operation) and the level of transaction costs on technical and overall efficiency. The first DEA approach, which is solely concerned with technical efficiency controls for these factors by applying second-step Tobit regressions to the bootstrapped (bias-corrected) technical efficiency scores of the DEA. The second DEA approach extends the analysis to consider allocative and overall efficiency as well as technical efficiency (again with a second stage to analyse the drivers of efficiency). Our preliminary results show that vertical separation by itself has no effect on technical and cost efficiency, but a marginal negative effect on allocative efficiency. In addition, competition in the freight train operation market decreases efficiency. Interestingly, we found the most significant effects in relation to the level of transaction costs. The dominating effect in that respect is the negative association between the level of transaction costs (per train-km) and all three types of efficiency.
