Author Zhu, H
Gong, Z
Wen, X
Affiliation Northern Jiaotong University, Beijing, China
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The methods for vision simulation in a locomotive cab fall into two categories, i.e. passive playing back of stored frames and active real-time animation based on three dimensional models. The recorded video of the front view of the cab has the shortcoming of discontinuity between the frames when the simulated train speed differs from that of the locomotive carrying the camcorder to make the front view records. The same problem prevents the approach of playing back the pre-generated three dimensional animation. On the other hand the real-time active three dimensional animation can solve this problem at the expense of heavy graph computation. The task is usually carried out on high end workstations, such as SGI Onyx, but the cost of the firmware may reduce the competitiveness of the locomotive driver training system significantly. The solution of a 32-bit Pentium Pro 200 MHz PC plus an AGC-GL580 Pro graphic accelerating board provides a cost efficient way for the real-time active animation to be applied to the training systems in ordinary locomotive depots. Taking into consideration the difference of computing capacity between a PC and a workstation, the feature of visual environment of railroad running has been studied and special strategy and algorithms have been developed in order to take full advantages of the hardware and make maximum acceptable simplification in software. The analyses of frame rate, train speed, sleeper arrangement, signal brightness and fog effect are discussed in the paper. An innovative proposal of hybrid vision simulation is first time put forward.

Publication Date 2016
Abstract Recently, adoption of competency-based training schemes in safety-critical domains has increased. This includes rail where performance-based models have steadily replaced the older experience- and time-based curricula. The advancement of technology has catalysed this change by introducing new opportunities for enhancing training delivery. Among these innovations, competency development in sitro (conducting training in controlled simulations of the work setting) has received considerable attention in workplace training. The uses of train driving simulators have been gradually expanding but if these simulators are to contribute more meaningfully within the newly espoused competency-based approach, it needs to be encompassed within a structured pedagogical framework that is more inclusive of the broad variety of learning that can take place at work. This paper reviews recent advances in simulator-based pedagogies and presents a new conceptual framework for the development of train driver expertise with an aim to set the stage for discussions and iterative development of comparable theoretical models. First, the paper will present a cross-domain literature review to synthesize the key findings associated with the development of contemporary simulators that have a pedagogic application. Second, the paper will delve deeper into and review the Multi-Crew Pilots Licence (MPL) simulator-based training scheme, recently established to develop the competencies of trainee airline pilots. Drawing upon these findings and supplementing them with current thinking on constructivist pedagogical theories, a prototype conceptual framework for train driver competency development will be modelled to demonstrate and discuss: (1) new potentialities in simulators' mediation of training & learning; (2) more flexibility in the way that theoretical and practical aspects of training syllabus, and training media are blended; and (3) a general need to foster theoretical and empirical research to continue studying how simulators could be integrated into train driver development curricula more effectively.

Title A risk-based approach to training
 
Author Cheeseman, P

Affiliation Lloyd's Register Rail
Ausglyn Consulting
Rail Corporation New South Wales
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2005, 22-24 November 2005, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2005
Abstract Throughout the world the concepts of a risk-based approach to the management of safety are being embraced by railway regulators, operators and suppliers. Since the tragic Waterfall accident, Rail Corporation New South Wales (RailCorp) has embarked on the development and implementation of an ambitious organisational Risk Management Framework (RMF). Yet absolute safety is not achievable in the real world and therefore the success of the RMF relies on two fundamentals: RailCorp having in place good processes and good people, such that when there is a problem or failure in one, the railway can be sustained by the other. This paper will describe the application of the RMF in the training field, based on work carried out by the authors for RailCorp. The extrapolation of the principles behind the RMF are explained, supported by case studies showing practical application on real projects. A novel extension of the Difficulty, Importance Frequency (DIF) analysis has been developed to explicitly consider the risk. As far as is know, this approach has not been tried elsewhere, yet seems essential to enable the overall "case for safety" for the change to be made. The alternative is simply to assume that training will catch, and fix, all residual issues that are "too hard" to deal with another way. In short, the risk-based approach that has been developed, takes the tools and techniques of the risk practitioner, that were originally introduced for use on safety change projects and applies them to the detailed design, development, delivery and assessment of training activity.

Title Human error in railway operations control
 
Author Williams, C
Affiliation Interfleet Technology, Sydney, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2009, Doing More with Less, 17-19 November 2009, Adelaide, South Australia
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Human error remains as a major contributor to railway incidents and accidents. Its consequences are not only evident at the front end of railway operations such as those involving train drivers, signallers and shunters, but also within the complex organisations of individuals responsible for controlling, supporting and managing the railway system. In recent years human factors has emerged in the Rail Industry throughout the world as one of the most integral features of rail operations considerations. In most cases of human error the negative effects do not manifest into an incident because good training, appropriate standards, safe working rules and procedures, combined with the protection afforded from a modern signalling system is effective in mitigating errors and ensuring safe and efficient operations. There are however, times where certain types of operations present much higher risk, such as the working of track vehicles during testing and commissioning, maintenance periods, degraded operations or emergencies that warrant the passenger and crew evacuation of a train. This kind of working depends more upon human action than engineering controls to achieve a safe and successful outcome of the task to be performed. Data available through Australian and international regulatory bodies indicates that human error is a contributory cause of the majority of incidents in the rail industry. Identifying an occurrence as being attributable to human error is of little value unless we are able to determine âwhyâ the cause was one of human error. Not until we have identified the reasons for human error are we able to effectively design and implement remedial and preventative measures. This paper discusses the successes and potential pitfalls in the management of rail human error through the use of real case studies based on recent experiences. These case studies combine actual operational practice and human factors engineering skills gained across the rail industry sectors. The paperâs dominant theme is the effect of workload on controller performance, while discussing various human factor analysis techniques and the application of Threat and Error Management, which at the conclusion, form the foundation upon which a number of good practice initiatives are based.
