Title Worksite protection systems in urban rail: organisational factors and human capabilities
 
Author Fletcher, C
Gliddon, V
Butt, B
Hely, M
Affiliation Ergonomie Australia Pty Ltd
Sydney Trains
Group Rail
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract The increasing demand in recent times on Australian passenger rail operations to provide higher frequency services over greater portions of their daily timetables has placed significant pressure on safety-critical support activities such as infrastructure maintenance. Fewer and shorter maintenance windows have resulted in significant workload pressures at both organisational and individual levels. These, in turn, may adversely affect the capacity of operators to allocate sufficient time, personnel and resources to the careful planning needed for high level worksite protection such as possessions, and lead to the use of lower level forms of protection. This paper examines the proposition that organisational workload manifests in a similar, yet up-scaled, manner to excessive workload at the individual level through practices and behaviours that may compromise safety through otherwise well-intentioned efforts to provide expedient and economical maintenance services, and explores whether enough is being done to keep track workers safe. Data will be presented from worksite incidents investigation records, qualitative feedback from subject matter experts and anecdotal evidence which point to the nature and extent of these worksite protection demands, responses and limitations. The opportunities to interpret organisational-level work demands in terms of workload identification and management, and proposed solutions to these emerging risks and issues will be discussed.

Title Crew scheduling for railways with Carmen systems
 
Author Andersson, E
Hjorring, C
Drott, J
Affiliation Carmen Systems AB, Gothenburg, Sweden
Swedish State Railways, Stockholm, Sweden
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract For many years large airlines have used Operations Research techniques, along with increasingly powerful computer systems to help schedule their operating crew. This has resulted in significant cost savings, and is vital in today's competitive environment. The techniques and systems have now developed sufficiently so that they can be applied to the larger scheduling problems of major railway companies. In this paper we focus on the planning stage of crew scheduling, specifically the planning of trips for crew personnel, and look in depth at the Carmen Pairing System. Carmen Pairing is used by most of the major European airlines, and has been delivered to the Swedish State Railways (SJ). We shall identify the features that allow this system to be applied to a variety of planning problems, and comment specifically on customizations required for the railways. A key feature is the Carmen Rule System, which provides an easy to use dialog between the user and the system. This is very important in the heterogeneous European environment since it allows the users to easily customize a Carmen system to their specific problems. The user can specify all costs and the rules governing legality of the trips. The system also includes an interactive module where the user can make manual changes to the schedule, print user defined reports, and analyze using different graphical views. The user can control all input and easily try out different scenarios by making simulated changes to the timetable, costs and other parameters. Since the number of tasks to be planned for a railway is usually much larger than for an airline, special efforts have been made to further improve the performance of Carmen Pairing. As one example of this we report on an investigation into the parallelization of the pairing generator on a network of workstations. Parallelization on this architecture provides shorter execution times without requiring a large investment in computing hardware. The shorter execution time will allow SJ to further shorten their planning cycle, and thus respond more quickly to market demands. Initial tests at SJ show that Carmen Pairing is able to significantly improve on manually created solutions with a reduction in the excess costs of up to 5%.

Title L'adequation entre les aptitudes des individus et les exigences des metiers, une amelioration de la fiabilite humaine
 
Author Effertz, P
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In French Railways, Security is the most important preoccupation. The Company sets up researches to reduce the probability of technological or/and human errors. To do so, the Psychological Department works on the measure of the human aptitudes and how they fit the job. Then, the Department create and use tests. The reliability of the evaluation depends on the strictness of the construct method of test. Generally, this process consists in six stages. First, we have to analyse the job. Thus, we can expect the aptitude to succeed in the activities of this work. For example, concerning the engine driver, we have observed cognitive aptitudes, psychomotor aptitudes and personality area. Second, we have to study the researches about these aptitudes. For the cognitive, we use the factorial intelligence theory. For the psychomotor, we use the attention and lateralisation theory. For the personality, we use the Big Five Theory. Third, we create the items. To do so, we refer to the theory and we choose also items which exist or items to create following the procedure of construct. For cognitive tests, we take Multiple choice items, concrete actions form for the psychomotor and binary choice questions for personality. Fourth, the experimentation consists in passing the test to the referent population and check validities. In this step, we select the items. We use statistical analysis to estimate the validities: construct, content, and face validity. Fifth, we standardise the results to help the users (psychologists only) for interpretation. Sixth, we checked the professional validity: comparison between predictive evaluation and success in the job. If we respect these steps described in this poster, we can measure the human aptitudes and evaluate the appropriateness between a person and a job. In other terms, we improve the human reliability. This procedure must follow the evolution of technology and jobs. We improve the human reliability with this evaluation but to reduce more too the security errors, we need not only human, but organizational and managerial studies. Now, the human factors must have their own place in a Railways Compagny.

Title Analytic tools for human factors evaluation of new in-cab and train control technologies
 
Description Background:
Rapid advancements in train control and in-cab technologies provide opportunities for rail operators to improve efficiency and enhance operations. However, new technologies are a significant form of risk. Neglect of human factors issues can mean that new technologies have unintended consequences in the work environment (particularly in the more challenging situations) leading to either rejection by users or to situations where the job is made more difficult rather than easier.
Objective:
This project aims to develop a toolkit of methods that human factors specialists can use to evaluate new technologies before they are implemented in trains and other safety critical train operation related environments.
Outcomes:
This project will provide the Australian rail industry with a standardised method to evaluate the human factors issues associated with introducing new technology. There is a high probability that this project will reduce the costs associated with introducing technology with significant usability issues.
Benefits:
Developing such a toolkit of methods has the potential to reduce the risk associated with introducing new technologies. It is expected that the outputs of this research will become an industry standard for the assessment of new technologies.
Project number or code R2.102
Status Completed
 
 
 
Research Project
 
Title Simulator scenarios for accelerated training #2: the acquisition of driving strategy and technique
 
Description The main goal of this study was to develop a model and software to accurately predict rail-track temperatures 24 hours in advance, in order to provide data that can be used in managing rail buckling. Three major tasks were undertaken to achieve this goal: designing field experimentation and procedures; developing a multivariate regression model of rail-track temperatures; and verifying the regression model against measured rail temperatures. The researchers concluded that Australian Bureau of Meteorology NWP ACCESS-A* data can be used to accurately predict rail temperatures 24 to 48 hours in advance, using a software code developed in MATLAB. Data produced in this way could give rail-industry operators at least one day’s advance notice of when rail temperatures may become critical. This has the potential to deliver substantial benefits to operators and their customers.
Project number or code R2.112
Status Completed
 
 
 
Research Project
 
Title Evaluation of simulators in train driver training
 
Project leader Lydia Kavanagh
Description The proposal covers the post-scoping phases of the Project P4.101 Driver Performance Monitoring and Feedback Systems. Whilst a great deal of work has been done from a Human Factors perspective on the design of simulators, there have been very few studies of transfer of learning outside the simulator or on the implementation of simulators in organisational contexts. The consensus of opinion amongst the industry members of the project steering committee is that simulators are under-used. Hence, it is important that the industry indentify how best to use simulators to improve driver performance. This project aims to identify what simulator-based training is most effective for, and how training organisations need to organise themselves to make the most effective use of simulators. This project follows on from P4.101 (Scoping Current Practices in Driver Performance Monitoring and Feedback).
Project number or code P4.103
Conducting organisation University of Queensland
Project leader contact details Ph: (07) 3365 4264
l.kavanagh@uq.edu.au
Project commencement 1 March 2009
Expected completion date 29 February 2012
Status Completed
Outputs The project benefits include improved safety and reduced operational costs, more efficient and effective use of simulators, better and more consistently trained drivers, and savings on training through efficiently targeting simulator use.
Outcomes While simulators are widely agreed to be useful training tools, especially in preparing drivers for degraded conditions, no information currently exists about: what particular skills should be targeted for simulator training; exactly what role simulators should play in the whole training package; or how in-simulator training compares logistically and financially with other methods of training. This project aims to address these issues, thereby delivering increased efficiency in the use of simulators and better training results, with obvious benefits for industry.

Title Appraising the design, deployment and evaluation of leadership and management development in the rail industry to develop a common framework for building future capability and competence
 
Project leader Tom Stehlik
Description The need for this project has arisen from the findings of the scoping project, P4.102 that examined the existing practices used when developing the capability of leaders and managers. Although there was evidence of management training taking place, the scoping report concluded that much more needs to be known about how the specific learning curriculum of management development is determined and how capability is assessed and evaluated against the needs of rail. The report also suggested that more in-depth and quantitative research was needed to gain in-depth knowledge on the range of management capabilities currently being developed in the rail industry. The key objective of this project is to seek harmonisation on leadership and management development (L&MD) within the rail industry through the uptake of a leadership capability framework. First, research will identify and understand how leaders and managers in rail are currently developed in terms of program goals, learning content, delivery mechanisms and alignment with business needs. Comparisons will be made with international findings on leadership and management development. Secondly, a conceptual model will be developed to evaluate the strengths and limitations of current approaches. Finally, in consulation with stakeholders, a capability framework will be developed to provide a strategic platform on which future leadership and management development can be based.
Project number or code P4.104
Conducting organisation University of South Australia
Project leader contact details Ph: (08) 8302 5421
tom.stehlik@unisa.edu.au
Project commencement 1 April 2009
Expected completion date 31 March 2011
Status Completed
Outputs The research will contribute to make the rail sector an 'industry of choice' for the next generation of leaders and managers. It will enable rail executives to learn what L&MD capabilities are being developed currently, how participants view the relevance of this training in regard to their work roles and validate in-house L&MD programs. Furthermore it will provide guidance for company HR practitioners and external training providers on strategies for developing leaders and managers in rail.
Outcomes The expected outcomes are to: evaluate the effectiveness of existing L&MD programs in terms of their relevance to business needs, individual learning requirements, effective design criteria and methods of learning delivery; develop a comprehensive capability framework of competencies to inform and guide rail leadership and management development programs; identify strategies on how rail leaders and managers might benchmark their capability in relation to other managers.
