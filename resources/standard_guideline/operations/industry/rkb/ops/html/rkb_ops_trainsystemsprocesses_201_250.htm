Lanselle, R
Thiounn, M

Abstract Stimulated by the scarcity and the increasing price of energy, the European Union and the top-level French meeting on the environment (le Grenelle de l'Environnement) tend to make the most important companies aware of energy saving and trading. Receptive to this demand, and even if rail transport is considered as a clean transport mode, the SNCF group (French National Railway Company) proposes new sustainable development approaches in order to improve its energy performance, in particular through the Department of the Electric Traction of the Direction of the Engineering (IG-TE) which, within the context of its designer's role of installation of electric traction, is a driving element in the development of innovations. Even though the use of "clean" energy electricity combined to the latest years' technical evolutions, enable transportation systems to develop more efficiently and ecologically. SNCF is still a big energy consumer and electrical traction represents 90% of the whole SNCF energy consumption. This energy consumption must be optimised for a more energy efficient railway. That is why the SNCF Engineering Department and SNCF Research Department are looking at every option: the study for a better use of braking energy is one of the solutions. To work, the electric rolling stock needs a power supply network corresponding to their drive chain. Linked with the High-voltage network, the electric traction substations convert the energy with transformer-rectifier groups to supply with the 1500V in direct current. The energy forwarded by catenary is consumed by motors during the driving phases. Conversely, during the braking phases, the traction motors can send back into the catenary the energy produced during these braking phases. The installations on the 25 000V alternative power supply network allow naturally the recovery of the braking energy. On the RFF's current 1500V power supply network, this recovered energy can be consumed only by the train's cruisers in the driving phase. However there are applications of small powers under 750V in direct current (Subway or Tramway types for instance) which allow the storage of this energy by ultracapacitors or flywheels. For installations with heavy powers under 1500V (typical Regional Express Train, Intercity), which use the unidirectional structure of the converters used in substation, the absence of systems of recovery or trains cruisers implies that electrical energy cannot go back through the power supply network during the braking phases of trains. The advantages of the electric traction for the exploitation of the railway lines with high traffic are widely recognized. To continue to develop this transport by improving energy efficiency, modern rolling stock is equipped with a system which allows the return of electrical energy during braking. It represents a possibility to avoid the wasting of already produced energy and so, to decrease electrical consumption. The introduction of a new system named 'Active Substation' which uses new generation components not only allows the recovery of energy, but also complementary functions.

Abstract A big challenge for GB rail is to develop a modern, sustainable system that is accessible and easy for passengers to use. This has been documented in the UK Department for Transport's 2007 White Paper, but is also reinforced in the rail industry's own strategic agenda developed by the cross industry Technical Strategy Leadership Group (TSLG), to halve cost and carbon while doubling capacity and maximising customer satisfaction. It's in this context that RSSB conducted a research project on driver advisory information on behalf of the cross-industry Vehicle/Train Control & Communications System Interface Committee (VTC&C SIC), to support industry's goals to become more sustainable. The benefits from implementation of a Driver Advisory System (DAS) include reductions in energy consumption by avoiding unnecessary braking and running at reduced speed whilst maintaining ontime arrival. Operational benefits include reduced train delays and better utilisation of track capacity by running through junctions and station approaches at higher speeds whilst reducing maintenance costs as a result of reduced brake wear. There are also potential safety improvements through fewer red signals approached if the DAS is effectively implemented across the network. The research developed and analysed the generic functionality of a driver advisory system. In addition to engineering considerations and analysis, the work was underpinned by risk assessment and human factors assessment to ensure that information would be provided in a user-friendly way and to ensure safety would not be compromised. This analysis considered the factors that would need to be included to produce a robust and detailed business case that estimates the costs of implementation and the potential for reduced energy consumption and potential traffic performance improvements. The paper summarises the main research activities including human factors and safety analysis, the definition of data interface requirements, the evaluation of benefits of a proposed system using simulation software, a review of the timetable for operational factors such as allowances and hidden slack, and assessment of alternative system architectures based on driver advice via Global System for Mobile Communications - Railway (GSM-R) text messaging. The results from the simulations have demonstrated that substantial energy savings are possible from a driver advisory system based on the pre-planned timetable and that a safety benefit is delivered through a reduction in red signal approaches. However, the simulation shows that there is a small increase in lateness of trains arriving at timing points. The research has identified the need for a business case before the introduction of a driver advisory system considering factors such as performance impact, energy saving, greenhouse gas emissions and the cost of implementation.

Following this, a research project was commissioned for the GB rail industry's Operations Focus Group, to document its collective understanding of the approaches, benefits and risks presented by efficient driving as well as case studies from individual operators. The project was managed as part of the rail industry?s research and development programme, funded primarily by the UK Department for Transport, and managed by RSSB on industry and government's behalf. Title Metamaterial for trainborne antenna integration and reduction of EMI between onboard systems in the railway environment
 
Subtitle Meeting the challenges for future mobility: proceedings
Author Seetharamdoo, D
Castiblanco JA
Berbineau, M
Affiliation IFSTTAR, LEOST, Villeneuve d?Ascq, France
Conference 9th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR) May 22-26 2011, Lille, France
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Minimising the energy consumption of railway vehicles is nowadays a real economic and technological challenge. Weight reduction using composites for the vehicle structure is one of the solutions currently considered. Indeed, composite materials offer high mechanical characteristics for an overall lighter structure. These composites are however not designed for an optimal electromagnetic (EM) characteristics and their impact on the performance of on-board wireless communication systems requiring a high level of reliability is not negligible. Equipping railway vehicles with a wireless communication system is thus getting more complicated. Another constraint is the increasing number of wireless systems on board and antenna integration on such platforms is becoming a real challenge. Recent research results in the field of wave-matter interaction and antenna technology suggest the design of wireless systems antennas accounting for the EM characteristic of the surrounding environment by using metamaterials. Metamaterials are artificial EM composites which can be tailored for required properties. They provide, for instance, the same reflective properties as metals but with a much lower density inline with the weight reduction constraint of railway vehicles. Research on metamaterials is a promising and active field of research and its application to railway systems is new. To date and to the authors' knowledge, the first research activities in this field in the railway industry are performed in the framework of the METAPHORT project initiated by IFSTTAR, funded by the French ANR-VTT program and supported by the ITRANS railway cluster. The aim of this project is to address the relevant EM bottlenecks to enable a first technological transfer of metamaterials to the railway industry. The main expected result is a better management of EM pollution from the design level of wireless systems such that in the long term, EM field exposure of public transport passengers will be reduced without decreasing the level of service and the number of wireless onboard systems. There are several existing techniques to manage EMI between onboard systems such as antenna processing schemes but these techniques do not contribute to reduce EM pollution. They can only ensure a better signal processing in the context EM interference. However, a metamaterial solution, by proposing a higher degree of freedom for the design of antennas or wireless devices, can contribute to a more efficient multiple-system management while accounting for a lower net EM pollution. Specific, user-defined spatial or frequency filtering can be realised beyond the fundamental limits of natural materials. This approach can allow, for instance, to compensate or to take advantage of the materials surrounding a wireless system to propose novel antenna solutions which can be integrated directly into the vehicle body. Different methodologies will be developed within the railway industry constraints such as: (i) EM material and environment characterisation at high frequencies, (ii) Design methods for metamaterial antenna for integration on railway vehicles, (iii) Numerical and physical models and laboratory test methods for metamaterial structures integration on large structures to restrict the number of in-situ tests. This paper is organised as follows. In section 2, the METAPHORT project is described in terms of its objectives and methodologies which will be developed. The scope of the project is defined and the research work required to attempt a technological transfer of the metamaterial technology to the railway industry is explained. In section 3, the focus is mainly on the antenna design and integration methodology developed in the context of this project. The introduction of metamaterials in the proposed methodology is described. Finally in section 4, the first results obtained on this topic are presented.

Abstract This work deals with the experimental characterization of the spectrum of electromagnetic radiated emissions (RE) of moving trains. Sources with this type of broad band character could be emphasized in the blend and, potentially, also artificial sound could be added to create masking effects or change the character. In the studio, the different scenarios can be listened to and also the cost impact and constraints for different design solutions can be indicated. A demonstration case of a sound studio prototype displayed at the Innotrans fair 2010 is described in the paper.
