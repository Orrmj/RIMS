Abstract This document is the deliverable of Milestone 1 of the project R2.111 Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS) for Safer Level Crossings. It provides information about possible technological solutions to reduce the number of crashes on RLXs. Title Automatic classification of ballast condition using GPR
 
Author Su, L-J
Rujikiatkamjorn, C
Indraratna, B
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No R3.102
Publisher CRC for Rail Innovation
Place Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2013
Physical Description 14p. ; PDF
Abstract This report presents the applications of Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) and multi-channel analysis of surface wave (MASW) techniques to the assessment of ballast fouling conditions. GPR data collected from the laboratory model track was further processed and presented. The dielectric permittivity of ballast in each section was calculated and used to evaluate the ballast fouling condition. An automatic classification method based on frequency characteristics of the GPR signal using support vector machines was developed. The classification rate of the method is very high. Multi-channel analysis of the surface wave (MASW) survey was conducted on the laboratory model track. The shear modulus was measured using the MASW technique to evaluate the ballast fouling condition and a critical fouling point (CFP) was defined with respect to the shear stiffness and drainage. Beyond the CFP, the shear stiffness and permeability of the ballast decreased substantially, which meant the ballast should be cleaned as soon as possible.

Abstract Ballast performance is a major contributing factor to track reliability and the annual cost of maintaining track geometry to an acceptable standard that provides a significant commercial return is a major issue for rail operators. Ballast degradation as well as the infiltration of fine particles such as coal and soft subgrade reduces drainage, which leads to unfavourable track deformation and high maintenance costs. Railway tracks are also subjected to two types of loads: (i) conventional cyclic loads, and (ii) occasional high impact loads due to wheel and track imperfections. This project was aimed at providing an integrated ballast-formation-track design and analysis addressing these two important issues of ballast fouling and degradation of ballast under high impact loads. In order to address these issues, large-scale hydraulic conductivity tests, triaxial tests and drop hammer tests were conducted. Numerical analyses were conducted using SEEP/W and PLAXIS. This report presents results of large-scale laboratory tests, predictions of finite element packages and applications of a supplementary method of analysis for railway tracks (SMART) tool.

Abstract Railway bridges are lifelong assets that deteriorate with age, use and poor maintenance practice and the rail industry is suffering from their ever-increasing maintenance costs and continued budget constraints. Maintenance optimisation is the key to treating such issues. Based on that, this report suggests a comprehensive procedure for maintenance optimisation by considering structural adequacy and remaining service potential while prioritising the maintenance actions by considering costs of material, plant and labour along with budget constraints and risk. Title Rail flash-butt welding for heavy haul applications
 
Author Phelan, D
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No HH9
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2018-10
Physical Description 126p. ; PDF
Abstract Flash-butt welding (FBW) uses a combination of electrical heating and mechanical upsetting to produce high quality welded joints, and is used to produce the majority of rail joints in the Australian heavy haul rail industry. In response to demand for increased rail performance, rail suppliers have recently developed new rail steel grades that allow for greater axle loads, reduced wear and greater fatigue resistance. While the use of FBW for the joining of rails is well established in the heavy haul rail industry, the metallurgical response of these new rail steel compositions to the thermo-mechanical cycle of the FBW process is not well understood at present. If the FBW process is not appropriately applied, the performance benefits gained from using the new compositions will be lost to unnecessarily degraded performance at the weld joints
The key aims of this project were to better understand the metallurgical response of the new rail steels to the FBW process as employed in the heavy haul industry, and to provide guidelines for optimizing the process to achieve welded joints with the best possible performance. To produce and to use these guidelines, a number of different aspects need to be integrated.
1) In-depth knowledge of the phase transformations undergone by the advanced head-hardened rail (HHR) grades can be encapsulated by continuous cooling transformation (CCT) curves, together with data concerning concentrations of deleterious phases as a function of cooling rate.
2) Measurement of the temperature profiles during qualification trials can be performed with appropriately applied monitoring equipment. This will enable decisions to be made regarding the suitability of the welding program for producing temperature profiles, particularly cooling rates, that will avoid creating poor microstructures in the weld zone and heat affected zone (HAZ).
3) This same information can be used to assess whether the process has a sufficiently wide safe operating envelope, so that the process that is applied in the field is tolerant of typical changes in conditions.
4) The variations in chemical composition of rail steels can markedly alter the melting point. Significant differences in melting temperatures may occur not just between steels from different manufacturers using different compositions, but also between steels of the same nominal type if the manufacturerâs compositional tolerance allows for large changes in carbon and silicon contents.
5) In either case, shifts in melting temperature produce a measurable change in the force needed to produce the required upset. Where melting temperature differences between two dissimilar rails are not large, the weld program can be adjusted to produce the correct amount of heating so that upset force can be maintained in the acceptable range, while achieving the desired displacement for a void-free joint.
6) Where there are large differences in melting temperatures of dissimilar rails, it may not be possible to achieve sound welds unless an intermediate material is used a buffer.
7) The proposed guidelines are not intended in any way as a substitute for existing qualification procedures, particularly bend test requirements and metallographic examination.
This document provides technical detail in the areas listed above, with the objective of creating a guideline that will be useful for industry in optimising the FBW process to produce reliable, high quality welds using the new rail steel grades. As the proposed guideline is applied in practice and additional information becomes available, it is expected that improvements and refinements will follow.

Title Drone track inspection: assessing suitability of drones for heavy haul track inspection
 
Corporate Author Central Queensland University, Centre for Railway Engineering (CRE)
Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No HH15
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2019-04
Physical Description 32p. ; PDF
Abstract The desire for continual safety improvements and increases in economic efficiency are the catalysts driving research into alternative approaches to conduct visual track inspections. This report discusses the feasibility of using a commercially available, small (under 7kg), unmanned, remotely piloted aircraft (drone) to capture track images for the automated identification of track defects using machine vision technology in a specific and challenging geographic environment. The selected drone was equipped with a 20 Megapixel camera, a precision GPS system and followed the track geometry using predetermined waypoints. Images were taken at different heights above the track and at different times of the day (to identify changes in shadow pattern ranges). The drone captured hundreds of images at 10m, 15m and 20m above the tracks. The images taken at 10m are very clear and show the rail clips in the highest resolution. The images from 20m are clear and the rail clips are visible, albeit not as easy to distinguish due to poor resolution, dust and heat shimmer. A machine vision system was trained to recognise missing Pandrol sleeper clips from these track images. The system was developed using three steps: Hough transformation to detect rail, the Support Vector Machine (SVM) tool to identify the area where a clip should be found and a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to detect the presence of clips. Automatic clip detection in the railway environment has been examined based on the set of images obtained during field trials. The results of the combined processes indicate that missing clips can be detected with an accuracy greater than 95% under standard track conditions. A preliminary cost analysis comparing drone and traditional hi-rail track inspections has been presented.

Title National low-cost level crossing trial program: affordable level crossings: final report: volume 1 of 2
 
Author Larue, G
Wullums, C
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No R3.122
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2015-12
Physical Description 106p. ; PDF
Abstract Level crossings continue to represent a significant safety concern for railway operators in Australia. Approximately a third of all rail-related fatalities during the ten year period between 2000 and 2009 'occurred as a result of collisions between road vehicles and trains at level crossings. During this period there were a total of 695 collisions that resulted in 97 fatalities. These figures exclude suicides and pedestrian fatalities at level crossings. Due to the large expanse of land traversed by Australian railways, there are in excess of 23,500 level crossings of which approximately 21% have active protection (flashing lights, boom barriers) and 8,838 interface with public roads. While the risk at low-exposure level crossings with passive controls may not be significant, collectively they contribute to a substantial proportion of operating risk. Approximately 45% of collisions (312) occurred at level crossings with passive controls between 2000 and 2009, the majority occurring at public level crossings. The cost of upgrading public level crossings with passive controls (approximately 5,900) based on a 2012 lifecycle costing estimate of current type-approved level crossing technology, would exceed AU$3.25 Billion. Low-cost level crossing warning technology has the potential to provide railway operators with a cost-effective control that can significantly improve safety.
The lower cost enables rail operators to upgrade more level crossings for a given budget, which may result in a larger and earlier safety benefits than incremental upgrades using existing level crossing warning devices. The Australian industry has been investigating low-cost level crossings for more than 20 years. In 1989, the Victorian coroner, G. Johnstone, recommended the urgent consideration and implementation of low-cost alternatives for protecting level crossings. Since then, the rail industry has struggled to move forward on the adoption of such alternatives, confronted by technical and legal obstacles.
A national approach was needed to progress such work and, at the request of the rail industry, a low cost level crossing national trial programme was developed. This is that project. Technologies from three suppliers were installed at sites hosted by railways in Queensland, New South Wales and Victoria. These systems were trialled in shadow-mode (installed in parallel with existing level crossing warning systems based on track circuits, without operating the road user Interface). The systems evaluated, provided comparative performance data without affecting the safety of the rail level crossings.
The results of this project aimed to provide information for the development and introduction of Low Cost Level Warning Devices that meet existing safety standards.

Abstract Level crossings continue to represent a significant safety concern for railway operators in Australia. Approximately a third of all rail-related fatalities during the ten year period between 2000 and 2009 'occurred as a result of collisions between road vehicles and trains at level crossings. During this period there were a total of 695 collisions that resulted in 97 fatalities. These figures exclude suicides and pedestrian fatalities at level crossings. Due to the large expanse of land traversed by Australian railways, there are in excess of 23,500 level crossings of which approximately 21% have active protection (flashing lights, boom barriers) and 8,838 interface with public roads. While the risk at low-exposure level crossings with passive controls may not be significant, collectively they contribute to a substantial proportion of operating risk. Approximately 45% of collisions (312) occurred at level crossings with passive controls between 2000 and 2009, the majority occurring at public level crossings. The cost of upgrading public level crossings with passive controls (approximately 5,900) based on a 2012 lifecycle costing estimate of current type-approved level crossing technology, would exceed AU$3.25 Billion. Low-cost level crossing warning technology has the potential to provide railway operators with a cost-effective control that can significantly improve safety.
The lower cost enables rail operators to upgrade more level crossings for a given budget, which may result in a larger and earlier safety benefits than incremental upgrades using existing level crossing warning devices. Abstract Under heavy axle loads, the bending and thermal stresses in rails are sufficiently high that any small notch or other damage in the foot of the rail will result in a high cycle fatigue crack initiation and growth. Despite significant technological advancements made towards the safety of trains and the associated strategies of railway track maintenance, rail foot flaws resulting in breakages of rails still occur. In the worst case scenario, such failures can lead to major train derailments. Early detection of rail foot flaws will make it possible for the rail maintenance crews to take timely action to avoid such disastrous situations. Although there has been extensive research into various aspects of railâs âpermanent way,â there has been little centred on rail foot flaw detection despite its obvious contribution to rail breakage. Hence, new technologies for detecting rail foot flaws are urgently required in order to increase the performance and safety of the railway operations via reducing the risks of broken rails and the potentially disastrous consequences arising from derailments. This report delivers a comprehensive review of state-of-the-art technologies relevant to rail foot flaw detection, giving emphasis to their use in detection of rail foot flaw defects at practical inspection vehicle speeds. The review not only looks at the research being carried out but also investigates the commercial products available for rail flaw detection and their applicability for a moving vehicle rail foot flaw detection system. As the outcome of the review, the report reveals the fact that there are no reliable off-the-shelf rail foot flaw detection technologies suitable for being adopted in a moving vehicle rail foot flaw detection system manufactured by commercial vendors. It continues further to identify the methods suitable to be adopted in a moving vehicle rail foot flaw detection system. Finally the report presents a strategic direction for future research in developing a reliable moving vehicle rail foot flaw detection system by combining some of the rail flaw detection methodologies that it identified as suitable for being used in rail foot flaw detection.
