Title Rail as a tourism tool
 
Author Annells, R
Affiliation Tourism Victoria
Conference AusRAIL 97, Rail Solutions for Modern Cities, 5-7 November 1997, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The development of national rail systems over the past century are synonymous with the opening of frontiers and nation building. This process, which has been repeated in several countries, has also demonstrated the close relationship between the quality and diversity of transport infrastructure and the rate of tourism development. The viability of a rail network depends on many factors more significant than tourism. In Australia's case, most State rail systems were in place by the early 1900s but the development of inter-State and transcontinental passenger services have lagged well behind. It is remarkable that the first standard rail link between Sydney and Perth was competed as recently as 1962, the inaugural transcontinental service begun in 1970, and the first national rail management and marketing body formed in 1975. The relative slowness in developing inter-State and transcontinental passenger services has implications for how rail tourism might be developed into the future.

Title Behavioral response to promotion-based public transport demand management: longitudinal analysis and implications for optimal promotion design
 
Author Ma, Z
Koutsopoulos, HN
Liu, T
Basu, AA
Affiliation Public Transport Research Group, Department of Civil Engineering, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Northeastern University, Boston, USA
Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Cambridge, USA
Journal Transportation Research Part A: Policy and Practice
Publication Date 2020-11
Abstract Increasing ridership in the urban rail systems in major cities is outpacing their designed capacity. Promotion based demand management can facilitate better utilization of the available capacity of the existing system when the investment and opportunity to expand the system are limited. While several studies address short-term behavioral responses to such promotions using before and after analysis, how behavioral changes are sustained in the long run is also very important as well as differences in response among different user groups. Using an extensive smart card dataset over two years from Hong Kongâs urban heavy railway system, this paper explores the longitudinal behavior of passengers in response to a promotion aiming at changing passengersâ travel period from peak to the pre-peak. The approach uses customer segmentation to understand the heterogeneous response of different groups. Users who have high flexibility in schedule and familiarity with the system and travel long distances tend to be more likely to change their travel periods to take advantage of the discount. The longitudinal promotion analysis reveals that 35â40% of passengers who initially adopted the promotion will eventually revert to their previous travel time periods. The results suggest that the promotion designs should be adjusted/renewed over time to sustain the initial response given the attrition of early adopters. Based on the behavioral analysis, an âoptimalâ promotion design approach is applied to examine the effectiveness of promotion strategies given different behavioral responses over time, heterogeneous group behavior, and constraints on the investment budgets and performance requirements. The promotion design using group-specific response can better target price-sensitive users, hence improves its effectiveness over time, while the design based on the average response shows a significant performance decrease. However, the optimal design schemes using different behavioral responses are relatively consistent in terms of the selected stations for promotion, though some differences exist in the discount levels and time periods for the areas where there can be more early morning travelers. From a design perspective, there is not much difference in the promotion effectiveness regardless of the behavioral response assumed for the design.

Abstract The Young Persons Railcard, Family Railcard and Senior Railcard have been available for many years on Britain's railways, with an upfront cost of Â£20 for the Railcard allowing a 34% discount on rail travel for one year subject to certain restrictions. The Railcards are known to generate off-peak travel, and therefore expanding their take-up can create additional off peak revenue and use up spare capacity on the railways. The optimum formulation of the Railcards has never been analysed.
