Title The Parisian suburb project
 
Author Didier, M
Pinson, J
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract In the early 70's, the transportation companies of the Parisian suburban area decided to have an automatic fare collection (AFC) system based on the use of an Edmonson type paper ticket with a magnetic stripe. Because of counterfeiting problems and of the end of the life span of some AFC machines, it has been decided to switch to another technological system - smart cards - which better copes with the customers' needs (see [1] and [2] ).Since March 1997 and for about 18 months, the Parisian transport authority (STP) has been performing an experiment with clients in order to test the two following scenarios: pre debit; This is what is working right now in all the transportation companies. Every time a client wants to travel, he must tell where he wants to go and the company takes the exact fare at the entrance of the network. It is well fitted for open networks. post debit; This time, the client must validate the ticket >> at the entrance and at the exit so that the exact fare can be calculated at the exit and taken then. This is particularly well fitted for close networks. Each scenario has its own advantages and drawbacks and thus the purpose of the experiment is to see which one is better accepted by the customers. All transport operators - that is bus organisations ADATRIF and APTR, subway company RATP and the French railway company SNCF - are involved in the experiment. The main targets are the season ticket holders and frequent travellers which could have in the future a prepaid smart card with a season ticket and a money reserve dedicated to public transport. The contactless smart card is currently designed by INNOVATRON and takes into account the pre and post debit scenarios. Two different <<objects>> will be given to the recruited clients for the experiment. They will hold either an ISO contactless smart card or a smart card with contacts inserted into a wallet which can communicate with a distant validation machine and which can also give some information thanks to a small LCD screen on it. The main goal of this experiment is to have the maximum of information on the customers' acceptance. While testing the different scenarios, half a dozen working groups will study other aspects of the project which should concern at the end more than 4 millions of travellers. Thus, at the end of 1998, the final specifications should be written for a call for tender.

Title What is the future of ticketing with the emergence of the universal electronic purse?
 
Author Didier, M
Pinson, J
Affiliation SNCF, Paris, France
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract The border line between ticketing and the universal electronic purse is very thin and mainly depends on the customers' habits in the transportation activities. One must consider the frequency of use of the transportation system - highly frequent travellers, frequent travellers, occasional travellers - and the kind of travel made - from home to the office, leisure, business... - in order to precisely define the requirements of a new tool taking into account the ticketing and the payment relations. In order to cope with the customers' needs and the company's requirements, SNCF is considering switching from paper tickets to smart cards in all the urban and suburban traffic activities. Even though the final choices are not made yet, this represents an important step in the new commercial relations between a transportation company and its clients. Multimodality is thus the key word of what customers are expecting and SNCF is willing to offer. In that case, the clients will have access to several services and also several providers belonging to more than one industry sector. For this reason, at least in France, a financial institution must manage the purse system. Both parties, that is transportation companies and banks, can take advantage of the market of the other one. An electronic purse cannot be profitable without the numerous transactions made in transport, and a transportation money reserve would not be as satisfactory for clients if a universal electronic purse was developed.

Title SIPAX - the new information system of the Italian state railways
 
Author Ascoli, R
Fascioli, S
Affiliation Ferrovie dello Stato S.p.A., Roma, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract SIPAX is a new Passenger Information System projected to integrate management of all the phases associate to issue of passenger raiway services. With SIPAX Italian State Railways (FS) will achieve a better implementation of their ticketing, booking and information systems. Besides, our company could supply accessory services through different channels, both internal (ticket offices, information offices, etc.) and external (travel agents', foreign railway companies, banks etc.). In brief, the main targets FS want to achieve by operating SIPAX are the following: cut waiting times at the ticket offices; develop information, offer, sales and reservations services, in order to increase market penetration. This will also imply innovations in distribution channels; integrate rail and other operators' services (car-rental, buses, shipping and airline companies, etc.) and also other sale networks (travel agents', Global Distribution Systems, etc.); improve rail traffic information, through the main automatic information channels (i.e. telephone, fax, teletext, Internet, self.service, etc...); simplify back-office accounting operations (accountancy, receipts and payments reports, ticket supply etc...); support FS Management, with regard to marketing strategy options and general administration. The main system features are: integration in a single sale counter of all services related to railway travel: timetable information, ticket issuing, reservations, refunds and also the reservation of services by other operators (hotels, bus, car-rental, etc..); automatic performance of all back-office activities that are currently performed by hand, with a huge waste of time. In 1997 were started up the basic system functions. In particular, it is now possible to implement the integrate functions of ticketing and timetabling information and a new reservation system (TPF) for the EUROSTAR ITALIA trains. In the 1998 other functions (namely: accountancy, receipts and payments report and reservation system TPF for all the services) will be added to the aboves ones.

Taipei, Taiwan
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract Capturing the dynamics in passenger trips and system utilization over time and space is extremely important for railway operators. The conventional railway operator, Taiwan Railways Administration (TRA), provides five types of train services with a number of different stopping patterns through four types of tickets. Each type of tickets has specific characteristics and record in terms of transaction and gate data. Consequently, using ticket transaction and gate data to determine passenger flow and evaluate system utilization is a highly complex task. In this study, we develop a comprehensive process and evaluation framework to map passenger flow and evaluate system utilization. Empirical case study indicates that the developed tool can successfully match passengers with train services to present passenger trips, section utilization, station utilization, and train utilization over time and space. With this tool, passenger trips distribution and service utilization can be efficiently computed and examined; operators can quickly adjust their service strategies accordingly to improve system performance.

Title A high-efficiency method of boarding and alighting for passengers in intercity railway transport of China
 
Author Lv, X
Li, J
Duan, J
Li, J
Wang, Z
Affiliation Institute of Computing Technology, China Academy of Railway Sciences, Beijing 100081, China
China Academy of Railway Sciences, Beijing 100081, China
Source ACRI
Conference 11th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), research and innovation from today towards 2050, 29 May-2 June 2016, Milan, Italy
Publication Date 2016
Abstract The intercity railway travel is attracting more and more passengers with its advantages of safety, environmental and fastness, which alleviates the traffic pressure on the intercity road transport to a large extent. However, there exist some aspects can be improved for the operations of intercity railway transport in China compared with other developed countries. Currently, the boarding and alighting of intercity railway transport for passengers utilize the following way in China, checking tickets at the station entrance, the entrance to platform and the station exit in turn, which consumes huge amounts of manpower and material resources due to the tedious examinations of machine or artificiality. Meanwhile, the tedious examinations lead to a low efficiency and plenty of passengers staying in the station. To improve the efficiency of boarding and alighting for intercity railway transport of China, a new method used to passengers’ boarding and alighting is presented in this paper. In the proposed method, the duty of station is no longer checking tickets for passengers but guides the passengers to board the trains by determining the current number of remaining seats on the train based on the described terminal APP. The process of checking tickets is completed through the self-serve checking on the described on-board ticket examiner when the passengers enter into the carriages. Then the passengers who have completed verification select the desired seats via the on-board ticket examiner or terminal APP. At last, the train attendants only check the identity information of the passengers who have not seat or sit on the seats that should be vacant by the terminal APP. The proposed method for passengers’ boarding and alighting avoids the tedious process of checking tickets in the station and can quickly guides the passengers access to carriages or leave the station, thus the transport efficiency for passengers in the intercity railways is improved. Meanwhile, the proposed method allows the passengers who have the boarding certificate to board the trains at any time during the operation of the trains.

Title A quick cost-effective solution to rail overcrowding: the Melbourne free "early bird" ticket experience
 
Author Currie, G
Affiliation Monash University. Institute of Transport Studies
Source ATRI
Conference Transportation Research Board Annual Meeting, 89th, 2010, Washington, DC, USA
Publication Date 2010
Abstract Rail overcrowding has become endemic for many major cities. Solutions are problematic since they require substantial funding and years to implement. This paper reviews the performance of a new approach implemented in Melbourne, Australia. Called the 'early bird' ticket, the promotion offers passengers free rail travel if trips are completed before 7 am. The program costs $Aust 6M per annum in lost fare revenues and around 8-9,000 passengers use the ticket each weekday. Some 23 per cent of these have shifted the time of travel (around 2,000 to 2,600 passengers) by an average of 42 minutes. This has reduced demand in the peak by between 1.2 per cent and 1.5 per cent from previous levels and is the equivalent of a maximum of 5 average train loads (or 3 per cent of total peak trains). Demand growth during this period has far outweighed this effect so overloading rose after early bird was introduced. Its effect was to reduce the scale of increased overloading. Overall it is unclear to what degree it has acted to reduce overloading. Peak travel in the less critical 7-8 peak hour has been reduced however its impact in the critical 8-9 peak hour is low. However the program pays for itself by providing relief equivalent to 2.5 to 5 peak trains. Economic benefits are likely to be higher. When considering alternatives there is no equivalent measure which could be implemented at such a cost so quickly.

Title Evaluating passenger journey times through smart-card demand modelling
 
Author Aiton, J
Affiliation Public Transport Authority, Perth, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract Today, the information and evaluation tools available to transport planners and operators provide a unique opportunity to gain a better understanding of the demand-side performance of railway systems at a system-wide and individual passenger level. Public transport railways are facing ever growing demand. As a result, there is a drive for continuous technology innovation and an insistence towards more accurate, reliable and detailed information to assist informed decision-making. In order to continue to meet demand in the future, transport authorities need to efficiently utilise available data and software tools to enhance performance evaluation and better inform decisions on how the railway should be structured and operated. This paper presents a methodology for evaluating the demand-side performance through determining passenger journey times using smart-card data and advanced simulation. Smart-cards now allow transport authorities to tap into detailed individual passenger travel data. The value of this data set can be realised by using a constrained capacity analysis at the individual passenger level on a train-by-train basis. This paper defines generalised journey time (GJT) as the key performance measure to evaluate demand performace, and introduces the notion of standardised mean wait time (SMWT) as a technique to quickly identify issues with passengers unable to board services due to capacity constraints. Finally, this paper discusses how GJTs can be calculated through simulation modelling and integrated into utilisation strategies.

This analysis ofMTA's ridership demographics shows that this fare structure is neither efficient, effective, nor equitable, though it does offer some operational simplicity. Journal Transportation Research Part B: Methodological
Publication Date 2021-12
Abstract This study analyzes the scheduling of unpredictable fare inspections in proof-of-payment transit systems, where the transit operator chooses a collection of patrol paths (one for each patrol) every day with some probability in order to avoid any regularity that could be exploited by opportunistic passengers. The authors use a Stackelberg game approach to represent the hierarchical decision-making process between the transit operator and opportunistic passengers, whose decision on whether to evade the fare depends on the inspection probabilities set by the transit operator. Unlike previous work, the authors use an exact formulation of the inspection probabilities that allows them to develop new heuristics for the fare inspection scheduling problem, and to assess their solution quality in terms of their optimality gap.
