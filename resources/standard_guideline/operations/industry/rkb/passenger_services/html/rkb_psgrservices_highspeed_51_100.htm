Title Optimization of the dynamic behavior of the ICE 3 passenger car by numerical simulations
 
Author Troidi, H
Kammerhofer, G
Affiliation IndustrieanIagen-Betriebsgesellschaff mbH, Ottobrunn, Germany
ABB Daimler-Benz Transportation, DonauwÃ¶rth, Germany
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Two cars were considered for a first analysis step within an agreement between Siemens/Adtranz and IABG to promote the optimization of the riding comfort of the future German high speed train ICE 3: The so-called 'Mitteiwagen', a high-capacity coach, and the 'Trafowagen' that was supposed to be very critical due to the heavy transformer. Both cars were modelled with Finite Elements in detail, while the bogie SGP 500 was modelled as a non-linear multi-body system. The analyses first concentrated on the vibrational modes of the car body. It was expected that the lowest eigenfrequency would have to be close to 11 Hz. Even for the less critical 'Mittelwagen' the frequencies were found to be below this limit. To check the consequences with respect to the riding comfort, the accelerations were computed for typical passenger seat locations, simulating rides on different sections of German railway lines, at velocities up to 360 km/h, using the MBS code SIMPACK at IABG, and in-house software at Adtranz. Both simulations showed that the accelerations of the car body were above the limit values. To solve this problem the mode shapes were analysed visually, and with the help of strain energy distributions, to find areas with low local stiffness. Reinforcement measures were discussed, and their validity was checked by sensitivity analyses. More than 50 % of the aluminium extruded sections were finally reinforced by changing the height of the profiles, including additional diagonal elements, straightening curved lines or changing the wall thickness. An additional important uncertainty was the influence of heavy masses, as the 8.5 t transformer, on the dynamic behavior of the car. The results showed that the acceleration values on the floor of the car body were clearly dependent on the coupling spring stiffness. No minimum could be detected for a specific elastic spring stiffness but rather constantly decreasing accelerations when reducing the spring stiffness.

Abstract Axle velocity detection is of greater importance in observing slip/slide phenomena and has more restrictions than in other applications such as elevators, mill controllers of steel industry, etc. that usually use encoders or speed sensors of much higher resolution. So we have to develop a method of velocity detection focused on the wheel slip/slide phenomena, which differs from rotational velocity detection for motor control at a few points. Slip detection and readhesion control is needed mainly under bad wheel-rail condition. We analyzed running tests results with water sprayed between wheel and rail. Analyzing slip phenomena we propose a new practical readhesion control for electric railway vehicles with a three-phase drive. Title The impacts of a high speed train on the geomorphological assessment. The surface hydrographical network: a case study
 
Author Benedetto, A
Affiliation University of Rome, Rome, Italy
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Due to strict development rules, the High Speed Train planning involves areas and environmental balances that affect different spatial scales than those of the preliminary and building stages. Historical data shows that surface hydrographical networks are the most sensitive environmental systems of all. Embankments and cuttings change the direction of surface water, which causes significant runoff variations. The current planning models are sometimes unsuitable and unfit to evaluate the kind of surface hydraulic networks variation, produced by a High Speed Train. This is one of the reasons why the evaluation of the effects of exceptional rainfall events are frequently incorrect. New studies in the field of surface hydrology have substantially proved the direct link between the rainfall-runoff relationship and the surface hydraulic network structure (Eagleson 1978, Rinaldo et alii 1991, Orlandini et alii 1996, Orlandini and Rosso 1996). It has been proven that it is advantageous to adopt advanced computing models to analyze geomorphology. These models are based on electronic databases called Digital Terrain Models (DTM). DTMs allow for detailed studies of environmental impacts. Moreover these databases dynamically update the geolitological and soil use characteristics and the geomorphological data (Moore et alii 1991). Geomorphological data is represented in the Digital Elevation Models (DEM). The surface hydraulic network can be automatically extracted from DEM and the changes of the network produced by a High Speed Train can be evaluated (Band 1986, Chorowicz et alii 1992). This work demonstrates and analyzes the most common and some of the unusual interferences of a High Speed Train on environmental systems. It also shows an experimental case study for the evaluation of the changes of an hydrographical network and of the effects on the basin balance produced from a H.S.T. (Benedetto (2 and 3)1997). The mathematical model is a phisically based distributed rainfall-runoff model. It suggests that it is possible to interface an evaluation of the impacts on geomorphological assessment system with G.I.S. architecture. This hypothesis brings optimism to the future application of this type of integrated systems in the field of linear transport infrastructure planning.

Abstract A satisfying level comfort for passengers of a high speed train, passing through a tunnel, is possible only if the pressure fluctuations inside cars do not exceed the limits for aural discomfort. The air-tightness of the cars is the first requirement to obtain low fluctuations of internal pressure during tunnel passing. The achievement and the maintenance of a sufficient air-tightness increase the life cycle costs of rolling stock, so the level of air-tightness is not to be higher than it needs. But the sealing of cars is not sufficient to assure the attenuation of pressure fluctuations. In fact, pressure waves generated in the tunnel can propagate inside the cars through the ducts of air-conditioning system thus producing high pressure changes. A device to control or to attenuate pressure variations during tunnel passing has to be linked with the air conditioning system. A very simple device for pressure control, at present employed on ETR 460 and on the power cars of ETR 500, is obtained employing, for the ventilating system, turbofans with a very flat flow rate-pressure curve. After a brief description of the system, the paper presents a simplified model to analyze pressure propagation inside cars which considers both the air-tightness of the cars and the effects of the device for pressure control. The model is applied to simulate pressure inside cars using, as input of the model, a simulation of external pressure inside a tunnel. By the application of the model, the paper describes a simplified criteria which allow to choose the pressure-flow rate curve related to the air-tightness of the car, so that pressure fluctuations inside cars can satisfy the limits for aural discomfort of passengers.

Title Low-noise pantographs and insulators
 
Author Nakagawa, S
Affiliation East Japan Railway Company, Tokyo, Japan
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract To run commercial high speed trains still faster without high costs for additional noise barriers, quieter car design is mandatory. Aerodynamic noise, especially that from pantographs, is dominant at high speed. Existing countermeasures are pantograph covers, or fairing, which flow disturbance and generate aerodynamic noise themselves. JR East developed pantographs with components generating less noise. To reduce the members of supporting frames, link mechanisms which keep contact strips vertical are contained in simple rods: second, contact strips have streamlined cross-section for low aerodynamic noise as well as for a stable, desirable lift force. They have put in service on commercial trains in March 1997. With those low-noise pantographs, pantograph-covers are still necessary as fairing for insulators of pantographs, so further noise reduction can be only achieved by eliminating such covers. The mechanism of the aerodynamic noise from insulators is considered to be some "jet-stream effect" generated from the wake of the accelerated air flow due to the insulator shape. By extending the length of insulators to the flow direction, the flow is expected to slow down before reaching the end of the insulators, causing less disturbances in the wake and less noise emission. Wind-tunnel experiment revealed that the hypothesis about the flow was right. Succeeding tests in low-noise wind-tunnels and real train experiment showed that there is certainly noise reduction effect.
