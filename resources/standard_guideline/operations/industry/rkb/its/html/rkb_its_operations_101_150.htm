Title Opportunities for increasing suburban train and passenger throughput
 
Author Wardrop, A
Affiliation WorleyParsons, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 2014, Rail Transport for a Vital Economy, Conference on Railway Excellence, Adelaide, South Australia, 5-7 May, 2014
Publication Date 2014-05
Abstract There is a definite trend to install ATP systems across suburban railway networks in Australia, primarily to improve safety. However, there is a concern that the lower levels of the ETCS, for example, might improve safety at the expense of train flow. Taking Sydney and Melbourne as test beds, this paper explored the constraints and opportunities for jointly improving safety and increasing train and passenger flows on suburban railways through the network-wide implementation of ATP. This follows international examples, such as the Danish State Railways and the Copenhagen S-tog which are planning to roll out ETCS, or even CBTC, nationally to increase train flows. Train performance and signal system simulation were carried out to explore the physical issues of increasing train flows within the constraints of legacy infrastructure and legacy rolling stock. However, railways must be viewed as a system where infrastructure, rolling stock and operations must be integrated to achieve the best outcomes. Therefore, the distribution of passenger demand and the configurations of alternative rolling stock designs were examined to explore whether signalling improvements to increase train flows could also lead to usefully higher passenger flows. Furthermore, the opportunities for higher passenger flows also had to take into account the nature of Australian suburban rail passenger demand.

Title Railway technology and innovation: driverless trains
 
Author Trestour, A
De-Reuck, A
Affiliation Alstom Transport
Source Permanent Way Institution (PWI)
Conference Permanent Way Institution (PWI) NSW 2015 Annual Convention: Fifty Shades of Perway: the Allure of the Rail Industry
Publication Date 2015-10-30
Abstract The notion of Automatic Train Operation (ATO) defines the operational safety levels against functional requirements needed to help automate train operations. The International Association of Public Transport (UITP) has defined five Grades of Automation (GoA) for trains. The GoA’s are defined according to basic functions of train operation and split in operational responsibilities whether it is for humans or the system itself.
– GoA 0 is a train driver on-sight operation, similar to a tram system running in street traffic, reliant entirely on train driver to manage the system safety.
– GoA 1 is a train operation where a train driver controls starting and stopping, operation of doors and handling of emergencies or sudden diversions, but with an Automatic Train Protection (speed and signal past at danger protection control).
– GoA 2 is semi-automatic train operation (STO) where stopping is automated but a driver in the cab starts the train, operates the doors, drives the train if needed and handles emergencies. Many ATO systems are GoA 2.
– GoA 3 is driverless train operation (DTO) where starting and stopping are automated but a train attendant operates the doors and drives the train in case of emergencies.
– GoA 4 is unattended train operation (UTO) where starting and stopping, operation of doors and handling of emergencies are fully automated without any on-train staff.

Title MPLS based networks in railway deployments
 
Author Oliver, A
Affiliation Calibre Global
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2014, Making Making Innovation Work, 11-12 November 2014, Perth, WA, Australia
Publication Date 2014
Abstract Safe and reliable communications are a critical component in operating modern railway networks. Voice radio, signalling, asset protection, CCTV, station services and alarms form the basis of a typical rail system, with telephony, data and internet connectivity between offices shaping corporate requirements. These services are traditionally provisioned through a combination of separate Synchronous Digital Hierarchy (SDH), Plesiochronous Digital Hierarchy (PDH), Ethernet and legacy analogue circuits, resulting in increased maintenance and support requirements. As more railways move towards Communications Based Signalling (CBS) systems bandwidth requirements increase, with a corresponding focus on reliability and resilience. Coupled with growing focus on Ethernet connectivity for hardware, an opportunity exists to explore alternate network arrangements that are more holistically encompassing of all supported technologies and their requirements. Implementation of an IP/MPLS (Multi-Protocol Label Switched) network allows convergence of these disparate networks into a single unified system. Key infrastructure can be shared between different facets of an organisation on a single physical network, whilst maintaining completely independent circuits for the various services being transported. In contrast to an IP-only implementation, where scalability and traffic engineering can be challenging as network size increases, an IP/MPLS solution allows a network to grow as needed, while providing excellent load balancing to make better use of existing network infrastructure. This in turn can lead to savings in both OPEX and CAPEX, as both infrastructure and maintenance costs are reduced. Ideally suited to both green and brown field rollouts, an IP/MPLS based network is a viable solution for any network rollout.

Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2014, Making Making Innovation Work, 11-12 November 2014, Perth, WA, Australia
Publication Date 2014
Abstract The passenger railway in Perth has grown in yearly patronage by over 110% over the last ten years. Another doubling in patronage has been forecast over the coming 10-15 years which will get at least the busy north south corridor (Joondalup and Mandurah lines) of the Perth network into the same league as the busiest lines in much larger cities like Sydney or Melbourne. Plans for extensive rail transport capacity enhancement are already underway, with substantial augmentation of the train fleet and diverse supporting infrastructure measures. Modern in-cab signalling is being planned for introduction along with a broader suite of strategic network improvements to exceed the limitations of the current conventional signalling system and support the future operation of train headways between two and three minutes. The globally most popular high capacity signalling system, Communications Based Train Control (CBTC), has been identified as preferred technology for the next signalling generation in Perth to achieve the long-term capacity targets. This paper outlines how the technology selection was achieved and discusses some of the key changes that the move to in-cab signalling will introduce to the rail operations in Perth.

Abstract Corporate networks and Information Technology (IT) data centers have revolutionized all aspects of business operations. The heavy freight locomotive is a complex machine, operating in an increasingly restricted track environment, yet is pivotal to revenues. Operational Technology (OT) involves the control or flow of assets in the railroad. The convergence of these technologies drives commonality in the processing and management of data. Locomotives to date have been treated and considered as unique assets, largely outside of corporate IT management and control, requiring specialty personnel and processes to manage and maintain them. Title Standardisation of minimal data collection for condition monitoring and performance management in the Australian rail industry

Affiliation SCN Lavalin, Melbourne, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2017, Rail's Digital Revolution, 21-23 November 2017, Brisbane, Qld, Australia
Publication Date 2017
Abstract SNC-Lavalin believes there is a strong case for introducing standardisation of condition monitoring and performance data collection in the Australian rail industry. The motivation behind this topic is that it is pertinent in the current market. The developments in technology, along with the increased need for more cost effective operations and maintenance, has led to a recent surge in interest towards data collection and more informed decision making. This has been accelerated by the introduction of ISO55001 in 2014. The benefits which data collection could bring to our industry are broad and significant; from increased safety through condition awareness to increased efficiency in maintenance and operations. Standardised data collection allows for a common language to be developed within the industry, facilitating collaboration and reducing process inefficiencies. This has been demonstrated in the UK by the Rail Delivery Group (RDG) and in the petroleum, petrochemical and natural gas industries with the introduction of ISO14224 - Collection and exchange of reliability and maintenance data for equipment. Many stakeholders are working individually to generate their own data and create solutions for condition monitoring. There is not yet a joint effort across the Australian industry to work together and speed up the advancements. Incentivisation by industry leadership is required to encourage collaborative behaviour for the benefit of the industry as a whole.

Title Improving enforcement algorithms for Communications-Based Train Control using adaptive methods
 
Author Moore Ede, B
Polivka, A
Brosseau, J
Tse, Y
Reinschmidt, A
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center (TTCI)
Federal Railroad Administration (FRA)
Conference 9th International Heavy Haul Conference (IHHC 2009), Shanghai, China, 22nd-25th June, 2009
Publication Date 2009
Abstract Communications-Based Train Control (CBTC), referred to as Positive Train Control (PTC) by freight railroads in the USA, was originally conceived to be a safer, more productive and more cost-effective system of train control than conventional signalling systems. One aspect of the added safety was to be attained by fail-safe enforcement brought about by a locomotive-borne system that knows where the train is within the limits of the authority provided to it and is able to apply the brakes in time to prevent violation of limits in the event that the train crew fails to respond. Another aspect is to use PTC enforcement of speed limits. In practice, however, the use of enforcement has decreased capacity and slowed the operation in many situations. The reason is that braking distance can vary substantially from one train to the next because of factors which are not known precisely-factors such as train weight, the rate at which the train brakes will apply or the overall braking efficiency of the train. Enforcement braking is usually based on a full service penalty brake application that is an action of last resort when the onboard system determines that the train will overrun a target if the penalty application is not invoked. Because of the uncertainty of the factors affecting the braking distances, enforcement algorithms are almost invariably based on worst-case braking characteristics in order to assure not to overrun the limits of the authority. The problem is that there are many circumstances when the system calls for enforcement sooner than the train crew would start normal service braking. This is particularly acute for trains operating at slow speeds or on down-gradients. Very often, capacity and efficiency are affected when trains are forced to stop well short of the authority limits or to start slowing earlier than normal operating practice. This paper will investigate the elements that contribute to the problem of the variability of braking distance, describe the issues associated with using a static enforcement algorithm and review techniques being analyzed and tested at the Transportation Technology Center, Incorporated (TTCI ) to change a static enforcement algorithm into one which is dynamic and adaptive. The paper will conclude with the potential improvements to the operation that an adaptive algorithm can produce.
