Title A method of recovering from derailment of Shinkansen rolling stock in the event of disaster
 
Author Egashira, H
Affiliation Kyushu Railway Company, Fukuoka, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Within their own limited environments, railway companies are always studying how to safely and efficiently recover from derailment in a disaster. These studies anticipate all sorts of conditions. However, during actual derailments, safety measures and recovery methods will vary greatly depending on the derailment status and environment, so recovery usually depends on experience. Moreover, each railway operator manages its recovery individually, and there are few opportunities to publicize details about recovery work for outside parties. This paper describes a Shinkansen derailment recovery following the Kumamoto earthquakes of April 2016. JR Kyushu hopes that publicizing the safety measures and recovery methods it used will help increase safety and speed up recovery from other derailments during disaster. It is also hoped that by releasing this information, other operators will be encouraged to share information about their own derailment recovery efforts and thereby promote research and cooperation, development of new recovery equipment, a framework for cooperation and support, and expanded education concerning derailment during disaster.

Title Development of derailment detector with twin-sensor
 
Author Oyama, H
Nagamoto, M
Matsuoka, S
Yano, K
Maeda, T
Affiliation Japan Transport Engineering Company, Tokyo, Japan
Tokyo Metro Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Derailment detector with Twin-sensor has been developed and achieved high accuracy in detection, high reliability, space saving, high cost effectiveness. Conventional one-body type derailment detector has been used on each train for JR East line in Greater Tokyo Area since 2008.This device monitors vibration of a running vehicle to detect the accidents (e.g. derailment, roll-over and collision). In case of these accidents, the trains around the accident will stop due to the detection alert from the derailed train. Although the conventional device has high detection performance, the failure rate is high due to the single system configuration. In addition, since the specification of conventional device is based on the assumption that the device is mounted on a driver's cab of a leading car only, two devices are necessary in each car to detect the accident in each bogie. For this reason, the Twin-sensor type derailment detector consisting of two sensors and one controller has been developed.

Title The effect of commentary driving for preventing excessive speed
 
Author Sato, A
Onoma, N
Masuda, T
Affiliation Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Excessive train speed might cause fatal accidents. Therefore, a train safety method must be developed that drivers can use solely for preventing excessive train speed. One reason for excessive train speed was drivers’ failure to remember the existence of a speed restrain region at the time when the train approached that region. This study focused on risk-triggered commentary driving (RTC) and the imaging commentary (IC). RTC and IC were methods for preventing drivers from forgetting about the speed restrain region. RTC involves train drivers speaking aloud what they usually just think to themselves when driving to prevent failing to remember. IC involves train drivers imagining the view from the train that they drive under limited speed. This study conducted an experiment to investigate whether RTC and IC can prevent excessive train speed. Thirty train drivers in Japan participated in the experiment and allocated randomly to two conditions. The first condition was the RTC and IC condition, in which participants used RTC and IC while driving their simulator train. The second condition was the no-RTC and no-IC condition, in which participants do not use RTC and IC while driving their simulator train. The number of participants who failed to remember the existence of a speed restrain region and caused excessive train speed was compared between the two conditions. The results indicated that the number of participants in the RTC and IC condition who caused excessive train speed was lower than that in the other condition. Thus, RTC and IC prevent excessive train speed caused by the failure to remember the existence of a speed restrain region.

Title Influence of the size and geometry of cavities inside railway tunnels on the pressure loads on safety doors
 
Author Heine, D
Kuehnelt, H
Rudolph, M
Vorwagner, A
Lachinger, S
Saliger, F
Heine, G
Ehrenfried, K
Affiliation Tokyo Metro Co., Ltd, Tokyo, Japan
Railway Technical Research Institute, Tokyo, Japan
Coms Engineering Corporation, Tokyo, Japan
YOKOHAMA National University, Tokyo, Japan
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract The passage of trains through tunnels may lead to strong pressure waves resulting in high loads on the installations of the tunnels and the occurrence of the so-called tunnel-boom or passenger discomfort due to the high pressure amplitude. During the train-tunnel passage, the safety doors inside the tunnel are subject to these pressure loads and can even be severely damaged. The purpose of this study is to investigate the loads on the safety doors caused by the pressure waves in more detail. The idea is to adapt the geometry or size of the cavity in which the door is located in order to reduce the pressure amplitude and therefore the loads. The influence of the cavities on the pressure waves was studied both experimentally and numerically. Tests using a moving-model rig were performed, where the pressure inside of a model tunnel with different cavity geometries was measured with pressure transducers. The train-tunnel passage was also studied numerically to validate the experimental results and to extend the range of parameters. The purpose of the cavities is to reduce the pressure loads on the doors. However, the study showed an increase in loads as additional pressure waves are caused by the cavities, which superpose with the initial ones. Because of the additional pressure waves, the smallest pressure amplitudes were found for the reference case without cavity.

Title Improving the safety performance of passenger rail car glazing systems
 
Author Gordon, J
Llana, P
Severson, K
Prabhakaran, A
Gantoi, FM
Robitaille, A
Radovich, M
Affiliation USDOT/Federal Railroad Administration, Washington D.C., USA
USDOT/Volpe Center, Cambridge, MA, USA
Sharma and Associates, Inc., Countryside, IL, USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract A review of passenger train accidents shows that there is a significant risk for passengers to be ejected through window openings if the cars derail, roll over, and slide along their sides. In such scenarios, the gaskets that retain the glazing panes shear off and the glazing pane is pushed into the car leaving an opening in the carbody, resulting in a significant potential for passenger ejection. This paper describes on-going work aimed at reducing the risk of fatalities/injuries from such incidents. As part of this effort, previous relevant accidents were analyzed to better understand the modes of failure and conceptualize methods by which they can be addressed. New or updated glazing retention methods are thought to be necessary in the pursuit of safety. Considering feasibility, implementation time, likelihood of success, and the potential for retrofit, a few concepts including various methods of zip-strip (an element of the gasketing that facilitates installation and removal, including under emergency conditions) protection, a revised zip-strip location, and recessed window glazing have been ideated and the top rated concepts developed further. In addition, test methods to quantify the relative performance and safety benefits have been proposed. In the next phase, a series of tests are planned to quantify the effectiveness of new concepts in mitigating passenger ejection. It is anticipated that a successful concept can be retrofitted into current fleets quickly and cost effectively, delivering an immediate positive impact on public safety.

Title Exploring non-technical skills linked with inadvertent increases in safety-risk in rail network control: a preliminary scenario-based analysis
 
Author Naweed, A
Murphy, P
Affiliation Appleton Institute for Behavioural Science, Central Queensland University, Adelaide, Australia
Rail Safety and Standards Board, London, United Kingdom
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR), railway research to enhance the customer experience, 28 October-1 November 2019, Tokyo, Japan
Publication Date 2019
Abstract In rail driving, the person or crew within the cab physically operate the train but only by working closely with Network Controllers as part of a distributed system. Team synergies can often be disrupted and/or distorted across time and space; thus, good non-technical skills are required to maintain its integrity. One-to-one interviews using a scenario-based paradigm were undertaken with Network Controllers to determine which non-technical skills were most linked with operational risk. A total of 20 participants were recruited from four rail organizations across Australia. Scenarios were analysed using the RSSB non-technical skills framework developed for Rail Drivers. Content analysis using behavioural markers (to assess and link specific skills with specific behaviours) revealed non-technical skills in all of categories were associated with risk to some extent, though situational awareness, workload management, and communication categories were more represented. The most frequently coded nontechnical skills were maintaining concentration (situational awareness), checking (communication), multi-tasking and selective attention and remaining calm under pressure (workload management), and considering others’ needs, (cooperation & working with others). The results of this initial analysis provide an invaluable picture of the skills a Network Controller uses in safety-critical situations, and outlines those most substantive for error/failure potential in an operational context. The findings also lend evidence to the notion that, while the Controller performs a fundamental and gatekeeping role, they can easily influence the actions, reactions and decisions of drivers, as part of distributed cognitive system— and in ways that lead to decreases in operational safety. The findings hint at the beginning of a Network Controller-specific non-technical skills framework and future research directions are given.

Abstract Safety is the JR Kyushu Group’s greatest duty to society. The railway’s Kokura Electric Zone is working every day to eliminate dangerous accidents and achieve safety with no exceptions. Abstract In Japan, “design standard for railway structures (Track)” was enacted in 2012, and the design of the track structure is based on the standard. Regarding the evaluation of the train running safety, we use a simplified track model simulating the geometrical alignment of turnout by beam elements. The vehicle is modelled as a MBD model.
