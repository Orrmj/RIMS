Title Establishing baseline rail level crossing incident occurrence and behaviours using video data
 
Author Larue, G
Wullems, C
Aminmansour, S
Source ACRI
Report or Manual No R2.119
Publisher Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation (ACRI)
Place Canberra, ACT, Australia
Publication Date 2015-07
Physical Description 135p. ; PDF
Abstract Current reporting practices for precursor events such as such as Signals Passed At Danger (SPADs) and near-misses at level crossings may not reveal the less visible influences leading to an incident. This report describes an alternate approach, with a safety data recording and data analysis system designed and developed to capture data from train-borne systems and high-definition forward-facing videos from train cabs. The resulting objective data can then be used by analysts and incident investigators to assist in determining potential causes of accidents and ultimately lead to action to reduce incidents.
Aims and objectives
» develop a data collection and analysis system to support the analysis of near misses at level crossings.
» establish precise definitions of near-misses using objectively measurable information.
» establish technical data capture performance criteria to underpin essential data capture.
» improve understanding of the context of near-misses and causal factors and their reporting, which will inform measures to improve safety at level crossings
Summary of findings:
The project developed an approach for detecting and localising vehicles with respect to the rail track and calculating the distance of the detected vehicles from the rail track by analysing video data of trains with computer vision algorithms. The project collected data from three high-speed passenger trains equipped with high-definition forward facing video cameras and automatic train protection, traversing a section of track with over 800 level crossings, 450 of which were level crossings that intersect with public roads.
Forward-facing video footage was downloaded from trains and video analytics used to provide parameters around the detection, localisation and tracking of objects, including persons and vehicles; detection of the rail; and detection of signals and signal state. To support analysis of precursor safety events at railway level crossings, several image processing plugins were developed to detect and track objects visible in forward-facing video footage captured on approach to railway level crossings. These objects included but were not limited to cars, trucks, buses, and pedestrians.
The distance between the detected vehicle and the rail for each frame was determined through the interpolation of the location of the vehicle along the road segment that intersects with the rail centreline. This allowed for accurate distances to be determined irrespective of the road approach geometries.
A socio-technical accident causation model ws developed to support predictive and retrospective analysis of level crossing occurrences, including collisions between trains and vehicles/pedestrians.
The model prompts analysts to consider predefined factors when identifying potential causes of incidents, without precluding the addition of new causal relationships or factors to the data analysis platform. The model is based on a combination of engineering and human factors theories and includes:
» organisation and management failures;
» failures in governance;
» engineering and technical failures;
» failures in level crossing life cycle processes;.
» operational failures;
» sub-optimal human-system interfaces; and
» human performance failures.
The model is advantageous for investigators and railway safety data analysts because it provides a resource from multiple disciplines spanning engineering to human factors for precursor events.
The model is also a valuable tool for railway practitioners to inform the design of new interventions.

Title Preparing railway stakeholders against CBRNe threats through better cooperation with security practitioners
 
Author Havârneanu, GM
Petersen, L
Arnold, A
Carbon, D
Görgen, T
Affiliation International Union of Railways (UIC), France
Deutsche Hochschule der Polizei (DHPol), Germany
Journal Applied Ergonomics
Publication Date 2022-07
Abstract This paper presents partial results from the Horizon2020 PROACTIVE project, following a set of literature reviews and surveys conducted with first responder organisations and rail security experts. Qualitative and quantitative data from two surveys are being presented. The results provide an overview of the CBRNe (Chemical, Biological, Radiological, Nuclear and explosive) preparedness and response capabilities of railway stakeholders and how these relate to a wider context represented by CBRNe first responders. The results highlight a set of challenges as well as five core skills that railway staff need to develop or improve: 1) understand the specific characteristics of the CBRNe threat, 2) develop basic response measures, 3) cooperate with authorities and train with specialised first responders, 4) improve public awareness about this threat, and 5) optimise crisis communication. In line with these, project PROACTIVE will further help update rail crisis management plans with practical recommendations concerning the CBRNe threat.

Title Investigation and MASH full-scale crash testing of the practice of raising blockouts on w-beam rail systems
 
Author Dobrovolny, CS
Bligh, R
Kiani, M
Hangul, A
Affiliation Texas A&M Transportation Institute, Bryan, TX, USA
Tennessee Department of Transportation, Headquarters Roadway Design and Office of Aerial Surveys, Nashville, TN, USA
Journal Transportation Research Record
Publication Date 2022-04
Abstract The Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) clarifies appropriate height measures for W-beam guardrails. Identification of existing locations where rail height is lower than recommended by FHWA is common. A research study was conducted to investigate the crashworthiness of raising blockouts on posts to restore barrier height and provide clarification on implementation of such methodology. The researchers evaluated the crashworthiness of raising blockouts by conducting a full-scale Manual for Assessing Safety Hardware (MASH) Crash Test 3-11 of a 28-in. W-beam guardrail system with composite blockouts raised 4 in. on posts. The 28-in. W-beam guardrail system with raised composite blockouts contained and redirected the 2270P vehicle, and it performed acceptably for MASH Test 3-11. The results of this study include guidance on the procedure for raising blockout mounting height on steel posts to achieve recommended rail height for a W-beam guardrail.

Title A quantitative risk-based approach to derailment containment on elevated structures
 
Author Pearse, M
Affiliation WSP
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2021, 28 February-2 March 2022, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2022
Abstract In mass transit systems, derailment remains the highest risk for a multi-fatality event. The likelihood of a derailment and the risk of the railway’s height escalating a derailment event must be mitigated, so far as is reasonably practicable (SFAIRP). The concept of SFAIRP is enshrined in Rail Safety National Law (RSNL) [1] and State and Territory Health & Safety at Work Acts. Section 46 of RSNL[1] places obligations on Duty Holders to (a) to eliminate risks to safety so far as is reasonably practicable; and (b) if it is not reasonably practicable to eliminate risks to safety, to minimise those risks so far as is reasonably practicable. The Office of National Rail Safety Regulator (ONRSR) Guideline Meaning of duty to ensure safety so far as is reasonably practicable – SFAIRP [2] provides guidance to duty holders on the interpretation and application of SFAIRP under RSNL[1]. This paper discusses a quantitate approach to assessing the relative effectiveness of derailment containment measures to demonstrate that the trackform and containment are safe SFAIRP.
