Title Rolling stock and track geometry influences on derailment risk due to longitudinal forces
 
Author Skerman, D
Birkin, D
Affiliation Rail Engineering Consultant (formerly with QR/Aurizon), Brisbane, QLD
Aurizon, Brisbane, QLD
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract The geometry of a wagon and the other rolling stock to which it is coupled affects the angles developed by the couplers as the train negotiates various geometrical features in the track. Large coupler angles on lightly loaded wagons combined with significant in-train forces can give rise to wheel lift or flange climb leading to derailment. Changes to train operations such as increased train length, modified marshalling practices, locomotive type and placement or new routes require consideration of the rolling stock and track geometry and the likely in-train forces to assess the derailment risk. Section 10 of AS7509 Longitudinal Forces in Curves requires assessment of a candidate wagon marshalled between another wagon of the same type and a base vehicle in the circular part of a curve. This configuration does not always give the largest coupler angles or represent the highest derailment risk scenario. This paper provides examples of curve entry, reverse curves, cross-overs and tangent track which can give a higher risk of derailment than the AS7509 case when all rolling stock is in the circular part of the curve. Methods for determining realistic forces throughout the train over a given route are also discussed. These factors need consideration when using AS 7509.

Title Advancement in track technology: composite foundations for increased speed and axle loads
 
Author Indraratna, B
Ngo, NT
Rujikiatkamjorn, C
Affiliation University of Wollongong, Wollongong, NSW
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract The increasing demand for fast heavy haul services with greater axle loads, as well as high speed commuter trains, poses a serious challenge for the stability of tracks on the problematic ground. The use of artificial inclusions such as polymeric geosynthetics for enhanced soil-structure interaction and rubber mats made from recycled rubber tyres to absorb the strain energy and reduce ballast breakage and track damage is described in this paper as a cost-effective option. In this study, a series of large-scale laboratory tests took place to determine how well these geogrids could attenuate the impact, cyclic stress, and corresponding mitigation of ballast degradation. Comprehensive field trials took place on two full-scale rail tracks in the towns of Bulli and Singleton in New Souh Wales. These trials facilitated the evaluation of different types of geogrids, geo-composites and rubber mats installed in fully instrumented track sections, as well as the possible reuse of spent ballast. This paper focuses mainly on research projects at the University of Wollongong to enhance track performance by highlighting some examples of innovation from theory to practice; including case studies.

Title Canberra rail: to be or not to be?
 
Author Laird, P
Michell, M
Affiliation University of Wollongong, Wollongong, NSW
Consultant, Victoria
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract By many standards, current Sydney to Canberra train services are too few and too slow to be commercially attractive in the 21st Century. High Speed Rail options with trains capable of 250 km/h or more on dedicated track for Sydney to Canberra, and beyond, have been studied extensively since 1984. However, none of these studies to date have led to any firm proposals, and even less to any real action. The paper takes a high level view of the issue of faster train services, including reviewing past studies into Medium Speed Rail options, with trains between Sydney to Canberra operating over upgraded existing track. It concludes that such options should be given further consideration, with a view to using incremental steps to significantly reduce Sydney - Canberra passenger train transit times, while providing some benefits to rail freight operations. At the very least, some upgrades of the existing track to allow new trains to offer three-hour Sydney - Canberra train services are recommended along with more frequent train services.

Publication Date 2018
Abstract Asset Management involves managing physical assets across their lifecycle within planned operational budgets and as part of core business decisions.In this context, successful initiation and completion of capital rehabilitation and replacement programs require core business decision making, especially related to infrastructure management. A crucial decision to make is ensuring that appropriate projects are selected based on current risks and priorities. The goal of this paper is to present a framework to develop Strategic Asset Management Plan (SAMP) for a rail transportation company to ensure reliability at least cost while controlling the risk of failure. Under the SAMP, the railway infrastructure will be renewed gradually to ensure sustainability, following a predetermined order of priority. The maintenance strategy and the necessary budget are established accordingly. The primary output of asset investment analysis is an optimized master plan that will improve performance and reliability, and ensure that capital investment are spent where the greatest needs exist. The SAMP deploys a risk-informed, evidence-based approach to its investment decision making processes, both to align with best practices and to meet current and future needs of customers. The paper will present the decision supporting platform used to develop a Canadian railway long term CAMP, Direxyon Integrated Decision Support System (IDSS), to reach for the most cost-effective investments to optimize capital spending and maintenance practices while extending the life of existing assets. The paper concludes with comparing the rail benefits assumed by using strategic asset management approach versus traditional asset management approach.

Title Developments in track feeder protection for AC autotransformer traction networks
 
Author Perin, I
Goffey, J
Cant, C
Grzesiczak, D
Affiliation Aurizon, Brisbane, Qld
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Distance protection is the most commonly used scheme for protecting track feeders in AC electric railways. It operates based on measured impedance, which translates into distance to fault. This enables schemes to be built around protection zones, with progressively increasing reaches and time delays. Unlike transmission and distribution networks, where the feeder impedance is directly proportional to distance, autotransformer rail networks introduce a non-linearity. A particular case, involving asymmetrical earthing of the protected line, resulted in significant distortion of the impedance vs distance function. This scenario prompted a detailed study of the feeder impedance for various network feeding arrangements, with a revision of relevant distance zone reaches. Following the study, a new automated distance protection system was introduced and consequently rolled out for Aurizon's entire traction network, with zone reaches adapting to the network configuration in real time, ensuring maximum possible coverage of the traction network without encroaching into adjacent zones. This paper presents a review of autotransformer track feeder distance protection, together with Aurizon's latest developments.

Title Prediction of rolling contact fatigue damage associated with rail flashbutt welds
 
Author Pun, J
Welsby, D
Mutton, P
Cookson, J
Yan, W
Affiliation Monash University, Clayton, Vic
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract High strength heat treated pearlitic rail steels are used extensively in heavy haul rail systems to mitigate rail degradation due to wear and rolling contact fatigue damage, and improve rail life. However, these benefits may be offset by the potential occurrance of localised degradation at flashbutt welds due to the discontinuous nature of both microstructure and mechanical properties relative to those of the parent rail. Of particular concern is the higher sensitivity to cumulative plastic deformation (ratcheting) and rolling contact fatigue damage in the heat-softened zones. As part of ongoing research into improving the performance of welds in these rail grades, the influence of different welding parameters on the ratcheting behaviour of the heat-softened zone in flashbutt welds has been systematically investigated. A constitutive cyclic plasticity material model, which can describe the ratcheting behaviour of flashbutt welds in a high strength rail steel under cyclic loading conditions. Simulation of the wheel-rail cyclic rolling contact was carried out by the finite element method. Different widths of the softened zone were considered. The results show that both maximum ratcheting strain and maximum ratcheting strain rate can be significantly reduced and so the reliability and the performance of flashbutt welds can be improved by narrowing the width of the softened zones.

Title Aluminothermic welding: the current state of practice, performance and development a century on
 
Author Salehi, I
Mutton, P
Affiliation Monash University, Clayton, Vic
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Aluminothermic welding (ATW), with a history of more than a century, is the oldest rail welding technology which is still widely used even in modern railway systems. Despite considerable improvements in the process, consumables and workmanship over this period, the process is still prone to inconsistent quality, leading to inferior performance compared to other methods, and premature failures which challenge and endanger the safety and productivity of railway systems all around the world. The major issue lies with the manual installation process, its heavy dependence on operator experience, and in most cases a failure to correctly follow procedures established by the consumable manufacturers and railway authorities. This paper aims to provide an overview of the aluminothermic welding process, the different failure modes that occur, the implications in terms of rail performance, and lessons learnt from the failures. The current state of aluminothermic welding practice and developments in the process and consumables achieved by the major supplier are briefly reviewed. The main failure modes are discussed and the important parameters (the majority of which related to the various stages in the welding process) which contribute to initiation of these failures are described. Examples of current services failures are presented and failure assessment tools are utilized to demonstrate a variety of scenarios in which incorrect procedures have resulted in severely defective welds. For each case the required activities and preventative measures are briefly discussed. The importance of managerial interventions including a regulated system for training and auditing of the welding operators and processes are also described. An overview of research activties aimed at providing a theoretical basis for failure modes in aluminothermic welds, utilizing numerical methods and fracture mechanics to evaulate the sensitivity of performance to a number of imperfections in the process, will also be covered.

Abstract At a mountainous location in central Queensland, rail has been observed as heating well beyond the normally expected range of rail temperatures. While there has been substantial research into the effect of heavy-haul traffic heating the rail on significant grades, minimal research has been made into the impacts of sustained, high-frequency heavy haul traffic. Rail has been observed to be heating around 5-6 C per loaded coal train, and at the scheduled headway, there is insufficient time between trains to allow the rail to cool back to its normal, steady state temperature, causing ratcheting increase in rail temperatures. It was found that intensive rail traffic can increase rail temperatures above its normal state by over 17 C. This paper describes the investigation into the impact of high-frequency heavy haul traffic on curvy, mountainous grades, as well as the development of a model to analyse and predict the ultimate impact of increasing rail traffic. Furthermore, risk mitigation strategies are described.

Title Continuous measurement of passenger ride comfort and track condition on the VLine network
 
Author Thompson, C
Hardie, G
Shamandi, A
Wakeling, C
Buscuttil, M
Affiliation Monash University, Clayton, Vic
V/Line, Melbourne, Vic
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract The assessment of a rail vehicle's dynamic ride is critical to a rail operation for both passenger comfort and safety. Poor ride can lead to low passenger satisfaction, increased track damage and likelihood of derailment. Vibrations experienced within a passenger cabin can cause uncomfortable ride depending on the magnitudes of the accelerations and the perceived comfort sensitivity of passengers to the frequency content. Overall ride quality is also affected by factors such as wheel and rail profile interactions, suspension design and the condition of both the track geometry and rail running surfaces. Several standards exist that link acceleration levels with passenger comfort and these are the generally accepted methods for objectively evaluating ride comfort. Few standards, however, currently address ride safety of passengers due to jerky ride conditions. The majority of railroads in Australia currently use either subjective reports of poor ride or one off measurement programs which provide an objective snap shot of the status of the rolling stock and track conditions. This paper discusses the implementation of continuous ride monitoring systems called IPVs (Instrumented Passenger Vehicles) developed by the Institute of Railway Technology (IRT) for use on V/Line's Victorian regional rail network. The IPV systems retrofitted to V/Lines's N-Series passenger wagons include acceleration, displacement and roll rate sensors that provide track geometry information, while body mounted sensors are used to quantify responses. Acceleration data collected at several locations within the carriage are used to calculate passenger comfort and safety metrics and this paper discusses practical application of these methods. IPVs provide real time feedback of track and rolling stock issues when combined with wide area communication network coverage. Data from the IPV system is automatically downloaded during the trip which facilitates a variety of analyses and reports that are provided to V/Line. IPVs also provide a method of collecting track condition and rolling stock data which is complementary to existing inspection methods such as visual inspection and track recording vehicles. The measurement of the dynamic responses via IPV also provides a direct indication of the loads being imposed on the rail network in-service which are not nominally defined in data collected by other methods. This paper discusses the development of the IPV systems and how data is being used to assess passenger comfort and safety on V/Line's rail network. The IPVs provided track geometry information, wagon dynamic responses and calculated passenger comfort and safety metrics, provides a holistic understanding of passenger railway systems and can be used to identify track defects, allocate temporary speed restrictions and prioritise track inspection and maintenance activities.

Title Simulation of track-locomotive interactions
 
Author Wu, Q
Sun, Y
Spiryagin, M
Cole, C
Affiliation Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Qld
Australasian Centre for Rail Innovation, Canberra, ACT
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract This paper introduces a new method to study track dynamics and locomotive dynamics as well as track-locomotive interactions. The method uses a two-way co-simulation technique to link a detailed track model that is developed using an in-house software package and a detailed locomotive model that is developed using a commercial software package. The track model has considered the rails, fasteners, sleepers, ballast, subballast, and subgrade. The locomotive model has considered the carbody, bogie frames, wheelsets, traction motors and gear boxes. A detailed wheel-rail contact model with up to three simultaneous contact points for every wheel on each rail is used. In the two-way co-simulation technique, wheel-rail contact forces that are simulated in the commerical software package are sent to the track model and attached to their corresponding positions on the rails. With these attached wheel-rail contact forces, the track model can determine the positions and postures of the rail which are then sent to the commercial software package. The rail profiles in the commercial software are updated according to the rail positions and rail postures. Compared with conventional methods, this method has the advantages of being able to use both detailed track models and detailed locomotive models. The feasibility of the method has been demonstrated by using two simulations to model a locomotive negotiating a section of track with and without an unsupported sleeper.

Title Predicting the effectiveness of rail dampers on noise emissions: the first Australian trial
 
Author Zoontjens, L
Croft, B
Teng, L
Affiliation SLR Consulting Australia Pty Ltd, Perth, WA
SLR Consulting (Canada) Ltd, British Columbia, Canada
Public Transport Authority, Perth, WA
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Rail dampers are a rolling noise mitigation measure developed in Europe. The first field trails of this track component have previously been undertaken in Australia (Sydney). There, rail dampers have been shown to offer a moderate noise reduction of around 4 dB for passenger trains on slab track and resilient fasteners. On typical ballasted tracks in Sydney rail dampers are less effective and have not been found to be a cost-effective noise control measure. The benefit achievable through rail dampers varies considerably between different systems, depending on the design of the wheel and track. STARDAMP is a modelling tool to predict the noise reduction achievable by rail dampers (or wheel dampers) in a particular situation. This paper presents a study predicting the noise reduction provided by rail dampers on highly resilient slab track and on ballasted track, and comparing model results with field measurements in Perth. Both model and measurements demonstrate that noise from the Perth metropolitan passenger train network can be effectively attenuated using rail dampers. In terms of overall A-weighted levels, a rolling noise reduction of up to 5 dB was identified for passenger trains on ballasted track. In a tunnel situation with slab track (Pandrol Vanhuard), a rolling noise reduction of up to 8 dB was achieved. The effectiveness of rail dampers as a noise control measure in Perth is attributed to two main factors. Firstly, the Perth rolling stock wheel design is relatively quiet. Modelling indicates that in the undamped base camp rolling noise is dominated by rail emissions. If the wheel contributions were higher, the benefit of the dampers would be less. Secondly, both the ballasted and slab tracks tested in Perth utilise resilient elements with relatively low dynamic stiffness. As a result of these two factors, the noise control benefit of rail dampers is greater for the Perth passenger network than is the case on the Sydney passenger network. The paper outlines the modelling and field measurement approaches taken and the results of the Perth rail damper trials. The methods represent an advance in railway environmental impact assessment and forecasting noise control performance that could be applied to rail systems elsewhere.

Title Research, development and use of stabilisation geogrids to extend track maintenance intervals and reduce costs
 
Author Horton, MA
Dobie, M
Buckley, J
Affiliation Tensar International Limited, Blackburn, UK
Tensar International Limited, Jakarta, Indonesia
Tensar International Limited, Queensland, Australia
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Geogrids have been incorporated in comparative testing in rail track-bed applications for many years and have shown a consistent ability to improve the settlement performance, and hence the potential to reduce maintenance cost and extend track life. Early work concentrated on the use of biaxial geogrids in laboratory environments providing full-scale testing but with the ability to contraol variables adequately. This work allowed geogrid dimensions to be optimised to suit rail aggregates to permit efficient interlock and confinement of aggregate particles and thereby preserve rail levels. Live-line testing was carried out to measure the performance of stabilised track-bed under real conditions, which has resulted in the development of guidance for designers on the use of stabilisation geogrids. With the introduction of multi-axial hexagonal geogrids and an appropriate definition of the stabilisation function, laboratory work has shown the improved performance associated with this form of geogrid through enhanced confinement of aggregate particles compared to the older biaxial product. The use of instrumented artifical ballast particles (SmartRock) has been used to track individual particle movements in full-scale laboratory testing to allow a real-time functional assessment to be made of geogrid performance. Full-scale testing has shown the potential to save on maintenance cost by extending maintenance intervals. This has recently been confirmed in a live line monitoring exercise. The paper shows the depth of research that has developed with the use of biaxial and multiaxial geogrids in rail track-bed which continues to this day, now extended into high speed rail applications.

Title Reducing dynamic loads at rail welds via improved running surface alignment requirements
 
Author Cookson, JM
Hardie, G
Mutton, PJ
Affiliation Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 11th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2017), Advancing heavy haul technologies and operations in a changing world, 2-6 September, 2017, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 2017
Abstract Elevated dynamic loads can arise in new track at welds if the alignment is not sufficiently well controlled, increasing the risk of damage to either welds or the underlying track structure. Recent measurements in heavy haul railways have established that the dynamic loads are much better correlated to short range variations in the running surface rather than the overall peak over a 1m span. Measurements of both alignment and accelerations showed at a constant speed there is an essentially linear relationship between the dynamic load and the maximum slope of the alignment deviation. Electronic straightedge units provide significantly better resolution and a permanent record of the running surface alignment, and have identified considerable variability in finished welds from mobile flashbutt welding machines. The need for better control of alignment using electronic straightedges has been identified in high speed railways, but the acceptance criteria should be reviewed for heavy haul systems. Railways are advised to consider tighter acceptance limits for alignment based on the correlation between dynamic load measurements at welds. The Australian standard for qualification of welds requires a maximum slope of 7mrad (7 in 1000), but based on the field data, this corresponds to a relatively high dynamic load equivalent to an acceleration of 4g. An improved alignment can readily be justified by the reduced risk of damage due to impact at new welds.

Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract In 2003, BC Rail and Kelsan Technologies carried out a project to quantify the effect of Top of Rail ( TOR) Friction Control on fuel consumption and Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions by reducing the curving resistance of a standard 3 piece North American truck. Measurable savings were based on monitoring changes in diesel fuel consumption, as well as mechanical drawbar forces. Results indicated a strong correlation between curve density and fuel savings when the test consist was under tractive effort. Calculated fuel savings ranged from 155 Liters/Million Ton-miles to a 744 Liters/Millions Ton-miles (2 to 12%) on track ranging in curve density from 25 to 51 percent. Corresponding GHG Emission reductions (CO) ranged from 0.3 metric tonnes per million ton-miles to 2 metric tonnes per million ton-miles. No fuel savings/ GHG emission reductions were achieved when the consist was under dynamic braking.

Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc., Pueblo, Colorado, USA
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)

Publication Date 2005
Abstract North American railroads have improved performance of special trackwork significantly during the period when 129,727 kilogram (286,000 pound) cars were being implemented. As a result, operating costs have decreased while network reliability has increased. These track work improvements have concentrated on turnouts, leaving diamond crossings and bonded insulated rail joints (IJ) as weak locations in the rail network. This paper describes failure modes analysis, modeling, prototype development, and performance testing of improved designs for special trackwork frogs, foundations, and insulated joints. Frogs designed for diamond crossings where two tracks have different traffic patterns are being implemented, maximize service life by providing different levels of performance for each route. The design known as One Way Low Speed (OWLS) flange bearing crossing diamonds provide significant life cycle cost and operations improvements over conventional designs. Work on foundations has shown there is an optimal level of track damping to minimize forces at fixed point frogs. Evaluation of potential track structures to achieve this damping level will be discussed. The service life of IJ under 32. 7 tonne (36-ton) axle load unit train traffic is unacceptably short. Average IJ lives of 182 million gross tones (200 MGT) have been reported on lines with 200 MGT/yr or more of traffic. We will discuss the development of new and improved IJ designs, from failure modes analysis to prototype testing.

Publication Date 2005
Abstract Trackside top of rail friction control has been evaluated on a high tonnage uni-directional line on the Canadian National Railway Co. (CN). The goal was to quantify lateral force and rail wear rate reductions potentially achievable with this technology. Initial work with a single trackside unit identified the potential for significant rail wear reduction, but also showed the difficulties in generating meaningful comparisons in a limited area due to the inherent variability in curve wear rates. Localized spalling around top of rail (TOR) bars was noted when the unit was installed in a spiral, and guidelines were developed to avoid this problem. Title Force-based assessment of weld geometry
 
Author Esveld, C
Steenbergen, MJMM
Affiliation Delft University of Technology, The Netherlands
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract TU Delft developed new geometrical standards for rail welds, for the Dutch Railway administrator ProRail. The current standards were based on the limitation of maximum vertical and horizontal tolerances, as is common practice worldwide. In this approach the geometrical shape of the weld has no significance, thus neglecting the important direct relation to dynamic wheel-rail contact forces and stresses. In the new approach the influence of the complete geometry is included. A theoretical model is presented to translate the complex dynamic problem of calculating dynamic wheelrail contact forces into a purely geometrical problem, which is fast to solve and easy to implement in a numerical code for the small processor of a measuring device. Intervention values for the dynamic force have been determined, dependent on the train line speed, by evaluation of a substantial number of weld geometry measurements, and subsequently expressed in terms of the 1st derivative of the weld geometry. The new assessment method asks for new measuring equipment: digital straightedges are necessary to obtain the quality level corresponding to the new standards.

Abstract This paper describes the measurement of field resilient and permanent deformation on a heavy haul track formation. The current state of the art railway foundation design methods rely heavily on the prediction of permanent deformation due to cyclic train loading. Permanent deformation is estimated from conventional cyclic triaxial tests that do not reproduce the complex track foundation stress paths, which include the phenomenon of principal stress rotation. Hollow cylinder tests on typical track formation materials have showed that triaxial tests under-estimate permanent strain development during cyclic loading. Author Ossberger, H
Affiliation VAE GMBH, Zeltweg, Austria
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract This paper describes a new way to optimize turnouts in the heavy-haul environment in order to reduce wear and increase the life-span. Traditional turnout designs pay very little attention to the behaviour of the vehicle in a turnout. The turnout is considered as an interruption of the tangent track and for a short distance it is accepted that the vehicle doesn't run smoothly. Especially Heavy Haul Railroads have learned to live with this deficiency and with the performance results of the turnouts. But this need not be the case for the future. In a switch one wheel is always running on a single rail, the other is running on the stock rail and switch point simultaneously. This fact introduces a turning moment to the axle. The sinus running curve is interrupted and the wheels are scraping along the switch point and causing wear. With the KGO design the negative influence of the overrunning on one side of the switch is compensated for and wear is minimized. The KGO is already well known and highly appreciated in the high-speed and suburban sectors. A few years ago this system was also introduced to the heavy-haul area and is showing excellent results. This paper will give you information on what happens in the switch and how the behaviour of the vehicle can be positively influenced in order to reduce wear and to increase the service life. It will give you a direct feedback from a well-known heavyhaul track in Australia.

Title A practical approach to controlling rolling contact fatigue in railways
 
Author Magel, EE
Sawley, KJ
Sroba, P
Kalousek, J
Affiliation National Research Council of Canada
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract Rolling contact fatigue (RCF) is a problem for most railways, heavy or light axle load, slow or high speed. A practical strategy for controlling RCF is discussed based on a multi-pronged approach of increasing component resistance to initiation and propagation of RCF, reducing applied stresses that cause RCF, and providing a maintenance regime for both controlling RCF and managing the risk associated with rail or wheel failure due to RCF. RCF is influenced by, and can be controlled by improvements to, the wheel profiles, rail profiles, wheel and rail metallurgy, gauge-face and top-of-rail friction coefficients, track geometry standards, and rolling stock characteristics. Mitigation can include wholesale changes to one parameter or selective and more modest changes to several parameters. Since RCF is a threshold phenomena, even a small reduction in the stresses, or modest increase in the strength of the component, can dramatically reduce the number of contact cycles that promote fatigue. For any given railway, the most appropriate approach to controlling fatigue will depend on its specific operating and maintenance strategies.
