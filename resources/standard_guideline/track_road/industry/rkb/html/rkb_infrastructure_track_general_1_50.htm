Title Advancements in squats management: a Queensland rail experience
 
Author Valente, CHG
Affiliation Queensland Rail, Australia
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract Queensland Rail has been investigating the means to manage rail squats rationally for over five years. The experience and the data collected has led to a series of improvements, with promising results based on initial results. The present work builds on previous research and discusses the effectiveness of the implemented initiatives. New findings still pending more investigation are also introduced, such as: (a) the optimization of grinding parameters for light axle load networks. (b) The introduction of friction induced martensite (FIM) helps to explain the initiation of squats in areas where this type of defects is not expected. (c) New non-destructive testing methods were introduced. Most noticeably the use of vehicle mounted eddy current to detect FIM. (d) The initial results obtained by pre-profiling vertical rail in turnouts to improve steering is discussed. Growth rate of thermally initiated squats in bi-directional track is also presented and discussed. Gauge variation and changes in steering characteristics inside turnouts also helps to better understand squats. The present works close with a discussion of the results for effective squats management and future directions to improve track reliability.

Abstract The paper outlines the development of the NSW Main West line linking Sydney through Lithgow to Orange that was completed in 1877 as single track for most of its length. During the 1914-1922 period, duplication and grade easing between Lithgow and Orange was undertaken at the expense of 15 km of additional distance and with many tight radius curves. Current Sydney to Orange passenger train (XPT) times are 4h 44m for a 323 km journey. This paper starts from the premise that an average transit speed of 80 km/h should be the minimum achievable in this day and age, equivalent to a four-hour journey time between Sydney and Orange. The authors look at how such a gain might be achieved and to what extent even better times might be achieved with more substantial improvements. This paper complements earlier papers by the authors including strategies to reduce train transit times on each of the Sydney – Newcastle, Sydney - Wollongong and Sydney – Canberra lines.

Title New methods of assessing stray current interference using simulation software
 
Author Urban, R
Affiliation Beca Pty Ltd
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL PLUS 2019, Delivering growth; creating opportunity; embracing technology, 3-5 December 2019, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract Both DC and AC rail traction systems utilise the rails as a partial return current path. These rails are generally isolated from earth with various forms of insulation but the accumulation of pollution across this insulation and contact between the rails and ballast results in a small amount of the return current leaking into the ground. The stray currents that enter the soil are conducted through earthed metallic structures and services that parallel the tracks. This creates a corrosion risk in parallel structures and services subjected to DC stray currents. AC stray currents may affect neutral voltages in nearby power systems and create a risk of nuisance tripping. Both DC and AC stray currents may also cause interference to sensitive receivers, such as track circuits, medical equipment and sensitive research equipment, even up to a few kilometres from the rail alignment. Beca has developed a number of DC and AC stray current modelling methods using simulation software and has applied these methods to assess stray current effects on existing assets as well as for the design of new rail systems. The accuracy of the calculation methods has been verified by current injection and interference current tests.

Publication Date 2019-12
Abstract As new measurement technologies are developed, ARTC is challenging the existing clearance outlines to be more flexible to meet the demands of our customers. Operators often seek permission to move loads that may infringe the existing load outlines known as an out of gauge movement. ARTC have varying rolling stock and loading outlines permissible for each rail corridor that are published in our Route Access Standard. Operating outside of these loads requires an “Out of Gauge Load” application. The applications are submitted to ARTC and are analysed on a case by case basis, operating restrictions may then be applied where applicable. The introduction of laser scanning equipment such as Trimble GEDO, has allowed ARTC to gather more efficient and detailed information on the clearances for both structures and track centrelines on the ARTC Network. Clearance information is captured, exported as point cloud and rail geometry data is used in specialised CAD software to model variable rolling stock and loading kinematic envelopes through the scanned corridor point cloud. This new technology has created opportunities for ARTC to evaluate and define allowances and where necessary apply operating restrictions for our customers to move loads that may not previously have been allowed.

Title Predictive mathematical modeling of the flash-butt welding process to optimize the properties of welds of premium and super premium rails
 
Author Alves, LHD
Lagares, ML
Moreia Filho, RM
Tepedino, T
Affiliation Federal University of Juiz de Fora, Juiz de Fora, Brazil
State University of São Paulo, São Paulo, Brazil
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract This work presents the results of mathematical modeling to predict the properties of the weld and regions affected by welding of rails used in heavy haul. The Taguchi method was used for its development, with an L8 orthogonal arrangement and four repetitions. The 32 experiments were carried out at the Vale-EFC welding shop. The factors studied were: pulse current, number of pulses, upset force, and equivalent carbon, calculated from the chemical composition of premium and super premium rails. The response variables were: HAZ size, drop in hardness, and softened area width. After the welds were done, they were ultrasounded and cut for metallographic characterizations (micro and macro structures) and mechanical tests. The predictive models provided by the Taguchi method for the factors studied and their interactions, considering all the response variables jointly, as well as the Signal/Noise ratios generated, demonstrated that the best configuration occurs with the factors "current", "number of pulses", and "equivalent carbon" near the low level and the "upset force" at the higher level.

Title Water damage in cracked prestressed concrete sleepers
 
Author Bastos, JC
Dersh, MS
Edwards, JR
Barken, CPL
Affiliation RailTEC, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, Illinois, USA
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Unlike some concrete beams, concrete sleepers are typically designed not to crack under service loads, a conservative approach to prevent premature failures, especially when aiming for infinite fatigue life. Despite design considerations, however, concrete sleepers may sometimes crack at its center top region when center binding support conditions develop in heavy haul axle load environments. The cracks open during each axle pass, but tend to close in the absence of wheel loads due to the action of prestressing forces. Because sleepers are exposed to weather conditions, it is likely that precipitation will enter the cracks. Any accumulated water would be expelled (or trapped) when the prestressing elements force the cracks to close in the absence of wheel loads. Therefore, this may result in water flow in cracks, which can lead to concrete deterioration. This work assesses the effect of the presence of water inside cracks on the life of pretensioned concrete sleepers through laboratory experiments with scaled beams and full-size concrete sleepers subjected to cyclic loading in moist conditions. Results show accelerated deterioration when water is present, which is an indication that allowing pretensioned concrete sleepers to crack would be inadvisable in railway lines located in areas of high precipitation.

Title Key issues in evaluating the weldability of rail steels for heavy haul railways
 
Author Cookson, JM
Micheletto, AJ
Qui, C
Mutton, PJ
Chen, B
Affiliation Monash University, Melbourne, Australia
Source Prof. C. Cole, Centre for Railway Engineering, Central Queensland University, Rockhampton, Queensland, Australia
Conference 12th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2019), Achieving breakthrough performance levels, 12-14 June, 2019, Narvik, Norway
Publication Date 2019
Abstract Rail steels used for heavy haul applications are using progressively more alloying elements, in conjunction with heat treatment, to increase hardness and reduce the sensitivity to wear and rolling contact fatigue. However this can mean the rails are significantly more difficult to weld. This is a particular issue for mobile flashbutt welding machines, as these generally have lower power and lower forging capacity than larger fixed machines. As a result, the bond line region may be more prone to develop defects leading to significantly reduced strength. In the context of flashbutt welding, especially for mobile welding machines, “weldability” also needs to include the influence of the composition on the risk of forming bond line defects. Weldability in this context is also affected by the presence of segregation in the rail, so variable segregation levels in the rail needs to be included in an objective way to provide a sound basis to rate a rail grade. Possible options to quantify segregation need to be practical, but also provide sufficient resolution to be meaningful; these include advanced ultrasonic techniques.

Publication Date 2019
Abstract The choice of railroad track maintenance intervals aims to balance the trade-off between maintenance costs and costs resulting from low-quality track, including those arising from potential safety hazards. Track's geometry deteriorates through time, and a good maintenance strategy should keep the track's condition at an acceptable level; this can be achieved by monitoring various important parameters and ensuring they are under control. One such parameter used to assess a track's geometry condition is the track's twist, defined as the amount by which the difference in elevation of rails increases or decreases in a given length of track. In this work, spatial-time data from a railroad company operating in Brazil is considered and a Kriging model with a Gaussian correlation function is used to study a strategic portion of the track used for heavy-haul transport. In particular, four years of training data are used to build Ordinary and Limit Kriging models, and an extrapolation for the fifth year is made and compared with actual test data. A CUSUM control chart approach with a correction for autocorrelated data is proposed to identify out-of-control track sections. Then, a simple Markov Chain model based on out-of-control points is proposed and used to compare different maintenance policies aimed at cost minimization for different thresholds of twist standard deviation. We find that the spatiotemporal approach can be very promising in the monitoring of track statistics.

Abstract In France, a thorough study on the fatigue contact defect called « squat » began about ten years ago. Title Performance-based measurement of track strength & derailment testing
 
Author Kalay, S
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
Conference World Congress on Railway Research (WCRR): optimizing costs and productivity while respecting the environment, 16-19 November 1997, Florence, Italy
Publication Date 1997
Abstract Substantial savings could be realized by the railroads if positive identification of weak track locations associated with defective ties, poor ballast and subgrade locations, and rails having with high thermal stresses can be improved using performance-based track strength measurement systems such as the Track Loading Vehicle (TLV). Revenue track tests conducted using the TLV show that it is feasible to implement automated inspection methods to effectively measure the available track strength, to identify and repair the weak spots, and to develop and implement long-term, performance-based track maintenance. To address the North American Railroad industry's goal of improving the quality of service to its customers, the Association of American Railroads (AAR) embarked on a major research project several years ago. The objective was to develop and implement automated inspection methods for additional effective measurement of the available track strength, and to identify and repair the weak spots before increased maintenance is needed. The TLV was designed and constructed by the AAR. It has successfully tested several thousand miles of revenue track measuring track lateral, gage widening strength, and rail longitudinal forces. The TLV utilizes a number of interchangeable load bogies which are attached to the car frame applying loads using the vertical actuators suspended from the car body and measures responses while stationary or moving. This paper presents the test results from track gage widening, panel shift, rail uplift, and derailment tests conducted at the Transportation Technology Center (TIC) in Pueblo, Colorado and includes testing performed on various North American Railroads.
