Title Aluminothermic head repair weld (HRW) method for railway applications
 
Author Gehrmann, R
Keichel, J

Affiliation Elektro-Thermit GmbH & Co. KG, Halle, Germany

Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Track maintenance and the reduction of associated maintenance costs are essential to keep the permanent way in good condition. Localized rail defects, like squats, are usually single defects in the head of the rail that do not affect the rail over a long distance. Depending on the severity of the defect, the rail either needs to be cut out and replaced by a plug rail, or it may be possible to repair the defect in the rail head. One of the most common welding methods employed for rail head defect repair is shielded metal arc welding (SMAW). As an alternative process, the aluminothermic welding method known as a âHead Repair Weldâ (HRW) can be used for the repair of rail head defects. Aluminothermic welding is a well known and proven welding process for various types of rail grades and continuous welded rail (CWR). The application of the aluminothermic technology for the repair of rail head defects offers an inexpensive and reliable repair welding process. Here, the HRW process will be described and the resulting properties of the head repair welds will be presented. The advantages and limitations of the aluminothermic head repair method will also be discussed.

Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Both track works planning and secure train operation require feasible long-term and short-term rail temperature predictions to prevent track buckling hazards which may occur under high compression thermal stresses for welded track and joint track alike. Conversely, chord straightening may occur under traction thermal stresses. From a research which has been conducted for several years by SNCF, rail temperature predictions are now being made available from climatologic and meteorological data. This applies to long-term as well as to short-term predictions. An extensive rail and ambient temperature sampling has first been performed over at least 5 years at 29 different track locations spread over SNCFâs network. From these measurements, close relationships between air and rail temperature could be established depending upon location and year period. For short-term daytime predictions, more meteorological factors like wind, rain or clouds are playing a role and may randomly but significantly alter day to day maximum rail temperature. Based upon the above results, software tools with a specific railway interface (network maps, lines, mileposts) have been developed and tested by SNCF. They are now being installed for its planning and operational services to better choose track-works periods or perform track inspections.

Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Independent studies have proven that modern railroads, especially heavy haul lines,are increasingly affected by rolling contact fatigue (RCF) problems (head-checks, squats).Furthermore, continuously increasing railway traffic reduces the availability of tracks for railmaintenance and increases the costs of possession time. To prevent RCF, a new rail grinding method called High Speed Grinding (HSG) has been developed. Because of working speeds of 60-100 km/h (35-60 mph), this technique is able to grind long distances in short closing periods or even without interrupting scheduled traffic at all. HSG has been in regular use on tracks in Europe since 2007. The approach follows the most consequent way of preventive rail maintenance by using grinding cycles as often as necessary to keep rails RCF free and the results of systematic measurements prove the effectiveness of the technology. Measurements conducted before and after grinding cycles have shown that even after several years the rail is maintained and displays a smooth cross and longitudinal profile with no indication of degradation. Drawing on the experience of maintaining some of the busiest railroad lines in Europe, this paper discusses potential solutions for upcoming difficulties on modern heavy haul lines, with a special focus on limited track access for rail maintenance.

Abstract Queensland Rail (QRN) commenced rail profiling operations in 1982, using a Rail Planing Machine. The initial focus of this operation was to reshape the head of existing flat based rail to cater for newly introduced tapered wheels. This process generally required 10 or more passes per rail at approximately 1.5km/hr to achieve the desired shape. In 1987, a 32 stone, 15kW, rail grinder was purchased and designated as MMY003. MMY003 worked in consist with the planer to remove rail flow, corrugation, and minor surface anomalies, and to establish a consistent rail shape across 5,500 kilometres of the most utilised track of a network total of 9,500 kilometres. When the establishment work was complete in 1995, the planer was decommissioned and the grinder continued to maintain rail condition on a cyclic preventive maintenance program. With traffic loading increasing during the nineties, it was recognised that greater grinding capacity was required. In 1997 a 40 stone grinder (designated MMY019) was purchased with higher powered 18.5kW grind motors and automated stone angle adjustment. MMY003, which was due to be shut down, was upgraded in 2001 and reused as a turnout grinder with a process developed to treat 90% of the turnout rails.

Abstract Stationary plants as well as mobile machines are used for flash butt welding. Historically, stationary plants were set up to support construction of a railway and were subsequently used for maintenance and renewal programs. The mobile machines were primarily used for on-site welding. A stationary plant generally provides better quality control and improved productivity but requires large capital investment, lead time for installation and long welded rail trains. Mobile machines are more flexible but require stricter controls to achieve required quality and are inefficient when demand is spread on wider areas. Capacity utilisation and resultant inefficiencies due to idling of productive asset is a continuing challenge with both modes. With the advancement of mobile flash butt machine technology in the recent years, the issues associated with the predictability of weld quality have been progressively addressed. This has supported the industry trend of hybrid plants which can be easily and efficiently switched between stationary and mobile modes. Selection of an appropriate flash butt welding solution for a particular rail project or network is influenced by a number of factors. This paper provides an outline of the key drivers that influence the selection of flash butt welding solution in different contexts, considering the current state of commercially available technology options available to a railway. The paper makes recommendation on the selection of an appropriate flash butt welding option and solution for various categories of requirements to assist project planners and network managers in making effective and efficient decisions.
