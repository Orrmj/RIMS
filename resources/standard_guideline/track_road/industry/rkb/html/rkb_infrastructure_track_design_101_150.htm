Title Development of the special trackform for Tsing Ma suspension bridge, Hong Kong
 
Author Bishop, PE
Davis, SG
Affiliation Connell Wagner Ply Ltd, Melbourne, Australia
Mott Connell Ltd, Hong Kong
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)
Conference CORE 1995: World Best Practice - The What, Why and How, Conference on Railway Engineering, 29 October-1 November 1995, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 1995
Abstract A new 30km long passenger railway will provide a vital transport link between Hong Kong's new airport and the City Centre. Along the route the railway will pass over the 1,377 metre span Tsing Ma Suspension Bridge which will be the longest combined road/rail suspension bridge in the world. The railway trackform for the bridge was designed in steel for minimum self weight and with an isolation system to maximise attenuation of noise and vibration to the steel deck structure of the bridge. With a line speed of 130kmh and a target 120 year design life, the trackform was carefully designed using dynamic analysis and full scale prototype testing. The substantial thermal and liveload movements of the bridge were catered for by a purpose-designed rail movement joint which was also subject to fatigue testing and full scale prototype testing. The "cohabitation" of the road and rail operations on the bridge was examined in a preliminary hazards assessment and the trackform was the subject of a quantified risk analysis. The outcome of prototype testing validated the dynamic and static design processes and fabrication of trackform components is proceeding on schedule.

Title Providing capacity on regional lines by the strategic installation of steel sleepers
 
Author Schweiger, R
Affiliation OneSteel Trak-Lok
Source Informa
Conference AusRAIL 2008, Capacity, Climate & Culture, 1-2 December 2008, Melbourne, Victoria, Australia
Publication Date 2008
Abstract A railway track structure must be cost effective relative to the traffic task required by a railway. This is especially the case on regional lines where the traffic task is relatively low and there are long lengths of track to be maintained with only limited funds available. Railways have found that a combination of steel and timber sleepers has provided the improved capacity and cost reductions to meet their ongoing business requirements. At the same time axle loads on intrastate lines have steadily increased and existing track structures are reaching their limits of capacity to carry these loads. The replacement components especially sleepers must also be capable of supporting future traffic requirements and provide sufficient versatility to accommodate the upgrading of other track components, especially rails, all at an efficient cost. The strategic installation of steel sleepers progressively from an interspersed 1 in 4 pattern to a fully steel sleepered track has the cost effectiveness, versatility and performance required to meet most regional railways current and future requirements. The railways have developed successful techniques to install steel sleepers amongst the existing timber sleepers thereby providing a cost effective solution for the improvement of track structure, whilst achieving significant track maintenance savings.

Title Macro synthetic fibres for innovative concrete track slab design and construction
 
Author Clarke, T
Affiliation Elasto Plastic Concrete
Source ATRI
Conference Australian Society for Concrete Pavements Conference, 4th, 2017, Kingscliff, New South Wales, Australia
Publication Date 2017-07
Abstract Concrete track slabs have recently been used as an alternative to ballast material, particularly for high speed rail. They have also become commonplace in metro light rail construction, where the rail corridor is shared by road traffic, and the rail is embedded into the concrete track slab. Concrete track slabs are used as they provide a longer design life and require less maintenance than traditional ballast systems. Their application is seen more regularly in situations, such as tunnels or bridges, where ballast is difficult to place, or using it impacts on the alignment of the structure. Traditional concrete track slabs have higher construction costs than ballasted systems, making the option less attractive. The use of macro synthetic fibre reinforcement allows a significant reduction in the initial cost of using concrete track slabs, by increasing construction speeds, as there is no need to cut, place and tie conventional steel reinforcement, while removing the durability concerns associated with stray current corrosion of reinforcement. This paper aims to initiate discussion between track slab designers, owners and operators on the use of macro synthetic fibres in track slab construction and includes some international case studies. The design and construction practices that were used for each of these will be discussed. It will provide an outline of the methods used for design and analysis of the macro synthetic fibre reinforced track slabs, as well as guidance on construction practices that can be used to optimise the installation and performance of the track slab.

Title Non-intrusive assessment of trackbed and pavement conditions
 
Author Gilbert, PJ
Alobaidi, I
Gascoyne, J
Smith, V
Source CRC for Rail Innovation
Conference Railway Foundations: RailFound 06: 1st International conference on railway foundations: applied research and application to underpin improved railway track design and performance, 11-13 September 2006, Birmingham, UK
Publication Date 2006
Abstract This paper describes a methodology for assessing the asset condition of road or rail foundations by non-intrusive methods. The methodology has been developed through a road research programme and rail projects undertaken in the UK and Europe. The method combines track performance data, non-destructive scanning by high speed ground penetrating radar ('GPR'), track performance data, and various earthworks asset information sources to enable an assessment to be made of likely sub-grade conditions and subdivide a route into a series of performance categories. The results have been used as a planning stage of trackbed design projects. High speed geophysics and a risk based approach have been used to determine a cost model for upgrade of part of a Trans European railway. Bringing existing data sets together in this way and overlaying current GPR data enables an initial assessment of sites, minimising the need for intrusive ground investigation (GI) and enabling that GI to be tailored to suite the nature of the problems. This has significant benefits in terms of programme, cost and safety.

Abstract A concrete track is very susceptible for the settlement, since its rehabilitation requires much time and cost. When a concrete track is constructed on fine-grained sub grade soil, reasonable predictions of excessive cumulative plastic settlements are necessary for the effective track design and maintenance.
