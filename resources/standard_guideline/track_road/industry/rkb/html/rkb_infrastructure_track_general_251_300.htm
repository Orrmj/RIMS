Queensland Rail (QR)
Source Railway Technical Society of Australasia (RTSA)

Publication Date 2000
Abstract Collaborative research agreements between Queensland Rail and Central Queensland University resulted in the development of a train dynamics test platform, the Multi-Wagon System (MWS). The MWS was used to record dynamic parameters on six wagons and driver actions and dynamic parameters at the locomotive over sixteen hundred kilometres of track from Brisbane to Cairns and return for a series of tests. Each test involved revenue-raising trains that were marshalled for load destination. Queensland Rail's driver training simulators and the Centre for Railway Engineering's longitudinal simulators were used to replay the recorded driving strategies. The train dynamics, fuel and brake' usage, and running times resulting from these simulator runs were then compared with a range of alternative driving strategies. Driving strategies were developed for this particular section of track that optimised the three criteria:
1. Driving to meet schedule.
2. Driving to minimise energy consumption.
3. Driving to minimise train dynamics.
The paper presents the details of the track section, details of the driving strategies that were recorded; details of the driving strategies that were trialed on the simulators and detailed analysis of the train dynamics that resulted from each strategy with respect to the above criteria.

Title Comparison of the fatigue and impact fracture behavior of 5 different steel grades used in the frog of a turnout
 
Author Eck, S
Ossberger, H
Ossberger, U
Marsoner, S
Ebner, R
Affiliation Materials Centre Leoben Forschung GmbH, Leoben, Austria
VAE GmbH, Zeltweg, Austria
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference 10th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2013), Capacity Building through Heavy Haul Operation, 4th-6th February, 2013, New Delhi, India.
Publication Date 2013
Abstract The material requirements for the frog of a turnout are quite complex. The region of the frog represents a discontinuity in the track as the wheel needs to change from one rail to the other. This causes increased dynamic contact forces and slip between wheel and frog. The high contact pressures and the slip can cause damage such as wear, rolling contact fatigue and severe plastic deformation in the frog. The frog material plays a crucial role in the development of damage. On the one hand, the plastic material response can reduce the contact pressures by an adaption of the frog geometry. On the other hand, materials have a different resistance to wear, crack initiation and growth. To clarify the requirements for frog materials, five different steel grades that are used as frogs in various railway turnouts were investigated. The chosen materials were “Hadfield steel” Mn-13, abrasion resistant grain refined steel with 400 HV, bainitic railway steel, tempered steel 51CrV4 (DIN 1.8159) and maraging steel. They were investigated in the same condition as they were used in service, covering a variety of steel microstructures and macroscopic hardness values. To compare the material response to the high impact forces, their impact fracture toughness was measured and selected fracture surfaces were investigated with scanning electron microscopy (SEM). To compare their response to the cyclic loading, the frog materials were tested with respect to their monotonic tensile strength as well as fatigue strength by means of low cycle fatigue (LCF) tests. Measurements of the fracture toughness KIC were performed on all materials to compare their fracture resistance. The achieved results shall serve as material data base for finite element (FE) based calculations of the local loading conditions in railway turnouts. Additionally, the comparison shall help to choose the best material for specific loading conditions, i.e. specific crossing geometries and track loading conditions.

Title Effect of heavy axle loads on super premium rail steels and rail joining methods
 
Author Szablewski, D
Gutscher, D
LoPresti, J
Kalay, S
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc.
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference 10th International Heavy Haul Conference, (IHHA 2013), Capacity Building through Heavy Haul Operation, 4th-6th February, 2013, New Delhi, India.
Publication Date 2013
Abstract North American heavy axle load (HAL) freight traffic requires rail with superior mechanical and wear performance to maintain rail-life cycles at acceptable levels. Rail manufacturers have been steadily improving rail performance and the implemented innovations can be credited with extending rail life. The improvements we see today were accomplished through either chemistry modifications (with increased carbon content targeted at wear improvement in particular) or thermomechanical processing changes to attain the desired microstructural improvements in the rail. As a result of these modifications, head hardness has steadily climbed over the last two decades. Transportation Technology Center, Inc. (TTCI) has been testing premium rail steels for the past 20 years. The current test is comprised of 10 super premium rail grades with an average head hardness of 407 Brinell hardness (HB). These rails are being tested in a 349-meter (m) radius dry curve under a ~35-tonne/axle load train running at a speed of ~64 kilometers per hour (km/h). Current tonnage accumulation in this curve is ~350 million gross metric tonnes (MGMT). Results presented in this work detail the wear performance of these rail grades as well as their resistance to internal and surface fatigue. The increase in super premium rail hardness and strength is accompanied by challenges to rail weld performance. In particular, the heat affected zone (HAZ), which is located immediately adjacent to the weld, effectively eliminates the benefits of the rail thermo-mechanical processing achieved during rail manufacture. Metallurgical changes that occur in the HAZ result in a significant decrease in hardness. Since railhead hardness of the super premium rails is steadily increasing with each consecutive generation, the disparity between the head hardness of the rails and the hardness of the HAZ are increasing. This creates a greater variation in the material properties for the soft HAZ in super premium rails than for the soft HAZ in standard rails, where the difference in hardness is lower. The hardness difference results in greater HAZ batter at the running surface. As a result, welds that may perform well in standard rails can experience accelerated degradation in super premium rails. The work presented here addresses observed degradation mechanisms and HAZ mitigation methods.
