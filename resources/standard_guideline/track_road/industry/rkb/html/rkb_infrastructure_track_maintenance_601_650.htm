Title Rolling contact fatigue of rails: characteristics, causes and treatments
 
Author Grassie, SL
Kalousek, J
Affiliation Loram Rail Limited, Glasgow, United Kingdom
National Research Council, Vancouver, Canada
Conference 6th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1997), 6-10 April, 1997, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 1997
Abstract RCF defects, including head checks, squats, shells, and tache ovales cost railway and mass transit operators around the globe hundreds of millions of dollars per annum. If the transverse cracks initiating from shells, squats and head checks are not detected early they can lead to broken rails and subsequent derailments. This paper reviews the current understanding of rolling contact defects including their characteristics, cause of development and means by which they can be alleviated. The reduction in RCF can be achieved by following clean steel practices, adopting specific rail and wheel profiles, and minor or major modifications to bogies. Rail grinding, however, is the most effective and widely accepted method of combating surface initiated head checks and squats. Lubrication and friction management, in synergy with grinding, clean steel practice and bogie modifications, can completely eliminate an appreciable number of rolling contact defects.

Title Requirements for transverse railhead profile and railhead roughness following grinding
 
Author Grassie, SL
Affiliation Loram Rail Ltd, Kirkintilloch, UK
Conference 6th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1997), 6-10 April, 1997, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 1997
Abstract A field test was established to examine the significance of the tolerance on transverse railhead profile and of small-scale roughness remaining after grinding to a specified profile. Test sites in three curves were ground with a variety of tolerances and subsequently monitored. Head checks recurred quickly in one curve while the profile elsewhere changed insignificantly, in both cases regardless of the tolerance on transverse profile. The tolerance accordingly appears to be relatively unimportant in circumstances such as these, in which improved life of the rail and performance of the vehicle/track system might instead be obtained by adopting entirely different rail or wheel profiles. Surface roughness is reduced by an order of magnitude, to about 1 micron R., after the passage of less than 50,000 tonnes of traffic.

Title The track system and its maintenance
 
Author Lichtberger, BW
Affiliation Franz Plasser, Linz, Austria
Conference 6th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 1997), 6-10 April, 1997, Cape Town, South Africa
Publication Date 1997
Abstract A high quality of the track will ensure low-maintenance, stable behaviour of the track, even under high traffic volume, rising travelling speeds and heavier axle loads. The track should be regarded as a whole, i.e. as a "system". The individual parts of the system, from the rail, the sleeper, through the plasto-elastic properties of the ballast bed to the formation protective layer and the subsoil are the components for the inherent track quality. The main task of these components is to transmit and dissipate the forces caused by the trains into the subsoil. Insufficient bearing strength of the track can be remedied in most cases by subsoil rehabilitation involving the insertion of a formation protective layer (FPL). The following article describes the most efficient methods of mechanised formation rehabilitation. Computer-assisted methods are used to an increasing extent to achieve a high initial quality of the track. A further important emphasis in this article is therefore to introduce new computer-assisted control and regulating systems.
