Title Rail seat deterioration evaluation and attenuation with polyurethane polymers
 
Author Loomis, R

Pagni, A
Affiliation Willamette Valley Company, Eugene, Oregon, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Concrete railroad ties have become more widely used in tracks worldwide, and the need to extend their service life through tie design and in-field repair has become increasingly important. Several concrete tie designs and repair methods have been deployed to combat rail seat deterioration based on empirical test results and ahead of a full understanding of the deterioration mechanism. Current test methods using a complete rail assembly on a concrete tie are time consuming, expensive, and complex to perform. Furthermore, the results of these tests are often qualitative, making comparisons relative and transient. In this study, dynamic cyclic load testing based on AREMA standards was performed and rail seat deterioration results were evaluated 2- dimentionally with photographs, as well as, topographically with a specially designed instrument. In one of the tests, rail seat deterioration was reduced by 76% when CTR-100-20 was applied to the rail seat as compared to non-coated concrete ties.

Title Track model validation for simulation of train–turnout dynamics
 
Author Palsson, B
Nielsen, JCO
Affiliation Department of Applied Mechanics / CHARMEC, Chalmers University of Technology, Gothenburg, Sweden
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Based on track stiffness and wheel–rail contact forces measured in a demonstrator turnout (switch & crossing, S&C), a viscously damped track model for simulation of vehicle dynamics is developed and validated. Track stiffness at nominal wheel loads, in the frequency range up to 20 Hz, was measured using a rolling stiffness measurement vehicle (RSMV). Vertical and lateral contact forces were measured by an instrumented wheelset mounted in a freight car featuring Y25 bogies. The measurements were performed for traffic both in the through and diverging routes, and in the facing and trailing moves. The full set of test runs was repeated with different types of rail pad to investigate the influence of track stiffness on contact forces. It is concluded that wheel–rail impact loads at the crossing can be reduced by using more resilient rail pads. The track model is a mass-spring-damper model that is moving with each wheelset in the vehicle model. Reasonable agreement between simulated and measured vertical contact forces at the crossing can be obtained when using a track model with one vertical degree of freedom (dof) per rail, each with neglected inertia, coupled to one track mass with one vertical and one rotational dof. This model can be tuned to capture the large phase delay in dynamic track stiffness at low frequencies, as measured by the RSMV, and remaining sufficiently resilient at higher frequencies.

Abstract With the emergence of high speed passenger rail (“HSR”) in North America, there will be increased pressure on freight railroads to collaborate with public transportation agencies in the establishment of “shared use” corridors. Title A wheel passing a frog nose- dynamic finite element investigation of high axle loads
 
Author Pletz, M
Daves, W
Ossberger, H
Affiliation Materials Center Leoben Forschung GmbH, Leoben, Austria
VAE GmbH, Zeltweg, Austria
Institute of Mechanics, Montanuniversität Leoben, Leoben, Austria
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Turnouts are an important part of the railway track system. They consist of a switch and a crossing panel. In this work, the dynamic process in the area of the crossing panel is studied. There, a vertical impact of the wheel on the crossing nose (facing direction) or on the wing rail (opposite direction) occurs. In these areas of high dynamic loading, also slip between the wheel and the crossing parts occur. Due to that, the crossing parts feature high wear and high damage. Crossings thus have a big influence on the maintenance costs of the tracks. For the service or the replacement of crossings in the track, there are only small time intervals. This is another reason for the importance of increasing the service time and the lifetime of the crossing parts. One approach to increase the turnout’s performance is to optimize the impacts to minimize the dynamic effects. Numerical models play an important role in this optimization. However, for a complex system as the crossing with its wing rails and the crossing nose as shown in Figure 1, it is a huge task to develop realistic numerical models. These simulations should regard all main influences like e.g. the dynamic impact, the slip between the wheel and the crossing parts and remain able to calculate the resulting stresses and strains in the crossing. During the passing of a wheel over the crossing, there is an area where the wheel is both in contact with the wing rail and the crossing nose. This area is of particular interest and will be called the transition area. In the literature, simplified models are reported that investigate the impact of the wheel on the crossing nose either analytically or using geometrically simplified finite element (FE) models. The other approaches are multi-body simulations in which the whole train and track masses are regarded as connected by spring- and damper elements. These models are able only to describe elastic material behaviour only and have certain limitations in the calculation of the contact points. Such simulations are done by Kassa et al. Based on the multi-body simulations, a method for the change of the crossings geometry due to several wheel passages (caused by wear and plastification) is presented by Johannsson et al. In the work done by Wiest et al., results of the multi-body simulations are used as boundary conditions in a model to investigate the local contact situation on the wing rail and the crossing nose.

Title Frost susceptibility of railway subballast materials
 
Author Pylkkänen, K
Nurmikolu, A
Affiliation Tampere University of Technology, Tampere, Finland
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract In the areas of cold climate frost action in ballast, subballast or subgrade can cause severe problems in maintaining the smoothness of track in required level. The major concern in frost protection is to avoid the freezing of frost susceptible subsoil by using sufficient thickness of subballast and relying on the material itself being non-frost-susceptible. In this paper the role of subballast materials of the existing rail network of Finland is studied in respect to their possible cause behind repeated frost heave problems in 30 sites observed during winters 2003-2007. In the sites the calculated frost depth did not reach the level of subsoil. Frost susceptibility of 60 subballast samples was evaluated by grading analyses and constant temperature frost heave tests. About 60% of the samples exceeded the limits for practically non-frost-susceptible materials suggested in earlier studies. This indicates that frost susceptibility of subballast may be a cause of frost damages on certain spots of Finnish railway network. Still over 25% of subballast samples were classified to be non-frost-susceptible although the sampling was targeted to frost problem locations, which shows that also other causes exist.

Title Analysis of damaging factors in thermite welds through multi-axial fatigue criterion
 
Author Salehi, I
Mutton, PJ
Kapoor, A
Affiliation Faculty of Engineering and Industrial Sciences, Swinburne University of Technology, Hawthorn, Vic, Australia
Institute of Railway Technology, Monash University, Clayton, Vic, Australia
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Thermite welding is widely used in heavy haul railway systems for in-track welding due to the low capital cost of equipment and the flexibility it provides. However thermite welds typically exhibit variable and often inferior performance compared to flashbutt welds. Fatigue failure as a result of surface defects or harsh geometrical features in the presence of high axle loads and tractive forces gives rise to the so-called straight break and horizontal split web fractures. In this study, the reduction of fatigue performance due to the geometrical inhomogeneity and the presence of defects is investigated using a multi-axial high cycle fatigue criterion based on the concept of critical plane. A thermo-structural finite element model of a track containing a thermite weld is linked with a customized computer code to perform fatigue crack initiation analysis; the effect of inclusions and defects is implicitly invoked through their effect on material fatigue parameters. Two geometrically different thermite welds which are widely used in Australian heavy haul railways are investigated in terms of fatigue probability, to facilitate a better insight into the effect of weld collar shape and toe geometrical parameters on fatigue behaviour. The effect of track support conditions on fatigue damage is also included in the analysis, which enables the effect of ballast deterioration, a common problem at the vicinity of thermite welds, to be quantified. The results show that the amount of fatigue damage critically depends on the geometrical features of the collar edge, especially at the underhead fillet which undergoes severe fatigue at some operational conditions. The condition of track support also shows to be highly influential in the probability of fatigue crack nucleation at regions of the weld foot.

Title The mechanisms of squat initiation and growth on train rails
 
Author Steenbergen, MJMM
van Bezooijen, GW
Dollevoet, RPBJ
Affiliation Delft University of Technology, Delft, the Netherlands
ID2, Kedichem, the Netherlands
ProRail, Utrecht, the Netherlands
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract A theory is presented for the mechanisms of the origination and subsequent growth stages ofsquat defects on train rails. The initiation is explained as a wedge-shaped, transverse failuremechanism of the strain-hardened and anisotropic top layer of the rail, developing within thetheoretically elliptical contact patch, and driven by the transverse shear component of the wheel load. During the early-stage growth after occurrence of the mechanism, the originally semiellipsoidal normal stresses in the contact area redistribute around the failed wedge, which becomes enveloped by a dark-coloured and double-lobbed ‘hardness wall’. During the secondstage growth the hardness envelope is pressed into the deeper elastic material, accompanied by a gradual expansion of the contact band as a function of the rail surface deformation in normal direction. The inherent surface settlement triggers a dynamic wheel-rail interaction, and impact loading leads to material crumbling and fatal crack growth. Available field evidence supports the presented theory in all aspects.

Title Rail grinding within scheduled traffic
 
Author Taubert, M
Hartenstein, M
Affiliation Vossloh Rail Services GmbH, Seevetal, Germany
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Independent studies have proven that modern railroads, especially heavy haul lines,are increasingly affected by rolling contact fatigue (RCF) problems (head-checks, squats).Furthermore, continuously increasing railway traffic reduces the availability of tracks for railmaintenance and increases the costs of possession time. To prevent RCF, a new rail grinding method called High Speed Grinding (HSG) has been developed. Because of working speeds of 60-100 km/h (35-60 mph), this technique is able to grind long distances in short closing periods or even without interrupting scheduled traffic at all. HSG has been in regular use on tracks in Europe since 2007. The approach follows the most consequent way of preventive rail maintenance by using grinding cycles as often as necessary to keep rails RCF free and the results of systematic measurements prove the effectiveness of the technology. Untreated sections show growing RCF problems, while grinded sections (under identical basic conditions) indicate no signs of RCF. Measurements conducted before and after grinding cycles have shown that even after several years the rail is maintained and displays a smooth cross and longitudinal profile with no indication of degradation. Drawing on the experience of maintaining some of the busiest railroad lines in Europe, this paper discusses potential solutions for upcoming difficulties on modern heavy haul lines, with a special focus on limited track access for rail maintenance.

Title Rail maintenance improvements through capital investment and technological advancements
 
Author Turner, M
Cadman, N
Daftari, D
Smith, T
Affiliation QR National, Brisbane, Australia
Loram Maintenance of Way Inc., Hamel, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Queensland Rail (QRN) commenced rail profiling operations in 1982, using a Rail Planing Machine. The initial focus of this operation was to reshape the head of existing flat based rail to cater for newly introduced tapered wheels. This process generally required 10 or more passes per rail at approximately 1.5km/hr to achieve the desired shape. In 1987, a 32 stone, 15kW, rail grinder was purchased and designated as MMY003. MMY003 worked in consist with the planer to remove rail flow, corrugation, and minor surface anomalies, and to establish a consistent rail shape across 5,500 kilometres of the most utilised track of a network total of 9,500 kilometres. Production rates greatly improved with the planer only removing the rail head flow and the faster grinder finishing the defect removal and shaping process at 3 to 4km/hr. When the establishment work was complete in 1995, the planer was decommissioned and the grinder continued to maintain rail condition on a cyclic preventive maintenance program. With traffic loading increasing during the nineties, it was recognised that greater grinding capacity was required. In 1997 a 40 stone grinder (designated MMY019) was purchased with higher powered 18.5kW grind motors and automated stone angle adjustment. MMY003, which was due to be shut down, was upgraded in 2001 and reused as a turnout grinder with a process developed to treat 90% of the turnout rails.

Title Investigation of coal dust composition affecting railroad ballast behavior
 
Author Tutumluer, E
Moaveni, M
Read, D
Li, D
Lees, H
Clark, D
Affiliation University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, IL, USA
Transportation Technology Center, Pueblo, CO, USA
BNSF, Topeka, KS, USA
Union Pacific Railroad, Omaha, NE, USA
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Railroad ballast is an important structural element in the track system that provides rail lateral stability and distributes wheel stresses on top of subballast/subgrade. The ballast layer is an assembly of uniformly graded coarse aggregate particles that are commonly crushed and clean of finer particles to provide adequate shear strength and friction, sufficient aggregate interlock, and proper drainage. Fouling refers to the condition of railroad ballast when voids in this unbound aggregate layer are filled with finer materials. Fouling has a detrimental effect on the track structure; it reduces the aggregate interlock in ballast. As a fouling agent, coal dust coming from heavy haul coal trains and accumulating in ballast has become a major concern for railroads especially during the wet conditions. Although pure coal is what is commonly transported in coal trains, samples of coal dust collected from different track locations and track substructure layers i.e., ballast, subballast, may be found as mixed with other fine particles such as plastic soil dust and mineral filler. In 2005, two derailments occurred in the BNSF/UP joint coal line in Powder River Basin (PRB), which threatened to interrupt the supply of coal to power plants. In 2008, Tutumluer et al. used a large direct shear box equipment to run shear strength tests on ballast samples that were fouled by different percentages of coal dust collected from the South PRB Orin Sub in Wyoming. They found that with an increase in coal dust percentage by weight of clean aggregate, the shear strength properties of the ballast samples decreased significantly. This paper aims to identify different coal dust compositions that may exist around heavy haul railroad track structures focusing on the characteristics of coal dust obtained from the PRB Basin in Wyoming. Findings are from an ongoing track substructure research initiative in the US aimed to investigate properties and detrimental effects of various coal dust compositions on drainage and load carrying functions of railroad ballast layers serving heavy haul coal lines.

Author Wegner, A
Hofmann, M
Radmann, P
Zhang, R
Dubbins, F
Affiliation Elektro-Thermit GmbH & Co.KG, Halle, Germany
Thermit Australia Pty Ltd, Somersby, N.S.W. Australia
Goldschmidt-Thermit GmbH, Leipzig, Germany
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2011: railroading in extreme conditions, 2011, Calgary, Alberta, Canada
Publication Date 2011
Abstract Longitudinal forces are generated in continuously welded (cw) tracks due to temperature changes. The Stress Free Temperature (SFT) defines the ratio of the stresses and therefore is a key track parameter. Safety against buckles and breaks and ride comfort depend decisively on the SFT. Particularly in times of increasing competition, increased prices of raw materials and energy as well as greater track loading, it is important to maximize drive comfort, track safety particularly against excessive stresses and to minimize track maintenance costs with maximum track availability. A new technique enabling reliable non-destructive determination of the actual SFT of the track under traffic for the first time has been further developed, completely redesigned and adapted for this purpose. Measurements to an accuracy of ±3° C and in special cases up to only ±1° C can be made at any hour without rail unfastening providing assurance of track stability and quality, reduction of maintenance costs and decreasing limits to SFT tolerances. The technology, already approved in several European countries, is based on a micro-magnetic method. Thereby, a known interaction between magnetic field and ferromagnetic material to be checked enables the determination of its load stress state. The new development and inspection examples are presented where assurance that the cw track has been welded within the required stressing tolerance was made by SFT measurements excluding any subsequent re-establishment of required stress conditions.

Abstract The railway between the iron ore mines in Kiruna, Sweden and the port in Narvik, Norway has a downward slope continuously from the national border to the harbour. Braking trains combined with weak rail fastening produce rail creep and skewed sleepers. In 2005 MTAB/MTAS started to use new IORE-locomotives as traction in the ore trains. These locomotives have both pneumatic and electro dynamic brakes. To investigate the influence of regenerative braking forces on the track in relation to rail creep, Jernbaneverket established a test site with three measuring locations; one in a curve with Pandrol fastening, another one in a transition curve also with Pandrol fastening and a third one in tangent track with Hey-Back fastening. The movements of the rails and the sleepers were measured according to fixed points alongside the track and relatively to each other. The measurements at the test site have taken place in two years since November 2004 and will continue indefinitely. The maximum regenerative braking effort has been up to 650 kN. So far the measuring results indicate small tendencies of rail creep and skewed sleepers at the test site, but there are no significant indications that this is due to utilization of electro dynamic brake on the IORE-locomotives. Maximum rail creep happens in track with Hey-Back fastening, and skewed sleepers arise mostly in curves.
