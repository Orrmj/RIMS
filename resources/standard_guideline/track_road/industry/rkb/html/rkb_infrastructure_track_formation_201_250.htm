Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Stabilisation of rail trackbed using geogrids has become established technology over many years. Applications in both rail ballast and sub-ballast have been shown to reduce the rate of track settlement and increase the level of track support respectively, offering to railway engineers practical benefits in extending the time between expensive maintenance operations. The benefits of punched and drawn biaxial geogrids have been built on extensive research into the actual effects of the geogrid when installed within the trackbed, with evidence of performance gathered both in the laboratory and on âlive lineâ sites from around the world. Since their introduction in 2007, multiaxial geogrids have demonstrated their ability to improve the performance of granular layers under trafficking still further. They have been shown to out-perform the previous generation of punched and drawn biaxial geogrids and, as a result, have given engineers the opportunity to take advantage of improved performance. Research and development programmes have taken place to demonstrate that multiaxial geogrids will improve the performance of rail trackbed. The recent award of a European Technical Approval (ETA), which defines and confirms the distinct stabilisation function for a range of multiaxial geogrids, acknowledges the benefits of confining aggregate particles during trafficking, improving the load bearing capacity and design life of granular layers in rail applications.

Title Assessment of VHAL on track: short-term test of 41- and 45-tonne axle loads
 
Author Davis, D
Klopp, A
Jimenez, R
Rael, B
Satre, E
Affiliation Transportation Technology Center, Inc., Colorado USA
Source International Heavy Haul Association (IHHA)
Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract TTCI tested the effects of overloaded rail cars on track in the following configurations:
- 5-degree curves with timber crossties
- Tangent track with timber crossties on native subgrade soil
- Tangent track with timber crossties on soft subgrade soil
- Mainline AREMA style turnout on timber crossties
The overloaded cars included two consecutive cars with gross rail loads of 163,265 KG (360 kips) (41 tonne axle loads) and two consecutive cars with gross rail loads of 181,406 KG (400 kips) (45 tonne axle loads). For comparison, consecutive cars with gross rail loads of 142,857 KG (315 kips) (36 tonne axle loads) were also included, as this is the current standard operating load at the Facility for Accelerated Service Testing at Transportation Technology Center near Pueblo, Colorado USA. The track tested is normally operated at 64.4 km/h (40 mph) and is maintained above Federal Railroad Administration Class 4 track safety standards. Under the overloaded cars, the tracks performed similar to predictions. The wheel loads applied to the track essentially increased linearly and proportionally with the increases in axle loads. The effects of speed were relatively small on track loads and deflections. This is due to the relatively high quality of the track tested and the speed range tested (at or below the normal operating speed). The variability of the measurements recorded in the tangent test section is believed to be due to the disturbance of the track for installation of ballast pressure cells prior to testing. There may have been insufficient traffic after installation to return the track to normal condition. The mainline AREMA style turnout lateral and vertical deflections showed relatively little effects from wheel load or speed over the range tested. The turnout had a standard fixed point frog, which behaved mostly uniformly regardless of axle load or train speed; however a semi-permanent deformation lasting 10 to 15 minutes was noted when the raw data measurements were analysed.

Conference International Heavy Haul (IHHA) specialist technical sessions 2015, Operational Excellence, 21st-24th June, 2015, Perth, Western Australia, Australia
Publication Date 2015
Abstract Settlement of railway track caused by the cyclic loading and vibration of network traffic, leads to degradation of geometry which therefore needs to be systematically maintained. Tamping is an effective maintenance procedure which repacks ballast particles in order to restore the correct geometrical position of track. The goal of this study has been the development of a tool to evaluate the effectiveness of track tamping. Continuously measured performance data from instrumented ore cars (IOCs) is used for the analysis. The wagon-track dynamic interaction is studied by investigating the dynamic behaviour of the IOC's suspension system. The wagon suspension response data is then utilized to identify locations where tamping has been effective or ineffective. A maintenance planning system is used to conduct predictive modelling and forecast wagon dynamic responses to identify priority tamping locations. Using linear regression, the rate of track degradation over time and locations where tamping is required can be identified. The results of this work facilitate the development and improvement of maintenance planning operations. Particular tamping strategies or equipment that has had an adverse impact on track can also be identified. It therefore becomes feasible to develop a preventative tamping program that reduces surface and lining requirements and consequently the need for the introduction of temporary speed restrictions.

Abstract Types of potential ballast cementing mechanisms are identified, and laboratory cementing tests for ballast previously used in North America are described. A laboratory study of ballast cementing was conducted using a variety of procedures based on the previous tests. Fourteen different rocks were tested. Cementing strength varied significantly with partide size of the fine rock material used to make the specimens, with consolidation pressure applied during specimen preparation, and with rock type. Carbonate rocks tended to have higher cementing strength than the igneous or metamorphic rocks, but some exceptions were noted. Correlation with field observations of ballast cementing is needed to determine the relevance of the laboratory tests and the nature of reported cementing.

Title Research, development and use of stabilisation geogrids to extend track maintenance intervals and reduce costs
 
Author Horton, MA
Dobie, M
Buckley, J
Affiliation Tensar International Limited, Blackburn, UK
Tensar International Limited, Jakarta, Indonesia
Tensar International Limited, Queensland, Australia
Conference CORE 2018: Rail: Smart, Automated, Sustainable, Conference on Railway Excellence, 30 April-2 May 2018, Sydney, NSW, Australia
Publication Date 2018
Abstract Geogrids have been incorporated in comparative testing in rail track-bed applications for many years and have shown a consistent ability to improve the settlement performance, and hence the potential to reduce maintenance cost and extend track life. Early work concentrated on the use of biaxial geogrids in laboratory environments providing full-scale testing but with the ability to contraol variables adequately. This work allowed geogrid dimensions to be optimised to suit rail aggregates to permit efficient interlock and confinement of aggregate particles and thereby preserve rail levels. Live-line testing was carried out to measure the performance of stabilised track-bed under real conditions, which has resulted in the development of guidance for designers on the use of stabilisation geogrids. With the introduction of multi-axial hexagonal geogrids and an appropriate definition of the stabilisation function, laboratory work has shown the improved performance associated with this form of geogrid through enhanced confinement of aggregate particles compared to the older biaxial product. The use of instrumented artifical ballast particles (SmartRock) has been used to track individual particle movements in full-scale laboratory testing to allow a real-time functional assessment to be made of geogrid performance. Full-scale testing has shown the potential to save on maintenance cost by extending maintenance intervals. This has recently been confirmed in a live line monitoring exercise. The paper shows the depth of research that has developed with the use of biaxial and multiaxial geogrids in rail track-bed which continues to this day, now extended into high speed rail applications.

Author Grabe, PJ
Clayton, CRI
Shaw, FJ
Affiliation Spoornet, Pretoria, South Africa
University of Southampton, United Kingdom
Source Queensland University of Technology (QUT)
Conference 8th International Heavy Haul Railway Conference, (IHHA 2005), 14 - 16 June, 2005, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Publication Date 2005
Abstract This paper describes the measurement of field resilient and permanent deformation on a heavy haul track formation. The current state of the art railway foundation design methods rely heavily on the prediction of permanent deformation due to cyclic train loading. Permanent deformation is estimated from conventional cyclic triaxial tests that do not reproduce the complex track foundation stress paths, which include the phenomenon of principal stress rotation. Hollow cylinder tests on typical track formation materials have showed that triaxial tests under-estimate permanent strain development during cyclic loading. Various aspects related to the measurement of formation deflections and stresses are described and compared to other measurement techniques and track analyses. It is shown how the field measurement of resilient and permanent deformation, combined with the results of laboratory HCA testing on similar track formation materials will form the basis of a model to predict permanent deformation and ultimately the design life of track formations.
